BSPE Cheat Sheet · built for the 3 exam questions

3 questions, 600 + 200 + 200 = 1000 words · 2 hours (needs ~1) · read all Qs, outline, then write; never exceed the word limit · A4 two-sided · S1–6 only · apply (IA)³ and NAME the tactics (his #1 complaint: "just lobby", lobby WHO?)
Q1 · 600 words / 60 pointsYou read an article (unseen) → pick ONE company → its MARKET + NON-MARKET strategy + TACTICS
(IA)³ · run the company down these six rows, then prescribe six lenses, three pairs · WHO/WHAT → WHERE → HOW
LensThe question it forcesWhat to look for / the move
Issues (WHAT)What is the issue, what created it?Sources: new tech, new laws, shifting expectations. Issues are constructed, whoever frames it shapes the fight (could the firm reframe it?).
Actors (WHO)Who is in, ally or adversary?Organisation matters; simplify to the few that matter. Political / market / media-public.
Interests (WHERE)What do they want, how badly, how aligned?Find the internal split, your way in. Work the friendly faction.

AssetsWhat is needed to win here?Capital is rarely enough. Knowledge of decision-makers, reputation, control of authority, alliances.
▶ How to write the 600-word answer
Read the article → pick the company → run the six rows: issue (reframe?) → simplify actors → split in interests → name the arena → then give BOTH a market strategy (goal: quality / price / cost, via R&D, automation, training, M&A) AND a non-market strategy, each with named tactics from the eight. Match tactics to the country's decision mode (below). Against a government aim "well enough", not total victory. Close: well / badly / differently.

The non-market toolkit · the 8 tactics, what each is

▸ Strategy = the goal (block imports, win a subsidy); tactics = the named moves. "Just lobby, lobby who?" = a fail.
1 LobbyingDirect persuasion of officials / legislators; hire ex-insiders for access.
2 Electoral / financialDonations, PACs, fundraising, buy access & goodwill.
3 Coalition-buildingAlly with rivals, trade associations, NGOs ("strange bedfellows").
4 Grassroots / constituencyMobilise employees, customers, voters (incl. astroturf).
5 Testimony & expertiseSupply studies / data to committees & regulators (funded research).
6 Public advocacy / commsPR, ad campaigns, framing the issue in the media.
7 Judicial actionLitigation in courts (US courts, the ECJ).
8 Advisory cttees / standardsSit on technical / standard-setting bodies (EU committees, ISO).

Which tactics suit which country (the mapping)

▸ Divergence, not convergence, the SAME eight tactics, but deploy a different subset per decision mode.

Market strategy · the OTHER half of Q1

▸ He insists on both: "a market strategy AND tactics". A non-market-only answer loses marks.
Quality upR&D, innovation, train workers, better product, automate for quality.
Cost / price downAutomate, cheaper inputs, scale, restructure the supply chain, bargain suppliers, cut labour.
RepositionDifferentiate, branding, change segment, exit a losing line.
InorganicM&A (buy tech / a rival), JV, vertical integration, diversify.
Integrate (Baron): the two halves reinforce, e.g. improve quality AND lobby for breathing room. Market = compete via Porter's Five Forces; non-market = the 8 tactics. Name the goal + tactics for each.

Securitization & framing (Q1 + Q3)

  • Securitization: reframe a commercial matter as a security threat → hostile arena (a tactic in itself).
  • Media's two levers: agenda-setting + framing. Issues are constructed, can the firm reframe?
  • Expert vs public divergence: the cleared-by-experts deal loses in the public arena (DP World).

Where to lobby in the EU (Arena)

New / major rules & subsidies→ big member-state governments.
Technical / implementation→ the Commission (its committees).
Trade→ the EU Trade Commissioner.
But power sits with the big states: when the Trade Commissioner curbed Chinese solar panels, China phoned Merkel, who overruled him. Big issue → member state.
Case bank · worked (IA)³ examples to cite illustrations, not facts to memorise · only Bombardier is confirmed off-exam
CaseWhat happenedThe lesson
DP World (S2)UAE state-owned DP World bought Britain's P&O (Feb 2006, $6.8bn), inheriting terminal operations at ~20 US ports. CFIUS cleared it without consulting the Treasury Secretary or President; a rival (Eller) hired a lobbyist (~$150k) who took it to Congress, where media framed it as "handing US ports to the country of two 9/11 hijackers" (~70% opposed). Bush threatened a veto; Congress moved to block anyway; DP World divested the US operations.The arena was constructed: the real venue was Congress and the media, not the CFIUS review. Legal clearance was necessary but not sufficient. A small rival (Eller + one lobbyist) generated huge non-market action by securitising the deal. The fix: build coalitions and start the political / PR work before the deal, and name the real arena early.
TikTok (S3)Facing a US divest-or-ban law over data-security fears, ByteDance ran an integrated campaign: Project Texas (~$1.5bn to hold US data on Oracle servers), US-based managers, heavy lobbying, third-party allies (NetChoice), and mobilising creators. An in-app prompt telling users to call Congress backfired ("too clever", it looked like foreign manipulation). It won delay, not a clean win.Defuse, don't fight or exit. Run an integrated market + non-market campaign and match tactics to the (pluralist) arena, lobbying, allies, grassroots, but don't overplay it: the in-app "call Congress" push looked like foreign manipulation and backfired ("too clever"). Against a government aim "well enough", not total victory.
Shell: Brent Spar (S1)The UK government approved sinking Shell's redundant Brent Spar oil platform in the deep Atlantic (1995). Greenpeace occupied it and triggered a German-led consumer boycott (Shell sales fell ~30%, some stations firebombed). Shell reversed and dismantled it on land, even though sea disposal was arguably greener (Greenpeace later admitted its contamination figures were wrong).Legal compliance can still lose to the informal channel: Shell obeyed the law and the regulator but lost to an activist occupation and a consumer boycott. Reputation is the exploitable vulnerability, and being factually "right" (sea disposal was arguably greener) does not win the public arena.
Dev states (S3/4)Japan (MITI) and Korea (Park Chung-hee) directed cheap credit + protection conditional on hitting export targets, building POSCO steel then semiconductors (Samsung). India's protected car duopoly (Hindustan Motors) and Brazil's protected computer industry got unconditional protection with no exit, and stagnated / fell ~a decade behind.Industrial policy is only as good as the state running it. Success needs an insulated, meritocratic bureaucracy and discipline, support conditional on hitting export targets, plus a credible threat to let failures fail. Unconditional protection with no exit (India steel, Brazil computers) breeds permanent loss-making zombie firms.
THAAD (S3)Korea let the US deploy the THAAD missile-defence system (2017); Lotte provided the land. China retaliated economically: shut ~85 Lotte stores, banned group tourism to Korea, and squeezed Hyundai and K-pop, costing Korea an estimated $7.5bn. The firms had no say.Firms are collateral damage in great-power disputes, the textbook case of economic statecraft (a state weaponising market access for a security goal, the core Q3 linkage). Lotte and Hyundai had no say. The lesson: read your geopolitical exposure early and diversify before you become a hostage.
Bombardier v Embraer (S5, off-exam)A long Canada (Bombardier) vs Brazil (Embraer) WTO subsidy war; Quebec subsidised Bombardier's C-Series jet; Boeing then won a ~300% US tariff against it; Bombardier handed the C-Series to Airbus (rebranded the A220) and orders recovered.Not always US vs China: two middle powers can wage a damaging subsidy war through the WTO. A firm hit by a foreign subsidy has three options, talk, sanction, or match, and each risks a retaliation spiral. (Off the exam, but the mechanics are fair game.)
Q2 · 200 words · Industrial policytopic confirmed; he flagged the FT article "China's comparative advantage is industrial policy"
▶ The frame, and how to write the 200 words
Horizontal (skills, infra, R&D, picks no one) vs vertical (picks sectors/firms) = the debate. Aggarwal: IP is "necessary", debate = how, not whether. The one marking question: market failure vs government failure, which is worse here, under what conditions?   Shape (200w): verdict (targeted yes, copy China no) → run market-vs-gov failure → cite one success + one failure → name the instruments → close "targeted, security-critical, not blanket emulation".
Should a country do vertical industrial policy? The arguments FOR splits into backing EXISTING strengths vs building NEW ones (each with an example), then AGAINST
FOR · enhance EXISTING industryFOR · build NEW industry (infant industry)AGAINST
• Back proven strengths / national champions. India IT: the state built the IITs, software parks and tax holidays on an existing engineering base → Infosys, TCS. China solar: subsidised an existing base → ~80% of world supply.
• Economies of scale in sectors you already lead. Airbus: Europe pooled launch subsidies so one champion could match Boeing.
• Conform to latent comparative advantage (Lin): back the sector you genuinely have an edge in (Vietnam garments), lower-risk, smoother returns. 		• Infant industry → dynamic comparative advantage. South Korea had no steel or chips; the state forced credit + protection + export targets, POSCO → DRAM → Samsung leads the world. CA can be made.
• Coordination failure: suppliers, engineers and power won't arrive at once without a coordinator. Taiwan's Hsinchu Science Park → TSMC.
• Knowledge / learning spillovers: the first mover can't capture all the value, so the market under-invests. US DARPA → the internet, GPS.
• Scale / increasing returns: a fab costs ~$20bn; no national firm reaches the cost curve unaided (China's SMIC, CATL).
• Capital-market failure: banks won't fund 15-year horizons; China's state banks did → BYD, CATL. 		• Knowledge problem (Hayek): the state can't know which tech wins. US Solyndra, a $535m bust.
• Capture & rent-seeking: a small lobby wins permanent subsidies the public pays for (US sugar & corn); zombie firms.
• Wrong incentives: firms chase subsidies not customers. China's solar/EV glut: profits competed to zero.
• No exit discipline: can't credibly withdraw support, India steel and Brazil computers ran losses for decades.
• Fiscal cost / picking losers: US steel tariffs ~$900k per job; Concorde, British Leyland.
• Global spillovers: subsidy wars + retaliation (US IRA → EU); "kicking away the ladder".
• Or just specialise: static comparative advantage, back what you do best (Hong Kong).

Instruments + what makes it work

Name & classify the instruments (he marks down vague "facilitation"): at-border (tariffs, quotas) · behind-border (subsidies, procurement, SOEs, export-performance discipline) · beyond-border (standards, JETRO).
What separates success from failure: an insulated meritocratic bureaucracy + a credible commitment to let failures fail, support conditional on export targets (Korea ✓) vs unconditional protection, no exit (India steel, Brazil computers ✗).
Political rationales (FOR, but NOT a market failure, his tell): national security · employment · redistribution (Malaysia).

The China article (Parikh) · what it argues

  • Thesis: China's edge is the capacity to run industrial policy itself (the governance), not any one sector, so out-subsidising it is the wrong game.
  • Why: centralised coordination (provinces, state banks, regulators) + long horizons + performance-conditional decentralised competition → output & innovation over profit.
  • Evidence: <15% of BYD's cost lead over Tesla is subsidy (Rhodium); the rest is scale + supply chains → copying the instruments without the state fails.
  • Prescription: the West should play to its own edge, frontier innovation (open research + VC), narrow IP to security-critical chains, use cheap Chinese inputs, upskill workers. "Don't fight the last war."
  • Exam correction: "comparative advantage in IP" is loose, really competitive advantage (execution); don't attribute the phrase to Aggarwal.
Q3 · 200 words · National security + investment + trade + linkageconfirmed: "it's going to be national security" (his words, twice)
▶ How to attack Q3
Spot the security frame → identify who is linking what to what (and which direction) → name the weapon / arena (below) → assess the firm's exposure and its response.

Linkage · the core concept

▸ Deliberately tying one issue to another so progress on A depends on B.
  • Two directions: (1) security reshapes commerce (securitization, investment screening, security tariffs, export bans); (2) commerce serves security (FTAs as alliances, sanctions, statecraft).
  • Positive vs negative linkage: rewards (market access, aid) vs threats (tariffs, sanctions).
  • FTAs often NOT economics, but security: US–Morocco/Bahrain/Oman post-9/11 (gains tiny); TPP = "another aircraft carrier".
  • Trump's everything-linkage: tariffs as a general lever for drugs, immigration, security; bilateral deals let a big power push weak ones.

National security as a business issue

  • Securitization: reframe a commercial matter as a security threat → hostile arena (DP World, TikTok).
  • Security-as-cover: a security claim often masks protectionism (1959 wool blankets, lace/epaulettes, Pirelli sensors). The rationale "doesn't have to be a market failure".
  • Firm = collateral damage: caught in a state-to-state fight; respond before it is unmanageable; aim "well enough".

Investment screening

  • CFIUS: US Treasury panel vetting foreign takeovers for security; clearance grants immunity (not vs Congress, DP World).
  • FIRRMA (2018): widened it to non-controlling stakes in critical tech / infrastructure / personal data; China-tech driven; allies white-listed; copied by EU/UK/Germany → M&A harder everywhere.
  • Domestic-content requirements (China, India) force multinationals to buy local (reverse linkage).

Trade weapons & economic statecraft

  • US trade weapons (each a legal tool to restrict trade): 201 = emergency tariffs/quotas vs an import surge · 232 = tariffs on national-security grounds (steel/aluminium) · 301 = punish a country's unfair practices / IP theft (the US–China tariff war) · IEEPA = sanctions or import bans in a declared emergency · export controls = ban strategic tech (advanced chips); China's rare-earths curbs are the same weapon back.
  • Economic statecraft: economic tools for geo-political ends. THAAD (China vs Korea/Lotte); Japan–Korea 2019 chemicals; de Gucht solar panels → Merkel. Benign (funds) vs malign (coerces) power.
  • WTO hooks: national-security exception + safeguards legitimise protection; broken Appellate Body = weak enforcement. Hit by a subsidy → talk / sanction / match.
Trade-governance toolkit supports Q1 (which Arena owns the rule) and Q3 (trade)
ConceptWhat it is, and why it matters
Integration ladderFive rising degrees of integration, each cedes more sovereignty. FTA: drop tariffs between members but each keeps its own external tariff, so you need rules of origin (proof a good is really "made here", ≥75% N.American content under USMCA, up from NAFTA's 62.5%, Ford sued Honda for failing this) to stop goods sneaking in via the lowest-tariff member. Customs union: an FTA + one common external tariff (the EU). Common market: adds the 4 freedoms (goods, services, capital, people move freely). Economic / monetary union: + a shared currency (the euro). Political union: + shared institutions (the EU is part-way).
Mutual recognitionTwo ways to integrate a market: harmonise (everyone agrees one common rule, slow) or mutually recognise (a product legal in one EU state is automatically legal in all). Cassis de Dijon (1979) established mutual recognition, much faster. When the EU instead tried to harmonise food rules (the "British banger" sausage fight), the backlash fed Brexit.
Minilateralism vs nestingPeople confuse these. Minilateralism = how MANY are in the deal, a small group (more than bilateral, fewer than the whole WTO), e.g. RCEP, CPTPP. Nesting = whether the deal FITS INSIDE the wider system: GATT Article 24 only allows a bloc if it lowers average tariffs and covers all products. A minilateral deal can be nested (reinforces the WTO), a division of labour, or in conflict (undermines it).
Classify any dealAggarwal's tool to place ANY trade or security grouping on two axes: how many participants (1 to 4+), and whether members are geographically concentrated or dispersed. So CPTPP / RCEP / IPEF = minilateral + dispersed; the Quad / AUKUS = few + security-focused. APEC just talks ("a poor excuse to chat", non-binding).
Why blocs form/failThe EU held together because of four things: a common Soviet enemy, Franco-German leadership, US backing, and the Commission driving integration. New members must meet the Copenhagen criteria (a democracy + a working market economy + adopting EU law). Blocs shrink from backlash: India walked out of RCEP (fear for its manufacturers and farmers); the US quit TPP, so Japan led the rest into CPTPP.
WTO rules (5)The WTO runs world trade on a few principles a firm must work around. MFN (Most-Favoured-Nation): treat every member equally, no favourites (FTAs are the Article-24 exception). National Treatment: once a foreign good is inside your border, tax and regulate it like a domestic one. Reciprocity: concessions are traded, not given away. Transparency: publish your trade measures. Safeguards: temporary protection is allowed, including a national-security exception. Development: lighter rules for developing countries. Watch for disguised non-tariff barriers, "standards" that secretly block imports (Japan's ski-safety rules, Ontario's beer rules, French farm rules).
WTO dispute settlementHow trade fights are judged: consultation → a panel rules → the Appellate Body hears appeals → binding ruling → if ignored, the WTO authorises retaliation. But the US has refused to appoint Appellate Body judges, so it is paralysed: a loser can "appeal into the void" and stall, so enforcement is now weak. It has NOT "failed" though (it worked for decades and most cases). A firm hit by a foreign subsidy has three options: talk, sanction, or match (mind the retaliation spiral).
