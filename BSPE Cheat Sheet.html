▶ The 600-word question: the procedure (run the six rows above, in order)
Issue (and could it be reframed?) → simplify actors → find the split in interests → name the arena (fixes which info & assets win) → then prescribe BOTH a market strategy (goal: quality/price/cost) and a non-market strategy, each with named tactics (not just "lobbying"). Against a government, aim "well enough", not total victory. Close: what did they do well / badly / differently.
Session 1 · Understanding politics & the (IA)³ framework the spine matrix above is S1's output

What "politics" is

Politics = the struggle to control how resources are allocated. Three elements: power/leadership · formalised authority · allocation of resources.
  • The one question to hold every case to: "Who has the power and authority to allocate resources?"
  • OPM: politicians allocate other people's money (taxes); parties tell you how.
  • Firm is a social & political being, not a pure profit-maximiser; opting out is itself a position. "Not at the table → on the menu."

Two channels & four levels

  • Two channels of pressure: formal (laws, regulation, "call my lawyer") vs informal (social pressure, activism, boycotts). Obeying the law misses the informal one.
Four levels of analysis (nested, locate the issue)
Global political system → global political economy → national politics → firm's organisational & market environment. An issue can start at any layer and cascade.

Market vs non-market + integrated strategy

MarketNon-market
Price mechanism; voluntary; collusion illegal; private benefit (leadership, flexibility, value). Porter's Five Forces.Currency is information; voluntary or involuntary (regulation); coalitions with rivals normal; public benefit (info, coalitions).
Integrated strategy (Baron): a firm is in both at once, so link them, don't bolt lobbying on after.
5 non-market problems for firms
  • Multiple/contradictory audiences (ad boycotts) · NGO globalisation (Greenpeace vs Shell via Lego) · regulatory paradigm shifts (EU DSA/DMA/AI Act) · reputational fragility · geopolitical risk (TSMC silicon shield, app bans).
Myth: "comply with the law and you're safe", no, legal compliance can lose to the informal channel (Shell, Brent Spar 1995).
Session 2 · Securitization & the politicization of business case: Dubai Ports World

The 8 non-market tactics · NAME THESE

▸ Strategy = the goal (block imports, win a subsidy); tactics = the named moves. He marks you on naming the right one.
1 Lobbying · 2 Electoral/financial support · 3 Coalition-building (with rivals) · 4 Grassroots/constituency · 5 Testimony & expertise · 6 Public advocacy/communications · 7 Judicial action · 8 Advisory committees/standard-setting.
Trap: calling everything "lobbying"; a national-security claim is often a cover for protectionism (1959 wool blankets, lace/epaulettes, Pirelli tyre sensors).

Securitization & politicization

  • Securitization: reframe a commercial matter as a national-security threat → moves it to a more hostile arena.
  • Politicization: a routine deal becomes a public political contest before legislators & media.
  • Media's two levers: agenda-setting (what we discuss) + framing (how).
  • Expert vs public divergence: the cleared-by-experts deal loses in the public arena.

Worked playbook (the exam template)

▸ "Company A is losing money, imports rising." Give BOTH:
Market strategyNon-market strategy
Goal: improve quality (R&D, train workers, automate) or cut price (bargain suppliers, cut cost).Goal: block imports / win protection. Tactics: testify, build a coalition, frame as jobs/security, lobby, judicial (anti-dumping).

DP World · the arena-shift mechanism

CFIUS clears → rival (Eller) hires a lobbyist (~$150k) → arena shifts regulator → Congress → media frames ("ports to 9/11 country") → public turns (~70%) → deal dies. Small firm, large non-market action.
What they could have done: JV with a US firm; rename ("American Port Co."); buy out the rival; start public/political work early. Lesson: legal clearance ≠ safe; name the real arena (Congress/media, not CFIUS).
Session 3 · Variation among countries cases: TikTok · THAAD · developmental states

Four state types (goal × how it governs)

▸ Divergence, not convergence (End-of-History bet failed). Match tactics to the state.
TypeThe end it pursues / how
Regulatory / laissez-faireEquality of opportunity; sets rules, lets markets run (laissez-faire still comes after the rules: Russia → oligarchs).
WelfareEquality of outcomes; redistributes, cushions.
DevelopmentalDevelopment; actively picks & disciplines winners (Japan, Korea, China).
PredatoryElite enrichment; extract for themselves.

FDI screening · FIRRMA & CFIUS

  • CFIUS: Treasury-chaired panel vetting foreign takeovers for security; clearance grants immunity (not vs Congress).
  • FIRRMA (2018): widened it from "control" deals to non-controlling stakes in critical tech / infrastructure / personal data; China-tech driven; allies white-listed (less scrutiny).
  • Contagion: EU, UK, Germany copied it → cross-border M&A harder everywhere.

Three decision modes → your move

ModeHow decisions are made → move
PluralismOpen competition among groups → lobby & mobilise (US).
CorporatismPeak associations bargain; slow to decide, fast to implement → work the associations (Europe).
StatismGoverning elite decides; quick but error-prone (info-flow problem) → reach the elite (developmental Asia).
5 things the typology tells a firm
Model of intervention · whom to approach · decision speed ("2 years or 20?") · policy predictability · type of corruption (lobbying $ vs guanxi).

ISI, terms of trade & statecraft

  • ISI (import-substitution industrialisation): protect to build local industry; Prebisch's declining terms of trade argument. Often failed (India's protected auto duopoly).
  • New economic statecraft: trade/investment curbs for geo-economic ends, THAAD (China vs Korea/Lotte), Japan–Korea 2019 chemicals. Firms = collateral damage.
Session 4 · The industrial-policy debate high-yield short-answer topic

Definition & the three positions

▸ Horizontal (economy-wide: skills, infra, R&D) vs vertical (pick sectors/firms). Debate is about the vertical kind. Not macro policy.
1 · No verticalMarkets pick winners; static comparative advantage.
2 · Enhance existing"PE game": back current strengths (India IT, China solar).
3 · Build new"VC game": create future advantage (Korea steel → semis → Samsung). Dynamic comparative advantage.

FOR vs AGAINST

FORAGAINST
Dynamic comp. advantage / infant industry; coordination failure; knowledge spillovers & scale; + political rationales (security, jobs).The knowledge problem (states can't pick winners); capture & rent-seeking; wrong incentives; global spillovers / WTO collision.
The decisive line: market failure vs government failure, which is worse here, and under what conditions?

Instruments & preconditions

Instruments (name & classify, don't be vague)
At-the-borderTariffs, quotas, FDI/investment restrictions.
Behind-the-borderSubsidies, procurement, IP, export-performance conditionality (the Korea discipline).
Beyond-the-borderStandard-setting (EU AI rule), import-promotion (JETRO).
Developmental-state preconditions
Rule of law · meritocratic insulated bureaucracy · embedded autonomy · credible commitment to let failures fail. (Korea/Japan ✓; India steel, Brazil computers ✗.)
China (exam-hint FT article): its comparative advantage is industrial policy itself (the governance); copying it = "fighting the last war" (<15% of BYD's edge is subsidy). "Kicking away the ladder" (Chang): rich countries (IRA, CHIPS Act) ban the tools they used.
Session 5 · International institutions & the WTO

Institutions: what & why

Definition: principles, norms, rules & decision-making procedures. The lighthouse = collective-action problem they solve.
5 functions
Enforce agreements · control behaviour · lower transaction cost · disseminate information · build reputations.
5 regime traits business fights over
Membership · stringency (rules + dispute settlement) · issue scope · goals · delegation.
The post-war order & GATT
  • 3 pillars: IMF (money) · World Bank/IBRD (development) · GATT (trade).
  • GATT = "accidental success": 1947 provisional (23 parties); the ITO failed (US wouldn't ratify, 1950); GATT → WTO 1995. Uruguay Round created the WTO + GATS + TRIPS + binding dispute settlement.
  • Benign vs malign hegemon (funds vs coerces; GDP = the power). Exit/Voice/Loyalty + lock-in.

WTO rules, disputes & weapons

Core rules / principles
MFNTreat all members alike (exception: Art. 24 FTAs/customs unions; US "NTR" rebrand softened China-MFN).
National TreatmentForeign goods = domestic once inside the border.
ReciprocityMutual concessions, across sectors (cut cheese for steel), not sector-by-sector.
TransparencyPublish measures (GlobalTradeAlert tracks breaches).
SafeguardsTemporary protection / national-security exception.
DevelopmentSpecial & differential treatment (Part IV; "everyone claims developing status").
Disguised NTBs: "standards" that block imports (Japanese skis, Ontario beer, French farms).
Dispute settlement & its crisis
Consultation → panel → (Appellate Body) → binding ruling → authorised retaliation. Appellate Body paralysed (US blocks judges) → appeals "into the void", but it did not "fail". Hit by a subsidy? 3 options: talk / sanction / match (mind the spiral).
US unilateral weapons: Section 201 (safeguard), 232 (security), 301 (unfair practice); IEEPA; China's rare-earths counter. 4 WTO problems: NGO critique · developed-vs-developing ("ladder") · weak dispute settlement · neopopulism.
Session 6 · Mega-regional institutions · the EU & Asia-Pacific

Integration, minilateralism & nesting

Integration ladder (deepening)
FTA → customs union → common market (4 freedoms: goods, services, capital, people) → economic/monetary union → political union. Rules of origin; Cassis de Dijon = mutual recognition beats harmonisation.
MinilateralismNumber: small group (>bilateral, <multilateral).
NestingHierarchy: does it fit inside a wider institution (GATT Art. 24)?
3 fates of a minilateral deal: nested (reinforces the system) · division of labour · conflict (undermines it).
Aggarwal's 2-axis classification
Participants (1–4+) × geography (concentrated / dispersed). Place a deal: APEC (many/dispersed) · RCEP, CPTPP (regional) · Quad, AUKUS (few/security) · IPEF (rogue, non-nested).

EU as an arena · where to lobby

▸ The (IA)³ Arena payoff: match the issue to the right EU venue.
New / major rules→ big member-state governments.
Distributional fights→ member-state governments.
Technical / implementation→ the Commission.
Trade→ the EU Trade Commissioner.
  • Why the EU held together: common Soviet enemy · Franco-German leadership · US backing · the Commission.
  • Copenhagen criteria (1993): political (democracy/rights) · economic (functioning market) · administrative (adopt the acquis).
EU vs Asia-Pacific: EU deep, rules-based, supranational. Asia-Pacific shallow, flexible, a "noodle bowl". Stepping-stone logic: RCEP (low bar) → build capacity → CPTPP (China now wants in). P4 → TPP → CPTPP (US sold TPP as "another aircraft carrier"; Trump withdrew 2017; 11 signed CPTPP 2018). India walked from RCEP (2019: trade-deficit + farmer protests).
