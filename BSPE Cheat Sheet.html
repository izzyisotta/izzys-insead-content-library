BSPE Cheat Sheet · the (IA)³ engine, applied

Business & Society: Political Environment · Prof. Aggarwal · Sessions 1–6 examinable (S7 is off the exam) · the marks are for applying the framework, not reciting it
(IA)³ · the spine of every answer six lenses, three pairs · WHO/WHAT → WHERE → HOW · run any case down these six rows
LensThe question it forcesWhat to look for / the move
Issues (WHO/WHAT)What is the issue, and what created it?Sources: new technology, new laws/institutions, shifting societal expectations. Issues are constructed, not given, whoever frames it shapes the fight.
ActorsWho is involved, and what are their relationships (ally/adversary)?Organisation matters, organised actors win. Simplify to the few who can actually bring pressure. Political / market / media-public.
Interests (WHERE)What do they want, how badly (absolute & relative), how internally aligned?Look for the internal split / weak alignment, that is your way in. Work the friendly faction against the unfriendly one.
ArenaWhere is it settled, and how formal are the rules?Arena governs everything downstream. Court (formal) vs legislature vs agency vs media vs bribe (informal, illegal). Name it before picking tactics.
Information (HOW)What knowledge moves this arena?In the market money works everywhere; in the non-market, information varies by arena (funded studies, expertise, framing).
AssetsWhat resources are needed to win here?Capital is rarely enough. Knowledge (of decision-makers), reputation, control (of authority), alliances.
▶ The 600-word question: how to attack it
"You read an article; there is a company. Analyse its market and non-market strategy." Run (IA)³, then prescribe.
  1. Issue + source → what is it, what created it, how is it framed (and could it be reframed)?
  2. Actors → map them, flag who is organised; simplify to the few that matter.
  3. Interests → what each wants + how badly; find the internal split.
  4. Arena → name where it is decided (this fixes which info & assets matter).
  5. Information + Assets → what wins in that arena.
  6. Market strategy (the goal: quality / price / cost) + named tactics.
  7. Non-market strategy + name the right tactics from the eight (not just "lobbying").
  8. Verdict: did they do well/badly, what would you do differently? Against governments aim for "well enough", not total victory.

Market vs non-market S1

▸ Integrated strategy (Baron): a firm is in both at once, so link them, do not bolt lobbying on after.
MarketRivals/suppliers/buyers · price mechanism · voluntary · collusion illegal · Porter's Five Forces lives here.
Non-marketCourts, media, NGOs, regulators · currency is information not money · coalitions with rivals are normal.
Myth: "comply with the law and you're safe." No, legal compliance can lose to the informal channel (Shell, Brent Spar).

The 8 non-market tactics S2 · NAME THESE

▸ Strategy = the goal (block imports, win a subsidy); tactics = the moves. He marks you on naming the right one.
1 Lobbying · 2 Electoral / financial support · 3 Coalition-building (with rivals) · 4 Grassroots / constituency mobilisation · 5 Testimony & expertise · 6 Public advocacy / communications · 7 Judicial action (courts) · 8 Advisory committees / standard-setting.
Trap: calling everything "lobbying", and a national-security claim is often a cover for protectionism.

Securitization & politicization S2

  • Securitization: reframe a commercial matter as a national-security threat to move it to a more hostile arena.
  • Politicization of business: a routine deal becomes a public political contest fought before legislators and media.
  • Expert vs public divergence: the cleared-by-experts deal still loses in the public arena (DP World, TikTok).

FDI screening: FIRRMA & CFIUS S3

  • CFIUS: the US Treasury-chaired panel that vets foreign takeovers for security; clearance grants legal immunity (but not against Congress).
  • FIRRMA (2018): widened CFIUS from "control" deals to non-controlling stakes in critical tech / infrastructure / personal data. China-tech driven.
  • Contagion: EU, UK, Germany, etc. copied it, so cross-border M&A is harder everywhere ("what goes around comes around").

Varieties of states (4 types) S3

▸ Divergence, not convergence, match tactics to the state. Keyed to its goal + how it governs the economy.
Regulatory / laissez-faireSets rules, lets markets run (US).
WelfareRedistributes, cushions (much of Europe).
DevelopmentalActively picks & disciplines winners (Japan, Korea, China).
PredatoryElites extract for themselves.

3 decision modes → what business does S3

ModeHow decisions are made → your move
PluralismOpen competition among groups → lobby & mobilise (US).
CorporatismBargained via peak associations → work the associations (Europe).
StatismGoverning elite decides → reach the elite (developmental Asia).
Exam move: "would these tactics work elsewhere?" A pluralist grassroots playbook fails in a statist/corporatist system.

5 non-market problems for firms S1

  • Multiple audiences wanting contradictory things (ad boycotts).
  • Globalisation of NGOs (Greenpeace vs Shell via Lego).
  • Regulatory paradigm shifts (EU DSA / DMA / AI Act).
  • Reputational fragility (reputation = exploitable vulnerability).
  • Geopolitical risk ("butterfly effects": TSMC silicon shield, app bans).

Take-aways to bank S1

  • Politics is virtually omnipresent; the firm is a social & political being, opting out is itself a position.
  • Recognise the real market vs non-market difference.
  • Use an integrated, coherent strategy across both arenas.
  • "If you are not at the table, you are on the menu."

The industrial-policy debate S4 · high-yield

▸ Definition: horizontal (economy-wide: skills, infra, R&D) vs vertical (picking sectors/firms). Debate is about the vertical kind.
FORAGAINST
Dynamic comparative advantage / infant industry; coordination failure; knowledge spillovers & scale; + political rationales (security, jobs).The knowledge problem (states can't pick winners); capture & rent-seeking; wrong incentives; global spillovers / WTO collision.
The decisive line: market failure vs government failure, which is worse here, and under what conditions?
Instruments (name & classify, don't be vague)
At-the-border (tariffs, quotas) · behind-the-border (subsidies, procurement, IP, standards) · beyond-the-border (export finance, FDI rules).
Developmental-state preconditions
Rule of law · meritocratic insulated bureaucracy · embedded autonomy · export discipline / credible commitment to let failures fail. (Korea/Japan ✓; India steel, Brazil computers ✗.)
China (the exam-hint article): China's comparative advantage is industrial policy itself, the governance behind it; copying it with subsidies is "fighting the last war" (under 15% of BYD's cost edge is subsidy).

Regionalism S6

Integration ladder (deepening)
FTA → customs union → common market → economic / monetary union → political union. (Rules of origin; Cassis de Dijon mutual recognition.)
MinilateralismAbout number: a small group (more than bilateral, fewer than multilateral): RCEP, CPTPP, Quad, AUKUS.
NestingAbout hierarchy: does the deal fit inside a wider institution (GATT Art. 24)?
Don't blur them: a deal can be minilateral-and-nested (RCEP stepping-stone) or minilateral-and-rogue (IPEF). EU = deep/rules-based; Asia-Pacific = shallow/flexible.

The WTO & institutions S5

▸ Why institutions exist (the lighthouse): solve collective-action problems, cut transaction costs, stabilise expectations, monitor.
The 5 rules business contests
MFN (treat all members alike) · National Treatment (foreign goods = domestic once inside) · Reciprocity · Transparency · Safeguards / national-security exceptions.
Dispute settlement
Consultation → panel → (Appellate Body) → binding ruling → authorised retaliation.
Appellate Body is paralysed (US blocks judges), so appeals go "into the void", but the WTO did not "fail"; firms still use it.
Disguised NTBs"Standards" that block imports (Japanese skis, Ontario beer, French farms).
HegemonBenign (funds the system) vs malign (coerces); GDP measures the power.
Hit by a subsidy?3 options: talk / sanction / match, mind the retaliation spiral.
Off-exam but cited: Bombardier vs Embraer; "kicking away the ladder"; Exit/Voice/Loyalty + lock-in.

Quick-fire glossary (short answers) def + example

  • Issue is constructed: framing + arena choice decide who matters.
  • "Organisation matters": organised actors beat larger but diffuse ones.
  • Aim "well enough": against a government, total victory is the wrong target.
  • Collateral damage: in cross-border disputes firms are caught in the middle, act before it is unmanageable.
Case bank · application ammo use as worked examples of (IA)³, not facts to memorise · only Bombardier is confirmed off-exam
CaseWhat happenedThe lesson
DP World (S2)UAE state-owned DP World bought P&O (2006, ~$6.8bn), inheriting US ports. CFIUS cleared it; Congress + media securitized it (~70% opposed); it divested the US ops.The arena was constructed, the real venue was Congress/media, not CFIUS. Legal clearance ≠ safe. The canonical (IA)³ case.
TikTok (S3)Facing divest-or-ban, ByteDance ran Project Texas + US hires + lobbying + allies (NetChoice) + grassroots creators.Match tactics to the arena (pluralist); securitization of data; "too clever" backlash.
Shell: Brent Spar (S1)Legally-approved plan to sink a North Sea platform (1995) reversed by Greenpeace + consumer boycott.Legal compliance can still lose to the informal channel.
Dev states (S3/4)Japan/Korea built industries via insulated bureaucracy + export discipline; India steel & Brazil computers failed via unconditional protection.Industrial policy is only as good as state capacity + discipline.
THAAD (S3)Korea hosted US THAAD; China retaliated economically against Korean firms (Lotte) & tourism.Firms are collateral damage in great-power statecraft.
Bombardier v Embraer (S5, off-exam)Canada vs Brazil WTO subsidy fight; Quebec subsidised the C-Series; Airbus later took it over (A220).Not always US vs China; talk/sanction/match + the retaliation spiral.

Format: 5 short answers (200 words) + one 600-word applied (IA)³ analysis of a company's market & non-market strategy. Scope: Sessions 1–6 only (S7 off). Allowed: one A4 two-sided sheet (this one). Short-answer steer: read "China's comparative advantage is industrial policy" (FT).   He rewards: applying (IA)³ not reciting it · separating strategy from tactics and naming the tactics · matching tactics to the state type/decision mode · concrete instruments not "facilitation" · treating issues as constructed · market-failure vs government-failure.
