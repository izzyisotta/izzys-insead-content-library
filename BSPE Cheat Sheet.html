BSPE Cheat Sheet · built for the 3 exam questions

3 questions, 600 + 200 + 200 = 1000 words · 2 hours (needs ~1) · read all Qs, outline, then write; never exceed the word limit · A4 two-sided · S1–6 only · apply (IA)³ and NAME the tactics (his #1 complaint: "just lobby", lobby WHO?)
Q1 · 600 words / 60 pointsYou read an article (unseen) → pick ONE company → its MARKET + NON-MARKET strategy + TACTICS
(IA)³ · run the company down these six rows, then prescribe six lenses, three pairs · WHO/WHAT → WHERE → HOW
LensThe question it forcesWhat to look for / the move
Issues (WHAT)What is the issue, what created it?Sources: new tech, new laws, shifting expectations. Issues are constructed, whoever frames it shapes the fight (could the firm reframe it?).
Actors (WHO)Who is in, ally or adversary?Organisation matters; simplify to the few that matter. Political / market / media-public.
Interests (WHERE)What do they want, how badly, how aligned?Find the internal split, your way in. Work the friendly faction.
ArenaWhere is it settled, how formal?Arena governs everything downstream. Court / legislature / agency / media. EU: member state vs Commission (below). Name it before tactics.
Information (HOW)What knowledge moves this arena?Market: money works everywhere. Non-market: information varies by arena (funded studies, expertise, framing).
AssetsWhat is needed to win here?Capital is rarely enough. Knowledge of decision-makers, reputation, control of authority, alliances.
▶ How to write the 600-word answer
Read the article → pick the company → run the six rows: issue (reframe?) → simplify actors → split in interests → name the arena → then give BOTH a market strategy (goal: quality / price / cost, via R&D, automation, training, M&A) AND a non-market strategy, each with named tactics from the eight. Match tactics to the country's decision mode (below). Against a government aim "well enough", not total victory. Close: well / badly / differently.

The non-market toolkit · the 8 tactics, what each is

▸ Strategy = the goal (block imports, win a subsidy); tactics = the named moves. "Just lobby, lobby who?" = a fail.
1 LobbyingDirect persuasion of officials / legislators; hire ex-insiders for access.
2 Electoral / financialDonations, PACs, fundraising, buy access & goodwill.
3 Coalition-buildingAlly with rivals, trade associations, NGOs ("strange bedfellows").
4 Grassroots / constituencyMobilise employees, customers, voters (incl. astroturf).
5 Testimony & expertiseSupply studies / data to committees & regulators (funded research).
6 Public advocacy / commsPR, ad campaigns, framing the issue in the media.
7 Judicial actionLitigation in courts (US courts, the ECJ).
8 Advisory cttees / standardsSit on technical / standard-setting bodies (EU committees, ISO).

Market strategy · the OTHER half of Q1

▸ He insists on both: "a market strategy AND tactics". A non-market-only answer loses marks.
Quality upR&D, innovation, train workers, better product, automate for quality.
Cost / price downAutomate, cheaper inputs, scale, restructure the supply chain, bargain suppliers, cut labour.
RepositionDifferentiate, branding, change segment, exit a losing line.
InorganicM&A (buy tech / a rival), JV, vertical integration, diversify.
Integrate (Baron): the two halves reinforce, e.g. improve quality AND lobby for breathing room. Market = compete via Porter's Five Forces; non-market = the 8 tactics. Name the goal + tactics for each.

Securitization & framing (Q1 + Q3)

  • Securitization: reframe a commercial matter as a security threat → hostile arena (a tactic in itself).
  • Media's two levers: agenda-setting + framing. Issues are constructed, can the firm reframe?
  • Expert vs public divergence: the cleared-by-experts deal loses in the public arena (DP World).

Where to lobby in the EU (Arena)

New / major rules & subsidies→ big member-state governments.
Technical / implementation→ the Commission (its committees).
Trade→ the EU Trade Commissioner.
But power sits with the big states: when the Trade Commissioner curbed Chinese solar panels, China phoned Merkel, who overruled him. Big issue → member state.

Q2 · 200 words · Industrial policystrong steer: he flagged the FT article "China's comparative advantage is industrial policy"

The debate · the toolkit

▸ Horizontal (skills, infra, R&D) vs vertical (pick sectors/firms). Debate = the vertical kind.
1 · No verticalMarkets pick winners; static comparative advantage.
2 · Enhance existing"PE game": back current strengths (India IT, China solar).
3 · Build new"VC game": create future advantage (Korea steel→Samsung). Dynamic comparative advantage.
Decisive line: market failure vs government failure, which is worse, under what conditions? (Full for/against below.)
Instruments · preconditions
At-border (tariffs/quotas) · behind-border (subsidies, procurement, export-performance discipline) · beyond-border (standards, JETRO). Dev-state needs: insulated meritocratic bureaucracy + credible commitment to let failures fail.

★ Q2 model answer · the 5-move spine

▸ Topic confirmed; wording unknown. 200w = position → frame → payoff. Three views (see the note): Parikh (FT) vs Noah Smith (Noahpinion) vs Aggarwal.
The key distinction: "comparative advantage in industrial policy" is the article's (Parikh) phrase, NOT a real comparative advantage. Noah Smith: you can't have CA in an input, every country has CA in something, it's competitive advantage (execution + state capacity). Aggarwal agrees, recasts it as "better at the instruments"; IP is "necessary", debate = how not whether.
  • 1 Position: IP is necessary but conditional, do it narrow, don't copy China wholesale.
  • 2 Defuse the phrase: CA is about traded goods → this is competitive advantage / execution, not CA. (Top-mark line.)
  • 3 Frame + evidence: market failure vs government failure. China's edge is real (<15% of BYD's lead is subsidy; rest = scale / supply chains / governance) but rests on an insulated, disciplined developmental state, copy the tools without the capacity → capture + subsidy wars.
  • 4 The payoff (the tension): efficiency says specialise (China=goods, West=services), BUT trade is unbalanced (goods for IOUs) + security dependence (drones, rare earths) = a security externality CA ignores → "rationale doesn't have to be a market failure".
  • 5 Conclude: targeted, security-critical IP + don't fully retreat to services (dynamic-CA trap), not blanket emulation. (Parikh = narrow carve-out; Smith = wider barriers, the disagreement is scope.)
Industrial policy: the full case FOR splits into backing EXISTING strengths vs building NEW ones · plus political rationales · then AGAINST
FOR · enhance EXISTING industry ("PE game")FOR · build NEW industry ("VC game" / infant industry)AGAINST
• Back proven strengths / national champions (India IT, China solar).
• Economies of scale in sectors you already lead → global competitiveness.
• Conform to latent comparative advantage (Lin): lower-risk, smoother returns. 		• Infant industry → dynamic comparative advantage (Korea steel→semis→Samsung); CA can be made, not just inherited.
• Coordination failure: complementary investments (suppliers, skills, infrastructure) won't happen without a coordinator.
• Knowledge / learning spillovers: the first mover's knowledge is free-ridden → the market under-invests in experimentation.
• Scale / increasing returns: huge fixed costs + learning curves → no domestic firm reaches efficient scale unaided.
• Capital-market failure: banks won't fund long-horizon risk; state credit fills the gap. 		• Knowledge problem (Hayek): states can't pick winners or know when to stop.
• Capture & rent-seeking: connected incumbents grab subsidies; zombie firms.
• Wrong incentives: firms chase subsidies not customers; lobby not innovate.
• No exit discipline / time-inconsistency: can't credibly withdraw support (India steel, Brazil computers ran losses for decades).
• Fiscal cost / picking losers: expensive failures + opportunity cost of public funds.
• Global spillovers: subsidy wars, retaliation, WTO collision, "kicking away the ladder".
• Or just specialise: static comparative advantage says back what you already do best.
Political rationales (FOR, but NOT a market failure, his tell): national security · employment / avoid deindustrialisation · redistribution (Malaysia pro-Malay quotas) · supply-chain resilience · geopolitical catch-up. The discipline that separates success from failure: insulated meritocratic bureaucracy + credible commitment to let failures fail.
Q3 · 200 words · National security + investment + trade + linkageconfirmed: "it's going to be national security" (his words, twice)
▶ How to attack Q3
Spot the security frame → identify who is linking what to what (and which direction) → name the weapon / arena → assess the firm's exposure and its response. The unifying idea is linkage: trade, investment and security are deliberately fused.

Linkage · the core concept

▸ Deliberately tying one issue to another so progress on A depends on B.
  • Two directions: (1) security reshapes commerce (securitization, FDI screening, 232 tariffs, export controls); (2) commerce serves security (FTAs as alliances, sanctions, statecraft).
  • Positive vs negative linkage: rewards (market access, aid) vs threats (tariffs, sanctions).
  • FTAs often NOT economics, but security: US–Morocco/Bahrain/Oman post-9/11 (gains tiny); TPP = "another aircraft carrier".
  • Trump's everything-linkage: tariffs as a general lever for drugs, immigration, security; bilateral deals let a big power push weak ones.

National security as a business issue

  • Securitization: reframe a commercial matter as a security threat → hostile arena (DP World, TikTok).
  • Security-as-cover: a security claim often masks protectionism (1959 wool blankets, lace/epaulettes, Pirelli sensors). The rationale "doesn't have to be a market failure".
  • Firm = collateral damage: caught in a state-to-state fight; respond before it is unmanageable; aim "well enough".

Investment screening

  • CFIUS: US Treasury panel vetting foreign takeovers for security; clearance grants immunity (not vs Congress, DP World).
  • FIRRMA (2018): widened it to non-controlling stakes in critical tech / infrastructure / personal data; China-tech driven; allies white-listed; copied by EU/UK/Germany → M&A harder everywhere.
  • Domestic-content requirements (China, India) force multinationals to buy local (reverse linkage).

Trade weapons & economic statecraft

  • US weapons: Section 201 (safeguard), 232 (national security), 301 (unfair practice); IEEPA; chip export controls; China's rare-earths counter.
  • Economic statecraft: economic tools for geo-political ends. THAAD (China vs Korea/Lotte); Japan–Korea 2019 chemicals; de Gucht solar panels → Merkel. Benign (funds) vs malign (coerces) power.
  • WTO hooks: national-security exception + safeguards legitimise protection; broken Appellate Body = weak enforcement. Hit by a subsidy → talk / sanction / match.
