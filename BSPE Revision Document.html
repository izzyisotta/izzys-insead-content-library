The sessions

1 · Understanding Politics and the IA3 Framework

The course's foundation session: it defines what "politics" means for a manager, separates the firm's market from its non-market environment, and hands you the analytical spine you reuse on every later case, Aggarwal's (IA)³ framework (drawn from Bach and Allen / Baron). Everything from S2 onwards is this framework applied.

🎙 In class"In politics, if you are not at the table, you are on the menu." The whole course is about getting business to the table: the firm is never just an economic agent, it is a "social and political being", and pretending to have no political position is itself a position.

What Aggarwal means by "politics"

Politics = the struggle to control how resources get allocated.
  • Three elements: the struggle for power and leadership; the pursuit of (formalised) authority; the allocation of resources.
  • The one question: "Who has the power and authority to allocate resources?" Hold every case up to this question.
  • "OPM" in class: politicians do not give away their own money, they allocate other people's money (taxes) and parties exist to tell you how. Resource allocation is the prize all actors fight over.
Why a manager cannot opt out.
  • Not just economic agents: firms and CEOs are social and political beings, not pure profit-maximisers.
  • Two channels of pressure: formal (laws and regulations, "call my lawyer") and informal (social pressure, activism, boycotts).
  • The trap: obeying the law alone misses the informal channel; Aggarwal's Brent Spar example (Shell, North Sea, 1990s) is legal compliance defeated by activist pressure.

The four levels of analysis

Aggarwal frames where politics plays out as four nested levels; a manager must locate an issue on the right one before acting.

Market vs non-market environment

Intuition: two playing fieldsPorter's Five Forces describes the market field (rivals, suppliers, buyers, competing on price and value). But courts, media, NGOs and regulators play on a second field where the currency is information, not money. Five Forces is "a good framework" but blind to that second field, hence the need for non-market strategy.

Don't equate non-market with market logic. In the market, competitors collude only illegally and money buys outcomes everywhere. In the non-market, coalitions with rivals are normal (tech firms jointly opposing the UK Online Safety Act, jointly lobbying against AI regulation), and money alone rarely wins, information and reputation do.
Integrated strategy (David Baron).
  • Market strategy: concerted actions through competition and exchange to improve firm performance (pricing, cost, innovation).
  • Non-market strategy: concerted actions through political, legal and social actors and processes to influence firm performance.
  • The point: a firm operates in both environments at once, so it needs both strategies linked, not lobbying bolted on as an afterthought. Non-market tactics (lobbying, coalitions, grassroots, testimony, legal, public advocacy) are empirical, not "the anti-ethics course".

Five non-market problems Aggarwal flags

The recurring non-market challenges a firm faces.
  • Multiple audiences: different groups want contradictory things (Facebook ad boycott, "stop hate for profit"); geo-economic competition and transnational networks.
  • Globalisation of NGOs: coordinated pressure campaigns (Greenpeace vs Shell via Lego; Lego then dropped the Shell partnership and re-tooled its market strategy toward WWF endangered-animal sets).
  • Regulatory paradigm shifts: EU Digital Services Act, Digital Markets Act and 2024 AI Act, largely aimed at big US tech.
  • Competitive edge / reputational fragility: reputation as an exploitable vulnerability.
  • Geopolitical risk ("butterfly effects"): e.g. TSMC's "Silicon Shield" and the irony that off-shoring chip production to the US/Europe erodes Taiwan's own protection; India banning Chinese apps to retaliate over the border conflict.

The (IA)³ framework, the spine of the course

Intuition: three questions, six lensesRead any political situation through three plain questions: WHO cares about WHAT, WHERE is it settled, and HOW do actors get what they want. Each question is a pair of lenses, giving six in total. The name "(IA)³" is just those six lenses grouped as three I/A pairs.
(IA)³ expands to three pairs, each tied to a question word.
  • Issues & Actors → WHO is concerned about WHAT?
  • Interests & Arena → WHERE are preferences reconciled?
  • Information & Assets → HOW do actors pursue what they want?

How a manager USES (IA)³ to read a situation

Spot the issue & its source→ map actors (organised?)→ read interests (split?)→ locate the arena→ find the decisive information→ marshal the assets that win there
Why the sequence is the point, not a checklist.
  • Arena governs everything downstream: which information counts and which assets win is determined by where the fight is settled, so you cannot pick tactics before naming the arena.
  • Look for the opening: the framework is built to surface an actor's internal split or weak alignment as the entry point for engagement.
  • Optimise against others' goals: the goal is to optimise your strategy with attention to other actors' goals, not to list facts.
Take-aways (Aggarwal's closing slide).
  • Politics is virtually omnipresent.
  • Executives must recognise the real differences between the market and non-market environments.
  • Executives need an integrated, coherent, strategic approach that links both arenas.

2 · IA3 in Practice: Securitization and the Politicization of Business

This session runs the IA3 framework from L01 through one live case, Dubai Ports World's attempt to operate six US ports, and then names the toolkit: the eight nonmarket tactics a firm uses to shift a political outcome. The reusable takeaways come first (securitization, market vs nonmarket, the eight tactics); the DPW deep-dive at the end shows them all in motion.

🎙 In class "Today we are going to be talking about DP World. And we'll talk about the politicization of business. And then we're going to talk about tactics. These two sessions are the critical sessions in which if you don't show up, you probably will fail." Aggarwal flags this as one of the load-bearing weeks.

The two core concepts

Securitization, reframing an ordinary commercial deal as a national-security threat.
  • What it is: a deal recast from "foreign firm buys assets" into "a country with ties to terrorism gains control of critical infrastructure."
  • Why it matters: once an issue is securitized, experts and legal clearance stop deciding it; public fear and Congress do.
  • The tell: the substance of the deal does not change, only the frame does. DPW cleared CFIUS, yet the framed version ("ports sold to the country that flew planes into the towers") overrode that.
Politicization of business, when a transaction that "should" be commercial becomes a political contest.
  • What it is: any deal can be pulled out of the market arena into the political arena by an interested actor.
  • Why it matters: playing by the official rulebook (CFIUS approval) is necessary but never sufficient; the real fight can move to a different arena entirely.
  • The tell: a small, otherwise-powerless firm (Eller & Co.) generates outsized political action via a lobbyist and the media.
  • National-security framing is often fake: Aggarwal's running joke, the 1959 wool lobby claimed "150 to 200 million woolen blankets" were needed to survive an atomic war; the lace industry claimed lace was vital because generals' epaulettes are woven from it. Pirelli more recently claimed Chinese-owned tyre sensors were a data-security threat. Cover stories for protectionism.

The mechanism: how a commercial deal becomes a security crisis

Commercial deal cleared (CFIUS approves)⟹ losing rival hires a lobbyist⟹ arena shifts: regulator → Congress⟹ media frames it as a security threat⟹ public opinion turns (70% opposed)⟹ vote-seeking Congress piles on⟹ politicized: deal dies

The non-obvious link is the third: clearing the official regulator does not close the matter, because a rival can re-open it in a different arena (Congress) where the rules are political, not legal.

🎙 In class "Issues are constructed. Issues are not just out there. When you frame and construct the issue a particular way, then you will have different actors involved than initially." The same physical facts, framed differently, summon a different cast.

Case deep-dive: Dubai Ports World in the USA

In one breath. A Dubai state-owned operator legally cleared every US gate to run terminals at six American ports, then lost the deal in three weeks, not because the facts changed, but because a tiny rival's lobbyist, the media, and an election-year Congress reframed a commercial transaction as handing US ports to "the country that flew the planes into the towers."

Setup. Oct 2005, DP World bids to buy UK-based P&O. Most of P&O sits in Asia/Australia; only 10% of revenue is P&O Ports North America, running terminals at 20 US ports. DP World approaches Treasury, files with CFIUS, and negotiates security concessions with DHS (Container Security Initiative, US protocols enacted in Dubai). Late Jan 2006, CFIUS approves without consulting the Treasury Secretary or President. 10 Feb, DP World wins P&O at $6.8bn after Singapore's PSA declines to match. The deal looks done.

What happened. 11 Feb, an Associated Press story (sourced from Eller & Co.'s lobbyist Joe Muldoon) frames the deal as a UAE company taking "operations at six American ports" in a country "with ties to the Sept. 11 hijackers." Within 48 hours Lou Dobbs and Michael Savage run nightly attacks. Senator Schumer holds press conferences flanked by 9/11 victims' families. Bush, at his lowest approval, post-Katrina, a lame duck, defends the deal ("played by the rules… I'll deal with it, with a veto"). It doesn't hold: 70% of the public opposes, Republican leaders (Frist) turn, Congress claims the votes to override a veto, the Port Authority of NY/NJ and Eller sue. DP World, which initially ignored the row because the accusations "seemed so off base," ultimately agrees to divest its US operations and exits.

What DP World could have done (in class).
  • Pursue a JV with an American company so it never reads as "foreign takeover."
  • Change the name, Aggarwal's joke: "the American Port Company."
  • Buy out Eller, a private transaction; pay the small rival off, Silicon-Valley style, and remove the spark.
  • Start the public/political work earlier, build perception before the story breaks, not after.
The IA3 lesson (slide 8 takeaways).
  • Any deal can become politically sensitive, even a routine, already-foreign-owned asset.
  • Playing by the book is necessary but not enough; map the political atmosphere, including regime type.
  • Small firms can generate large nonmarket action (one lobbyist, ~$150k).
  • Media framing of the public discussion is decisive; positive public perception must be built early.
  • Exiting can be the best strategy, the question is when and how, not whether.
🎙 In class "NGOs are much better at nonmarket strategy than big companies. Big companies think they can throw money around. People love the myth of David versus Goliath, the little company fighting the big one, and the media plays it up." Eller was the David here.
Expert vs public divergence (slide 15).
  • DPW: experts said "no security threat"; the public said "yes!", and the public won.
  • TikTok (preview of next case): experts said "yes, a threat"; the public said "no!", the mirror image.
  • Takeaway: firms must learn to read, and leverage, the gap between expert judgement and public perception.
Exam lens. Aggarwal twice flagged this material as make-or-break ("if you don't show up, you probably will fail"). 3 · IA3 in Practice: Variation Among Countries

This session takes the IA3 toolkit and asks the question that decides whether it travels: why does the same business-government issue play out completely differently across countries? Two halves. First, cross-border investment restrictions (the rise of FDI screening, and the TikTok case as a worked non-market campaign). Second, the spine of the day: all capitalism is not American, a four-state typology and three decision-making modes that tell you whom to approach, how fast a decision moves, and which tactics even work.

🎙 In class "We have divergence, not convergence." The End of History bet was that the fall of the Wall would push everyone toward Western liberal democracy. It did not. China grew richer without growing democratic; Western democracies drifted toward authoritarian populism. The map of capitalisms stays plural, so a single playbook fails.

Part A · Cross-border investment restrictions

FDI screening has gone global since FIRRMA (2018).
  • What FIRRMA is: the Foreign Investment Risk Review Modernization Act of 2018, the US law that overhauled CFIUS (the Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States, the secretive inter-agency panel, chaired by the Treasury, that screens foreign takeovers of US businesses for national-security risk, the same body that cleared the DP World deal). It was driven mainly by alarm at Chinese acquisition of US technology.
  • What changed: the old CFIUS test was "do you control a US business?"; FIRRMA widened it to cover non-controlling stakes in firms touching critical technology, critical infrastructure or sensitive personal data (so-called "TID" businesses), non-passive VC/PE investments, and real-estate deals near military sites. It also added mandatory filings for some deals, where review had been voluntary.
  • The white-list carve-out: investors from US allies face less scrutiny; everyone else gets reviewed.
  • The contagion: Germany, UK, Italy, France passed copycat laws; the European Commission pushed common investment-screening guidelines (so multinationals cannot arbitrage gaps between member states); Canada, China, Australia, India all invoke national security.
  • The takeaway: cross-border M&A is now structurally harder, everywhere, on national-security grounds.
🎙 In class "What goes around, comes around." The Dutch blocked an American firm's acquisition of a Dutch tech company on national-security grounds, the same logic the US uses, turned back on it. Screening is not a US weapon; it is a global norm.

New economic statecraft, trade/investment restrictions for geo-economic, not commercial, ends.

  • Japan–S. Korea (2019): Japan restricts three semiconductor chemicals (hitting Samsung) citing "national security"; Korea reads it as retaliation for a court ruling on WWII forced labour. Restrictions lifted 2023 as relations warmed.
  • The tell: "national security" is the universal cover story; companies become collateral damage in state-to-state disputes they did not start.

Developmental-state deep-dive: two paths, two outcomes

ISI (Import-Substitution Industrialisation), intervention that failed.
  • The problem: commodity-exporting developing countries faced declining terms of trade (Prebisch's insight; he headed ECLA).
  • The strategy: tariffs/quotas/licensing to keep imports out; block foreign firms; subsidise domestic and state-owned firms; restrict capital; overvalued exchange rate.
  • The outcome: domestic industries grew, but with low innovation, low productivity, no export competitiveness.
  • India's version: had the technology (it built nuclear devices) but a protected duopoly (Hindustan Motors) had no reason to compete, and the ultimate non-market tactic was a bag of cash to the minister to keep protection alive. ISI only ended with the 1990–91 financial crisis.
The successful developmental state, Japan, then China.
  • Japan's MITI/MoF model: ministries staffed by the "best and brightest" (Tokyo University law/public-policy graduates) centrally allocated capital, controlled it (no mobility, low rates), promoted exports, protected infant industries with no inward FDI, and coordinated technology diffusion (METI bought RCA's colour-TV tech and seeded all Japanese makers; a decade on, US TV makers were gone).
  • China copies it, with twists: same central planning, capital allocation, export promotion, but allows inward FDI to learn and grow, and does not enforce property rights, trading market access for technology.
Intuition: terms of trade The recurring thread under both halves of the typology is Ricardo: trade produces global gains, but the terms of trade decide who captures them. A country that always trades two units of wheat for one of steel keeps no gains, which is exactly the grievance that drove developing states toward ISI in the first place.
Exam lens. Aggarwal's tells point at one move: "Politics matters." Two recurring prompts from class: (1) "Would these tactics work just as well in other places?", the TikTok grassroots/lobbying playbook is pluralist and would fail in a statist or corporatist system. (2) "What separates this from DP World?", compare which of the eight influence tactics (lobbying, grassroots, coalition-building, electoral support, testimony, communications, judicial, advisory committees) each firm used. 4 · The Industrial Policy Debate

A structured class debate, not a lecture: Aggarwal split the room into three camps and made each defend a position on whether developing-country governments should run vertical (sector-specific) industrial policy. The durable takeaways are his typology of why states intervene, the menu of instruments they use, and the government-failure-vs-market-failure frame that decides the whole argument. This feeds Session 5 (the WTO and the Brazil-Canada case), because the question "are these instruments fair?" is exactly what the WTO adjudicates.

🎙 In class Aggarwal's punchline after the closing poll: "INSEAD is a school of government." Not a single hand went up for no intervention; the room split between moderate and aggressive. The debate is not "should we?" any more, it is "how, and can your government actually pull it off?"
Industrial policy, government action to change the sectoral mix of the economy.
  • What it is: deliberately steering resources toward activities the market would otherwise under-invest in or under-produce; NOT macroeconomic policy (the slide says so explicitly).
  • The fork that defines the debate: horizontal = help all industries equally, no winners picked (education, R&D, infrastructure); vertical = pick selected industries or firms (tariffs, subsidies, procurement). The whole debate is about the vertical half.
  • The tell: "picking winners" is the loaded phrase. Pro-side reframes it as "identifying sectors with high growth potential"; anti-side says governments simply cannot.

The three debate positions (Aggarwal's spine)

The deck sets out exactly three positions for developing countries. Everything in the class maps onto one of them.

The core argument: FOR vs AGAINST vertical policy

Lead with the verdict, then the mechanism, then a real case. Both columns concede the other has a point: the pro-side admits failures, the anti-side admits markets are imperfect. The debate turns on which failure is worse.

FOR, market failure justifies intervention
  • Dynamic scale economies (infant industry): nascent firms cannot bear the upfront cost or survive foreign competition; protect them and per-unit costs fall with scale until they are globally competitive. Mechanism: temporary protection → reach minimum scale → costs drop → export. Case: Korea, Samsung & Hyundai.
  • Coordination failure: upstream and downstream firms each wait for the other; nobody invests first. Government coordinates the simultaneous investment. Case: infrastructure + skilled-labour bundles many firms need at once.
  • Information / knowledge externality: the first firm to try an innovation bears the cost; rivals learn for free, so nobody experiments. Subsidy restores the incentive. Rodrik: IP is about eliciting what a country is good at, not dictating it.
  • Beyond market failure: national security (defence, medicine), employment/redistribution (help a poorer region or ethnic group). These are political rationales, not market failures.
AGAINST, government failure is worse
  • Knowledge problem: the information needed to pick the right sector is dispersed in markets; government cannot aggregate it. Case: US steel protection, a debater cited a striking (★ unverified) figure of "$900bn per job saved."
  • State capture & corruption: firms capture the policymakers picking winners; rents flow to the connected, not the competitive. Worst in extractive industries (oil, mining).
  • Wrong incentives: protection makes firms chase subsidies and preferential treatment instead of the technological frontier. Cases: British Leyland, Concorde, "waste of money, waste of time."
  • Global spillovers: if everyone intervenes → supply-chain fragmentation, trade wars (US-China), subsidy wars (IRA → EU response), and a direct collision with the WTO's non-discrimination rule.
The decisive question, as a chain:   market is imperfect ⇒ so intervention could help ⇒ but government is also imperfect (capture, ignorance) ⇒ verdict turns on market failure vs government failure: which is worse?

  • Why it matters: it imposes discipline, the state can punish non-performers, which is what stops protection becoming a permanent rent.
  • The tell: Korea (Samsung, Hyundai, POSCO) worked because support was conditional; Brazil's computer industry and India's loss-making steel SOEs failed because support was unconditional and politically impossible to withdraw.

Which governments can actually do this?

Students converged on the same short list of preconditions for a state capable of running vertical policy without it degenerating into capture. This is the "developmental state" model.

Preconditions for a capable interventionist state.
  • Rule of law, without it no policy can be executed credibly.
  • Competitive / meritocratic bureaucracy, technocrats recruited by exam (Korea, Japan), not political appointees.
  • Embedded autonomy, close enough to firms for information, autonomous enough to discipline them. Too distant = miss the bottlenecks; too captured = subsidies become permanent.
  • Credible long-horizon commitment, ability to let failing firms fail. Electoral pressure makes this nearly impossible; Korea under Park could credibly threaten firms, India's steel could not.
Trap: do not treat the developmental-state checklist as a recipe any country can follow. Aggarwal's reading argues these requirements are "daunting" and rarely met, and Singapore built state capacity while doing industrial policy, not before, so even the sequencing is not clean.

Honest verdict. Vertical industrial policy is neither snake oil nor a free lunch. It can work, but only where a real market failure meets a state with the rare capacity to discipline firms and credibly withdraw support; absent either, government failure dominates and you get British Leyland. 5 · International Institutions and the WTO: Global Business Dispute Resolution

This session takes one half of the (IA)³ framework, the Arena, and zooms into the most important non-market arena for global trade: the WTO. It asks why institutions exist at all, traces GATT→WTO, lists the WTO's core rules, walks the dispute-settlement process a firm actually uses to fight a foreign subsidy, and ends with the Bombardier/Embraer case (two middle powers, not the usual US-China story).

🎙 In class The lighthouse game. Aggarwal gives 80 students three ships each and asks each to donate one ship's worth to fund a lighthouse that would save everyone. Nobody volunteers; ships keep crashing. The point: a known technical fix (the lighthouse) fails without an institution to force contribution. Institutions exist to solve exactly this collective-action problem.

I. Why institutions exist, and what they are

International institution (definition).
  • What it is: "sets of principles, norms, rules and decision-making procedures among relevant actors in the international system."
  • For business: institutions create a predictable environment; this is the single reason business broadly backs them.
  • The tell: business hates Trump's tariffs less for their level than for their unpredictability ("is it 50% or 10%? just tell us the rule").
Five things institutions do (slide).
  • Enforce agreements made under the institution's purview.
  • Control actors' behaviour against international standards (a small country can take the US or China to dispute settlement and win).
  • Lower the cost of choosing, organising and negotiating an agreement (166 at one table beats bilateral spaghetti).
  • Disseminate reliable information on state policy and trade rules.
  • Help develop reputations (rule-followers attract more investment, ceteris paribus).
Five regime characteristics business fights over (slide).
  • Membership: who can join (Taiwan? China blocks it).
  • Stringency of rules + dispute settlement: business wants strong rules against expropriation, weak rules on itself.
  • Issue scope: what issues are in (EU common trade policy is easy; EU defence or services is not).
  • Goals: what it is trying to do (even "promote free trade" is now contested).
  • Delegation: how much power member states hand to the bureaucrats (Brexit's "Eurocrats telling us what to do").

Who provides the public good? Two kinds of hegemon

II. From GATT to WTO

The neoliberal international economic order (carried over from S4).
  • Three pillars: the IMF (balance-of-payments / exchange rates), the World Bank / IBRD (reconstruction, then development), and the GATT (trade).
  • The order under attack: 80 years old; being dismantled not by China (which built RCEP, the AIIB) but, unexpectedly, by the US.
GATT as an "accidental success".
  • 1944 Bretton Woods: a call for an International Trade Organization (ITO), prompted by pre-WWII trade wars.
  • 1947 GATT: a provisional treaty, 23 "Contracting Parties", meant only to initiate terms for ratification.
  • 1950 ITO fails: the US itself failed to ratify (internal fight: multinationals wanted investment included; the State Department resisted). GATT, the stopgap, became the institution; it became the WTO in 1995.
  • Why developing countries stayed out: they were running Import Substitution Industrialisation (ISI); GATT says "open your market", so they refused (even South Korea only joined in 1967).
Multilateralism: pros and cons (slide).
  • Pros: creates international standards / best practice; socialises members; all benefit from the rule of law.
  • Cons: large membership makes negotiation and procedure complex; major decisions collapse to the lowest common denominator (every one of 166 members has a veto). This is Trump's "we have to do it on our own" argument.

The WTO's core rules and principles

1. Most Favoured Nation (MFN), treat every member as well as your best-treated partner.
  • What it is: any concession two members agree spreads automatically to all members, so trade barriers fall fast.
  • Exceptions: Article 24 (free-trade agreements like NAFTA and customs unions like the EU may give each other better-than-MFN terms); special & differential treatment for developing countries (Part IV of GATT, 1964).
  • The naming trick: US public hated "giving China most favoured nation status", so USTR rebranded it NTR (Normal Trade Relations) and opposition faded. Same rule, softer label.
2. Reciprocity, concessions must be exchanged, not given free.
  • The subtlety: reciprocity is not sector-by-sector. If you sell cheese and I sell steel, matching my steel cut with your steel cut helps me nothing; you must cut cheese.
  • The exam-relevant error: Trump's "same tariff in every sector" ignores comparative advantage; different countries export different things, which is the whole basis of trade.
3. Transparency, members must publish their trade regulations.
  • Why it is weak: it relies on countries confessing their own bad behaviour ("like asking students who shared the QR code"); they don't.
  • The workaround: Simon Evenett's open-source GlobalTradeAlert.org independently tracks every country's discriminatory measures.
4. Safeguards, permitted exceptions to restrict trade.
  • Grounds: national security, dumping (selling below home-market price to grab share), and a rapid surge in imports.
  • National-security trap: if a country invokes national security it claims the measure cannot be reviewed by dispute settlement; once barely used, it is now "the basis of US trade policy".
5. Development, special and differential treatment for developing countries.
  • Origin: in 1964 developing countries formed UNCTAD and threatened to side with the USSR; the US conceded Part IV of GATT to keep them in.
  • The catch: being "developing" is a prize (lighter obligations), so everyone claims it, Singapore did until ~5 years ago.

Non-tariff barriers: the real game once tariffs fell

Intuition: the disguised NTB As tariffs dropped over eight GATT rounds, protection migrated to non-tariff barriers: "health and safety" rules engineered to block imports while applying equally to everyone, so they survive a WTO challenge.
Three classic disguised NTBs from class.
  • Japanese skis: foreign skis "unsafe because Japanese snow is different", yet Japan's World Cup racers all used European skis ("those are experts").
  • Ontario beer-in-bottles: a "recycling / environmental" rule (Molson's behind it) that made imported US canned beer heavier and breakable to transport. "Throwing sand in the wheels."
  • French farm protection: reframed over time from security → health → environment ("the pretty countryside brings tourists").

III. Problems in the WTO and the rise of unilateralism

Four problems (slide section III).
  • NGO complaints: environment, labour, inequality, "benefits big business".
  • Developed vs developing tension: "kicking away the ladder" (Ha-Joon Chang), rich countries used industrial policy, then deny it to others.
  • Ineffective dispute settlement: rulings are legally binding but hard to force on a determined great power.
  • Neopopulism / the Trump factor: the US openly undermines the WTO; rise of unilateralism and mercantilism toward "lawless" trade.
Why developing countries join anyway (Exit / Voice / Loyalty + lock-in).
  • Exit: staying out risks being excluded from trade growth.
  • Voice: group together to exert bargaining power.
  • Loyalty: the rule of law protects them; otherwise big countries dominate even more.
  • Lock-in: binding legal commitments to liberal trade make their own future defections costly (a commitment device).
The Appellate Body is "dead", but the WTO was not useless. Two facts coexist: (1) the appeals court is paralysed because the US (Trump 1, Biden, Trump 2) refuses to appoint judges, so any loser just appeals into the void ("then nothing happens"); yet (2) for ~30 years (645 cases as of May 2026) dispute settlement worked, even the US and China complied and changed policy. Don't confuse "a great power can break it when super-stressed" with "it failed". It served middle and developing countries well.

US unilateral trade actions (the alternative once the WTO is sidelined).

6 · Mega-Regional Institutions: The EU and Asia-Pacific

This session puts the Arena leg of (IA)³ under the microscope: when and where firms lobby depends on which institution owns the rule. It runs the comparison along two models of regional order, the deeply-integrated, supranational EU and the under-institutionalised, minilateral Asia-Pacific, using Aggarwal's own framework of nesting and minilateralism.

Intuition: the Arena leg An institution is just a fixed Arena with its own rulebook: choose the right one and your lobbying is leveraged, choose wrong and you are shouting into the void. The whole session is a map of which Arena makes which rule.

The two organising concepts (Aggarwal's framework)

Intuition: minilateralism Negotiating with 200 countries at once is nearly impossible (Aggarwal counts 19,900 bilateral pairs); a handful of like-minded states can strike a deal fast. Minilateralism is the small-group shortcut when the big multilateral table is deadlocked.
Minilateralism, small groupings of countries acting together on trade or security.
  • What it is: more than bilateral, fewer than multilateral; e.g. CPTPP, RCEP, IPEF in trade; the Quad, AUKUS, the China-Japan-Korea trilateral in security.
  • Why it has risen: global institutions (the WTO) are largely paralysed (US blocking Appellate Body judges since Trump and Biden); US-China economic statecraft pushes states to smaller, faster groupings.
  • Versus multilateralism: multilateralism cuts transaction and information costs, builds legitimacy and dispute settlement, and lets weak states pool pressure; but it is slow and usually only forms after wars or power transitions.
  • The trade-off: minilateral deals are easier to negotiate and can hit political payoffs quickly, but they fragment the rulebook into a patchwork.
Intuition: nesting The question is not just "how many small deals" but "do they stack neatly inside the bigger ones, or fight them?" Nesting is whether a minilateral deal is a Russian doll that fits inside the WTO, or a rogue that undermines it.
Nesting, arranging smaller, specific agreements hierarchically inside broader regional or global frameworks.
  • What it is: structuring accords so a narrow deal sits coherently within a wider one (a meta-regime of principles and norms, plus a regime of rules and procedures).
  • The clearest example: GATT Article 24, which permits customs unions and FTAs provided their rules conform to the GATT, the textbook case of a deal nested inside the multilateral system.
  • Three possible fates of a minilateral deal: it can be (a) nested within the broader architecture, (b) part of a division of labour, or (c) in conflict, undermining the wider institution.
  • Why it matters now: with the WTO Dispute Settlement Body broken, states pay little attention to GATT conformity, so the constraint that used to keep deals nested has weakened.
Don't confuse minilateralism with nesting. Minilateralism is about the number of players in a deal; nesting is about whether that deal fits inside a wider institution. A deal can be minilateral and well-nested (RCEP as a stepping stone), or minilateral and rogue (IPEF, which conforms to no broader trade rulebook).

🎙 In class Rules of origin are the real supply-chain trap. Ship a Toyota into Mexico, stamp it "hecho en México" and send it north tariff-free? No: under NAFTA 62.5% of the car's value (now 75% under USMCA) must be North American. When Ford sued Honda for missing the threshold, Honda had to take an entire Accord apart and cost every one of its ~10,000 parts to prove content ("the Full Employment Act for lawyers"). The payoff: everyone built plants in northern Mexico, so Trump's tariffs on Mexico and Canada hit Ford and GM's own 30-year North American supply chain, hence the USMCA carve-out. Domestic-content requirements (China, India) do the same job in reverse: force multinationals to buy local.
🎙 In class FTAs are not always about economic gains, often it is national security. Korea's first FTA was with Chile (opposite hemispheres, so Korean and Chilean fruit never compete in season, complementary economies; and Chile was "good practice" before negotiating with the US). But the US deals with Morocco, Bahrain and Oman after 9/11 had gains so small "it cost more to fly back and forth to negotiate than the annual benefit", they were about security, not trade. (Useful for Q3: trade agreements as instruments of national-security linkage.)
Cassis de Dijon (1979), why mutual recognition beats harmonisation.
  • The case: Germany barred a French blackcurrant liqueur (16% alcohol) from being sold as "liqueur" under a 25%-minimum German rule.
  • The ruling: the European Court of Justice held that a product lawfully sold in one member state must be recognised in the others; such barriers are "measures having an effect equivalent to quantitative restrictions" (Article 30) and are banned.
  • The lesson: mutual recognition leads to faster integration than harmonisation, you do not need every country to agree one rule, only to accept each other's.

Where to lobby: the EU's institutional Arena

Intuition: pick the right door The EU has several doors into the rule, and the firm's job is to knock on the one that owns the decision. Big new rules and money fights are settled by member governments; technical detail by the Commission.
EU nonmarket strategy, when to go where (deck).
  • New rules, especially major ones → member-country governments, especially the big ones.
  • Major distributional conflicts (e.g. farm subsidies) → member-country governments, especially the big ones.
  • Technical issues and implementation → the European Commission.
  • Trade (e.g. WTO) → the EU Trade Commissioner.
🎙 In class The de Gucht solar-panel case shows where real power sits. When Trade Commissioner Karel de Gucht put anti-dumping restrictions on Chinese solar panels (2013), China did not lobby Brussels, it phoned Chancellor Merkel directly. She said "I'll take care of it", because Germany feared Chinese retaliation against its huge China export market. The lesson: on a big issue, the member-state government (especially a large one) outranks the Commissioner, exactly the "go to the member state for major issues" rule, shown live.
Article 24's three conditions, and why the EU itself broke them.
  • The rule: a preferential bloc is GATT-legal only if (1) average external tariffs are lower after than before, (2) it covers all products, and (3) it was originally written to grandfather European colonial preferences (the British and French wanted to keep them; the US, with no colonies, wanted open markets).
  • The irony: the 1951 European Coal and Steel Community was illegal under Article 24 (coal and steel is not "all products"). The US granted a 5-year waiver because containing the Soviet Union mattered more than its own rule; the Europeans then covered all products with the Treaty of Rome (1957).
Why the EU project held together (deck's four-part answer).
  • A common enemy: the Soviet Union gave a shared external threat.
  • Franco-German leadership: the two anchors overcame mutual animosity to drive integration.
  • US backing: Washington wanted a "united" Europe (a WWII legacy).
  • The Commission: a standing body to promote effective coordination.
Copenhagen membership criteria (1993), the price of entry.
  • Political: stable institutions guaranteeing democracy, rule of law, human rights, protection of minorities.
  • Economic: a functioning market economy able to cope with Union competitive pressure.
  • Administrative / acquis: capacity to take on the obligations of membership and to apply and manage EU law in practice.

7 · International Relations: The Future World Order and Global Business Strategy

The closing session steps back from firm-level nonmarket tactics to the system level: it hands you a set of IR lenses for reading what drives state behaviour, because the politicians and governments a firm must navigate each see the world through one of these maps. The pay-off is strategic: theories of conflict inform your read on geopolitical risk, and theories of state decision-making inform your country and regional plans. This is where the course's (IA)³ spine meets the world stage: the IR lens is largely a theory of Actors (which states matter and how many poles there are), their Interests (power and survival vs. prosperity and peace), and the Arena in which they contend (anarchy, institutions, the market).

Intuition: theories as maps A theory is a Singapore-MRT map of world politics: it leaves out everything except what its author thinks matters. Each IR lens plots different variables (power, trade, regime type, institutions) and so highlights different risks and tells you which actors you actually need to engage.

The two camps and the case that frames them

US–China as the live case: which lens are you reading it through?
  • Engagement view: Obama's line that "we have more to fear from a weakened, threatened China than a successful rising China", interdependence and inclusion reduce conflict (a liberal read).
  • Inevitable-conflict view, the Thucydides Trap: when a dissatisfied rising power rivals a ruling power, military conflict becomes likely. Thucydides on Athens vs. Sparta; formalised as power transition theory (Organski). Precedents: Germany vs. Britain a century ago; China vs. the US now (a realist read).

Two broad perspectives (the spine of the session). Constructivist and Marxist lenses are flagged but left out of this session.

Don't confuse Realism with Neorealism. Both are power-first, but realism still looks inside states (geography, status, leaders); neorealism (Waltz) is systemic, it insists you need nothing beyond relative capabilities. Waltz borrows directly from market structure: atomistic→perfect competition, oligopoly→multipolar, duopoly→bipolar, monopoly→unipolar.
Waltz's three logical elements of a systemic IR theory.
  • The international system lacks hierarchy (anarchy, no sovereign above states).
  • States are functionally similar (all do the same basic things to survive).
  • The distribution of capabilities is key (so polarity, not personality, explains outcomes).
Neorealist predictions and the manager's takeaway.
  • States self-help: draw on self or allies; balances form against the strongest.
  • Smaller states may bandwagon (join the rising power) or hedge (keep options open).
  • States emulate each other's successful practices.
  • Polarity verdict: bipolar = most war-free; multipolar = war-prone; unipolar = unstable.
  • Implication: focus on the balance of power when assessing where conflict is likely.

Conclusions on IR theories (the deck's own wrap).
  • The lenses disagree on the causes of conflict and the preconditions of peace.
  • Realism, long dominant, is state-centric and says little about MNCs, transnational movements, terrorist groups, or globalisation's effect on state power, a real limit for business analysis.
  • Liberalism revolves around commerce, democracy and institutions.
  • Bottom line for firms: understand how the politicians you face see the world, because their lens drives their policy.

Reshoring vs. friendshoring, the efficiency–security trade-off.
  • Reshoring: bring operations home. Maximises security of supply via sovereign control of production, but at significant efficiency cost, it directly defies comparative advantage.
  • Friendshoring: bring operations to allied markets. Improves security by leaning on diplomatic and military ties to reduce (not eliminate) risk; more efficient than reshoring (allies can be competitive) but still less efficient than the free market.
  • The spectrum: free market (most efficient, least secure) → friendshoring → reshoring (most secure, least efficient).
Navigating geopolitics: risk vs. uncertainty (the closing managerial frame).
  • Firms are playing catch-up: they recognise geopolitical significance but stay reactive; political analysis can no longer be an after-thought.
  • Uncertainty, not just risk: traditional risks are managed by function (credit, legal, market, operational, regulatory). Geopolitical risk cuts across all functions and does not follow a quarterly calendar.
  • Turn risk into a premium: use stakeholder mapping and political-landscape mapping to make geopolitical fluency a competitive advantage, not just a cost.
Action plan for organisations (the deck's checklist).
  • Develop literacy and a common language on geopolitical risk: what are the issues and their implications?
  • Build professionalism around geopolitical issues.
  • Every month, the board discusses the most geopolitically exposed key markets and sets a game plan.
  • Translate geopolitics into the company's own language and tangible issues.
