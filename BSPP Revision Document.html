The exam, and how to pass it

A closed-book written final covering the whole course. The single most repeated instruction in Roulet's answers: define the concept precisely, give the economic mechanism, illustrate with the lecture's own example, then weigh the trade-off on both sides. These are "debatable outcome" questions, so a good answer argues both sides before concluding.

The sessions

1 · Market Failures

The course's foundation session: it sets up the one justification for government in the economy, that markets sometimes fail to deliver an efficient outcome, and walks three escalating examples, from a clear-cut failure (an unsafe drug) through a textbook failure (adverse selection in insurance) to a debatable one (the distribution of income). Everything later in the course (the green transition, Big Tech, taxation, trade) is a variation on "where does the market fail or produce a debatable outcome, and what should the state do about it."

Intuition: what is business for, what is it bad atMarkets are extremely good at allocating resources efficiently. The course exists for the residue: the cases where the market fails (too much pollution, no insurance for the sick) and the cases where it works but the result is debatable (who gets the gains). Government enters in exactly those two gaps, not everywhere.

What is a market failure

Market failure, the working definition.
  • Textbook: anything that causes the market economy to deliver an outcome that does not maximise efficiency.
  • Roulet's gloss in class: the abstract definition "is not that helpful"; learn it through concrete cases (unsafe product, people lacking insurance, monopoly).
  • Why it happens: firms maximise profit, so absent regulation they will not internalise the side effects (unsafe drugs, pollution) that society cares about.
Three buckets a market outcome can fall into (the opening framework).
  • Socially optimal: the market allocates well; leave it alone.
  • Market failure: e.g. too much pollution; the state intervenes to correct the failure.
  • Debatable outcome: e.g. the distribution of income and of productivity gains; the state may redistribute, and how it does so is contested.
Government "size" (public spending as a share of GDP) varies across countries but is never negligible, and it rises in crisis times.

Example 1 · Thalidomide and the regulation trade-off

🎙 In class"A German drugmaker comes to the market with a new drug, thalidomide, and it works super well. You take it, you fall asleep, you wake up fresh. Its sales become as large as aspirin, it floods sixty countries. But it so happens that when pregnant women take it in their first trimester, it alleviates morning sickness, and by 1961 there are abnormal rates of babies born with severe birth defects." The case is the live illustration of why a profit-maximising firm needs an external regulator.
The Thalidomide timeline (1950s onward).
  • Demand and entry: large 1950s demand for sedatives; thalidomide enters in Germany in 1957, the maker claims it is safe for everyone.
  • Scale: by 1960 marketed in 46 countries, sales almost as large as aspirin; also found to reduce morning sickness in pregnancy.
  • The harm: by 1961 serious birth defects linked to the drug surface across Europe, Britain, Canada, the Middle East.
  • The regulatory point: the regulation at the time was very lenient, so nothing stopped it; the crisis is what created modern drug regulation.
What governments can do, ex ante vs ex post (the in-class brainstorm).
  • Before it happens (prevent): require clinical trials; impose prescription barriers; restrict use for at-risk groups (e.g. forbid for pregnant women).
  • After it happens (respond): remove or restrict the drug; help victims (class-action lawsuits, compensation funds); punish the company (financial or criminal); and crucially, change the regulation for the future.
What actually happened: the crisis wrote the rulebook.
  • US: FDA inspector Frances Oldham Kelsey never approved it, despite pressure from the manufacturer and her own supervisors.
  • Europe: banned by 1961.
  • Seminal regulation: the 1962 Kefauver-Harris amendments to the FDA act (companies must prove safety and efficacy before marketing, a process that can take 8 to 12 years); the first EU pharmaceutical directive (1965).
  • Twist: in Brazil the drug was re-licensed in 1965 to treat leprosy; the molecule is genuinely useful for severe conditions (leprosy, skin cancer), which is why "ban it" is not the whole answer.
🎙 In class"The reason we have all this debate is because firms are just maximising profit. If they just maximise profit, they are not going to take into account the potential side effects of their products. That is why you want regulation." Note the open question Roulet plants for later: a useful drug with a harmful side effect is a negative externality, the topic of a future session.

The exam-shaped move: weigh the trade-off from two viewpoints. Roulet's recurring question is "pros and cons of strict drug/chemical regulation, from the point of view of (1) business and (2) consumers." Answer it as a matrix, not prose; the verdict is that you sit somewhere on a slider between innovation and protection depending on what is at stake.

The pattern recurs well beyond 1960s pharma (the "still happens today" slide).

  • The complication Roulet flags: responsibility is genuinely hard to assign, the company, the government/agency, the prescribing doctor (cf. the opioid crisis), and even the patient can each carry some.

Example 2 · Adverse selection in insurance

🎙 In class"The key word in adverse selection has got to be the fact that there is an asymmetry of information. One side of the market knows more than the other." That is the whole concept in one line: information asymmetry between buyer and seller, which the rest of the example just dramatises.
Adverse selection, the definition.
  • What it is: a market failure driven by an information asymmetry between buyer and seller.
  • Two directions: the buyer cannot verify the quality of what the seller offers (used cars), or the seller cannot verify the risk-type of the buyer (insurance).
  • The consequence (why it is a failure): the market unravels, mutually beneficial trades that should happen do not, because the informed side cannot credibly prove quality.
🎙 In class"I want to buy a used car. Felix's car is actually very good, he just does not want it; Kunal's car had a lot of issues, but of course he does not tell me. I offer the average price." This is Akerlof's market for lemons, run live as a classroom example.

The used-car unravelling (mechanism). State the WHY only on the non-obvious link.

Buyer offers average price→ only bad-car owners accept→ buyer anticipates this, offers less→ still only worse cars accept→ price spirals to lowest quality

Why the spiral: at any average price the good-car owner withdraws (he would be underpaid), so the average quality on offer falls, so the rational buyer bids lower again, repeating until only the worst quality clears. Good trades that would benefit both sides never happen.

Intuition: the insurance death spiralFlip who holds the information. Now the buyer knows their own health and the insurer does not. Price at the population average and only the people who are expensive to insure find it a good deal; the healthy walk away; the pool gets sicker; the insurer must raise the premium; more healthy people leave. The same lemons logic, ending in collapse rather than a low price.
Price at average health risk→ only high-risk people buy→ healthy opt out→ claims exceed premiums→ insurer raises premium→ more healthy leave (pool worsens)

Why each raise backfires: every premium increase pushes out the marginally-healthy first (they value cover least), so the average risk in the remaining pool rises, forcing the next increase. In class: "only the lifetime smokers buy it, so you go bankrupt." The only sustainable price corresponds to the highest-risk type.

How to solve adverse selection: two equilibria (slide framework). Read across, the two routes attack different things, solving the information problem itself vs solving its consequence.

RouteWhat it fixesHowThe catch
Separating equilibriumThe information asymmetry itselfUse technology to reveal the risk type (data monitoring, screening), so each type is priced on its own riskPrivacy and fairness concerns (the high-risk get priced out or excluded)
Pooling equilibriumThe consequence of the asymmetryForce everyone into one pool so the sick are subsidised by the healthy. Two variants: (a) mandatory private insurance; (b) universal public system(a) needs very dissuasive sanctions to enforce; (b) is universal for sure but has to be funded
Why bringing the healthy in is the whole game (take-away from example 2).
  • The core problem: unless insurance is mandatory, healthy people opt out, premiums rise, and even more people drop out because they cannot afford it.
  • Mandatory private route: only works with sanctions dissuasive enough to make opting out irrational.
  • Public provision route: guarantees universality, but the financing question (who pays, how) is the live political fight.
  • Real test case: Obamacare / the ACA (passed 2010, phased in to 2015) is the empirical question of whether the mandate-plus-subsidy design actually pulled the healthy into the pool. ★ the slide poses "Did Obamacare work?" but the deck shows the question, not a stated verdict, so no answer is asserted here.

Example 3 · Income distribution and inequality

Intuition: a "debatable" outcome, not a clean failureUnlike an unsafe drug or an unravelling insurance market, inequality is not obviously a failure of the market: the market may allocate efficiently and still produce a distribution we dislike. So this is the course's first "debatable" case, where government redistributes and the debate is about whether and how much.
The global vs within-country split (the central, counter-intuitive fact).
  • Across countries, inequality fell: far fewer people in extreme poverty (2 billion under $2/day in 1990 to ~700m in 2019), much more overlap between countries' income distributions, and a big purchasing-power gain for the emerging-market middle class, especially in Asia.
  • Within most countries, inequality rose since ~1980: shown by both Gini coefficients and income shares.
  • The reconciling picture: the elephant curve, who gained over 1988 to 2008.
Measuring within-country inequality, two tools.
  • Gini coefficient: a single 0-to-1 summary of how unequal the distribution is.
  • Income shares: the slice of total income going to the top 10% / top 1% vs the bottom 50%, measured pre-tax and pre-transfer.
  • What both show: in most countries, a rising top-1% / top-10% share and a stagnating-or-declining bottom-50% share between the 1980s and the 2010s (US and Western Europe most starkly; India and Russia also up).
Two caveats Roulet flags on the income-share story.
  • Growth rate matters: similar income-share trends in China and the US are not the same situation, fast aggregate growth means even a shrinking share can be a rising absolute income.
  • The starting point is cherry-pickable: the 1960s-70s were a period of exceptionally low inequality, so trends measured from there exaggerate the rise; a longer-run view of wealth inequality looks different.
  • Wealth > income concentration: Oxfam (2018), 82% of new wealth went to the top 1% and 0 to the bottom 50%; the 8 richest held as much as the poorest 3.6 billion.
What drives rising within-country inequality (market forces + policy).
  • Market forces: a rising skill premium from skill-biased technological change, plus increased globalisation.
  • Policy choices: falling top marginal tax rates; leniency on anti-trust.
  • The role of firms: widening wage gaps between firms (even within an industry); "superstar" firms concentrate the most-skilled people, focus on core competencies, outsource the rest, and pay up for top talent ("if you are offered a seat on a rocket ship, just get on").

Why a manager should care: inequality feeds back on itself. The reflection question "is inequality a market failure, and should corporate leaders care?" is answered by two mechanisms.

High inequality→ lower social mobility→ more inequality next generation

Why it compounds: the privileged secure better education for their children and capture political influence ("political capture"), so without a major shock more inequality begets more, an equity and an efficiency problem. This is the Great Gatsby curve: countries with higher inequality show children's incomes more tightly tied to their parents'.

Reasons to care, and the take-away (example 3).
  • Mobility / fairness: equality of opportunity is negatively correlated with the level of inequality.
  • Stability: inequality generates social and political unrest and feeds protectionist movements.
  • The honest verdict: globally we have seen falling absolute poverty and convergence between countries, but within most countries the top 1% captured the prosperity gains while the bottom 50% (especially in the US and Western Europe) were left out.

Session take-aways

The three threads, in one place.
  • Failure vs debate: the market fails when it delivers an inefficient allocation (unsafe product, uninsured people); some outcomes (the top-1% share, untaxed corporate profit) are debatable rather than clear failures.
  • Government corrects and redistributes, imperfectly: it steps in for failures and intervenes in debatable cases, but public policy has its own failures (too-strict regulation stifles innovation).
  • Adverse selection is the canonical failure: asymmetric information rations the market; the fix is to bring the low-risk in (mandatory or public insurance).
  • Inequality: down between countries, up within them; associated with lower mobility and political instability.
Exam lens.
  • Format: 2-hour final is 90% of the grade, 10% participation; "a few questions on the concepts covered in class, where you have to justify your answer with some reasoning." A sample exam and a model good-answer are posted under Course overview / Grading scheme.
  • The four moves she rewards: (1) define the concept cleanly (e.g. adverse selection = information asymmetry that rations the market); (2) give the mechanism (the death-spiral / lemons logic, step by step); (3) illustrate with the lecture's own example (thalidomide, used cars, insurance, the elephant curve), not an invented one; (4) weigh the trade-off from named viewpoints (business vs consumers, innovation vs protection) rather than asserting one side.
  • Bank these distinctions: market failure vs debatable outcome; separating vs pooling equilibrium; global vs within-country inequality; Gini vs income shares; market forces vs policy choices as drivers.
  • Likely trap: calling inequality a "market failure". Roulet frames it as a debatable outcome, the market can be efficient and still produce a distribution we reject, so the careful answer separates efficiency from distribution before arguing for intervention.

2 · Managing the Green Transition

The course's one economics-heavy session: it builds climate policy from a single graph (the wedge between private and social marginal cost of a polluting activity), then derives the whole policy toolkit from it, a Coasian private fix, a Pigouvian carbon tax (price approach), and a cap-and-trade market (quantity approach), before testing them against the real world (the gilets jaunes, the EU ETS, the BP/Shell retreat). The conceptual detour: externalities

Intuition — the missing price An externality is just a missing price: you impose a cost on someone (or hand them a benefit) and no money changes hands for it. Every policy in this lecture is an attempt to put a price on that missing transaction so the offending agent feels it.
Four flavours of externality (define before drawing anything).
  • Negative production externality: a firm's production harms a third party uncompensated. Slide example: a firm polluting a lake hurts fishermen; in-class: Sanofi's anti-epileptic plant linked to autism in neighbouring births. This is the climate case and the lecture's focus.
  • Negative consumption externality: a person's consumption harms others, e.g. smoking in a restaurant.
  • Positive externality: your action makes another party better off without you capturing the benefit, e.g. spillovers from a smart classmate sharing a business idea; green technology with knowledge spillovers.
  • The rule (closing slide): negative production externality ⇒ PMC < SMC; negative consumption externality ⇒ PMB > SMB.
PMC vs SMC, the wedge.
  • Private marginal cost (PMC): the direct cost to the producer of one more unit, the ordinary supply curve.
  • Social marginal cost (SMC): PMC plus any cost the production imposes on others, the supply curve as society sees it.
  • The wedge = the marginal external damage: the vertical gap between SMC and PMC at each quantity. Policy's job is to make the firm act on the red SMC curve, not the blue PMC curve.
  • Why demand still matters: the goal is NOT to shut the plant. The drug still cures epilepsy; the demand curve reminds you the activity serves a purpose, so you move from over-production to the social optimum, not to zero.

The private fix: Coasian payments

🎙 In class "It is not because we hear the word externality that we necessarily need government." In the smoking example: "I buy you a drink, you're fine with me smoking, we solve the externality." A negotiated side-payment can reach B with no state at all, in theory.
Coase theorem — bargaining can reach the social optimum without government.
  • What it says: private negotiation between the polluter and the harmed party can deliver the socially optimal quantity (move A→B) on its own.
  • Two conditions: (1) well-defined property rights; (2) costless bargaining.
  • Allocation-neutral: it does NOT matter who holds the right. If the fishermen own the lake, the firm pays them for the damage; if the firm owns the lake, the fishermen pay the firm to produce less. Either way you reach B.
  • The price is the same either way: the payment always equals the marginal damage (the SMC−PMC wedge), regardless of direction. Only who receives the money changes.
When the Coasian fix breaks down (closing slide + class discussion). Real-world climate change violates every assumption:
  • Property rights ill-defined: who owns the air or the climate? Unclear who pays whom.
  • Transaction costs / not costless: if it takes "two years and 2,000 lawyers," the side-payment logic collapses.
  • Large numbers ⇒ collective-action problem: billions of people and hundreds of countries cannot coordinate a side-payment.
  • Damage is hard to quantify: how do you price the cost of one tonne of CO₂, or the autism risk next door?
  • What government fixes vs doesn't: the state CAN help with ownership/enforcement and national coordination; it still struggles with damage quantification and international coordination.

The two policy approaches

Intuition — same target, two dials Both instruments aim at exactly the same point B; they just grab a different dial. The price approach sets the price (the tax) and lets quantity fall out. The quantity approach sets the quantity (the cap) and lets the price fall out. Under perfect information they land on the identical equilibrium; under uncertainty you control one variable and stay blind on the other.
The taxonomy (slides p22).
  • 1. Price approach (mimics the Coasian solution): corrective Pigouvian tax on the negative externality, or a subsidy for positive ones / clean alternatives.
  • 2. Quantity approach: a cap-and-trade system, or other direct regulation (bans, quotas).
  • Both share one idea: "finding the right price to make agents internalise the cost they impose on others."

Equivalence & the Weitzman trade-off (slide p27).
  • Under perfect information they are identical: if you knew all firms' costs, the demand curve, and the damage, price and quantity would reach the exact same B. The permit price in a cap system equals the optimal tax, both equal the marginal damage.
  • The real problem: "I don't know where to draw the red curve." With imperfect information you must guess, so the choice matters, you control one variable and accept uncertainty on the other.
  • Choosing between them = choosing what you must guarantee: guarantee firms' cost ⇒ use a tax; guarantee the pollution reduction ⇒ use a cap (the closing slide's exact framing).
Subsidies — the price approach in reverse.
  • Mechanism: make the green option cheaper (tax credits / rebates) rather than the dirty option dearer.
  • Price effect: deflationary, pushes prices down, vs a tax/cap which is mildly inflationary.
  • Who pays: the taxpayer funds subsidies (vs producers/consumers of the dirty good under a tax/cap).
  • Why rarer: it costs the public purse, so only the fiscally unconstrained (the US, running a large deficit) lean on it; most countries reach for tax or cap instead.
Don't say "firms just buy permits, so nothing changes" as if it disproves the system. If no one abates and everyone simply pays, you set the price (or cap) wrong, it's too cheap. The EU ETS literally did this at launch (too many allowances ⇒ permit price collapsed to zero); the fix was to tighten the cap repeatedly, not to abandon the instrument.

Policies in practice

The real-world toolkit, by jurisdiction.
  • Singapore — carbon tax: introduced 2019 at $5/tCO₂, raised to $25 (2024–25), $45 (2026–27), targeting $50–80 by 2030; levied only on large emitters (≥25,000 tCO₂e/yr). Start low, pre-commit to rising, so firms have time to adjust, conditional on the government being credible.
  • EU — ETS cap-and-trade: launched 2005 (a tax would have needed unanimity; ETS did not). Free allowances + auctioning; "Fit for 55" (2021) tightens the cap to −62% vs 2005 by 2030 and extends scope (maritime, ETS 2 for buildings/road).
  • CBAM (Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism): stops carbon leakage, importers report and pay for embedded emissions at the ETS price, with credit for any carbon price already paid abroad. Levels the field so firms don't just offshore the dirty step.
  • US — IRA (Inflation Reduction Act): the subsidy route, large tax credits for clean generation/storage and for consumers (EVs, home retrofits). The "carrot" to the EU's "stick."
  • China — EV quotas (regulation): EVs 1% of new sales in 2016 → mandated 8% by 2020, then 25% by 2025, 40% by 2030, plus demand boosters (subsidies, license-plate priority). Targets met, credible government + a market too big for carmakers to exit.

The recent pushback — why the price approach alone fails

🎙 In class "For a long time economists thought: climate change is simple, it's an externality, put a carbon price and let agents optimise. Conceptually 100% true. But in practice it's a bit more complicated, when you make things costly so people switch, they need something to switch to." That missing alternative is the whole story of the pushback.
France — the gilets jaunes (2019) and the turn to planning.
  • The trigger: a 2019 fuel-tax rise, textbook carbon pricing, made driving costlier. But rural households can't substitute away from driving (no public-transport alternative), so they just paid more, didn't cut emissions, and revolted.
  • The lesson: for consumers especially, the price signal alone backfires without a concrete alternative; cities that paired road-pricing with transit/biking succeeded where blanket fuel taxes failed.
  • The planning response: France created the Secrétariat Général à la Planification Écologique (2022) to plan sector-by-sector, coordinating anticipations and giving market players visibility.
  • The tension: planning that goes too granular rigidifies the system and pre-empts the technological breakthroughs only a price signal incentivises. The answer is a blend, keep the price signal for innovation, add planning for concrete alternatives.
  • 2024: agricultural riots over regulation, agriculture has the least ambition in the French plan precisely because food must be produced and low-emission alternatives (to pesticides, livestock) are not realistically available.
"Just put the price signal and let agents optimise" is the textbook answer, and the exam trap. Roulet's whole pushback section is the rebuttal: efficient on paper, but it ignores whether a switchable alternative exists. The sophisticated answer pairs the price approach (for innovation) with targeted planning/regulation (for sectors with no alternative). Don't write the naive version as the final word.

3 · Corporate Taxation and Firms’ Location Decisions

A session in two halves around one running case. The Apple / Ireland / European Commission saga is the deep-dive: it shows how a multinational legally shifts profit to a near-zero-tax shell, and why the EC had to fight it as a competition case, not a tax case. The second half steps back to the general question, should we even tax corporations, and if so how, setting up the where-to-tax principles, the race to the bottom, and the OECD’s 15% global minimum.

🎙 In class "This looks like a tax case. It is not. The European Commission cannot do tax." The whole case turns on a reframing: Brussels has no power over a member state’s tax rate (sovereignty), so it had to attack the Apple-Ireland arrangement as illegal state aid under competition law instead.

The general takeaways come first; the Apple deep-dive at the end shows them in motion.

The two questions corporate taxation must answer

Intuition: the two hard problems Profit is not a physical thing sitting in one country; it is an accounting figure a firm can legally relocate. So before you can tax it you must answer two questions the slides flag: what counts as taxable profit, and where it should be taxed.
What to tax? Defining "profit" itself changes firm behaviour.
  • Exempt interest payments? If interest is deductible but dividends are not, you tilt the firm’s debt vs equity choice toward debt.
  • Allow capital depreciation? How fast you let firms write off capital changes their investment decisions.
  • The point: the tax base is not given by nature; every definitional choice creates a behavioural distortion.

Where to tax? Three competing principles. Take the profit on iPhone sales in Europe: each principle sends the tax to a different country, and each creates its own problem. This is the gold-standard matrix for the lecture, read straight across a row.

PrincipleWhat it meansWhere the iPhone-Europe profit is taxedThe problem it creates
1. SourceTax where the profit is registered / bookedIreland (where ASI books the sales)Loss of public revenue for non-tax-haven countries: profit is booked wherever the rate is lowest, so the booking location is gamed.
2. ResidenceTax where the capital owners are locatedThe US (where Apple’s owners sit)Owners’ residence is itself mobile and easy to restructure around; this is Apple’s own claim, that it "should mostly be paying in the US".
3. DestinationTax where the goods/services are purchased / consumedEurope (where the phones are bought)Consumers are far less mobile, so this is hard to game, but it breaks with the historic source-based system the whole world was built on.
Don’t confuse the source principle with where value is created. Source taxes where profit is booked, not where the phone was designed, made, or sold. That gap is exactly the loophole: a firm books the profit in the lowest-rate jurisdiction even though almost no real activity happens there.

The 1920s League of Nations principles, and why they broke

The international system still rests on principles a group of economists set in the 1920s. Each was sensible then and is gameable now.

1920s principleWhat it saysWhy it now fails
Source principleTax profit where it is registered.Profit gets registered in tax havens, draining revenue from everywhere else.
Arm’s-length principleA parent and its foreign subsidiary must price internal transfers as if they were unrelated firms trading at world-market prices. (If B is a Brazilian subsidiary of US parent A, both compute profits as if B sold to A at the market price.)Tax authorities cannot verify the price of every internal transaction; and for IP, a logo or an algorithm, there is often no comparable market price because the asset is not replicable between third parties.
Bilateral (not multilateral) agreementsTax treaties are struck country-to-country."Treaty shopping": firms route profits through whichever chain of bilateral treaties minimises tax.
Transfer pricing is the mechanism the arm’s-length rule is meant to police.
  • What it is: the internal prices a multinational sets on transfers between its own subsidiaries (royalties, licence fees, goods).
  • Why it matters: by overpricing what the high-tax unit "buys" from the low-tax unit, profit is shifted to the low-tax unit, all on paper.
  • The tell: the harder a transferred asset is to price (IP, brand, algorithm), the easier the rule is to abuse.
The verdict from class: reform has been "widely acknowledged for a long time, but it’s not easy." No single principle is clean, the rules are old, and every fix runs into national sovereignty over tax.

How profit gets shifted: the Double Irish–Dutch Sandwich

Intuition: routing profit through a sandwich The trick is to make the high-tax operating company pay away its profit as royalties to a low- or zero-tax entity holding the IP. The "sandwich" is the chain of shells that moves the royalty from Ireland, through a Dutch shell (to dodge withholding tax), to a tax-free Bermudian holding company.
Sales booked in Ireland Ltd→ pays royalties for IP→ via Dutch shell (no withholding)→ to Bermuda-resident holding co→ profit taxed at ≈0%
This is a structure, not a one-off. The slides list the same logic across Starbucks (Netherlands), Fiat (Luxembourg), Engie (Luxembourg), Amazon (Luxembourg), IKEA (Netherlands). The professor stressed the case is "not to pick on Apple", it illustrates a broad pattern available to large firms.

Do the fixes work? Policy bites, then leaks elsewhere

Anti-evasion policy has real but partial effects.
  • What worked: ending bank secrecy (automatic exchange of information) sharply cut the share of offshore financial wealth that goes untaxed.
  • What leaked: it triggered a surge in offshore real estate, which is not covered by the automatic exchange of information.
  • The lesson: close one channel and the avoidance reroutes to the nearest uncovered asset class.

The OECD/G20 two-pillar solution (2021)

Intuition: coordinate to stop the race If countries compete one-by-one on tax rates, every country is pushed toward zero (the race to the bottom). The only escape is to agree a floor together. In October 2021, 137 countries signed an Inclusive Framework on Base Erosion and Profit Shifting (BEPS), built on two pillars.
Pillar 1: reallocate where the biggest firms’ profit is taxed (toward the market).
  • Who: multinationals with global revenue above $22.9bn and pre-tax profitability of at least 10%.
  • What: they must allocate 25% of residual profit to the market jurisdictions where goods/services were used or consumed.
  • Why it matters: a partial shift toward the destination principle, pulling tax back to where customers are (who cannot move).
Pillar 2: a 15% global minimum corporate tax.
  • Who: MNEs with annual sales above €750m.
  • What: a minimum 15% effective rate on income earned in each jurisdiction they operate in.
  • Why it matters: sets the floor that ends the race to the bottom in headline corporate-tax rates.
Don’t overstate the bite of Pillar 2. China, India and the US did not adopt it. Trump threatened a "revenge tax" on countries applying the minimum to US firms; the June 2025 G7 deal dropped the revenge tax but carved out American businesses. Several clauses weaken the 15% floor, e.g. tax credits are not counted toward it.

Should we tax corporations at all? The tax-base trade-off

🎙 In class "It is not about whether you want more tax or less tax. For a given amount of revenue R you must raise, what do you tax?" The class debated three bases, corporate profit, personal income, consumption (VAT), and there is no clean winner.
Tax baseArguments FORArguments AGAINST
Corporate profitVery large tax base (the key argument); makes firms, including foreign ones, pay for the public infrastructure they use; seen as fair / makes big firms pay their share.Mobile: profit and operations relocate to a lower-tax country, so the base runs away (the headline objection).

  • Falling corporate rates everywhere plus more countries adopting VAT, a shift from the mobile-but-fair base to the immobile-but-regressive one.
  • Distributional cost: because VAT is regressive, this shift moves the tax burden down the income scale.
  • The corrective: the OECD’s 15% minimum is the attempt to halt the corporate race to the bottom, hence the link back to Pillar 2.

Deep-dive: Apple, Ireland and the European Commission

🎙 In class "If you buy an iPhone anywhere in Europe, the Middle East, Africa, India, you are actually buying it from Apple Ireland." That is how worldwide sales profit lands in a 12.5% jurisdiction, and then a second step pushes the effective rate to almost nothing.

The scheme until 2014

The case’s six beats (how the professor walked it).
  • What was found: the EC ruled the stateless-HQ allocation was illegal state aid, a non-competitive favour. The rule broken was state aid, broken by Ireland, but the sanction fell on Apple.
  • The ask: pay the 12.5% Irish corporate tax on profit booked in Ireland 2003–2014 = ~€13bn ($14.5bn) plus interest (computed by deleting the disputed arrow and taxing at 12.5%).
  • Apple’s defence: nothing illegal; this is tax avoidance (optimising, legal), not evasion (hiding, illegal); it should mostly pay in the US; the EC violates member-state tax sovereignty; retroactivity creates terrible investment uncertainty; and it pays its "fair share" via jobs and its supplier network.
  • Who wanted Apple to pay: not Ireland (it wants to stay attractive to FDI), nor the US government at the time, hence the EC had to sue Ireland for failing to collect, and Apple parked ~€13bn in an escrow account in 2018.
  • Why the EC lost (July 2020): the General Court found insufficient evidence of state aid; arguing the Irish HQ lacked resources to generate the profit did not prove the profit was actually earned by the Irish branches.
  • Why it won (Sept 2024): on the EC’s appeal, the original decision was confirmed, to the surprise of many observers (the professor included).
Why a legal-looking case is really about power, and competition.
  • Not a tax case: the EC has no power over a member state’s tax rate (Irish sovereignty); it had to frame it as a competition / state-aid case within the single market.
  • The deeper question: "Apple broke no law, but was the law made to help Apple?" A firm this large never acts illegally, it changes the law so its conduct is legal.
  • Why it’s hard to prove: the favour was suggestive (nobody else used a 0% stateless HQ) but the arrangement was legal and others "could" have used it, so preferential treatment was hard to establish.
  • The competition logic of the fine: in competition law the standard remedy for unlawful state aid is to pay it back, so retroactivity (unusual in tax) is normal here.
Don’t confuse tax avoidance with tax evasion. Avoidance = legally optimising within the rules (what Apple claims). Evasion = illegally hiding income. Apple’s entire defence rests on being on the legal side of that line; the EC sidestepped it by suing on competition grounds, not tax.
Exam lens.
  • Roulet’s favourite move is the reframe: be ready to argue why Apple is a competition case, not a tax case (EC has no tax competence; member-state sovereignty over rates), and why the retroactive "pay it back" fits competition law.
  • The "for a given R, which tax?" prompt maps straight to the corporate-vs-PIT-vs-VAT matrix: lead with the headline trade-off (corporate = large base but mobile; VAT = stable but regressive; PIT = fair/progressive) rather than arguing for more or less tax.
  • Know the structures cold: the three where-to-tax principles (and each one’s problem), the arm’s-length / transfer-pricing weakness for IP, and the two BEPS pillars with their thresholds and limits (US/China/India out; carve-outs blunt the 15%).

It runs on one live case, Facebook's 2014 acquisition of WhatsApp and the FTC's failed attempt to unwind it, and closes on the breakup debate and the Stigler Report's menu of softer remedies.

WHY COMPETITION POLICY MATTERS

Goal of competition / antitrust policy.
  • What it is: ensuring fair competition and free-market principles hold in practice, so welfare (primarily consumer welfare) is maximised.
  • Two jobs: (1) assess the impact of M&A on market structure and competition levels; (2) investigate firm conduct, such as cartels and abuses of dominance.
  • The lineage: "born" with the US Sherman Act, 1890, which declared illegal "every contract, every combination in the form of trust or otherwise, in restraint of trade or commerce".
Why it matters for consumers (the Prices & Markets refresher).
  • Competitive markets are "efficient": they maximise gains from trade, the social surplus.
  • Monopoly does the opposite: higher prices, restricted output, and a loss of consumer surplus.
  • So the textbook harm is a price story: the welfare loss is measured by prices being higher than they should be. Hold onto this, it is exactly what breaks for Big Tech.
Worked examples of enforcement (from the slides).
  • Siemens / Alstom: the EU Commission prohibited the merger. The frequent alternative to outright prohibition is requiring partial divestments or "commitments".
  • Alibaba (China, Dec 2020): antitrust probe into "pick one of two" (forcing merchants to sell exclusively on its platform) → an 8% share drop and a $2.8B fine in 2021.
  • Epic v. Apple / Google: challenge to in-app payment commission rates. Epic won in Australia (last month) but lost against Apple in the US in 2021.

HOW THE REGULATOR ACTUALLY WORKS

The three questions a regulator asks of any merger, cartel, or dominant-firm conduct.
  • 1. Market boundaries: what is the relevant set of products / services that genuinely compete with each other? (This is the decisive lever, see the FTC v. Meta box.)
  • 2. The competition problem: is there, or could there be, economic "harm"? Mainly: could consumers be harmed, are prices too high, is quality poor, is there too little innovation, and how could it be fixed?
  • 3. The counterfactual: measure the market's outcomes against a benchmark, what would have happened absent the event or behaviour.
Difficulties: complex supply chains (most transactions are B2B, not B2C) and limited information.
Common criticisms of current competition policy (slide 13).
  • Too narrow: over-focused on a narrow definition of consumer welfare (i.e. price).
  • Too slow: investigations usually arise after the harm is done; hard to be timely.
  • Too lenient: regulators are too ready to hear out industries and their legal counsel.
  • Evidence-starved: the evidence is genuinely hard to come by.

WHY THE TOOLKIT BREAKS FOR DIGITAL MARKETS

Core service is zero-price⇒ no price to look "too high"⇒ the price-harm test reads "no harm"⇒ standard antitrust waves the deal through

The consumer-welfare standard judges harm by price. When the product is free (search, social, maps), there is no price to inflate, so the test that was built to catch monopolists goes quiet exactly where the modern platform is strongest. Harm migrates to non-price dimensions, quality, privacy, foreclosed innovation, which the old test does not see.

Why competition policy must evolve for the digital economy (slide 14).
  • Recognised gap: wide consensus that the framework must better reflect how digital markets differ from the textbook.
  • Shift the lens: more emphasis on the non-price characteristics of competition.
  • Hidden costs of data: data collection imposes costs on the public, data breaches, cyber-security, mis-information.

The economics that pushes digital markets to monopoly

Intuition — "free is not free"If you are not paying for the product, you are the product. The platform looks like a gift to consumers on price, but it is monetising attention and data, and the same features that make it useful (everyone is on it, it learns from your clicks) are exactly the forces that tip the market to a single winner.
Stigler Report (2019): why digital platforms tend to end up as monopolies.
  • Network effects: the platform is more valuable the more users it has, so users concentrate on the incumbent (winner-takes-most).
  • Economies of scale: large fixed costs spread over a huge user base.
  • Low marginal & distribution costs: serving one more user is nearly free, so size keeps paying off.
  • High and increasing returns to data: more users → more data → a better product → more users. The flywheel compounds the lead.
The other three Stigler warnings about digital platforms.
  • Free is not necessarily good for consumers: the price is your data and attention, not money.
  • Ad market power leverages into other markets: a platform can block or promote ads to kill or consolidate adjacent markets.
  • Addictive-by-design entrenches power: consumers stick with default options; Google pays to be the default search engine on iPhone, which consolidates its market power.

THE SPECIFIC HARMS

How digital dominance actually hurts, beyond price.
  • Killer acquisitions: the incumbent buys a nascent rival before it can grow into a threat (the Facebook–WhatsApp pattern below). Harm is the competition that never happens.
  • Self-preferencing / leverage: a dominant intermediary that also competes on its own platform can tilt the field toward its own products. Amazon is "great" on consumer prices yet "may raise some competition issues".
  • Non-price harms: degraded privacy and quality, which the price test cannot register.
  • Foreclosed innovation: if entrants cannot challenge incumbents, the engine of innovation and growth stalls.

The remedy menu: softer tools than breakup

🎤 In classFrom the fragment, on the burden of proof: today "if you cannot prove that consumers are harmed, it means that it is okay. Whereas if you shift the burden of proof, if you cannot prove that they're not harmed, then it is not okay." Flipping the default is the lever, because the evidence is always hard to come by.
Stigler Report (2019): possible solutions to the threats posed by digital platforms.
  • Force interoperability: make switching between platforms easy, which reduces network externalities (the main lock-in force).
  • Shift the burden of proof: on a platform acquisition, make the company prove the deal will not harm competition, rather than the regulator proving it will. This flips the default given how scarce the evidence is.
  • Regulate data privacy directly: use privacy regulation rather than antitrust where privacy is the real concern.
  • Reduce platforms' political power: e.g. mandatory disclosure of non-neutrality and of relationships with politicians.
"Other solutions besides breaking up" is the prof's actual emphasis. From the fragment, she endorses the student's point that breakup is not the only tool and "this is very true". Don't reduce the digital-antitrust answer to "break them up", the examinable nuance is the menu: regulate, force interoperability, shift the burden, or break up, matched to the specific harm.

Part A, Why countries trade (the free-trade benchmark)

Intuition, comparative advantageYou do not have to be the best at something to gain from trading it; you only have to be relatively less bad at it than at the alternative. Each side specialises where it gives up the least, and total output rises.
Comparative advantage (Ricardo), the free-trade benchmark.
  • Absolute vs comparative: Portugal has an absolute advantage in both cloth and wine; England is worse at both. Trade still pays.
  • Why: if capital and labour were perfectly mobile, everything would be made in Portugal. Since they are not, England specialises where its relative cost is lowest (cloth) and trades for wine.
  • The gains: if each country specialises in its comparative-advantage good, global production of both goods rises, England spends 220 labour-hours on 2.2 units of cloth, Portugal 170 hours on 2.125 units of wine, and both consume more of both than under autarky.
  • Tell: "autarky" = no trade, the baseline free trade beats.
Agglomeration economies, the second gain from trade.
  • What it is: productivity rises when activity clusters in one place (shared labour pools, suppliers, knowledge spillovers; the Silicon Valley effect).
  • Why it matters for trade: open trade lets production concentrate where it is most productive, so the world captures clustering gains on top of comparative advantage.
  • The sting in the tail: the same concentration creates strategic dependence, the gain and the vulnerability are two sides of one coin (the examined trade-off, Part D).
Two stylised facts the slides open with.
  • Trade has exploded: global trade rose substantially over the last ~70 years.
  • Driver = value-chain integration: production is sliced across borders (the iPhone is the canonical example, designed in one country, components from many, assembled in another).

Intuition, tariff incidenceA tariff is just a tax on imports, and like any tax the question is not who writes the cheque but who cannot escape. Whichever side has fewer alternatives (the more inelastic side) is the one that eats the cost.
The incidence rule, the inelastic side pays.
  • General rule: a tariff, like any tax, is shared between buyer and seller; the incidence falls more heavily on the more inelastic group (the one with fewer substitutes).
  • Distributional sting: tariffs are regressive, they cost small businesses and lower-income consumers proportionally more.

Part C, Do tariffs raise revenue or create jobs?

Raising revenue, yes, but bounded and self-undermining.
  • They do raise revenue: a tariff is a tax, so it collects.
  • The "optimal tariff" result: if tariffs are applied uniformly across all partners and nobody retaliates, the revenue-optimal rate is ~25% (Ignatenko et al. 2025).
  • The catch: there is almost always retaliation, and tariffs can be aimed "with laser precision" to hit a target government's constituents' jobs, so the two clean conditions rarely hold.
  • Efficiency cost stands regardless: tariffs distort comparative advantage and limit agglomeration economies whether or not they raise revenue.
Intuition, the revenue/jobs tensionTo create jobs you want tariffs to kill imports; to raise revenue you need imports to survive so there's something to tax. You cannot maximise both, drive imports to zero and revenue goes to zero too.
Creating jobs, the input-tax problem and the China shock.
  • A tariff is also a tax on inputs: protecting one stage taxes every downstream user of that input.
  • Steel worked example: a steel tariff makes domestic steel relatively cheaper → demand for domestic steel and steelworkers rises; but steel costs rise for carmakers, construction etc. → their demand and employment fall. Net effect: more jobs lost downstream than saved upstream.
  • Firms route around it: under Trump I, exports from China to Vietnam surged on goods the US used to buy from China, then Vietnam→US exports surged, production relocates to minimise cost, not necessarily to the US, unless the tariff is uniform across all countries.
  • The China shock (Pierce & Schott 2016): China's 2001 WTO entry guaranteed no big US tariff hikes; US manufacturing employment dropped sharply in the 2000s–2010s, with stark subnational heterogeneity, concentrated regional job losses fed the backlash to globalisation and the rise of Trump. Meanwhile Chinese manufacturing employment climbed.
Steel tariff→ domestic steel cheaper→ steel jobs ↑→ but input cost ↑ for carmakers (steel is their input)→ downstream demand ↓→ net jobs ↓
Don't treat a tariff as a tax on final goods only. Its largest employment effect often runs through inputs: protecting steelmakers can destroy more jobs in steel-using industries than it saves. "Tariffs create jobs" is true only at the protected stage, in isolation.

What industrial policy is

Industrial policy, definition and justifications.
  • Definition: the bundle of "government policies that explicitly target the transformation of the structure of economic activity in pursuit of some public goal."
  • Four justifications: (1) innovation-related externalities; (2) economic security; (3) promoting "good jobs"; (4) accelerating the green transition.
  • Distinct from tariffs: it acts on the domestic supply side (subsidies, preferential treatment, picking sectors) rather than taxing imports at the border.
China as the laboratory, two recent examples.
  • Made in China 2025 (MiC2025): singled out ten key sectors for upgrading (focus on next-gen IT & telecoms, advanced manufacturing, robotics); aims to foster "national manufacturing champions" in a few selected industries.
  • The 10,000 Little Giants: supports SMEs in niche "base industries" meant to be the backbone behind the national champions.
Industrial policy is not a free win. "Picking winners" can be expensive and inefficient, governments may back the wrong sectors, and duplicating strategic capacity everywhere ("each region its own Silicon Valley") throws away the very agglomeration gains free trade captured. The drawbacks are themselves an examined prompt ("what are the potential drawbacks of industrial policy?").

The closing synthesis (the take-away slides).

Decomposing the gap, channel by channel

Intuition — what "the gap" even measuresA headline "30% gap" is almost always a gap in annual labour earnings, which bundles three different things. The like-for-like gap (same job, same hours) is much smaller, around 5–10% in advanced economies. So before you argue about a number, ask which number it is.
Why women earn less, ruled out and ruled in.
  • Not education (any more): in many countries women now have more years of schooling, yet still earn less. The residual education story is the field of study (fewer women in STEM/engineering), which feeds occupational sorting.
  • Participation: in almost all countries more working-age women than men are out of the labour force, so they enter the earnings data as a zero.
  • Then, conditional on working, three further channels stack on top (below).
The three "conditional on working" channels (slide spine).
  • 1. Fewer hours: women are more likely to work part-time / fewer hours, largely because they do more caring for kids/elderly and home production.
  • 2. Lower-paying occupations: horizontal sorting (pilots vs cabin crew; doctors vs nurses), plus lower-paying industries and firms (women cluster in smaller firms).
  • 3. Lower hourly wage for the same occupation: they may negotiate less; may value non-wage job aspects more; may have less experience (maternity breaks); and may face discrimination.
Don't read a high headline gap as "unequal pay for equal work." A 30% figure is annual earnings (all three channels). The same-position hourly gap is roughly 5–10% (France ≈5%). Roulet's caveat: do not over-narrow to "equal pay for equal job" either, since the deeper problem is that men and women do not hold the same jobs.
🎙 In class "The flip side of women working fewer hours is them doing more unpaid work at home." The same cross-country survey shows a striking symmetry: the countries with the biggest gap in paid hours are the countries with the biggest gap in unpaid (home-production / caring) hours.
Decomposing a reported "30% gap" (slide).
  • Driver A: more men in higher-paid senior roles (vertical sorting).
  • Driver B: more women part-time / reduced hours.
  • Driver C: women paid less for the same position and hours.
  • The measurement rule: to isolate C's share you must compare the hourly or full-time-equivalent wage within a given occupation. But all three matter for understanding and narrowing the gap.

The child penalty — the dominant driver

Intuition — the child penaltyPlot the earnings of future-mothers and future-fathers around the year their first child arrives. The Kleven et al. (2019) child-penalty evidence.

  • Roughly equal thirds: the penalty is driven in about equal proportions by labour-force participation, hours of work, and wage rates.
  • Even in Norway: Roulet leads with Norway precisely because it is gender-equal in relative terms, yet still has not closed the gap; women's trajectory never fully recovers within 10 years.
  • Universal pattern, varying magnitude: same shape across Europe, North America, Latin America (larger, more drop-out), Asia and Africa (smaller); policy and culture move the size, not the sign.

Does technology destroy jobs? The recurring fear

Intuition, the Luddite reflexEvery wave of automation revives Keynes's 1930 fear of "technological unemployment", labour-saving running ahead of our ability to find new uses for labour. The slides open with exactly this quote, then with the Luddites, to set up the question and then answer it: the fear is old, recurrent, and historically wrong about the aggregate.

  • His own answer: a "temporary phase of maladjustment", growing pains, not old age; in the long run mankind is solving its economic problem.
  • His prediction: living standards in progressive countries 100 years hence four to eight times higher. The point of including it: the disruption is real but the doom is about transition, not the steady state.

Whether jobs disappear is just the question of which pull wins, and historically the answer has not been "substitution".

What matters is the price of different kinds of labour (wages, and the gap between them) and how the pie splits between labour and capital. Recent years saw profits grow faster than wages, the same direction as the past few decades, so even with stable employment, income has been drifting from workers to owners of capital.
The two things to watch instead of aggregate employment.
  • 1. Wages (and the labour share): the price of different kinds of labour, not just quantities, plus labour's share of income relative to capital.
  • 2. Well-being: the quality of work, social connections, and a sense of purpose, not just the pay-cheque.
What the recent data shows (per the slides).
  • Inequality across workers: evidence "so far is mixed", no clean verdict yet.
  • Profits vs wages: profits increased more than wages in the last few years.
  • The longer trend: this was also the direction of the past several decades, a falling labour share.

The political pay-off (why this matters for a public-policy course).
  • Electoral discontent: recent electoral outcomes worldwide reflect heavy citizen discontent with their labour-market situation.
  • The policy failure: labour-market policies of past decades often failed to make market forces work for all workers and businesses.
  • So: the distributional and well-being margins, not the aggregate job count, are where policy has under-delivered and where the politics now bites.

Economists' insights on working from home

Intuition, a benefit workers will pay forWFH is best read as a non-wage benefit that workers genuinely value, mostly to dodge the commute, and will trade real salary to keep. Economists are "quite positive" on it: workers value it, firms can benefit too, and Return-to-Office mandates show no measurable pay-off in performance or firm value. The live tension is the gap between how much WFH workers want and how much they are offered.
What we knew pre-Covid.
  • Workers value the option: Mas & Pallais (2017, AER), employees would give up on average 8% of wage for the option to work from home.
  • What they're paying to avoid: mostly the commute, not hours flexibility, except women with young children, employees prefer standard legal hours on usual business days.
  • Productivity can rise: remote work was associated with productivity gains, especially when employees choose it.
The C-trip experiment (Bloom, Liang, Roberts, Ying, 2015, QJE)
A listed Chinese travel agency ran a two-step call-centre experiment.
  • Step 1, randomised: some call-centre staff assigned to WFH for 9 months, others stayed in the office. WFH group's productivity rose 13%, two-thirds from more minutes per shift (fewer breaks and sick days), one-third from more calls per minute (quieter environment). Higher satisfaction and lower turnover, but a lower promotion rate conditional on performance.
  • Step 2, self-selected: employees then chose their group. Over half who had been WFH switched back to the office. Among those who stayed home, productivity gains rose to 22%, a selection effect: the people most productive at home are the ones who choose to keep it.
  • The lesson: employers struggle to predict who thrives at home, so it may be better to let employees optimise for themselves. (Selection could in principle run the other way, depending on monitoring and performance incentives.)
What we know today (2021–2023 surveys).
  • Sources: the Global Survey on Working Arrangements (34 countries, ~40,000 respondents, April–May 2023; Aksoy, Barrero, Bloom, Davis, Dolls, Zarate) and the US SWAA survey (Barrero, Bloom, Davis).
  • Prevalence: globally about a third of workers do some WFH every week; 43% in the US.
  • The wanted-vs-offered gap: employees would like to WFH even more than they currently do, and more so for lower-skilled people.
  • The wage trade-off: workers will give up on average 5% of salary for the option to WFH 2–3 days a week (controlling for gender, age, education, industry, survey wave).
  • Why: again, essentially to avoid the commute.
Firm-side and real-estate consequences.
  • No RTO pay-off: no significant change in financial performance or firm value after Return-to-Office mandates.
  • Cross-country hiring: the global market for remote white-collar jobs benefits firms and workers substantially (Cui & Solomon).
  • Commercial real estate: hybrid firms downsize but upgrade office quality; a centralisation effect leaves CBD-proximate space valuable while vacancies concentrate in suburbs and the periphery.

Policy responses, the way forward

Intuition, insure, steer, and educateRoulet's policy menu sorts into three jobs: insure workers against displacement, steer AI toward social value rather than just private profit, and adapt education so people can keep retooling. The market alone will push technology where profit expectations are highest, not where social value is, the climate-policy parallel, so there is a case for intervention.
The three policy levers (slide 35).
  • 1. Insure against displacement: e.g. universal basic capital, flexicurity (and adjacent profit-sharing / UBI ideas), cushion workers through transitions rather than freezing the labour market.
  • 2. Steer the incentives: the market deploys AI where expected profits are highest, not necessarily where social value is highest; as with climate, that gap can justify government intervention to push AI toward "good" uses.
  • 3. Adapt education: uncertainty hinders skill acquisition (hard to invest when you don't know which skills survive), but strong educational foundations always help people adapt, so build broad, flexible foundations rather than narrow bets.

Take-aways (the closing slides)

Technological progress and the future of work.
  • Tech destroys some jobs (substitution) but creates others (productivity), and there is no evidence yet that substitution dominates.
  • It drives sectoral shifts, the economy has absorbed shifts this large before (the decline of agriculture).
  • Distributional consequences are the real story: recent decades saw job polarisation, demand up for non-routine jobs at both the top (high-skill, high-pay) and bottom (low-pay services), down for routine intermediate jobs.
  • GenAI's distributional impact is too early to call.
Working from home going forward.
  • Employees WFH far more than pre-Covid, but on average less than they would like.
  • They will give up ~5% of earnings for the option to WFH 2–3 days; aversion to commuting is the key driver.
  • WFH's productivity impact depends on management and technology, but the evidence is more positive than people expect.
  • No evidence of improved financial performance or firm value after RTO mandates.
Exam lens.
  • The core argument to be able to run: "does technology destroy jobs?", answer with the three forces (substitution down, productivity up, reallocation shifts) and the historical verdict that aggregate employment never fell, while stressing it changed the composition of work and produced winners and losers.
  • The distributional turn: be ready to argue why head-count is the wrong sole metric, bring in wages, the labour share, and well-being, and link the falling labour share to the political discontent the slides flag.
  • What is different about AI: faster pace, hits the top of the distribution, 20–60% task exposure, but the same three forces apply and the net effect is genuinely uncertain, hold the "cautiously optimistic with the right policies" line.
  • WFH numbers worth banking: 8% wage value pre-Covid (Mas & Pallais), ~5% today, C-trip's 13% then 22% productivity gains and the selection effect, commuting as the driver, no RTO pay-off.
  • Policy: insure (flexicurity / universal basic capital) · steer AI to social value (the climate-policy analogy) · adapt education. Glossary & myths

The load-bearing terms, defined the way the exam wants them, plus the claims to be ready to push back on.

TermDefinition (exam-ready)
Market failureA situation where a free market does not allocate resources efficiently, so there is a potential role for government. The four canonical types: externalities, public goods, asymmetric information, market power.
ExternalityA cost or benefit imposed on a third party not reflected in the price; creates a wedge between private marginal cost (PMC) and social marginal cost (SMC). CO₂ is the standard negative production externality.
Pigouvian taxA tax set equal to the marginal external damage, shifting PMC up to SMC so the market internalises the externality and moves to the social optimum.
Cap-and-tradeA quantity instrument: government sets a total emissions cap and issues tradable allowances; the price emerges in the market and equalises marginal abatement cost across firms.
Asymmetric informationOne side of a transaction knows more than the other about quality or risk. Drives adverse selection and the Akerlof lemons market.
Adverse selectionWhen pricing on average risk drives the good risks out and leaves the bad, unravelling the market (the insurance death-spiral). Fixed by screening (separating) or mandates / public provision (pooling).
Consumer-welfare standardThe post-Bork antitrust test that judges harm by effect on price/output. Breaks down for zero-price digital services, where the price test sees "no harm".
Killer acquisitionA dominant firm buying a nascent rival to neutralise a future competitive threat (the Facebook–WhatsApp charge).
Transfer pricingThe internal prices a multinational sets on intra-group transactions; the lever used to shift profit to low-tax jurisdictions. Disciplined (imperfectly) by the arm's-length principle.
BEPS two pillarsThe 2021 OECD deal: Pillar 1 reallocates a share of the largest firms' residual profit to market jurisdictions; Pillar 2 sets a ~15% global minimum effective tax. Limited by non-adoption and havens.
Tariff incidenceWho actually bears a tariff: the more inelastic side of the market (the one with fewer alternatives), regardless of who legally pays it.
Deadweight lossThe value destroyed by a distortion (here, a tariff): output lost to less-efficient domestic supply plus consumption choked off by the higher price.
Agglomeration economiesThe productivity gains from clustering related firms in one place (shared labour, suppliers, knowledge spillovers). The flip side is strategic dependence.
Industrial policyTargeted state support to build or protect specific sectors (IRA, CHIPS Act); the smarter alternative to tariffs for strategic industries, but costly and prone to capture.
Child penaltyThe sharp, persistent fall in mothers' earnings after the first child (with no equivalent drop for fathers); the dominant driver of the residual gender pay gap.
Taste-based vs statistical discriminationDiscrimination from prejudice (a "taste") vs from using group averages as a proxy for unobserved individual traits. Different mechanisms, different policy fixes.
Labour shareThe fraction of national income paid to labour rather than capital. Its long decline (US roughly 58% to ~51%) is the headline of the future-of-work session.
Job polarisationThe hollowing-out of the middle of the skill distribution (1980–2020): high-skill cognitive and low-skill in-person work grew, routine middle-skill work shrank.
Myths to be ready to refute.
  • "A free market is always efficient." Only under strong assumptions; externalities, asymmetric information, market power and public goods all break it, which is the whole rationale for the course.
  • "Free digital services cannot harm consumers." The zero price hides harm in attention, data, privacy and foreclosed innovation; the price-based standard simply cannot see it.
  • "A carbon tax and a cap deliver the same thing." Only under perfect information; under uncertainty you choose a tax for cost certainty and a cap for emissions certainty (Weitzman).
  • "Tariffs are paid by the exporting country." Incidence falls on whichever side is more inelastic; often domestic consumers and downstream firms.
  • "Technology destroys jobs on net." Historically it changes work rather than destroying it (the productivity effect offsets substitution); the real problem is polarisation and distribution, not mass unemployment.
  • "The gender pay gap is mostly straight discrimination." The bulk of the residual gap now traces to the child penalty and occupational sorting, not like-for-like pay for the same role.
