2 ¬∑ Real vs nominal FX / PPP / convergence 18√ó

‚ñ∏ When you see it: real & nominal FX moving opposite ways; a PPP gap; a fast-converging or high-productivity currency.

4 ¬∑ Global saving-investment & world r* 15√ó

‚ñ∏ When you see it: a shift in desired global S or I (ageing, China rebalancing, savings glut) ‚Üí world real rate & CA imbalances.
World r* clears global desired S = global desired I (loanable funds). Map shock to a curve, then read the CA mirror.
Map it: ageing savers / China saving more ‚Üí S right ‚Üí r* ‚Üì ¬∑ China rebalances away from investment ‚Üí I left ‚Üí r* ‚Üì ¬∑ retirees DIS-save ‚Üí S left ‚Üí r* ‚Üë. A surplus country saving more widens imbalances; consuming more shrinks them.
Worked (2019): China raises consumption ‚Üí saves less ‚Üí global S left ‚Üí world r* rises, China's surplus shrinks ‚Üí global imbalances fall (the effect is material because China is large).
Traps: confusing an S-shift with an I-shift (both move r* but differ for growth/imbalances); the demographics sign (retirees dis-saving raises r*).

5 ¬∑ FX intervention / defend a peg 14√ó

‚ñ∏ When you see it: a CB managing its currency; reserves moving; balance-sheet size; a peg under pressure.
  • Unsterilised (buy FX, issue own currency): balance sheet swells both sides, monetary base grows ‚Üí ADDS liquidity, rates ‚Üì.
  • Sterilised = asset swap (sell a domestic bond to soak the cash back up): composition changes, size unchanged ‚Üí no macro effect.
  • Asymmetry: defend a strong currency forever (print your own), but a weak one only until FX reserves run out ‚üπ pegs break on the weak side (1992 EMS).
Worked (China T/F): "CB buying USD removes liquidity, rates up" ‚Üí FALSE, it pays in yuan, crediting reserves, so it ADDS liquidity, base grows, rates ‚Üì.
Traps: "buying FX removes liquidity" (false, it ADDS); forgetting only the weak-side defence runs out.

6 ¬∑ UIP: rates ‚Üí the FX path 12√ó

‚ñ∏ When you see it: a rate change/news and the currency; "priced-in" expectations; carry-trade claims.
r ‚àí r_F = expected depreciation of domestic currency
  • Surprise hike ‚üπ capital inflow ‚üπ immediate appreciation. Already priced-in ‚üπ no move on the day.
  • High level of rates (usually high inflation) ‚üπ currency expected to depreciate over time ‚üπ carry is no free lunch.
  • Credibility: a credible disinflation lowers the future path of nominal rates while supporting the currency.
Worked (Russia 2022): rates raised but the rouble didn't move because the hike was "priced in", UIP had already adjusted before the announcement, leaving nothing to react to.
Traps: equating high rates with a strong currency; forgetting only surprises move the rate. Distinguish NEWS of a hike (appreciation now) from the LEVEL of rates (expected depreciation later).

‚ñ∏ When you see it: will Debt/GDP stay constant? what primary surplus is needed? rising rates / downgrade.

8 ¬∑ Taylor rule / inflation targeting 11√ó

‚ñ∏ When you see it: an output-gap revision; why a CB does/doesn't move; does above-target inflation force a hike?

œÄ appears twice ‚üπ a 1pt inflation rise lifts i by 1.5pt (the real rate must rise to cool the economy, Taylor principle). A less negative gap ‚üπ higher prescribed rate. Worked: r*=2, œÄ*=2, œÄ=3, gap=0 ‚üπ i = 2+3+0.5 = 5.5%.

9 ¬∑ ZLB toolkit 11√ó

‚ñ∏ When you see it: QE and whether it's inflationary; raising the target to 4%; the inflation buffer; a low r*; exiting the ZLB.
At the ZLB the nominal rate can't fall further ‚üπ forward guidance (credible promise), QE (buy long bonds ‚Üí term premium ‚Üì, long yields ‚Üì), negative rates.
  • QE ‚âÝ automatic inflation: it swaps bonds for reserves; if the multiplier is dead (banks hoard reserves) broad money & spending needn't rise. Inflation depends on demand & expectations, not the base mechanically.
  • Higher target (4%) lifts average nominal rates ‚üπ more room to cut, valuable when r* is low so the ZLB binds often. Exit: sell assets back, or raise interest paid on reserves.
Worked (2020): "QE means big money-supply rises so is always inflationary" ‚Üí flaw: QE lifts the base, but if banks hoard reserves the multiplier is dead and broad money / spending needn't rise.
Traps: base‚Üë ‚üπ supply‚Üë ‚üπ inflation (multiplier can be dead); confusing a higher target's long-run insurance with short-run stimulus.

10 ¬∑ CA ‚Üî capital ‚Üî reserves 10√ó

‚ñ∏ When you see it: reconciling a CA surplus/deficit with private capital flows and reserve changes (usually a T/F).

CA surplus = net lender abroad (a capital outflow overall). Reserves are the residual: Œîreserves = CA ‚àí net private flow. Solve for whichever term the question asks.

Exam method (3 sentences max each)

  • Closed book, one two-sided A4, 2 hours. Q1 True/False/Uncertain (~20‚Äì25 of 70), verdict scores nothing, the mechanism is the grade. Q2‚ÄìQ6 short applications on a news snippet.
  • Each question has one precise intended insight, find it, don't brain-dump. "If you're confused you're not reading it right."
  • Write the causal chain explicitly: shift ‚Üí r vs rF ‚Üí flow ‚Üí appreciate/depreciate ‚Üí NX. Only one real calculation ever: debt sustainability.
  • Past papers are an exact guide, the exam looks like prior finals; the final review session gives real hints. Compress each idea to ~3 sentences; the most complex sum is "5 ‚àí 3".

Myths to refute

  • "Imports reduce GDP." No, the ‚àíM just strips out foreign goods double-counted in C.
  • "Inversion ‚üπ guaranteed recession." 2023 = soft landing (credible disinflation).
  • "QE is always inflationary." 2008‚Äì19 inflation stayed low; the multiplier can be dead.
  • "High rates ‚üπ strong currency." UIP: high-rate currencies are expected to depreciate.
  • "Fixed supply (Bitcoin/gold) ‚üπ price stability." An anchor is a commitment, not a cap.
  • "Disinflation always causes unemployment." 2021‚Äì24 was near-costless (anchored expectations).
  • "A trade surplus is virtue, a deficit weakness." Just S‚àíI: Germany ages & lends; the US borrows to invest.
  • "A government deficit must cause a capital outflow." No, depends whether the private sector's surplus covers it (sectoral identity).
  • "The ZLB floors the real interest rate." No, it floors the nominal rate; with i‚âà0, œÄ>0 the real rate is ‚àíœÄ < 0.
