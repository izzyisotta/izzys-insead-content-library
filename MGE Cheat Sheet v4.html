L1 · Macro variables & national accounting L1

▸ Framework: the GDP identity + real-vs-nominal split. Myth-buster: "imports reduce GDP" (−M just removes foreign goods double-counted in C).
GDP — production (final goods only)
$$\text{GDP}=P_AQ_A+P_BQ_B+\dots$$
GDP — expenditure approach
$$\text{GDP}=C+I+G+NX$$
National income
$$\text{GNI}=\text{GDP}+\text{NFP}$$
Growth decomposition (nominal vs real)
$$\text{Nom. GDP growth}=\text{Real GDP growth}+\pi$$
Real interest rate (Fisher, approx.)
$$r=i-\pi$$
Real exchange rate
$$e_R=\frac{e_N\cdot P}{P_F}\quad\text{(LOOP}\Rightarrow e_R=1)$$
Trap: a rise in \(e_N\) or \(e_R\) = appreciation (foreign cur./goods per domestic unit).

L2 · Saving, investment, CA & real rate L2 · core identity

▸ Framework: world r* clears global desired S = I (loanable funds); CA is the mirror. Tested as savings-glut / demographics shocks.
Saving builds to the central identity
$$S_{priv}=(\text{GDP}+\text{NFP})-T-C,\quad S_{gov}=T-G$$ $$S=S_{priv}+S_{gov}=(\text{GDP}+\text{NFP})-C-G$$ $$\boxed{\,S=I+CA\,}\quad CA=NX+\text{NFP}$$
Balance of payments
$$\text{Current Acct}=\text{Capital & Financial Acct}$$ $$(S_{priv}-I_{priv})+(S_{gov}-I_{gov})=CA$$
Trap: a CA surplus is net lending abroad (a capital outflow). Closed economy: \(S=I\). Demographics sign: retirees dis-save ⟹ S left ⟹ r* up.

L3 · Growth — why some countries are rich L3

▸ Framework: growth accounting splits output into hours × productivity. Productivity is the swing factor; demographics alone ≠ prosperity.
Growth accounting / per-capita decomposition
$$\frac{Y}{N}=\frac{Y}{H}\cdot\frac{H}{N}\quad(\text{productivity}\times\text{employment intensity})$$
Full chain: GDP/Pop = productivity × effort × employment × demographics. More working-age people raise total GDP, not necessarily per-capita GDP.
Trap: equating population / working-age growth with rising living standards (conflates total with per capita).

L4 · Growth & country analysis — the 4 Is L4

▸ Framework: conditional convergence — poor countries catch up only if fundamentals are in place.
4 Is: Investment (capital), Institutions, Innovation, Initial conditions ⟹ conditional convergence. Binding constraint is usually investment & institutions, not headcount.
Worked (2020): Africa's working-age pop overtaking Asia ≠ equal share of world GDP — that needs productivity (investment + institutions).

L5 · Money & central banks L5–L6, L9

▸ Framework: base vs supply, the multiplier is behavioural (not fixed), long-run money-growth = inflation.
The two money aggregates
$$\text{Base}=C+R\qquad \text{Supply}=C+D$$
Money multiplier
$$m=\frac{C+D}{C+R}=\frac{C/D+1}{C/D+R/D}$$
Money-growth rule (velocity const., \(MV=PY\))
$$\%\Delta M\approx\text{Nom. GDP growth}=\pi+\text{Real growth}$$
Money demand (real)
$$\frac{M}{P}=L(\text{GDP},\,i)\quad(+\text{ on Y},\ -\text{ on }i)$$
Trap: panic ⟹ C/D, R/D jump ⟹ m collapses, so supply moves while base is flat.

L6 · Business cycles & asset prices L6

▸ Framework: stock price = PV of cash flows discounted at the required return; the risk premium is the ex-ante wedge.
Required stock return & ex-ante risk premium
$$R=r+RP\qquad R\approx\frac{E}{P}\ \Rightarrow\ RP=\frac{E}{P}-r$$
Trap: higher r ⟹ lower prices, but if r rises because growth is up, higher cash flows offset. A high P/E ≠ bubble.

L7 · IS-LM-FX — the short-run model L7–L8 · 26×

▸ Framework: shift the curve the shock hits → compare r to rF → capital flow → appr./deprec. → NX adjusts → repeat until r=rF.
IS — goods market (aggregate demand)
$$\text{GDP}=C(\text{GDP},r,T,W)+I(r)+G+NX(e_R,\text{GDP},\text{GDP}_F)$$
LM — money market
$$\frac{M}{P}=L(\text{GDP},\,r)$$
FX — capital-flow arbitrage
$$r=r_F$$
Reversal (most-examined): monetary potent under float / dead under peg; fiscal the mirror. Impossible Trinity: keep 2 of 3 — fixed rate, capital mobility, monetary autonomy.

L8 · Inflation, supply side & the Taylor Rule L8, L10 · 11×

▸ Framework: the Taylor rule is the CB's reaction function. π enters twice ⟹ a 1pt inflation rise lifts i by 1.5pt (Taylor principle).
Baseline nominal rate
$$i=\pi+\text{"reasonable" real return}$$
The Taylor Rule (Taylor 1993)
$$i=\underbrace{r^*+\pi}_{\text{baseline}}+0.5(\pi-\pi^{*})+0.5\,(\text{output gap})$$
Worked: r*=2, π*=2, π=3, gap=0 ⟹ i = 2+3+0.5(1) = 5.5%. The real rate rises to cool the economy.
Trap: forgetting π enters twice; reacting to headline inflation without asking supply-shock vs true positive gap (CB may hold if temporary / anchored / re-anchoring).

L9 · Monetary policy & the ZLB L9 · 11×

▸ Framework: when the nominal rate can't fall further, switch to forward guidance, QE, negative rates. QE ≠ automatic inflation.
At the ZLB: forward guidance (credible promise), QE (buy long bonds → term premium ↓, long yields ↓), negative rates.
  • QE ≠ inflation: swaps bonds for reserves; if the multiplier is dead (banks hoard) broad money needn't rise.
  • Higher target (4%) lifts avg nominal rates ⟹ more room to cut when r* is low.
Trap: base↑ ⟹ supply↑ ⟹ inflation (multiplier can be dead). ZLB floors the nominal rate, not the real rate: i≈0, π>0 ⟹ r=−π<0.

L10 · Managing high inflation L10

▸ Framework: credibility & the nominal anchor. Disinflation can be near-costless if expectations are anchored.
Nominal anchor & credibility: long-run neutrality — the level of money/prices/initial FX is arbitrary; only the path (growth rate = inflation) is real. An anchor is a commitment (inflation target), not a quantity cap. Time inconsistency: pre-commitment beats discretion.
Trap: "fixed supply (Bitcoin/gold) ⟹ price stability" — an anchor is a commitment, not a cap.

L11 · Fiscal policy & debt sustainability L11 · 12× · the one calc

▸ Framework: the only real calculation in the course. Will Debt/GDP stay constant? what primary surplus is needed?
Government balances
$$\text{Primary balance}=T-G-\text{Transfers}$$ $$\text{Overall}=\text{Primary balance}-\text{Interest}$$ $$\text{Interest}=r_{nominal}\times\text{Debt}$$
★ Debt-stabilising primary surplus
$$\boxed{\ \frac{\text{Req. primary surplus}}{\text{GDP}}=(r-g)\times\frac{\text{Debt}}{\text{GDP}}\ }$$
Trap: keep r and g in the SAME terms (both nominal or both real). If g>r the required surplus is negative — run deficits forever, ratio still falls. Fiscal multiplier: ΔY=ΔG/(1−mpc); automatic stabilisers cushion the cycle.

L12 · Exchange rates L12 · 18×+12×

▸ Framework: LOOP/PPP anchors the long run; UIP drives the short run. Real & nominal FX can move opposite ways (Balassa-Samuelson).
Law of one price (long-run anchor)
$$e_N=\frac{P_F}{P}\quad(\text{from }e_R=1)$$
Similar productivity ⟹ real rate constant (PPP)
$$\%\Delta e_N=\pi_F-\pi_{domestic}$$
Short run — UIP (currencies as assets)
$$r-r_F=\text{expected depreciation of domestic cur.}$$
Balassa-Samuelson: a catching-up economy has rising tradables productivity → higher inflation → real appreciation (the price of convergence, not lost competitiveness). So nominal can fall while real rises.
Trap: news of a hike ⟹ appreciation now; a high level of rates ⟹ expected depreciation later. Don't equate high rates with a strong currency.

L13 · State of the global economy L13

▸ Framework: apply the whole toolkit to live news — global S-I imbalances, world r*, CA ↔ capital ↔ reserves.
CA, capital account & reserves close the loop
$$CA+KA+\Delta\text{reserves}=0\qquad CA=S-I$$
Worked (China): CA surplus +$238bn AND private inflow +$287bn ⟹ CB accumulates +$472bn reserves to balance. Sign trap: a CA surplus is a capital OUTFLOW even paired with a private INflow.

L14 · Review — whole course in one diagram L14

▸ Method: closed book, one two-sided A4, 2 hrs. Q1 True/False/Uncertain — verdict scores nothing, the mechanism is the grade. Q2–Q6 short applications on a news snippet.
Mechanism chains to have automatic:
  • Surprise hike → r>rF → inflow → appreciates now.
  • Monetary easing (float) → r↓ → deprec. → NX↑ → GDP↑.
  • Fiscal ↑G (float) → r↑ → appr. → NX↓ → crowded out.
  • Fiscal ↑G (peg) → CB prints to hold peg → GDP↑ amplified.
  • CB buys FX → issues own cur. → base↑ → liquidity↑, rates↓.
  • Credible disinflation → exp. inflation↓ → long yields↓ → curve inverts/flattens.
  • Higher r (downgrade) → (r−g) widens → bigger surplus needed.
Each question has one precise intended insight — find it, don't brain-dump. Past papers are an exact guide. The most complex sum is "5 − 3".
