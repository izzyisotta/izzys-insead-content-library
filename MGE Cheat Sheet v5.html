L1 · Macro variables & national accounting L1

▸ Framework: the GDP identity + real-vs-nominal split. Myth-buster: "imports reduce GDP" (−M just removes foreign goods double-counted in C).
GDP · production (final goods only)
$$\text{GDP}=P_AQ_A+P_BQ_B+\dots$$
GDP · expenditure approach
$$\text{GDP}=C+I+G+NX$$
National income
$$\text{GNI}=\text{GDP}+\text{NFP}$$
Growth decomposition (nominal vs real)
$$\text{Nom. GDP growth}=\text{Real GDP growth}+\pi$$
Real interest rate (Fisher, approx.)
$$r=i-\pi$$
↳ why: negative real rates only need π > i, so they happen with POSITIVE inflation too, not only deflation [2019 1a].
Real exchange rate
$$e_R=\frac{e_N\cdot P}{P_F}\quad\text{(LOOP}\Rightarrow e_R=1)$$
↳ why: market FX = power (buy weapons, cross-border M&A); PPP = living standards. Use whichever the question is actually asking about.
Trap: a rise in \(e_N\) or \(e_R\) = appreciation (foreign cur./goods per domestic unit).

L2 · Saving, investment, CA & real rate L2 · core identity

▸ Framework: world r* clears global desired S = I (loanable funds); CA is the mirror. Tested as savings-glut / demographics shocks.
Saving builds to the central identity
$$S_{priv}=(\text{GDP}+\text{NFP})-T-C,\quad S_{gov}=T-G$$ $$S=S_{priv}+S_{gov}=(\text{GDP}+\text{NFP})-C-G$$ $$\boxed{\,S=I+CA\,}\quad CA=NX+\text{NFP}$$
Balance of payments
$$\text{Current Acct}=\text{Capital & Financial Acct}$$ $$(S_{priv}-I_{priv})+(S_{gov}-I_{gov})=CA$$
↳ why: financing side: a CA deficit is covered by private inflows PLUS Δreserves, so private inflows need NOT equal the deficit (reserves can move) [2022 1c].
↳ why: saver vs borrower, neither is "better": a deficit can signal confidence; judge sustainability on Debt/GDP, not on the sign.
The S-I market (price = r*): world S up-sloping, I down-sloping, clearing at the world real rate r*. ↑desired saving (glut / ageing) ⟹ S right ⟹ r* falls; ↑investment ⟹ I right ⟹ r* rises. Myth: long-run real rates are set by global S&I, not by the central bank.
Trap: a CA surplus is net lending abroad (a capital outflow). Closed economy: \(S=I\). Demographics sign: retirees dis-save ⟹ S left ⟹ r* up.

L3 · Growth · why some countries are rich L3

▸ Framework: write GDP/capita as a chain of four ratios (each an identity, but each a separate lever). Productivity is the swing factor near the frontier.
Living-standards decomposition (4 ratios)
$$\frac{\text{GDP}}{\text{Pop}}=\underbrace{\frac{\text{GDP}}{\text{Hours}}}_{\text{productivity}}\cdot\underbrace{\frac{\text{Hours}}{\text{Workers}}}_{\text{effort}}\cdot\underbrace{\frac{\text{Workers}}{\text{WA-Pop}}}_{\text{employment}}\cdot\underbrace{\frac{\text{WA-Pop}}{\text{Pop}}}_{\text{demographics}}$$
Convergence (Solow): growth slows as a country nears the frontier; catch-up = imitation, a possibility not a guarantee (Asia converged, Latin America didn't → L4). "France isn't poor, it just works less": GDP/hour near-identical, the gap is hours + employment share.
Trap: equating population / working-age growth with rising living standards. More working-age people raise total GDP, not necessarily per-capita GDP.

L4 · Growth & country analysis · the 4 Is L4

▸ Framework: conditional convergence · poor countries catch up only if fundamentals are in place.
4 Is: Investment (capital), Institutions, Innovation, Initial conditions ⟹ conditional convergence. Binding constraint is usually investment & institutions, not headcount.
↳ why: of C/I/G/NX, INVESTMENT is the long-run growth engine (capital, then productivity), not consumption or net exports [2022 1d].
↳ why: two ways to be rich: strong institutions, or oil (only temporarily). Norway built institutions first then found oil; Venezuela had oil without them.
↳ why: middle-income trap: converge fast, then stall. Escaping needs the switch from investment-led to innovation-led growth.
Worked (2020): Africa's working-age pop overtaking Asia ≠ equal share of world GDP · that needs productivity (investment + institutions).

L5 · Money & central banks L5–L6, L9

▸ Framework: base vs supply, the multiplier is behavioural (not fixed), long-run money-growth = inflation.
Money IS the CB balance sheet (assets = liabilities, 1:1): the CB transforms assets, it doesn't print from nothing. Buys an asset (bonds, FX) ⟹ credits its own liability (reserves/currency) ⟹ liquidity↑, rates↓; sells ⟹ drains. Settlement: CB = "bank of banks"; cross-border needs correspondent banking (DBS→Chase→Fed→Citibank).
↳ why: China's CB buying USD = buying an asset ⟹ it ISSUES yuan ⟹ liquidity↑, rates↓ (NOT a drain) [2022 1b].
CB balance sheet · buy a bond (QE) ⟹ both sides +100

The two money aggregates
$$\text{Base}=C+R\qquad \text{Supply}=C+D$$
Money multiplier
$$m=\frac{C+D}{C+R}=\frac{C/D+1}{C/D+R/D}$$
Money-growth rule (velocity const., \(MV=PY\))
$$\%\Delta M\approx\text{Nom. GDP growth}=\pi+\text{Real growth}$$
↳ why: the CB forecasts real growth and CHOOSES inflation; we judge it on the inflation outcome, not on the money-supply number.
Money demand (real)
$$\frac{M}{P}=L(\text{GDP},\,i)\quad(+\text{ on Y},\ -\text{ on }i)$$
Trap: panic ⟹ C/D, R/D jump ⟹ m collapses, so supply moves while base is flat.

L6 · Business cycles & asset prices L6

▸ Framework: stock price = PV of cash flows discounted at the required return; the risk premium is the ex-ante wedge.
Required stock return & ex-ante risk premium
$$R=r+RP\qquad R\approx\frac{E}{P}\ \Rightarrow\ RP=\frac{E}{P}-r$$
↳ why: the discount rate is the REAL rate + RP. Inflation per se does not move it; "there is nothing special about 4%" [Q4b]. It bites only via the real rate.
↳ why: P/E = 1/(r+RP): a run of surprise real-rate falls re-prices stocks UP now (capital gains) but means LOWER future returns. Bubble only if RP is abnormally low.
Trap: higher r ⟹ lower prices, but if r rises because growth is up, higher cash flows offset. A high P/E ≠ bubble.

L7 · IS-LM-FX · the short-run model L7–L8 · 26×

▸ Framework: the one engine for all of Part II. Three blocks (goods, money, capital flows); L7 assumes π=0 so r=i. The rF line is the anchor any shock gets dragged back to.
IS · goods market (aggregate demand)
$$\text{GDP}=C(\text{GDP},r,T,W)+I(r)+G+NX(e_R,\text{GDP},\text{GDP}_F)$$
LM · money market (L7 assumes π=0 ⟹ r=i)
$$\frac{M}{P}=L(\text{GDP},\,r)$$
FX · capital-flow arbitrage
$$r=r_F$$
4-step routine: shift the curve the shock hits → r vs rF → capital flow → appr./deprec. → NX adjusts IS → repeat to r=rF. The potency reversal & Impossible Trinity live in the page-1 matrix.
FX intervention via the balance sheet (≈ yearly)
Defend WEAK ccySell reserves, buy own ccy ⟹ base shrinks; reserves finite ⟹ can run out
Defend STRONG ccyBuy FX, issue own ccy ⟹ base swells; sustainable (print own ccy)
STERILISEDOffset with a domestic-bond sale ⟹ size unchanged, composition only, no macro effect
Punchline: pegs break on the weak side (1992 EMS). T/F "sterilised intervention changes balance-sheet size" = false.
Attacking a "rates / FX" question
  1. What does the shock hit first — a curve (IS/LM), the expected future short-rate path, or expected inflation?
  2. Capital mobile? FX regime? (does r=rF bind · fixed vs flexible?)
  3. Short vs long end: CB sets the short rate (Taylor); long end = expected future path + term premium.
  4. News vs level (priced-in?), and real vs nominal — check both.
  5. Trace the chain, state the direction + why.

L8 · Inflation, supply side & the Taylor Rule L8, L10 · 11×

▸ Framework: the Taylor rule is the CB's reaction function. π enters twice ⟹ a 1pt inflation rise lifts i by 1.5pt (Taylor principle).
Baseline nominal rate
$$i=\pi+\text{"reasonable" real return}$$
The Taylor Rule (Taylor 1993)
$$i=\underbrace{r^*+\pi}_{\text{baseline}}+0.5(\pi-\pi^{*})+0.5\,(\text{output gap})$$
Worked: r*=2, π*=2, π=3, gap=0 ⟹ i = 2+3+0.5(1) = 5.5%. The real rate rises to cool the economy.
LRAS adjustment & Phillips (self-correction to potential)
LRAS vertical at potential. Below potential ⟹ prices↓ ⟹ M/P↑ ⟹ LM shifts right ⟹ back to potential (above potential = mirror). Phillips = the same story in inflation-unemployment space (axes flip when using unemployment instead of output).
Trap: forgetting π enters twice; reacting to headline inflation without asking supply-shock vs true positive gap (CB may hold if temporary / anchored / re-anchoring).

L9 · Monetary policy & the ZLB L9 · 11×

▸ Framework: the Taylor rule wants i<0 (low r*, deflation, deep negative gap) but cash floors nominal rates at 0. A self-reinforcing trap: stuck at 0 → demand weak → gap & deflation worsen → rule wants an even lower rate.
ZLB tools: forward guidance (credible promise), QE (buy long bonds → see L10 yield grid), negative rates. QE ≠ inflation (swaps bonds for reserves; broad money needn't rise) and QE ≠ bailout: the CB provides liquidity (often at a profit); governments do solvency bailouts (taxpayer cost). Higher target (4%) ⟹ more room to cut when r* is low.
Trap: the ZLB floors the nominal rate, not the real rate: i≈0, π<0 ⟹ r=−π>0 (1931: 2.6−(−10.1)=+12.7%), which is what deepens a depression. Deflation also raises the real value of debt ⟹ default ⟹ runs.

L10 · Managing high inflation L10

▸ Framework: credibility & the nominal anchor. Disinflation can be near-costless if expectations are anchored.
Nominal anchor & credibility: long-run neutrality · the level of money/prices/initial FX is arbitrary; only the path (growth rate = inflation) is real. An anchor is a commitment (inflation target), not a quantity cap. Time inconsistency: pre-commitment beats discretion.
↳ why: one tool = pain: raise rates, kill demand, firms can't pass prices, inflation falls but unemployment rises first. Everything else is about shrinking that pain.
↳ why: flat Phillips = credible (firms don't pre-hike, belief self-fulfilling); steep = not. Volcker: be tough early to prove it's real, then the "magic jump" to the new anchor once wages re-anchor.
Trap: "fixed supply (Bitcoin/gold) ⟹ price stability" · an anchor is a commitment, not a cap.

L10 · The yield curve L10 · heavily examined

▸ Framework: the slope is the market's forecast of future short rates. Every question = say if it shifts / steepens / flattens / inverts, and why.
Long rate = avg expected future short rates + term premium
$$i_t^{(n)}\approx\tfrac{1}{n}\big(\mathbb{E}[i_t]+\dots+\mathbb{E}[i_{t+n-1}]\big)+\phi^{(n)}$$
3-part decomposition
$$\text{long rate}=\underbrace{\text{LR real rate}}_{}+\underbrace{\text{exp. inflation}}_{}+\underbrace{\text{term premium}}_{\phi}$$
The short end = today's CB policy rate (set by the Taylor rule, L8). Longer maturities = average of the expected future policy-rate path + term premium. So the slope is the market's forecast of where the CB takes rates: upward = expects hikes, inverted = expects cuts.

L11 · Fiscal policy & debt sustainability L11 · 12× · the one calc

▸ Framework: the only real calculation in the course. Will Debt/GDP stay constant? what primary surplus is needed?
Government balances
$$\text{Primary balance}=T-G-\text{Transfers}$$ $$\text{Overall}=\text{Primary balance}-\text{Interest}$$ $$\text{Interest}=r_{nominal}\times\text{Debt}$$
★ Debt-stabilising primary surplus
$$\boxed{\ \frac{\text{Req. primary surplus}}{\text{GDP}}=(r-g)\times\frac{\text{Debt}}{\text{GDP}}\ }$$
↳ why: required surplus INCREASES in (r−g) [2021 1c]. Self-fulfilling crisis: doubt, r↑, harder to meet, default; only a credible lender of last resort (Draghi's "whatever it takes") breaks it.
Trap: keep r and g in the SAME terms (both nominal or both real). If g>r the required surplus is negative · run deficits forever, ratio still falls. Fiscal multiplier: ΔY=ΔG/(1−mpc); automatic stabilisers cushion the cycle.

L12 · Exchange rates L12 · 18×+12×

▸ Framework: LOOP/PPP anchors the long run; UIP drives the short run. Real & nominal FX can move opposite ways (Balassa-Samuelson).
Law of one price (long-run anchor)
$$e_N=\frac{P_F}{P}\quad(\text{from }e_R=1)$$
Similar productivity ⟹ real rate constant (PPP)
$$\%\Delta e_N=\pi_F-\pi_{domestic}$$
Short run · UIP (currencies as assets)
$$r-r_F=\text{expected depreciation of domestic cur.}$$
Balassa-Samuelson: a catching-up economy has rising tradables productivity → higher inflation → real appreciation (the price of convergence, not lost competitiveness). So nominal can fall while real rises.
Overshooting (sticky prices): permanent ↑M ⟹ prices sticky ⟹ r↓ below rF ⟹ UIP needs an expected appreciation ⟹ spot jumps PAST its weaker long-run level on impact, then drifts back up as prices rise. Bigger stickiness ⟹ bigger overshoot.
↳ why: Singapore MAS manages the appreciation SLOPE of the currency vs a basket, which is its interest rate: flatten the slope as productivity slows, steepen it on an inflation shock.
Trap: news of a hike ⟹ appreciation now; a high level of rates ⟹ expected depreciation later. Don't equate high rates with a strong currency.

L13 · State of the global economy L13

▸ Framework: apply the whole toolkit to live news · global S-I imbalances, world r*, CA ↔ capital ↔ reserves.
CA, capital account & reserves close the loop
$$CA+KA+\Delta\text{reserves}=0\qquad CA=S-I$$
Worked (China): CA surplus +$238bn AND private inflow +$287bn ⟹ CB accumulates +$472bn reserves to balance. Sign trap: a CA surplus is a capital OUTFLOW even paired with a private INflow.
