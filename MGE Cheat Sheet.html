1 · National accounts & identities

GDPGDP = C + I + G + NX = C + S + T
Core identityS = I + CA; CA = S − I = NX + net foreign income
  • Private S + public (T−G) = I + NX. S>I ⟹ lend abroad CA>0 (Germany, ageing). I>S ⟹ borrow CA<0 (US, opportunities). Not virtue/vice: depends on productive use.
  • Real vs nominal (deflator). Cross-country ⟹ PPP-GDP (one price, incl. non-traded). GNP = GDP + net factor payments. GDP/capita = living standards; GDP = size/power.

2 · Saving–Investment & the real rate

  • r* = price of the S/I market. S↑ (glut, ageing, ↑income) ⟹ r↓. I↑ (productive opp., confidence) ⟹ r↑. Open: S = I + CA at world r_W; gap = capital flow.
Fisheri (nominal) = r (real) + πe ⟹ r = i − πe

3 · Growth — why countries are rich

DecompositionY/N = (Y/H)×(H/N) = productivity × employment(hrs/wkr × wkrs/pop)
Accounting%ΔY = %ΔTFP + α·%ΔK + (1−α)·%ΔL
  • Solow: diminishing returns to K ⟹ convergence only toward the same steady state (conditional). Korea converged; Brazil didn't. K alone runs out ⟹ TFP/ideas drive long-run growth.
  • 4 Is: Investment (K, infra) · Innovation (TFP, ideas) · Institutions (rule of law, property) · Incentives (open markets). Institutions/TFP dominate.
  • Demographics: working-age growth ⟹ GDP; replacement fertility 2.1; Japan shrank working-age but raised participation. Same productivity, different hours ⟹ France ≈ US/hr but lower GDP/capita.

4 · Money & inflation (long run)

Real money demandM/P = L(Y, i): +Y (transactions), −i (opp. cost)
Quantity theoryMV = PY ⟹ π ≈ %ΔM − %ΔY (money neutral LR)
  • Inflation = monetary phenomenon LR. CB credibility anchors πe; un-anchoring is the danger. Seigniorage = printing to fund deficits ⟹ high inflation.

4b · Real vs nominal rates & inflation

  • Ex-ante real r = i − πe; ex-post = i − π_actual. Only the real rate drives saving/investment decisions.
  • Unexpected inflation redistributes: from creditors → debtors (erodes the real value of nominal debt). A government with nominal debt gains (the "inflation tax"); savers in cash lose.
  • Indexed bonds (TIPS): breakeven inflation = nominal yield − real yield = the market's πe.
  • ZLB twist: if πe falls with i stuck at 0, the real rate rises (deflation is contractionary, self-reinforcing).

5 · Exchange rates (theory) ★ most-tested

Convention (S12)e_N = foreign ccy per home unit; ↑e = appreciation
Real ratee_R = e_N·P/P_F (rel. price home vs foreign goods)
PPP (LR)%Δe_N ≈ π_F − π_dom (higher π ⟹ nominal depreciation)
UIP (asset)r − r_F = expected depreciation of home ccy
  • PPP binds only for tradables; non-tradable share lets e_R ≠ 1. %Δe_R = %Δe_N + (π_dom − π_F).
  • Balassa-Samuelson: catch-up productivity in tradables ⟹ wages↑ economy-wide ⟹ non-tradable P↑ ⟹ real appreciation (rich = "expensive"; Big Mac). Real & nominal can move opposite ways.
  • UIP: surprise hike ⟹ inflow ⟹ immediate appreciation; high level of rates ⟹ expected future depreciation (carry no free lunch). "Priced-in" ⟹ no move.
  • Overshooting (Dornbusch): sticky P ⟹ on monetary expansion e jumps past its weaker LR level then appreciates back (UIP+PPP reconciled). Bigger stickiness ⟹ bigger overshoot.

6 · IS-LM-FX — the short-run machine

  • IS: r↓ ⟹ I,C↑ ⟹ GDP↑. Shifts right: G↑, T↓, confidence, NX↑ (weaker e).
  • LM: CB sets policy r (M/P↑ ⟹ r↓). r = r_F under free capital = the third line.
  • Routine (any policy): curve shifts → r vs r_F → capital flow → apprec/deprec → NX → GDP. Write the chain + draw it.

8 · Taylor rule & supply side

Taylor rule (policy, NOT identity)i = r* + π + φ_π(π−π*) + φ_y(y−ȳ) [typ φ≈0.5]
  • Thermostat: neutral r*+π, then lean vs cycle (you choose φ). Taylor principle: 1pp π ⟹ nominal +(1+φ_π) ⟹ real rate rises (else loose). Inflation counts twice. Taylor-1999: φ_y up to 1.0.
  • Worked: r*=2,π=3,π*=2,gap=0 ⟹ i=2+3+0.5(1)+0=5.5% (real 2.5%). Recession π=1,gap=−4 ⟹ 2+1+0.5(−1)+0.5(−4)=0.5%.
  • Phillips/output gap: below potential ⟹ slack ⟹ wages,π↓ ⟹ M/P↑ ⟹ LM→ (self-corrects, slow). LRAS vertical at potential; NAIRU. Trap: with unemployment on x-axis the overheating/recession halves flip.

9 · ZLB & unconventional

  • i can't go far below 0 ⟹ liquidity trap. Real rate r=i−πe stuck high if πe low (deflation worsens it).
  • Tools: forward guidance (promise low rates), QE (buy long bonds ⟹ term premium↓ ⟹ long yield↓, flatter), negative rates, yield-curve control (BoJ).
  • QE ≠ automatic inflation (2008-19): depends on πe. Exit: sell securities or raise rate paid on reserves (no "optimal" balance-sheet size). ECB acted late ⟹ double-dip.

9b · Nominal anchor & credibility

  • Nominal anchor = the ONE nominal variable the CB commits to (inflation target ~2%, FX peg, or money growth) to pin πe. Modern default = inflation targeting + independent CB.
  • Time inconsistency: a CB free to surprise-inflate is tempted to ⟹ public anticipates ⟹ higher π for no output gain. Fix = rules + credibility + independence.
  • CB independence ⟹ lower, more stable inflation (Alesina-Summers). Threat = fiscal dominance / political pressure on the chair.
  • Disinflation cost depends on credibility: credible ⟹ expectations fall fast, low sacrifice (2021-24, no recession); not credible ⟹ recession needed (Volcker 1980-82).

11 · Fiscal, multiplier & debt

BalancesPrimary = T − G − transfers (excl. interest); Overall = Primary − r·Debt
Debt-stabilising surplus(Primary surplus)/GDP = (r − g) × Debt/GDP
  • Treadmill: (r−g) = belt speed, Debt/GDP = backpack. r>g ⟹ snowball, need surplus; g>r ⟹ falls even with deficits (China, Japan). e.g. r=4,g=2,D/Y=100% ⟹ +2% needed.
  • r carries a sovereign risk premium (self-fulfilling). Govt ≠ business: default = redistribution not destroyed wealth. No need to repay, just non-explosive path. Inflation ~neutral (raises r and g).
  • Multiplier ΔY = ΔG/(1−mpc); leaks via taxes/imports/saving. Automatic stabilisers (taxes fall, benefits rise in slumps) — strip them out ⟹ structural balance = true discretionary stance (Europe 2010-14 = real austerity). 3 Ts: timely, targeted, temporary.
  • Even a primary surplus can't stop debt rising if the interest bill r·Debt is large enough (the snowball). Fiscal space = room to borrow before markets balk; lost when r jumps above g or credibility breaks. Consolidation that works = gradual + credible + protect growth.

  • Unsterilised: both sides of the CB balance sheet grow ⟹ base↑ ⟹ macro effect. Sterilised: also sell a domestic asset (or issue CB bills) to offset ⟹ composition changes, base unchanged ⟹ ≈ no macro effect.
  • Asymmetry: defend a strong ccy forever (print own); defend a weak one only till reserves run out ⟹ pegs break on the weak side (1992 EMS, Soros). Sudden stops: capital flight ⟹ forced CA reversal.

12b · Global imbalances & crises

  • Saving glut (Bernanke): EM + oil + ageing savers ⟹ low world r* and large US deficits. Twin deficits: fiscal + CA move together.
  • Net IIP = cumulative CA + valuation effects (a depreciation can raise the value of foreign assets). The US runs deficits yet earns positive net income (exorbitant privilege; the $ is the reserve currency).
  • Sudden stop: capital inflows reverse ⟹ forced CA reversal + crisis (1997 Asia). Original sin: EMs borrow in $ ⟹ a depreciation raises the real debt burden (balance-sheet effect), so depreciation can be contractionary, not expansionary.

13 · Exam method & traps

Method: (1) what does the shock do to the curve/expected path? (2) is capital mobile — does r_F bind? (3) trace the chain, draw it, state the verdict + WHY. (4) check real vs nominal, news vs level, the e convention.
