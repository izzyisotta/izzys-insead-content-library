1 · National accounts & identities

GDP (expenditure)GDP = C + I + G + NX
The core identityS = I + CA  ⟹  CA = S − I  (=NX + net foreign income)
  • Income use: GDP = C + S + T ⟹ private S + public (T−G) = I + NX.
  • Surplus S>I ⟹ lend abroad, CA>0 (Germany, ageing). Deficit I>S ⟹ borrow, CA<0 (US, opportunities). Not virtue/vice: depends on productive use.
  • Real vs nominal (GDP deflator). Cross-country: use PPP-GDP (one price, incl. non-traded). GNP = GDP + net factor payments.

2 · Saving–Investment & the real rate

  • Price of the S/I market is the real rate r*. S↑ (saving glut, ageing, ↑income) ⟹ r↓. I↑ (productive opp., ↑confidence) ⟹ r↑.
  • Open economy: S = I + CA at world rate r_W; gap funded by capital flows.
Fisheri (nominal) = r (real) + πe  ⟹  r = i − πe

3 · Growth — why countries are rich

DecompositionY/N = (Y/H) × (H/N) = productivity × employment
Growth accounting%ΔY = %ΔTFP + α·%ΔK + (1−α)·%ΔL
  • Solow: diminishing returns to K ⟹ convergence (poor grow faster) only toward the same steady state (conditional convergence). Korea converged, Brazil didn't.
  • 4 Is: Investment (K, infra), Innovation (TFP, ideas), Institutions (rule of law, property rights), Incentives (open markets). TFP/institutions dominate long run.
  • Demographics: working-age growth drives GDP; per-capita drives living standards. Replacement fertility 2.1.

4 · Money & inflation (long run)

Real money demandM/P = L(Y, i):  +Y (transactions), −i (opp. cost)
Quantity theoryMV = PY ⟹ π ≈ %ΔM − %ΔY  (money neutral LR)
  • Inflation is a monetary phenomenon in the long run. CB credibility anchors πe; un-anchoring is the danger.

5 · Exchange rates (theory) ★ most-tested cluster

Convention (S12)e_N = foreign ccy per domestic unit; ↑e = appreciation
Real ratee_R = e_N × P/P_F  (rel. price of home vs foreign goods)
PPP (long run)%Δe_N ≈ π_F − π_dom  (higher inflation ⟹ nominal depreciation)
UIP (asset view)r − r_F = expected depreciation of home ccy
  • PPP binds only for tradables; the non-tradable share lets the real rate sit ≠ 1. %Δe_R = %Δe_N + (π_dom − π_F).
  • Balassa-Samuelson: catch-up productivity in tradables ⟹ economy-wide wages↑ ⟹ non-tradable prices↑ ⟹ real appreciation (rich = "expensive"). Real & nominal can move opposite ways.
  • UIP: a surprise hike ⟹ inflow ⟹ immediate appreciation; a high level of rates ⟹ expected future depreciation (carry is no free lunch). "Priced-in" ⟹ no move on the day.
  • Overshooting (Dornbusch): prices sticky ⟹ on a monetary expansion the rate jumps past its weaker long-run level, then appreciates back (reconciles UIP with PPP). Bigger stickiness ⟹ bigger overshoot.

6 · IS-LM-FX — the short-run machine

  • IS: r↓ ⟹ I,C↑ ⟹ GDP↑ (downward). Shifts right: G↑, T↓, confidence, NX↑ (weaker e).
  • LM: CB sets the policy rate (M/P↑ ⟹ r↓). r = r_F under perfect capital mobility = the third line.
  • Routine for ANY policy: which curve shifts → r vs r_F → capital flow → appreciation/depreciation → NX → GDP. Write the chain.

8 · Taylor Rule & the supply side

Taylor rule (a policy, not an identity)i = r* + π + φ_π(π − π*) + φ_y(y − ȳ)  [typ. φ≈0.5]
  • Thermostat: neutral setting r*+π, then lean against the cycle. You choose φ.
  • Taylor principle: 1pp ↑π must raise the nominal rate by 1+φ_π ⟹ the real rate rises (else policy is loose). Inflation counts twice.
  • Output gap y−ȳ: below potential ⟹ slack ⟹ wages/π↓ ⟹ M/P↑ ⟹ LM→ (self-corrects, slowly). LRAS vertical at potential; NAIRU.

9 · ZLB & unconventional policy

  • i can't go far below 0 ⟹ liquidity trap; conventional policy stuck.
  • Tools: forward guidance (promise low rates), QE (buy long bonds ⟹ term premium↓ ⟹ long yield↓, flatter curve), negative rates.
  • QE ≠ automatic inflation (2008-19): depends on πe. Exit: sell securities or raise rate paid on reserves.

10 · Yield curve ★ 18 past Qs

The one identitylong yield ≈ avg(expected future short rates) + term premium
= avg real rate + avg expected inflation + TP
  • Slope = forecast: upward ⟹ rates expected to rise; inverted (short>long) ⟹ expected to fall (recession OR credible disinflation).
  • Moves: inflation surprise pre-CB ⟹ long↑ steepen; credible disinflation ⟹ long↓ invert; hikes-expected/long-anchored ⟹ flatten; QE ⟹ flatten; heavy long issuance/fiscal risk ⟹ steepen.
  • Trap: inversion ≠ guaranteed recession (2023 = soft landing).

11 · Fiscal policy & debt sustainability

BalancesPrimary = T − G − transfers (excl. interest); Overall = Primary − r·Debt
Debt-stabilising surplus(Required primary surplus)/GDP = (r − g) × Debt/GDP
  • Treadmill: (r−g) = how fast the belt drags; Debt/GDP = backpack weight. r>g ⟹ snowball, need a surplus; g>r ⟹ ratio falls even with deficits (China, Japan).
  • r carries a sovereign risk premium (can be self-fulfilling). Govt ≠ business: default = redistribution, not destroyed wealth. No need to repay, just a non-explosive path. Inflation ~neutral (raises both r and g).

12 · Current account, BoP & FX intervention

Balance of paymentsCA + KA + Δreserves = 0;   CA = S − I = NX + net income
  • Unsterilised intervention: CB buys FX ⟹ monetary base↑ (both sides of CB balance sheet grow). Sterilised: sell a domestic asset to offset ⟹ composition changes only, ≈ no macro effect.
  • Asymmetry: defend a strong ccy forever (print your own); defend a weak one only until reserves run out ⟹ pegs break on the weak side (1992 EMS).

13 · Exam traps & myths to refute

  • "High debt ⟹ default" ✗ · "must repay govt debt" ✗ · "debt burdens next generation" ½ (they inherit the assets + higher incomes).
  • "Disinflation always ⟹ unemployment" ✗ (2021-24) · "high rates ⟹ strong currency" ✗ (UIP: expected to depreciate).
  • "Inversion ⟹ recession" not guaranteed · "deficit = bad" depends on productive use · fixing FX ⟹ you follow the other's policy (true), but bilateral trade need not balance (false).
  • Always check: real vs nominal; news vs level (priced-in); the e convention; whether capital is mobile (does r_F bind?).
