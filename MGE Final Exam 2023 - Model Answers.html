1 · True / False / Uncertain 25 points

State the verdict, then justify with a mechanism. The justification is the entire grade.

a) "An increase in government debt always leads to a capital outflow." 5

FALSE
BoP identity, by sector (L2)
$$(S_{priv}-I_{priv})+(S_{gov}-I_{gov})=CA, \qquad CA = S - I$$
Mechanism
more government debt = government borrowing (\(S_{gov}\downarrow\)). The capital-flow direction depends on whether private saving absorbs it. If domestic private saving does not cover the borrowing ⇒ \(S⇒ CA deficit ⇒ a capital inflow, the opposite of the claim.

The word "always" is what sinks it. (An "Uncertain" that explains both cases is also acceptable.)

b) "A country whose productivity is growing rapidly could see its currency depreciate in value." 5

TRUE
Productivity, the real rate and inflation (L12)
$$e_R\uparrow \text{ with relative productivity}; \qquad \%\Delta e_N = \pi_F - \pi_{domestic}$$
Mechanism
fast productivity growth ⇒ the country becomes more expensive in real terms (real appreciation), but that can show up either as higher inflation or as nominal appreciation. If domestic inflation runs high enough, \(\pi_{domestic}>\pi_F\) ⇒ \(\%\Delta e_N<0\) ⇒ the nominal currency depreciates despite the productivity gain.

c) "The existence of printed physical currency prevents real interest rates from going meaningfully below zero." 5

FALSE
Fisher / the zero lower bound (L9)
$$r = i - \pi; \qquad \text{cash floors } i \ge 0, \text{ not } r$$
Mechanism
physical cash pays exactly 0% in nominal terms, so it is the nominal rate that is floored at zero. Nothing stops the real rate \(r=i-\pi\) from being deeply negative when inflation exceeds the nominal rate.
The classic trap (the mirror is 1931: \(i=2.6\%\), \(\pi=-10.1\%\) gave \(r=+12.7\%\)). The ZLB binds nominal, never real.

d) "Increasing the inflation target from 2% to 4% would reduce the likelihood of central banks engaging in QE during crises." 5

TRUE
Baseline nominal rate + the ZLB (L8–L9)
$$i = \pi^{*} + r^{*} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \text{higher } \pi^{*} \Rightarrow \text{higher } i \text{ in normal times}$$
Mechanism
a 4% target lifts the normal-times nominal rate ⇒ more room to cut before hitting the zero bound ⇒ conventional rate cuts can do the work in a crisis ⇒ less need to resort to QE.

e) "Investing in currencies with high interest rates can guarantee a high return because, in addition to the high rates, the currency is likely to appreciate." 5

FALSE
Currencies as assets / arbitrage (UIP) (L12)
$$r - r_F = \text{expected depreciation of the domestic currency}$$
Mechanism
a high level of rates usually signals high inflation ⇒ the currency tends to depreciate, not appreciate. Under arbitrage the interest pick-up is exactly offset by the expected currency loss ⇒ returns are equalised across countries; no free lunch.
Don't conflate "news of a rate rise" (⇒ appreciation now) with "a high level of rates" (⇒ expected depreciation). The statement leans on the wrong one.

2 · China's reopening 15 points

Setup. A Financial Times article from 17 January 2023 reports that "China's economy grew by just 3 per cent in 2022, underscoring the heavy costs of the government's longstanding zero-Covid strategy before it was abruptly abandoned last month. The country's gross domestic product figures missed Beijing's official growth target, which at 5.5 per cent was already the lowest in decades." The article also notes China's economy in 2022 faced "a slump in exports as the global economy slows" and that "Real estate investment fell by 10 per cent in 2022 as part of a property crisis that drove home sales 24 per cent lower by floor space and 27 per cent lower by dollar value."

(For part c) As China's economy reopened, the FT reported "Drug shortfalls that hit hospitals in Beijing and Shanghai last month as Covid cases surged are expanding into rural areas as bureaucratic inefficiency, pricing disputes with pharmaceutical companies and overspending on testing capacity have resulted in a shortage of resources."

a) Given the state of China's economy, why did inflation stay lower in China than elsewhere in 2022? 5

Output gap & the Phillips curve (L6, L8)
$$\text{Output gap}=100\times\frac{\text{Actual}-\text{Potential}}{\text{Potential}}; \qquad \text{slack}\Rightarrow \text{low inflation}$$
Mechanism
growth of 3% (vs a 5.5% target), an export slump and a property crisis all point to actual output below potential (a negative output gap) ⇒ demand is weak, firms have little pricing power ⇒ low inflation.

This is the Phillips-curve logic: a slack economy sits on the low-inflation part of the curve, unlike the demand-driven overheating that drove 2022 inflation in the US and Europe.

b) What policy measures might the government and central bank use to encourage growth in 2023? 5

IS-LM (L7)
$$\text{GDP}=C+I+G+NX; \qquad \tfrac{M}{P}=L(\text{GDP},i)$$
Two levers
Fiscal: ↑G or ↓T ⇒ IS shifts right ⇒ demand and GDP rise.
Monetary: ↑M ⇒ LM shifts right ⇒ r falls ⇒ investment and consumption rise ⇒ GDP rises.

Both push the IS-LM equilibrium toward potential output.

c) How might health concerns and low consumer confidence affect the efficacy of these stimulus measures? 5

Framework: policy transmission depends on how responsive C and I are
Stimulus only works if lower rates and higher income actually translate into spending. Caution breaks that link.
Mechanism
Monetary: r↓ is weak if anxious firms and households won't invest or spend at any rate (interest-insensitive I, C) ⇒ a steep, hard-to-shift IS.
Fiscal (tax cuts): if cautious households save the windfall rather than spend it (low marginal propensity to consume) ⇒ a small multiplier ⇒ little boost to GDP.

Low confidence is, in effect, a precautionary-saving shock that blunts both levers, the same friction that makes liquidity-trap conditions so hard to escape.

3 · Fiscal and monetary policy in Brazil 10 points

Setup. Writing about Brazil on 27 December 2022, the Financial Times reports that "the economy is having to digest a series of interest rate rises aimed at reducing inflation." The central bank has implemented "rate rises to the current level of 13.75 per cent." The start of 2023 saw the inauguration of a new government under president Lula da Silva and, for many investors, "A key concern… is how the Lula administration approaches fiscal policy. On the campaign trail, the former trade unionist pledged to increase spending on social assistance programmes."

a) If the new government runs a larger-than-expected fiscal stimulus and the central bank does nothing, what happens to capital flows, the value of the real, and net exports? 5

IS-LM-FX, fiscal expansion under floating (L7)
$$\text{GDP}=C+I+G+NX(e,\dots); \qquad \text{capital flows until } r=r_F$$
Mechanism (4-step engine)
↑G ⇒ IS shifts right ⇒ r↑ above rF ⇒ capital inflow (foreign investors chase the higher return) ⇒ demand for the real rises ⇒ the real appreciates ⇒ Brazilian goods dearer ⇒ net exports fall.

b) If the central bank instead acts to stabilise GDP growth, what happens to interest rates and to the domestic components of expenditure (C, I, G)? 5

Monetary response to overheating (L8)
$$\text{aim at potential output} \Rightarrow \text{raise } i; \quad C(\,\cdot\,,r,\dots)\downarrow,\ I(r)\downarrow$$
Mechanism
the stimulus pushes demand above potential ⇒ inflation pressure ⇒ to keep output stable the CB raises interest rates (LM shifts left). Higher r ⇒ consumption and investment fall; government purchases (the stimulus) stay high.

So the composition of demand shifts from private (C, I) to public (G): the rate hike crowds out private spending to make room for the fiscal expansion while holding total GDP near potential.

4 · The yield curve in the US 10 points

Setup. In 2021 inflation increased faster than anticipated. By the end of 2021 the US Federal Reserve had not yet implemented any increase in interest rate and the market had already digested the fact that inflation would be higher in the near and medium term.
The macro view of the yield curve (L10)
$$\text{long rate} = \underbrace{\text{equilibrium real rate}}_{\text{global S \& I}} + \underbrace{\text{expected inflation}}_{\text{CB credibility}}; \qquad \text{short rate} = \text{CB (Taylor rule)}$$

a) How did the yield curve move as the news of higher inflation hit the markets, before the Fed raised rates? 5

Mechanism
higher expected inflation ⇒ long rates rise immediately; the Fed had not yet moved ⇒ short rates unchanged ⇒ the curve steepens.

b) Once the market is convinced the Fed will succeed and inflation expectations fall (while short rates stay high), how does the curve shift? Can this cause an inversion? Does inversion predict a recession here? 5

Mechanism
short rates held high by the Fed; lower expected inflation ⇒ long rates fall ⇒ the curve flattens, and can invert (long below short).

Does inversion predict a recession here? No. In this case the inversion is the market pricing successful disinflation, not a coming downturn: long rates are low because inflation is expected to fall, the soft-landing reading. Inversion is a recession signal only when it reflects expected rate cuts to fight a slump, which is a different cause.

5 · The ECB and interest rates 10 points

Setup. On January 16, 2023 the Financial Times interviewed Phil Lane, the European Central Bank (ECB) chief economist, asking whether the Euro area will return to an environment of low interest rates. His answer: "If expectations have now re-anchored at our 2% target, compared to being well below it before, interest rates will go to the level consistent with that 2% target, and not back to the super-low rates we needed to fight below-target inflation before. For nominal rates, that makes a big difference."

(For part b) Later in the interview, Lane compares trends in real interest rates to the period after the 2008 crisis: "We had deleveraging after the global financial crisis. In a number of countries, households had to reduce their household debt. Also, we had a number of governments that had to run austere fiscal policies (smaller deficits)… In the pandemic, however, governments had to run big deficits. That spending was transferred to households and firms. Also, the pandemic created 'forced savings', because there was less opportunity to spend. Now governments will have to pull back from the high level of fiscal support they offered during the pandemic… The fact that households have better balance sheets now also means that support for aggregate demand after the pandemic will probably be stronger than before the pandemic." (For information: after the global financial crisis the current account of the Euro area moved from balance to a large surplus.)

a) Is a world of higher nominal rates bad news for governments? Why does Lane say "if expectations are re-anchored at our 2% target"? What if expectations were above 2%? 5

Fisher + debt dynamics (L1, L11)
$$i = r + \pi; \qquad \frac{\text{Required primary surplus}}{\text{GDP}}=(r-g)\times\frac{\text{Debt}}{\text{GDP}}$$
Mechanism
Lane is talking about nominal rates. If they are higher only because inflation expectations are higher, the real rate is unchanged ⇒ governments (who care about \(r\), not \(i\)) are not worse off (inflation lifts both \(r\) and \(g\), which cancels in \((r-g)\)).

Why "if re-anchored at 2%": that is the benign case, where the higher nominal rate is just 2% inflation plus a normal real return. If expectations un-anchor above 2%, the ECB will refuse to let inflation run, so it must raise the real rate to bring inflation back, raising \((r-g)\) and the real debt burden, which is bad for governments.

b) Following Lane's logic, what happens to real rates this time vs after the 2008 crisis? And (if this is a Euro-area-only phenomenon) to the current account? 5

The Saving-Investment cross (L2)
$$r^{*} \text{ clears } S=I; \qquad CA = S - I$$
Mechanism
post-2008 there was deleveraging: high saving, weak investment ⇒ low real rates and a CA surplus. This time, healthier balance sheets mean no deleveraging ⇒ more consumption, less saving, more investment ⇒ S shifts left and I shifts right ⇒ higher real interest rate.

Current account: if this is Euro-area-specific (not mirrored abroad), then \(CA = S - I\) falls: the euro area moves toward smaller surpluses or larger deficits, the reverse of the post-2008 swing into surplus.
