1 · True / False / Uncertain 20 points

State the verdict, then justify with a mechanism. The justification is the entire grade.

a) "The only way to combine a current account surplus with a private capital inflow is through accumulation of foreign reserves." 5

TRUE
Balance-of-payments identity (L2)
$$CA = \text{Capital \& Financial Account}, \qquad (S_{priv}-I_{priv})+(S_{gov}-I_{gov}) = CA$$
Mechanism
CA surplus ⇒ the country is a net lender, i.e. a net capital outflow. A private capital inflow pushes the private account the wrong way, so the only entry left to restore the net outflow is a public capital outflow = reserve accumulation.

Equivalently: foreign currency arrives both as payment for the trade surplus and as the private inflow; if neither private agents nor the trade account absorb it, it can only pile up as official FX reserves. So the statement holds.

b) "When a country fixes its exchange rate to another currency it implicitly follows that country's policies. As a result the bilateral trade balance between the two will always be zero (balanced trade)." 5

FALSE

The first clause is correct (a peg with open capital markets imports the anchor's monetary policy: the Impossible Trinity). The conclusion is wrong.

What actually sets the trade balance (L2 / L13)
$$NX \approx CA = S - I$$
Mechanism
Trade balances are driven by saving versus investment, not by the currency regime. A fixed exchange rate fixes nothing about \(S-I\), and certainly nothing about the bilateral split. There is no mechanical link between exchange-rate stability and balanced trade.
Trap: conflating a stable currency with balanced trade. High-tariff Egypt still runs a deficit; zero-tariff Singapore a huge surplus. It is always \(S-I\).

c) "Last week China held the Renminbi stable vs the dollar (~Rmb 7.169) when markets had expected a depreciation. Given the news, expectations of inflation in China must have increased." 5

FALSE
Framework: currencies as assets (L12) + the nominal anchor (L8)
A currency stronger than the market priced in signals tighter-than-expected monetary policy, which lowers, not raises, expected inflation.
Mechanism (two routes, same sign)
stronger-than-expected Rmb ⇒ tighter MP than priced ⇒ lower expected inflation.
stronger Rmb ⇒ cheaper imports ⇒ lower inflation.

Both channels push expected inflation down. The premise points the opposite way to the conclusion, so the statement is false.

d) "An increasing employment rate among older workers can help alleviate the effects of ageing on GDP per capita." 5

TRUE
Growth decomposition (L3)
$$\frac{\text{GDP}}{\text{Pop}}=\underbrace{\frac{\text{GDP}}{\text{Hours}}}_{\text{productivity}}\times\underbrace{\frac{\text{Hours}}{\text{Workers}}}_{\text{effort}}\times\underbrace{\frac{\text{Workers}}{\text{Working-age}}}_{\text{labour mkt}}\times\underbrace{\frac{\text{Working-age}}{\text{Pop}}}_{\text{demographics}}$$
Mechanism
Ageing shrinks the demographics term (Working-age/Pop). Raising older-worker employment lifts the labour-market term (Workers/Working-age), which offsets the drag and keeps the labour force (and GDP per capita) from falling as much, or even rising.

This is exactly Japan "defying gravity": the working-age population fell by ~12m yet the labour force grew, via later retirement and higher participation. (Caveat: there is a ceiling: you cannot ask people to work to 85.)

2 · Inflation, interest rates and debt in Japan 10 points

a) Why might a central bank committed to its 2% target (the BoJ) not raise rates even though inflation ran above target in 2022–23? 5

Taylor rule (L8)
$$i = \underbrace{r^{*}+\pi}_{\text{baseline}} + 0.5(\pi-\pi^{*}) + 0.5\,(\text{output gap})$$
Two framework reasons
(1) Output gap. A large negative output gap (slack/unemployment) drags the prescribed rate down through the last term, so the rule need not call for a hike even with \(\pi>\pi^{*}\).
(2) Inflation expectations. After decades anchored on deflation, the BoJ wants expectations to re-anchor upward toward 2%; hiking early would kill the very inflation it spent 20 years trying to create.

Either point earns the marks; both together is the full answer. (A low \(r^{*}\) is an acceptable third reason.)

b) If higher inflation is driving the government's borrowing costs up, what does that imply for the sustainability of Japan's government debt? 5

Debt dynamics (L11) — the one formula you compute
$$\frac{\text{Required primary surplus}}{\text{GDP}} = (r-g)\times\frac{\text{Debt}}{\text{GDP}}$$
Mechanism
Inflation raises both the nominal borrowing rate \(r\) and nominal GDP growth \(g\). The gap \((r-g)\) is what drives the debt ratio, and it is roughly unchanged ⇒ little effect on sustainability.

The exception is the whole point: sustainability deteriorates only if \(r\) rises faster than inflation/growth, so that \((r-g)\) widens and the required primary surplus rises. So: higher nominal borrowing costs alone tell you almost nothing; what matters is \(r\) versus \(g\).

3 · Geopolitics and central banks 10 points

a) Saudi Arabia and China sell French/European government bonds and buy US bonds. How does this affect the value of the Euro? Any implications for euro-area inflation and growth? 5

Framework: short-run FX is an asset price set by capital flows (L12)
Mechanism
sell EUR assets to buy USD assets ⇒ capital outflow from the euro ⇒ EUR depreciates ⇒ (i) imported-goods prices rise ⇒ higher inflation; and (ii) cheaper euro exports ⇒ \(NX\uparrow\) ⇒ higher demand/GDP ⇒ further inflation pressure.

So: downward pressure on the Euro, with higher inflation and (modestly) higher growth in the euro area. (One could add that euro-government yields rise, with fiscal knock-ons, but that is not required.)

b) What action could the ECB take to eliminate all these effects, and what would it do to the size of the ECB's balance sheet? 5

Framework: sterilised intervention / asset swap (L5, L7)
The ECB buys the European bonds being dumped and pays with its foreign reserves (selling the very US bonds Saudi Arabia and China want).
Mechanism
ECB sells USD assets, buys EUR bonds ⇒ it exactly absorbs the portfolio shift ⇒ no effect on the exchange rate, inflation or growth.

Balance-sheet size: unchanged. It is one asset replacing another (US bonds out, European bonds in), so only the composition moves, not the size. That asymmetry (composition, not size) is the defining feature of a sterilised swap.

4 · Fiscal austerity in Indonesia 10 points

a) How do budget cuts affect consumption and GDP growth in this environment, and why would they affect a currency already near four-year lows? 5

IS-LM-FX, fiscal contraction (L7) — which term each effect hits
$$\text{GDP}=\underbrace{C(\text{GDP},r,T,W)}_{\text{induced (multiplier)}}+\underbrace{I(r)}_{\text{rate offset}}+\underbrace{G}_{\text{direct }\downarrow}+\underbrace{NX(e,\text{GDP},\text{GDP}_F)}_{\text{FX offset}}$$
Mechanism — in causal order
\(\downarrow G\) (direct demand cut) ⇒ IS shifts left ⇒ GDP & household income fall ⇒ induced \(\downarrow C\) (multiplier) ⇒ GDP growth falls further ⇒ money demand falls ⇒ \(r\) falls ⇒ \(r⇒ capital outflow ⇒ the rupiah depreciates.
Separating the effect on GDP
  • Direct (the cut itself): \(\downarrow G\) removes demand one-for-one — this is the size of the IS shift.
  • Indirect, amplifies: lower income \(\Rightarrow \downarrow C\) (the Keynesian multiplier) — already baked into the width of the IS shift (\(\Delta G \times \tfrac{1}{1-\text{MPC}}\)).
  • Indirect, offsets: lower GDP \(\Rightarrow r\downarrow\), which lifts \(I(r)\) and (via outflow \(\Rightarrow\) depreciation) lifts \(NX\) — the move along IS′ to E₁, so output falls by less than the full shift.

Why the currency moves: the depreciation comes from the indirect rate channel, not the direct cut. The induced fall in activity lowers the domestic interest rate below \(r_F\), and that is what drives capital out and pushes an already-weak rupiah down further. (Caveat: if the market has already priced the slowdown, only the unexpected part moves the currency.)

b) The central bank just cut its rate 6.00% → 5.75%. How might the austerity plan change its future monetary policy, and how will that affect the rupiah? 5

Taylor rule + uncovered interest parity (L8, L12)
$$i = r^{*}+\pi+0.5(\pi-\pi^{*})+0.5(\text{output gap}); \qquad r - r_F = \text{expected depreciation}$$
Mechanism
austerity ⇒ lower growth and lower inflation ⇒ Taylor rule prescribes more room to cut rates ⇒ (provided the cuts are not yet priced by the FX market) further depreciation of the rupiah.
Priced-in trick: if the market has already priced the future cuts, the currency does not move on the news. Only the unexpected portion of the easing depreciates the rupiah.

5 · Initial value of the Euro 10 points

a) How would choosing a different (lower) initial exchange rate have affected prices in the euro area? And inflation over the following ten years? 5

Framework: money neutrality / the initial price level is arbitrary (L5)
"Add a zero": the level of a currency is arbitrary; what matters is how monetary policy is run afterwards (the nominal anchor).
Mechanism
lower initial Euro ⇒ all initial euro prices would simply have been higher (a one-off level shift) ⇒ zero effect on inflation in the years that followed.

Future inflation is determined by the conduct of monetary policy (the 2% anchor), not by the starting value of the currency. The premise that a lower launch value would have meant lower subsequent inflation is wrong.

b) The Euro is now $1.03, down from $1.1686 at launch. Give two reasons, using solid economic logic, for the depreciation. 5

Long-run FX: productivity + inflation differential (L12)
$$\%\Delta e_N = \pi_F - \pi_{domestic} \quad\text{(similar productivity)}; \qquad e_R\uparrow \text{ with relative productivity}$$
  1. Lower productivity growth in the euro area. Slower tradables-productivity growth than the US implies the real exchange rate should depreciate; with similar inflation, that comes through as a nominal depreciation.
  2. Higher euro-area inflation than the US. By the inflation-differential rule, higher domestic inflation \(\Rightarrow\) \(\%\Delta e_N<0\) \(\Rightarrow\) a depreciating Euro.

They need not be factually accurate; the marks are for applying a correct driver of exchange rates.

6 · Retirement, interest rates and stock prices 10 points

a) As ageing surplus countries (Germany, Japan) save less or dissave, what happens to the global real interest rate? And to the current account of deficit countries like the US? 5

Framework: the global Saving-Investment cross (L2) — a saving glut in reverse
Mechanism
\(\downarrow\) desired saving ⇒ world Saving curve shifts left ⇒ the global real interest rate rises ⇒ tougher financing for deficit countries ⇒ the US current-account deficit narrows.

This is the saving glut (Bernanke) run backwards: less world saving, higher world \(r\), smaller imbalances.

b) If, in addition, investors rebalance toward bonds and away from stocks to lower risk, what happens to stock prices? And to the equity risk premium? 5

Asset pricing & the ex-ante risk premium (L6)
$$R = r + RP, \qquad R \approx \frac{E}{P} \;\Rightarrow\; RP = \frac{E}{P} - r$$
Two forces, both pushing the same way
(1) lower saving ⇒ higher real rate \(r\) ⇒ higher discount rate ⇒ lower asset prices across the board, stocks included.
(2) a shift away from stocks toward bonds = lower risk tolerance ⇒ a higher required equity risk premium ⇒ \(E/P\) must rise ⇒ stock prices fall further.

So stock prices fall (via both the higher rate and the higher risk premium), and the equity risk premium rises.
