MGE L14 — Review: The Whole Course in One Pass

Macroeconomics in the Global Economy · Prof. Antonio Fatás · the end-of-course review, walked end to end
The last session is Prof. Fatás walking the entire course end to end, in plain language, telling you what actually matters and how the pieces connect. This page follows his path. For each idea: the intuition (in words), the anchor (the equation or framework to hang it on), a concrete example he used, and the exam tell. Built from the review-session transcript.

0. The map (hold this in your head)

The whole course splits into two halves, and he teaches the review as two halves:

  • Long Run — fundamentals (L1–L5): what sets the level and trend of an economy. Living standards, growth, saving/investment, the long-run real interest rate, the nominal anchor.
  • Short Run — business cycles (L6–L14): what makes the economy fluctuate around that trend, and how monetary policy, fiscal policy and exchange rates stabilise it. All powered by one model: IS-LM-FX.

His order through the review: how the economy responds to events → policymakers and central banks → fiscal policy → exchange rates last.

1. Everything is consistent: \(S = I + CA\)

Intuition. A country's books have to balance. People throw around separate-sounding words (trade, deficits, government balance, saving, foreign reserves, sovereign wealth funds), but they are all the same line read different ways. You cannot have one number going one way and another going the other; something always has to give.

Anchor.

\[S = I + CA, \qquad CA = NX + \text{NFP} = -(\text{capital and financial account})\]

and the financing of a deficit = private capital flows + change in foreign reserves.

Example (his: Turkey). Turkey's reserves are falling toward zero. How do you rebuild them? You cannot just "decide" to buy dollars; the dollars have to come from somewhere. Either you sell more abroad (raise the current account, which is hard and needs real economic change), or you attract a capital inflow (for instance by raising interest rates) so foreign dollars arrive, land at the central bank, and become reserves. Something real must move.

Exam tell. T/F items that try to break consistency, e.g. "private capital inflows always equal the current-account deficit" → false (reserves can move, so the financing need not be 100% private).

2. The interest rate that matters is the fundamental real rate

Intuition. When someone says "the interest rate," do not jump to the central bank. Start with the global balance of saving vs investment: the real rate is the price that clears the world's desire to save against the world's desire to invest. The central bank rides on top of that in the short run, but the fundamental number is set globally.

Read it directly. A 10-year inflation-indexed government bond (TIPS) pays you X% above inflation. That X is the real interest rate, no calculation needed.

The story he told. Real rates were about −1% after COVID (people so desperate for a safe asset they would pay to lend to the US government), and have climbed to about +2% now. Why rising?

  • More investment demand (AI, data centres): the investment curve shifts right → real rate up.
  • Big government deficits (e.g. defence): a deficit is borrowing, which is negative saving → less saving → real rate up further.

The subtlety (moving along a curve vs shifting it). "If you pay me more, I save more" is the slope of one person's saving curve. The market question is what happens when the whole saving curve shifts (everyone saves more at once). Those are different questions and give different-looking correlations. Don't confuse moving along the curve with shifting it.

Anchor. World \(r^*\) clears global \(S=I\); Fisher \(r = i - \pi\) links real and nominal.

Exam tell. "The central bank controls long-run real rates" → false; the CB reacts to that number.

3. Myth-busters (his "sounds right, is wrong" slide = T/F bait)

He puts up sentences that sound reasonable and are wrong. Each is a likely True/False trap:

  • "Current-account deficits are bad." No: neither good nor bad. A deficit can reflect weakness or strength (confidence, foreign capital funding productive investment).
  • "Imports reduce GDP." No: the minus sign on imports is accounting (you subtract foreign goods already counted inside C), not a penalty. Importing more means you spent more on foreign goods, that is all.
  • "Long-run real rates are set by the central bank." No: they are set by global saving and investment; the CB uses that number to make decisions.
  • "Everyone should save like Singapore or Norway." Impossible: globally \(S=I\), so for every saver someone must borrow. Not everyone can run a surplus.

4. Growth: where living standards come from

The decomposition (anchor).

\[\frac{GDP}{Pop} = \underbrace{\frac{GDP}{hours}}_{\text{productivity}} \times \underbrace{\frac{hours}{\text{working-age}}}_{\text{employment/effort}} \times \underbrace{\frac{\text{working-age}}{Pop}}_{\text{demographics}}\]

Intuition. Three mechanical levers. To raise income per person you push one of: productivity, how much people work, or your age structure.

How to move each lever:

  • Productivity: education, training, capital, technology, AI. His AI take: genuinely uncertain, he swings optimistic/pessimistic by the day; most countries should use existing technology, not try to invent their own LLM. The easy gains are in catch-up/convergence, not frontier innovation.
  • Employment / hours: bring more people in (raise female labour-force participation), work more (extend retirement age), find younger workers (immigration).
  • Demographics (working-age ÷ population): the only lever that fixes an aging society is immigration (cash baby-bonuses barely move fertility).

The average hides the distribution (his most vivid point). "The US grows about 2%" was true-for-most in the 1950s–70s. But over the last ~30 years the average masks brutal inequality: most people did far worse than 2%, while a tiny few ran far ahead. In 2014 the top 25 hedge-fund managers averaged about $1 billion each in personal income in one year. Same average growth rate, totally different distribution. Always ask: is the average representative?

Institutions. Rule of law and stable institutions strongly correlate with growth, and the data is very strong. Be careful with "we just need a strong leader who knows the long term"; the data does not support crossing out rule of law. The 4 I's (Investment, Institutions, Innovation, Initial conditions) wrap it up; convergence is conditional on having them.

Exam tell. "More working-age people raises GDP per capita" → false (it raises total GDP, not necessarily per-capita). Of C/I/G/NX, investment is the growth engine, not consumption or net exports.

5. The nominal anchor: what a central bank is for

Intuition. A central bank's one job is to tell you what your money will be worth tomorrow. A euro buys a coffee today; what about next year? The CB gives a single signal, one of two kinds:

  • Inflation targeting (the currency loses X% per year against goods), or
  • Exchange-rate targeting (the currency is worth X against another currency, importing its inflation).

These are equivalent ways to anchor; both can be good policy. Give a CB any one nominal variable and the rest are pinned.

The big table (one anchor drives all nominal variables). Inflation, the nominal interest rate, liquidity, and the nominal exchange rate all move together, driven by that anchor. Two things fall out:

  • Real rates are similar across countries (capital flows equalise real returns).
  • No free lunch in FX (UIP): Italy's high interest rate looks attractive, but the lira depreciates by roughly the same amount, so carrying your money there earns you nothing net. High rate ≈ high inflation ≈ depreciating currency.

Anchor. \(r - r_F = \) expected depreciation of the domestic currency (UIP).

6. The short-run engine: IS-LM-FX in four lines

The four lines (what each one is):

1. Spending — IS. Consumers, firms (investment), government, exports. The demand block.

2. Money market — LM. The central bank. Crucial point he repeats: setting the quantity of liquidity and setting the interest rate are the same act. "Reduce liquidity" and "raise rates" are two descriptions of one move.

3. Capital flows — horizontal \(r = r_F\). Money chases the higher return; this drives currency appreciation/depreciation.

4. The limit — vertical at potential GDP. You cannot produce infinitely. To the right of potential you get inflation; to the left, unemployment.

The flagship worked example: the 1992 EMS crisis (he walks every line):

1. Start: Germany and Italy fixed to each other, same interest rate (capital is mobile, so rates must match).

2. German reunification → the German government spends big (deficit) → IS shifts right → German boom → German rates rise.

3. Fixed rate + higher German rates → capital flows Italy → Germany (sell lira, buy marks) → upward pressure on the mark. But it cannot rise (fixed).

4. So the Banca d'Italia must defend the lira: contract the money supply = raise Italian rates (same thing). That forces an Italian recession with no domestic reason for it.

5. Then Germany overheats → German inflation → the Bundesbank raises rates further → Italy must follow even higher → politically and economically unsustainable → the system breaks. Italy floats, the lira drops, exports rise, relief comes.

6. Sweden tried to hold the line by raising rates to 500% annual to make shorting the krona ruinously expensive. It just invited more attacks; Sweden gave up and never joined the euro.

7. Exception — France: the Bundesbank promised unlimited mark support, so markets did not dare attack the franc.

The lesson. The same movie played in Mexico (1994) and Asia (1997). "Never again" → the modern world floats. Pegs survive only where the benefit beats this pain: tiny open economies (Singapore, Hong Kong) and oil exporters (whose exports are priced in USD, so they want to move with the dollar).

Anchor. The Impossible Trinity: pick 2 of 3 — fixed exchange rate, independent monetary policy, free capital mobility.

Exam tell. A peg-defence / Impossible-Trinity question is his favourite set piece. "Sterilised intervention changes the balance-sheet size" → false (composition only).

7. The stock market: is it a bubble?

Intuition. Never judge from the price or the P/E alone. A high P/E is only expensive relative to interest rates. Put price, earnings and the interest rate together into the equity premium.

Anchor.

\[R = r + RP, \qquad R \approx \frac{E}{P} \;\Rightarrow\; RP = \frac{E}{P} - r\]

A low risk premium flags an expensive market.

His read. Today resembles 1998. Whether that is a bubble depends on the future (maybe technology justifies the valuation). At least the metric stops you fooling yourself with "it always goes up." Remember: it is the real rate that matters here, not inflation per se.

8. Fiscal policy and debt: who actually pays

The key framing (his, and a gap on most cheat sheets). The money is already spent. The only real question is who pays, and there are exactly three places to find someone:

1. Taxpayers (raise taxes / cut spending).

2. Bondholders (default or restructuring — same thing; you pass the bill to them).

3. Everyone holding the currency — create sudden inflation, an inflation tax that quietly wipes out the value of nominal assets (your bank balance buys less).

So "we'll just be fine" is wrong; someone is chosen to pay, which makes debt fundamentally political.

The one calculation in the course.

\[\frac{\text{required primary surplus}}{GDP} = (r - g)\times\frac{Debt}{GDP}\]

Intuition: shut the government for a year. Debt grows at \(r\), GDP grows at \(g\). If \(r>g\) the ratio drifts up and you need a surplus to offset it; if \(g>r\) you can run deficits forever and the ratio still falls. It is the \(r-g\) gap, not the debt level, that decides.

Japan vs Greece. Japan carries enormous debt at low rates because it is held domestically (it even ran ads telling women that men who buy government bonds are attractive). Convince your own people and you can survive a high debt.

Self-fulfilling crisis. The day markets lose confidence, rates jump, the required surplus explodes, and the crisis arrives. Only a credible lender of last resort breaks the loop.

9. The yield curve: the central bank's summary

  • Short end = today's policy rate, set by the Taylor rule (the CB reacts to growth, unemployment, inflation).
  • Long end = the fundamental real rate (global S-I) + expected inflation.
  • The CB's obsession is anchoring inflation expectations (the "break-even" inflation rate priced in the market). If 10-year expected inflation drifts above target, credibility is slipping and the CB will raise rates even before actual inflation shows up. That is why central bankers talk about "anchoring expectations" constantly.

10. Exchange rates: long run vs short run

Long run (the real rate). Driven by productivity (Balassa-Samuelson). A country whose productivity is catching up must get more expensive in real terms; that shows up either as nominal appreciation or as higher inflation. Singapore engineered steady nominal appreciation as its productivity rose.

The recipe he taught: productivity differences tell you what the real exchange rate must do; your central bank's behaviour (relative inflation) tells you the price ratio; combine the two to back out the missing piece, the nominal exchange rate.

Short run. Pure volatility, driven by interest-rate signals and central-bank talk; markets overshoot and run far with stories.

11. How to pass (his exam hints)

  • It is a reasoning exam, not a calculation exam. The logic is the grade. Use the frameworks from class.
  • No facts or numbers are asked. Don't memorise data.
  • The structure mirrors the past papers on the website ("almost the same, not quite").
  • One clear idea per question. Do not feel the need to fill the space; over-writing can hurt you. He is looking for one precise insight.
  • The only sum you ever compute is the debt-sustainability formula (\((r-g)\times Debt/GDP\)).
