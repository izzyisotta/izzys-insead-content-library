$$\boxed{\ \frac{\text{Required primary surplus}}{\text{GDP}} = (r - g)\times\frac{\text{Debt}}{\text{GDP}}\ }$$
\(r\), \(g\) both nominal (or both real). Example: \(r=4\%,\ g=2\%,\ \tfrac{Debt}{GDP}=120\% \Rightarrow (0.02)(1.2)=2.4\%\). If \(g>r\) the required surplus is negative — you can run deficits forever and debt/GDP still falls.

Part I — The Long Run (Fundamentals)

1 · Macroeconomic Variables & National Accounting

This lecture gives you the measuring stick for the whole course: what GDP is, what it misses, and how to strip out price effects so numbers are comparable across countries and years.

What GDP is

Gross Domestic Product = the market value of final goods and services produced within a country's borders over a period (it's a flow, like income, not a stock like wealth).

$$\text{GDP} = P_A Q_A + P_B Q_B + \dots$$

Only final goods count (or equivalently, sum the value added = revenues − intermediate inputs) to avoid double-counting. Three ways to measure it all give (roughly) the same number: Production = Expenditure ≈ Income.

GDP vs GNI. GDP is activity inside the borders. Gross National Income adds what residents earn abroad minus what foreigners earn here:
$$\text{GNI} = \text{GDP} + \text{NFP}$$
NFP (net factor payments) = remittances, repatriated profits, government transfers. Big for Ireland (profits flow out → GNI < GDP) and the Philippines (remittances flow in).

Is GDP the right measure of progress?

Perspective 1 — Business

GDP = market size, demand, income. You want it as comprehensive and accurate as possible.

Perspective 2 — Society

GDP measures materialistic wellbeing. Related to welfare, but not equal to it.

What GDP misses: non-market activity, environmental depreciation (it's a flow, ignores wealth), and the gap between "dollars of activity" and "things we actually value." Hence "beyond GDP" measures: Bhutan's Gross National Happiness, the Human Development Index.

Comparing GDP: quantities, not prices

GDP mixes prices and quantities. To compare, you must fix prices. Two cases:

ComparisonNo adjustmentAdjusted for prices
Two countriesGDP converted to USD at market exchange rates → market size / powerPPP-adjusted GDP (international prices) → real activity / living standards
Two yearsNominal GDPReal GDP (base-year prices)

What "PPP" actually means, and how it's calculated

The problem. To compare living standards you must convert every country's GDP into one currency. If you use the market exchange rate, you understate poor countries — because a haircut, a bus ride or a meal is far cheaper there, so the same dollar buys more real stuff. Purchasing-Power-Parity (PPP) fixes this by valuing everyone's output at a common set of international prices instead of at market rates.

How it's calculated: price an identical basket of goods in each country, then

PPP exchange rate
$$e_{PPP} = \frac{\text{price of the basket in local currency}}{\text{price of the same basket in USD}}\quad\Rightarrow\quad \text{GDP}_{PPP} = \frac{\text{nominal GDP (local)}}{e_{PPP}}$$
Worked example — the Big Mac as a one-good basket A Big Mac costs 22,900 pesos in Colombia and $6.01 in the US. The PPP rate is \(e_{PPP} = 22{,}900 / 6.01 \approx 3{,}810\) pesos per dollar. But the market rate is ~4,018 pesos per dollar. Because the market rate (4,018) is weaker than PPP (3,810), converting Colombian GDP at the market rate makes Colombia look poorer than it really is. Converting at PPP corrects that — so China's GDP is bigger at PPP than at market rates, which is why PPP is the right lens for living standards and market rates for "market size / power."
The relationship you'll use constantly
$$\text{Nominal GDP growth} = \text{Real GDP growth} + \text{Inflation}$$

Inflation is just the growth rate of the price index. Any nominal variable can be "realised" by stripping inflation out:

Real interest rate
$$r = i - \pi$$

The nominal rate is your return in money; the real rate is your return in goods. If real is positive you can buy more stuff at the end. (Japan 2005: nominal 0.0%, inflation −1.4%, so real = +1.4%.)

Real exchange rate
$$e_R = \frac{e_N\, P}{P_F}$$

The real exchange rate = how many foreign goods one domestic good buys. Big Mac example: a Colombian Big Mac costs $5.70 at market rates vs $6.01 in the US, so \(e_R = 5.70/6.01 = 0.95\) — Colombia is slightly "cheaper" in real terms.

Exam lens. L1 ideas surface as T/F traps: "imports reduce GDP" (false — see L2 identity), "a country with a higher GDP is richer" (no — that's size, not GDP per capita), and the constant real-vs-nominal distinction (initial price level vs inflation; nominal vs real rates). Memorise nominal = real + inflation in all its forms (GDP, interest rates, exchange rates).

2 · Saving, Investment, the Current Account & the Real Interest Rate

The most important lecture in Part I. It builds the identity S = I + CA and then turns saving and investment into a supply-and-demand market whose price is the real interest rate. This market reappears in the ZLB (L9), fiscal debt (L11) and the yield curve (L10).

Building the identity

The whole thing comes from two facts: (a) income that isn't consumed or taxed is saved, and (b) the same output can be written as expenditure, \(\text{GDP}=C+I+G+NX\). Watch what cancels at each step.

  1. \(S_{priv} = (\text{GDP}+\text{NFP}) - T - C\)Private saving = national income (GNI = GDP + NFP) minus what the private sector pays in tax \(T\) and spends on consumption \(C\).
  2. \(S_{gov} = T - G\)Government saving = its revenue \(T\) minus its spending \(G\).
  3. \(S = S_{priv}+S_{gov} = (\text{GDP}+\text{NFP}) - C - G\)Add the two. The \(+T\) and \(-T\) cancel — taxes are just a transfer inside the country, so national saving doesn't depend on them.
  4. \(= (C+I+G+NX+\text{NFP}) - C - G\)Substitute the expenditure identity \(\text{GDP}=C+I+G+NX\) for GDP.
  5. \(= I + (NX+\text{NFP})\)Now \(C\) and \(G\) cancel too, leaving investment plus the two external flows.
  6. \(\boxed{\,S = I + CA\,}\qquad CA \equiv NX+\text{NFP}\)Name the external part the current account. Saving is funnelled into either domestic investment or lending abroad (the CA).

For the whole world there is no "abroad", so CA = 0 and S = I. For one country, the gap between its saving and investment is its current account.

🎙 In class — intuition behind the identity

Why this is the most important line in the early course. "Keep this identity in mind — Saving = Investment + Current Account — because I will use it constantly when we talk about policy, currencies, and financial crises. This is an accounting identity — it must always be true. No assumptions, no model." Because it's an identity (not a behavioural model), it holds in every scenario — so any exam question about trade balances or capital flows can be cracked by rearranging it: \(CA = S - I\).

Why "− imports" is NOT "imports hurt GDP" (he says this trips people up). You survey households, they report buying a German BMW — you've wrongly logged it in \(C\). The "\(-M\)" simply strips it back out so you don't count German production as domestic. It's a double-counting correction, not a claim that trade is bad.

Why a surplus/deficit is not virtue/weakness. Germany runs a surplus because it's ageing — old people save, and there's little point building factories with no young workers (S > I) → it lends abroad. The US runs a deficit because it has productive opportunities that foreign savers want to fund (I > S). A deficit is a country borrowing to invest, not being robbed — whether it's a problem depends only on whether the borrowing funds productive returns and keeps Debt/GDP stable.

Balance of payments — a "balanced imbalance". Every transaction is double-recorded (you pay for foreign goods by handing over an asset), so the two halves of the balance of payments are mirror images:
$$\underbrace{\text{Current Account}}_{=\,S-I}\; =\; \underbrace{\text{Capital and Financial Account}}_{\text{net lending abroad}}$$
A current-account surplus (S > I) must equal a capital outflow: if you earn more than you spend, the difference is parked abroad. The sign convention trips people up, so here it is as a table:
TransactionDirectionSignWorked example
Foreigners buy your assets, or you sell foreign assetsCapital inflow−A US fund buys €1bn of German bonds → inflow for Germany (−)
You buy foreign assets, or foreigners sell yoursCapital outflow+Germany buys €1bn of US Treasuries → outflow (+)
Central bank accumulates FX reservesCapital outflow+China buys $472bn of US assets to hold as reserves → outflow (+)
Worked example — China 2013 Trade surplus \(NX=+\$238\text{bn}\) and a private capital inflow of \(+\$287\text{bn}\) (≈ $500bn arriving). To balance, the central bank had to send even more out: it accumulated \(+\$472\text{bn}\) of reserves (a public outflow). So China was a net lender to the world even while its private sector pulled capital in — the central bank more than offset it. (This is the "true if" case: combine a CA surplus with a private inflow and you must be piling up reserves.)
Splitting the same identity by sector shows who is saving or borrowing:
$$(S_{priv}-I_{priv}) + (S_{gov}-I_{gov}) = CA$$
e.g. a government deficit (\(S_{gov} \lt I_{gov}\)) need not cause a capital outflow — it depends on whether the private sector's surplus covers it.

The real interest rate as the price of funds

  • Saving = the supply of current funds. The reward for converting today's consumption into tomorrow's is the real rate ⟹ saving rises with \(r\) (upward-sloping).
  • Investment = the demand for current funds. Projects are funded if their return beats the rate ⟹ investment falls with \(r\) (downward-sloping).

Put them together: the real interest rate is the price that clears the market.

Adding the current account: world = savers + borrowers

World equilibrium requires Σ surpluses = Σ deficits. The single world real rate is what makes that hold. Countries whose Saving curve sits to the right of Investment at that rate run a surplus (lend abroad); the reverse run a deficit (borrow).

The Saving Glut

Bernanke's (2005) explanation for falling global real rates since the mid-1990s: a rise in desired saving in some countries (China, oil exporters, ageing surplus economies) shifts the world Saving curve right ⟹ the world real rate falls and global imbalances widen (bigger surpluses there, bigger deficits — chiefly the US — elsewhere).

Exam lens. This is the single richest exam topic in Part I. Recurring T/F items: "an increase in government debt always leads to a capital outflow" (false — depends on private saving), "private capital inflows always equal the current-account deficit" (false — reserves can move), "the only way to combine a CA surplus with a private capital inflow is reserve accumulation" (true). Drill the chain: more saving → lower world r → bigger imbalances; and remember long-run real rates are set by global S & I, not by the central bank (a listed "myth").
Don't confuse a fixed exchange rate with trade balance — there is no mechanical link. Trade balances are about S vs I, full stop.

3 · Growth — why some countries are rich

Long-run living standards = GDP per capita. The trick is to write it as a chain of four ratios where each denominator cancels the next numerator — so it's an identity (always true), but each factor is something you can reason about separately. Everything here is a headcount or a total, not an age:

Pop = total population (everyone) · Working-age Pop = number of people aged 15–64 · Workers = number of people actually employed · Hours = total hours they work in a year.
  1. \(\dfrac{\text{GDP}}{\text{Pop}} = \dfrac{\text{GDP}}{\text{Hours}} \times \dfrac{\text{Hours}}{\text{Workers}} \times \dfrac{\text{Workers}}{\text{Working-age Pop}} \times \dfrac{\text{Working-age Pop}}{\text{Pop}}\)Multiply four fractions. "Hours", "Workers" and "Working-age Pop" each appear once on top and once on the bottom, so they cancel — the right side collapses back to GDP/Pop. The identity holds by construction.
  2. \(\underbrace{\tfrac{\text{GDP}}{\text{Pop}}}_{\text{living standard}} = \underbrace{\tfrac{\text{GDP}}{\text{Hours}}}_{\substack{\text{productivity}\\ \text{(output per hour)}}} \times \underbrace{\tfrac{\text{Hours}}{\text{Workers}}}_{\substack{\text{effort}\\ \text{(hours per worker)}}} \times \underbrace{\tfrac{\text{Workers}}{\text{Working-age Pop}}}_{\substack{\text{labour market}\\ \text{(\% employed)}}} \times \underbrace{\tfrac{\text{Working-age Pop}}{\text{Pop}}}_{\substack{\text{demographics}\\ \text{(\% aged 15-64)}}}\)Read each factor as a lever. The last two are both shares of people: what fraction of working-age people have jobs, and what fraction of everyone is of working age.
  • Productivity (GDP/hour) — the big driver near the frontier; depends on innovation.
  • Effort (hours) — historically falls as countries get richer (people buy leisure), levelling at different points.
  • Labour market — participation & employment; large gaps by gender and age (e.g. Japan "defying gravity": employment rising even as the working-age population shrinks).
  • Demographics — the age structure; ageing is a headwind to GDP per capita.

Innovation & the frontier

US real GDP per hour has grown ~1.99%/yr on average since 1889 — remarkably stable, but with a clear slowdown after 1975 (bar the internet years). Take-away: trend growth is not guaranteed; it's the product of continuous effort to innovate. General-purpose technologies (electricity, internet, maybe AI) are what deliver step-changes.

Convergence & the Solow logic

The convergence dynamic (Solow, 1956). As a country approaches the frontier (US productivity), its growth rate declines — it nears its steady state. Steady states differ across countries, so convergence "levels off" at different points.
  • Advanced economies: strong convergence in early post-war decades, then flattening.
  • Emerging Asia: very fast convergence (Korea, Singapore, China, Vietnam).
  • Latin America: slow / no convergence — the contrast with Asia is the puzzle L4 answers.
🎙 In class — making the decomposition click
  • "France isn't poor — it just works less." Per capita: Singapore ~$157k, US ~$90k, France ~$66k. But per hour worked they're almost identical: ~$99 / $103 / $105. So the per-capita gap is almost entirely hours + employment share, not productivity. Kills the instinct that lower GDP/capita = less productive.
  • Convergence = imitation. A catch-up country "looks up, imitates, buys the technology, invites the factories, then shadows the frontier" — and growth slows as it nears the frontier (Korea 6–7% tapering; China "5, then 4, then 3, then 2"). The puzzle: Brazil/Mexico had Korea-level income in the 1960s and didn't converge → that's what institutions/human capital (L4) explain. Convergence is a possibility, not a guarantee.
  • Japan = the demographics worked example. Working-age population fell ~12m, yet the labour force grew ~4m — via later retirement and a big rise in female participation. Proof that policy levers can offset demographic decline (but with limits: "you can't keep asking people to work until 85").
  • Method signal: "Don't memorize numbers — see patterns." The exam rewards reading the mechanism, not recalling country figures.
Exam lens. Pure qualitative. Be ready to reason about ageing ("can higher employment of older workers offset ageing's drag on GDP per capita?" — yes), and to use the decomposition to locate which term is moving. No Solow algebra is ever asked — only the logic of convergence.

4 · Growth & Country Analysis — the 4Is

The framework for diagnosing any country's growth (and the backbone of your group assignment). Potential exists for many countries; only some realise it. Why? The 4 Is.

The 4 IsWhat it doesMarker
InnovationIncentives to innovate (IP rights) shift the technology frontier. Main growth force in developed countries.US, Japan, Germany
Initial conditionsDistance from the frontier = potential to catch up. Poorer ⟹ can grow faster (if it converges).Europe post-WWII, China
InvestmentConvergence requires building capital — physical, human, knowledge, managerial. "Miracles" invest >25% of GDP.Korea ~35%; steady-state US/Germany ~18%

Empirics from the slides: investment-rate vs growth is strongly positive; the World Bank's six Worldwide Governance Indicators correlate tightly with GDP per capita. Institutional reform looks different at different development stages ("the Wall" — institutions must rise as income rises; China 1980→2021, Poland 1990→2021 climb the diagonal).

🎙 In class — the 4Is, made intuitive
  • Growth ≈ a simple sum. "GDP growth = productivity growth + population growth. If Nigeria grows its workforce at 2.6% and productivity at ~1%, expect ~3.5–4% growth. You don't need anything fancy — it's the sum of the two numbers." This is the exact forecasting method he wants in country analysis.
  • Investment is accounting, not a miracle — but with diminishing returns. China invests ~40% of GDP → ~8% growth: build factories, workers get more capital, productivity rises. But the marginal return on capital falls once you already have a lot, so frontier economies (US/Germany ~18%) shouldn't copy China's 40% — there's less left to gain.
  • Institutions, quotably: "There is no rich country with weak institutions. There is no poor country with great institutions." Sequencing matters: Norway built institutions then found oil (rich); Venezuela found oil then destroyed institutions (poor). Institutions cause income, not just correlate.
  • The four questions for any country: what are its initial conditions? how is it investing? what are its institutions? is it generating or absorbing innovation? "Those four questions will get you far."
Exam lens. If a country-analysis question appears, structure the answer explicitly around the 4Is — that is the framework Fatás wants. Investment is "the main component that matters" for long-run GDP per capita (a past T/F: "the two components that matter most are consumption and net exports" → false, it's investment).

3. Adopters must be able to keep the profitSmith / North & Thomas — property rights and the profit motiveHis Institutions lever. A different incentive from #2: an institutional one.

Read down the table and it's a sequence, not a checklist: an idea has to exist → it has to be the cheapest way to produce (or no one invests in it) → and institutions have to let adopters keep the profit. His Innovation → Investment → Institutions are three links in that one chain, which is precisely why they feel cross-overy as separate boxes.

What my framework adds over the checklist (and why it's the better exam structure):

  • The conditions must hold simultaneously. Any one missing stalls innovation — that's why combinations of these factors existed for centuries across civilisations but ignited growth only when all three lined up at once.
  • The binding constraint shifts over time. Britain industrialised first not because it alone had ideas or institutions (much of Europe did) but because it uniquely satisfied #2: its high wage-to-energy/capital ratio made the early, inefficient machines worth adopting. Today #1 (ideas — internet access + adoption from the frontier) and #2 (capital and energy are cheap vs labour almost everywhere) are largely satisfied globally, so for stagnant countries the binding constraint is #3 — institutions.
  • This derives Fatás's quote rather than asserting it. "No rich country with weak institutions" is, in my framework, the conclusion that institutions are the binding condition now — not just a correlation.
Exam move. On a country-analysis question, don't just list the 4 Is — diagnose which condition binds (today: almost always institutions/incentives), then justify it with the coexistence logic above. That turns a descriptive list into a causal argument, which is the higher-scoring answer.

5 · Money & Central Banks

What money is, how it's created, and the central-bank balance sheet you'll use again in L6, L7 and L9.

Money does three jobs: medium of exchange (a liquid asset), unit of account (prices are quoted in it), store of value (moves value to the future).

From gold to fiat to digital

Under the gold standard, banknotes were backed 1-for-1 by gold; money supply = size of the central bank balance sheet. Fiat money swaps gold for financial assets and adds something new: a central-bank commitment to a stable value of the unit of account — a nominal anchor (an inflation target). Today money is a mix of paper and electronic, with commercial banks as intermediaries.

The two aggregates

$$\text{Monetary Base} = \text{Currency} + \text{Reserves} \qquad \text{Money Supply} = \text{Currency} + \text{Deposits}$$

The base is what the central bank controls (≈ its balance sheet). The money supply is liquidity as the private sector sees it. They're linked by the money multiplier \(m\): \(\text{Money Supply} = m \times \text{Base}\). Here \(C\) = currency, \(D\) = bank deposits, \(R\) = reserves; the behavioural ratios are \(C/D\) (how much cash people hold vs deposits) and \(R/D\) (how much banks keep in reserve vs deposits).

  1. \(m = \dfrac{\text{Money Supply}}{\text{Base}} = \dfrac{C+D}{C+R}\)Multiplier = supply ÷ base, by definition.
  2. \(= \dfrac{C/D + 1}{C/D + R/D}\)Divide top and bottom by \(D\). Since banks hold only a fraction in reserve (\(R \lt D\), so \(R/D \lt 1\)), this is \(>1\) — banks "multiply" the base by lending deposits out.
Worked example If people hold \(C/D=0.1\) and banks keep \(R/D=0.1\): \(m = \dfrac{0.1+1}{0.1+0.1} = \dfrac{1.1}{0.2} = 5.5\). So €1 of base supports €5.50 of money supply. In a panic both ratios jump (people grab cash, banks hoard reserves) → \(m\) collapses toward 1 → the money supply shrinks even with the base unchanged (this is the L9 Depression story).

Note the hinge: the central bank's liability "Reserves" is the commercial bank's asset "Reserves at central bank". The two balance sheets lock together at that line — which is exactly what QE and FX intervention move.

This balance sheet does two jobs, and a third set of players is now trying to disrupt both.

1. Settlements — the central bank is the "bank of banks." Every commercial bank holds a reserve account at the central bank, and they pay each other by moving reserves on the central bank's books. Worked example: you bank with Bank 1 and pay £100 to a friend at Bank 2.

  • Bank 1's reserve account at the central bank falls by £100; Bank 2's rises by £100.
  • The central bank's total liabilities don't change — the £100 just moves from one bank's row to another's. Across millions of daily payments, banks only settle the net amount they owe each other.
  • Why it matters: this is what reserves are for, and why the central bank sits at the centre of the payment system. It never has to "find" the money — it just debits one account and credits another.

2. Liquidity creation — the "unlimited power." The central bank can create reserves from nothing by buying an asset. It buys £100 of securities and pays by simply crediting the seller's reserve account:

  • Its balance sheet grows on both sides: assets (securities) +100, liabilities (reserves) +100. It didn't need the money first — it conjured the reserves on its own books.
  • Done in size during a crisis this is QE; done routinely it's ordinary open-market operations. Either way the mechanism is identical.
  • The catch: this power is "unlimited" only in the bank's own currency (it can always credit more reserves). It is not a free lunch — overused, the extra liquidity just shows up as inflation (long-run neutrality). Contrast defending a weak currency, which needs FX reserves, and those genuinely can run out.

3. Fintech & crypto — three different challenges to this system. They are often lumped together but attack different layers:

  • New payment rails (M-Pesa, UPI, Pix) — strip out the slow, expensive intermediaries between banks. But they still ultimately settle in central-bank money, so they challenge the banks, not the currency itself.
  • A rival unit of account (Bitcoin) — tries to be a new money with a hard-capped supply. But a fixed supply is not a stable value: price = supply ∩ demand, and demand swings violently, so Bitcoin is volatile. A real nominal anchor is a commitment to keep value stable (an inflation target), not a quantity cap — which is exactly why Bitcoin fails as a unit of account.
  • Stablecoins = "narrow banks" — they hold 100% safe, liquid assets (e.g. Treasuries) against every token they issue. Because they're fully backed, they take no credit risk and don't lend, so they create no money multiplier. Economically they're "boring" (like a currency board); the real novelty is regulatory: dollar access plus zero-cost reserves.

How much money is needed? Velocity & the long-run anchor

US nominal GDP (~$30tn) is far larger than the money supply (M1 ≈ $18tn — M1 is "narrow money": currency in circulation + checkable deposits, i.e. the \(C+D\) measure from above). How does $18tn of money fund $30tn of transactions? Each dollar gets spent more than once a year — that's velocity \(V\) (how many times the money stock turns over). This gives the quantity equation, and money growth follows from it:

(You don't need the full M0/M1/M2 ladder for this course — Fatás uses only two levels: the monetary base the central bank controls, and the money supply = M1 the multiplier links it to.)

  1. \(M \times V = P \times Y\)Quantity equation: money × turnover = price level × real output = nominal GDP. (\(V = PY/M = 30/18 \approx 1.67\) for the US.)
  2. \(\%\Delta M + \%\Delta V = \%\Delta P + \%\Delta Y\)Take growth rates of both sides (the growth of a product is the sum of the growths).
  3. \(\%\Delta M \approx \underbrace{\%\Delta P}_{\text{inflation}} + \underbrace{\%\Delta Y}_{\text{real growth}}\)Assume velocity is roughly stable (\(\%\Delta V\approx 0\)). So money growth = inflation + real GDP growth.

So the central bank forecasts real growth (demographics + capital + technology) and chooses inflation. This is the punchline of Part I's money story:

Long-run neutrality. In the long run money is neutral — it doesn't move real variables. It only anchors nominal ones through inflation. Inflation is ultimately a monetary phenomenon (cross-country: money growth ↔ inflation; hyperinflations are fiat-money events).
🎙 In class — why these formulae hold
  • "Add a zero" — why money is neutral. "Overnight I take every number with a Euro attached and add a zero (×10). Your salary, the price of coffee — all change by 10, and we're back where we started. These numbers are 100% arbitrary." That's why excess liquidity can't make you richer; it can only show up as inflation.
  • The coffee analogy — what \(\%\Delta M = \text{real growth} + \text{inflation}\) means. "How many more coffees will you have next year? I forecast that (real growth). Inflation? I don't forecast it — I decide it. I choose 2%." So the central bank forecasts the real part and chooses the inflation part.
  • Balance sheets: "transforming assets, not creating value out of thin air." Assets always equal liabilities. The central bank hands over a claim in exchange for an asset — money isn't conjured, it's a transformation. So QE is not "printing free money."
  • Fixed supply ≠ stable value. Bitcoin caps supply, yet US inflation measured in Bitcoin swung ~+300% then −80% — because price = supply ∩ demand, and demand is volatile. A nominal anchor is a commitment, not a quantity cap.
  • Stablecoin = narrow bank = currency board. Economically a stablecoin is "boring" — safe assets backing tokens, exactly like the Hong Kong dollar's currency board. The excitement is regulatory arbitrage (dollar access + zero-cost reserves), not new economics.
  • Judge central banks by outcomes. "We don't judge them on how much liquidity they created — did they deliver 2%? That's it."

The most-tested example, on the balance sheet. "When China's central bank buys US dollars, does it add or remove domestic liquidity?" Put it on the T-account and the answer is unambiguous:

6 · Business Cycles

The bridge from Part I to Part II: fluctuations of actual GDP around potential GDP (the long-run trend from L3–L4).

Measuring slack

Output gap
$$\text{Output gap} = 100\times\frac{\text{Actual} - \text{Potential}}{\text{Potential}}$$

The output gap is tightly (negatively) correlated with the unemployment rate — both measure slack. To define slack you need a benchmark for "normal": the natural rate of unemployment (structural unemployment — frictional job-to-job churn; zero unemployment is impossible). Deviations from the natural rate are cyclical. Get potential output or the natural rate wrong and your policy is wrong.

🎙 In class — how to read a cycle
  • The indicators disagree — don't expect them to line up. "When people tell you there's a recession, don't expect every indicator to send the same message." Europe in COVID: GDP collapsed but unemployment barely moved, because governments paid wages directly. So always say which measure of slack you're using.
  • His mental model of the cycle: recessions are V-shaped (sudden, sharp, short); expansions are a slow inverted-U. "All the uncertainty is at the turning point — between recessions, nothing much happens." The exam-relevant question is always "are we about to hit a wall?"

Phases & why recessions happen

Cycle = expansion → peak → recession → trough. "Technical" recession = two consecutive quarters of negative growth (a crude measure). Historically recessions have become shorter and milder, expansions longer ("The Great Moderation"), with 2008 and 2020 as reminders that bad luck happens. Two causes of downturns:

  1. Exogenous shocks transmitted by propagation mechanisms (a pandemic is the perfect example).
  2. Accumulated imbalances that eventually need correction — often visible in financial markets.

Financial markets, bubbles & the risk premium

Greenspan's "irrational exuberance": imbalances can persist far longer than you'd think. The lecture gives a precise way to judge whether stocks are "expensive":

Required return & ex-ante risk premium
$$R = r + RP, \qquad R \approx \tfrac{E}{P} \;\Rightarrow\; RP = \tfrac{E}{P} - r$$

Logic: required stock return = riskless real rate \(r\) + risk premium \(RP\); ex-ante real stock return ≈ inverse of the P/E ratio (E/P). So a high P/E means low expected returns — but that's only a "bubble" if the risk premium is unusually low relative to the (real) interest rate.

The key dynamic: hold RP constant. An unexpected fall in real interest rates re-prices stocks up (a capital gain now) and lowers expected future returns. A run of surprise rate cuts therefore delivers high realised returns even as expected returns decline — exactly the post-2009 P/E expansion.
Exam lens. Recurring 10-mark question: "is the stock market a bubble?" Right answer uses RP = E/P − r, and stresses it's the real rate that drives the discount rate — inflation per se doesn't, and "there's nothing special about 4% inflation." Myths to refute: "high P/E = bubble", "past returns predict future returns."

7 · A Model of Business Cycles — IS-LM-FX

This is the model. Master drawing and shifting it and most of Part II is automatic. It has three blocks; in this lecture we assume no inflation, so \(r = i\) and we just call the real exchange rate \(e\).

The three blocks

Block 1 — Aggregate Demand (becomes the IS curve)
$$\text{GDP} = C(\underset{+\ -\ -\ +}{\text{GDP},r,T,W}) + I(\underset{-}{r}) + G + NX(\underset{-\ -\ +}{e,\text{GDP},\text{GDP}_F})$$
C rises with income/wealth, falls with taxes & r. I falls with r. NX falls with a stronger e & higher domestic income, rises with foreign income.
Block 2 — Money market (becomes the LM curve)
$$\frac{M}{P} = L(\underset{+}{\text{GDP}},\underset{-}{\,i\,})$$
Money demand rises with transactions (GDP), falls with the nominal rate (opportunity cost).
Block 3 — Exchange rate / capital flows
$$r = r_F$$
Capital chases returns; arbitrage equalises real rates across countries.

Why IS slopes down and LM slopes up

Intuition — the IS curve (the goods market)
  • What it is: the combinations of interest rate \(r\) and output GDP at which total demand equals output — the goods market clears.
  • Why lower \(r\) means higher GDP: a lower rate makes borrowing cheaper, so firms invest more (\(I\)) and households consume more (\(C\)); in an open economy a lower rate also weakens the currency, so net exports rise. More demand ⟹ higher GDP.
  • So IS slopes down: lower \(r\) ⟹ higher GDP.
  • It shifts right when anything raises demand at a given rate: ↑G, tax cuts, higher confidence/wealth, stronger foreign demand, or a weaker currency.
Intuition — the LM equation \(M/P = L(\text{GDP}, i)\)
  • Left side \(M/P\) = the real money supply. \(M\) is how much money the central bank has put out; divide by the price level \(P\) because what matters isn't the number of notes, it's their purchasing power. £100 means something very different if a coffee costs £2 vs £20. So \(M/P\) is "real money".
  • Right side \(L(\text{GDP}, i)\) = money demand. \(L\) is for liquidity — how much people want to hold as cash / instant-access rather than tied up in bonds and stocks.
  • \(+\) GDP: a busier economy means more transactions, so people need more cash on hand to grease them. More income and spending ⟹ more money demanded.
  • \(-\) i (the one people miss): holding cash earns nothing, so the interest you give up by not holding a bond is the opportunity cost of cash. High rates (say 8%) make idle cash painful ⟹ people economise and hold less; near 0% there's no penalty ⟹ they hold loads. Money demand falls as \(i\) rises.
  • Why LM slopes up: if GDP rises, money demand rises (the \(+\)) — but the supply \(M/P\) is fixed. Something must choke demand back to match: the rate \(i\) rises until holding cash is painful enough to bring demand back down to the fixed supply. Higher GDP ⟹ higher \(i\) ⟹ upward-sloping LM.

In one line: the money market clears when the cash people want (more when busy, less when rates are high) equals the real cash that exists — so as the economy grows, rates must climb to keep that balance.

The money-market block can be drawn on its own (real money M/P on the x-axis, nominal rate i on y): money supply is vertical (set by the central bank), money demand slopes down. An increase in M shifts supply right ⟹ lower i. Equivalently the central bank just sets the short-term rate.

The full diagram

Intuition — the third line: the open economy (Mundell-Fleming)
  • The dashed line is the world interest rate \(r_F\), and it's flat because a small open economy is a price-taker on global capital: you face the world return, you can't set it.
  • Capital chases yield. If domestic \(r > r_F\), your assets pay more ⟹ foreign money floods in (buys your bonds ⟹ buys your currency ⟹ it appreciates). If \(r < r_F\), money flees ⟹ currency depreciates.
  • So the IS∩LM dot can't durably sit off the line. Land above \(r_F\): inflow → appreciation → exports dearer → NX falls → IS drifts left → back to \(r_F\). Land below: the reverse. The exchange rate is the adjustment valve that ties you to the world rate.
  • Punchline — the policy trilemma: with free capital flows you cannot also have BOTH a fixed exchange rate AND an independent interest rate. Pick two. This diagram is that squeeze drawn out.
The 4-step routine (use it every single time):
  1. Shift the curve that the shock hits (see triggers below).
  2. Compare r to \(r_F\) → capital inflow (r>\(r_F\)) or outflow (r<\(r_F\)) → appreciation or depreciation.
  3. Adjust NX if the exchange rate moved (appreciation ⟹ NX falls ⟹ IS shifts back).
  4. Repeat until r returns to \(r_F\).

8 · Inflation & Central Banks — the supply side & the Taylor Rule

L7 assumed no inflation. Now we add the supply side and let prices adjust, which is how the model reaches its long-run equilibrium — and gives us the Taylor Rule.

The nominal anchor (long run)

A central bank picks one nominal anchor, and that choice implicitly fixes all other nominal variables (nominal rates, the nominal exchange rate):

  1. Inflation targeting (commit to a target, be accountable). Most common; targets cluster near 2% with a buffer above zero to avoid deflation.
  2. Exchange-rate targeting (fix the currency — implicitly imports the anchor currency's inflation).
  3. Money targeting (announce a money-supply path; rare today; Bitcoin is a crude version).
  4. Commodity (gold standard).

Evidence: higher money growth ⟹ higher inflation ⟹ a depreciating nominal exchange rate (Italy vs Germany, 1960-98: Italy's higher money growth → higher inflation → 4.9%/yr lira depreciation). Central-bank independence keeps inflation low and stable.

Potential output & the vertical long-run supply curve

In the long run GDP is pinned by factors of production (labour, capital, productivity) — a vertical Long-Run Aggregate Supply at potential GDP. Add it to IS-LM:

Adjustment mechanism: below potential ⟹ prices fall ⟹ real money M/P rises ⟹ LM shifts right ⟹ economy returns to potential (and vice versa above). This is how the long-run equilibrium is reached.

Why do prices fall below potential? Below potential means slack — unemployed workers and idle capacity, i.e. excess supply of labour and goods. With more sellers than buyers, firms can't push prices up and workers can't bid wages up; the competition for scarce demand drags wages and prices down. Lower P mechanically raises real money M/P, which is what shifts LM right and pulls output back to potential. Above potential the mirror runs: an overheating economy (excess demand, labour shortages) bids wages and prices up, so M/P falls and LM shifts left.

🎙 In class — two anchors for the money side
  • Why money demand \(L(\text{GDP},i)\) falls with the interest rate (the ATM/wallet picture): at 0% you withdraw €200 and carry it; at 10% cash has a real opportunity cost, so you withdraw €10 at a time and keep your money earning. High rates ⟹ you hold less cash ⟹ money demand falls.
  • The CB's single objective: in every case its goal is to sit on the vertical potential-output line. Balanced growth ⟹ shift LM right and hold the rate constant; an overheating boom ⟹ hold LM and let the rate rise; a recession ⟹ push LM right / cut the rate. The rate change is the by-product of aiming at potential.

Setting the right rate — the Taylor Rule

The central bank has one job: keep inflation on target and output at potential. The interest rate is its lever, and Taylor (1993) wrote the lever rule down so cleanly that, in Fatás's words, "give me your inflation rate, give me a measure of the [output] gap, and I can tell you what the central bank should do." Build it in four moves:

  1. Start at the neutral rate \(r^{*}+\pi\).The setting that neither heats nor cools when inflation is on target and output is at potential. \(r^{*}\) is the natural real rate (Taylor used 2%); adding \(\pi\) just expresses it in nominal terms.
  2. Lean against the inflation gap: add \(\phi_{\pi}\,(\pi-\pi^{*})\).Inflation above target ⟹ push rates up. \(\phi_\pi\) is how hard the bank leans (Taylor: 0.5).
  3. Lean against the output gap: add \(\phi_{y}\,(y-\bar{y})\).Output below potential (negative gap, a recession) ⟹ pull rates down. \(\phi_y\) is the lean (Taylor: 0.5).
  4. The subtlety: to actually cool inflation you must raise the real rate \((i-\pi)\), i.e. out-pace inflation.So the response to inflation has to exceed one. Match inflation only one-for-one and the real rate doesn't move, and you have done nothing. This is why \(\pi\) appears in the baseline and in the gap term (the Taylor principle, below).
The Taylor Rule (Taylor 1993)
$$i = \underbrace{r^{*} + \pi}_{\text{neutral rate}} + \phi_{\pi}\,(\pi - \pi^{*}) + \phi_{y}\,(y - \bar{y})$$
\((y-\bar y)\) = the output gap, (actual − potential)/potential (positive = overheating, negative = recession). \(\phi_\pi,\ \phi_y\) = the bank's response coefficients. Worked cases use Taylor's \(r^{*}=2\%\), \(\pi^{*}=2\%\) (neutral rate 4%), \(\phi_\pi=\phi_y=0.5\).
The #1 exam trap (and the Taylor principle). A 1-point rise in inflation lifts the rate by 1.5 points, not 0.5: once through the standalone \(\pi\) in the neutral rate, and again through \(0.5(\pi-\pi^{*})\). Fatás flagged this as the classic mistake (a student audibly swore when the penny dropped). It is deliberate: the total inflation response \((1+\phi_\pi)\) must exceed one, or a jump in inflation actually lowers the real rate and inflation spirals. As he put it, "central banks need to be tough and fast… you have to claim to be crazy so that the world is very safe."
It's a reaction function, not an identity. Nothing forces \(i\) to equal the right-hand side; it is a guideline a bank chooses to follow and can deliberately deviate from. So where do the coefficients come from? (1) Descriptively: Taylor reverse-engineered \(\phi=0.5\) from how the Fed actually behaved, and the rule then tracks central-bank behaviour well over 1982–2008 (the Great Moderation: low, stable inflation and output); before 1982 banks were "too soft" relative to it. (2) Normatively: a bank picks \(\phi_\pi,\phi_y\) to match its mandate, a larger \(\phi_\pi\) leaning harder on inflation, a larger \(\phi_y\) harder on output (Taylor's 1999 revision raised \(\phi_y\) to 1.0). The one non-negotiable is the Taylor principle above.
Overheating — inflation above target \(\pi=3\%\), gap \(=0\): \(i = \underbrace{2+3}_{\text{neutral}} + 0.5(3-2) + 0.5(0) = \mathbf{5.5\%}\). The actual real rate \(r = i-\pi = 2.5\%\) now sits above the neutral \(r^{*}=2\%\): that 0.5pp above neutral is the brake (you out-pace inflation to cool things). Keep \(r\) (the real rate policy delivers) and \(r^{*}\) (the fixed neutral real rate in the formula) distinct.
Recession — output below potential \(\pi=1\%\), gap \(=-4\%\): \(i = \underbrace{2+1}_{\text{neutral}} + 0.5(1-2) + 0.5(-4) = 3 - 0.5 - 2 = \mathbf{0.5\%}\). Pile on a deeper gap or deflation and the rule wants a negative rate, which the zero lower bound forbids (L9).
Exam lens. You will usually reason with the rule, not plug numbers into it: e.g. "inflation is above target but the BoJ didn't hike, why?" (a negative output gap, or expectations un-anchoring downward). And remember the Phillips curve shifts when an anchor (the natural rate or the inflation target) moves: the setup for L10's disinflation.

9 · Monetary Policy & the Zero Lower Bound

What happens when the Taylor Rule says the rate should be negative but cash sets a floor at zero.

The ZLB trap

In a deep recession all three Taylor inputs point down: low natural real rate \(r^*\) (excess saving over investment — the L2 market), low/negative inflation, big negative output gap. Desired nominal rate < 0. But cash pays 0%, so nominal rates can't go negative. Rates are stuck too high, demand stays weak, more deflation ⟹ an even more negative desired rate. A trap.

The maths — how the rule goes negative Take a deep crisis with all three inputs down: the natural rate has fallen to \(r^{*}=0\) (excess saving over investment), deflation \(\pi=-1\%\), target \(\pi^{*}=2\%\), output gap \(=-7\%\). Plug into Taylor with \(\phi_\pi=\phi_y=0.5\): $$i = \underbrace{0+(-1)}_{r^{*}+\pi} + 0.5\,(-1-2) + 0.5\,(-7) = -1 - 1.5 - 3.5 = \mathbf{-6\%}.$$ The rule wants \(-6\%\), but cash pays 0% so the nominal rate is floored: \(i = \max(0,\,-6\%) = 0\). Policy is stuck a full 6 points too tight.
Why it's a trap (self-reinforcing). Stuck at 0 while the rule needs \(-6\%\) ⟹ demand stays weak ⟹ the output gap widens and deflation deepens (the inputs get worse) ⟹ feed the new numbers back in and the rule now wants, say, \(-8\%\) ⟹ still floored at 0. Every year the prescribed rate sinks further below a floor you cannot cross. (Fatás: "next year the rule says go to −6, I can't, I'm still stuck at zero.")
🎙 In class — the ZLB, drilled
  • The four ways the Taylor rule turns negative (he ran this as a Q&A — be able to list them): low/negative inflation; inflation below target; a deeply negative output gap; a low natural rate \(r^*\). Pile them up in a deep crisis and the prescribed rate is below zero.
  • Why zero is a hard floor (cash under the mattress): physical cash is a costless store that always pays exactly 0% nominal. Push deposit rates meaningfully below zero and money just leaves the banks for cash — so the central bank can't.
  • The trap is self-reinforcing: "stuck at zero → GDP falls → inflation falls → next year the rule says go to −6, I can't, I'm still stuck at zero. Every year you're in a worse place."
  • Maps onto Assignment 2. Of the six countries, three avoided the ZLB: India & Brazil (high inflation targets keep the Taylor rate positive) and China (no big 2009 crisis); US, Euro, Japan hit it. Expect to classify a country by whether its rule went negative and why.

Why deflation makes it worse: deflation raises the real value of debt ⟹ redistributes from borrowers to savers ⟹ deters consumption and can cause default, which spreads through banks. And in a panic everyone wants liquidity → bank runs (banks borrow short, lend long; a healthy bank can become illiquid; only the central bank can supply the liquidity).

10 · Managing High Inflation

The mirror image of L9: how central banks bring inflation down, the role of expectations, and supply shocks.

Disinflation & the shifting Phillips curve

Each short-run Phillips curve corresponds to one credible inflation target. Lowering inflation (Volcker, 1979: target <4%) means tight money ⟹ moving down along the short-run curve: high unemployment for several years. Once the new low-inflation policy is credible, expectations and contracts adjust, the short-run curve shifts down, and unemployment returns to the natural rate — but inflation is permanently lower.

Why it's costly: contracts (esp. wages) are nominal and sticky; expectations adjust slowly, at a speed set by policy credibility. Nominal rates spike, then fall as inflation falls, bringing the real rate back to normal. 2021-24 was the exception — a near-costless disinflation (inflation fell with little unemployment cost), which the slides flag as a puzzle (well-anchored expectations + a largely supply-driven spike unwinding).

🎙 In class — the shape of disinflation, and a trap
  • Disinflation is a "drift then jump", not a smooth slide. You drift down the short-run Phillips curve through painful high-unemployment years, then — the moment credibility flips and wage/price-setters accept the new target — you jump vertically to the new anchor. The jump is expectations re-anchoring in one negotiation round.
  • The "two very different statements" trap: a low-inflation country has low interest rates (a level fact); but to cut inflation you raise rates (an action). Students conflate these — keep them apart.
  • Oil-shock passthrough ∝ (lack of) credibility. The same oil shock barely lifts inflation where the central bank is credible, but spirals where it isn't — which is why anchored economies (and now even much of Latin America) ride out energy shocks far better than in the 1970s.
  • Inversion ≠ guaranteed recession. 2023's inverted yield curve produced a soft landing, not a crash — credible hikes killed inflation without a recession. Everyone who read "inversion = certain crisis" got it wrong that time.

Supply shocks & stagflation

An oil/energy shock for an importer = a leftward shift of LRAS (real income falls). Result: stagflation — falling GDP and rising inflation simultaneously. The 1970s lesson: countries that let inflation run (UK) then needed deeper recessions to wring it out; those that contained it (Germany) fared better — with little difference in GDP cost on average.

The reasoning, step by step. ① Energy is an input to almost everything, so a price spike for an importer destroys real productive capacity: the economy can make less at full employment, so potential output falls and LRAS shifts left. ② Why the price level rises: that same cost increase is passed straight through into output prices (energy feeds into nearly every good), which is cost-push inflation; equivalently, fewer goods are now available while spending is roughly unchanged, so more money chases fewer goods and \(P\) is bid up. ③ The central bank has not changed the nominal money stock \(M\), so a higher \(P\) mechanically cuts real balances \(M/P\) — and falling real money is a leftward shift of LM (less real liquidity ⟹ higher \(r\)). Output gets squeezed down toward the lower potential while prices are higher: recession and inflation at once, i.e. stagflation.

Policy rule for supply shocks: central banks should not react if inflation expectations are anchored — but post-COVID showed anchoring can't be assumed.

The Yield Curve

One of the most heavily-examined topics. Almost every yield-curve question is the same task in disguise: something changes → say whether the curve shifts up/down, steepens, flattens, or inverts, and why. Get the one idea below, then drill the scenario library.

🎙 Intuition A long rate is just the average of the short rates the market expects over the bond's life, plus a small premium for locking your money up. So the shape of the curve is a forecast of where short rates are going. Every answer comes from one question: "what does this news do to the expected path of future short rates (and to inflation)?"
The one idea: a long rate is an average of expected short rates
You have money to park for 2 years. Two ways to do it:
  1. Buy one 2-year bond, or
  2. Buy a 1-year bond now, then roll it into another 1-year bond next year.
These must pay roughly the same. If one were clearly better, money would pile into it until they evened out. So the 2-year rate is simply the average of today's 1-year rate and next year's expected 1-year rate.
With numbers Today's 1-year rate = 4%; the market expects next year's 1-year rate = 6%. Then the 2-year rate ≈ (4% + 6%) / 2 = 5%. The curve slopes up, not for its own sake, but because the market expects rates to rise.
Stretch the same logic to ten years: the 10-year yield = the average of all ten expected 1-year rates, plus a small term premium for locking up longer.
Formally, for an n-year bond: \( i_t^{(n)} \approx \tfrac{1}{n}\big(\mathbb{E}[i_t]+\mathbb{E}[i_{t+1}]+\dots+\mathbb{E}[i_{t+n-1}]\big) + \phi^{(n)} \). That is the same sentence written out (φ = the term premium).
The punchline: the slope is the market's forecast of future short rates. Upward (long > short) means rates are expected to rise. Inverted (long < short) means rates are expected to fall, and the central bank only cuts when a recession is coming or it has already beaten inflation. That one link drives every scenario below.
Same idea, the macro angle: the long rate has three parts.
  1. The equilibrium long-run real rate (global S & I, from L2: growth, demographics, technology).
  2. Expected inflation over the bond's life (central-bank credibility).
  3. A term premium (extra yield for duration / supply risk).
This is just the average above re-split: each expected future short rate is an expected real rate + expected inflation, so averaging them gives these three parts. Short rates today are set by the CB via the Taylor Rule (high in booms, low in slumps).
Worked decomposition A 4% 10-year yield ≈ 1.5% long-run real rate + 2.5% expected inflation (+ ~0 term premium). If the CB credibly promises to cut inflation, the expected-inflation piece falls to ~1.5%, so the 10y yield drops to ~3% even if today's short rate hasn't moved. That single move is the engine behind half the scenarios below.

Reading the slope

Upward (normal). Market expects short rates roughly stable-to-rising; positive term premium. Read: economy fine, no cuts priced.
Flat. Market unsure / late-cycle turning point. Expected future short rates ≈ today's. Often the step before an inversion.
Inverted (short > long). Market expects future short rates below today's. Two stories: (a) recession coming → CB will cut; (b) credible disinflation → real + inflation expectations fall. Inversion ≠ guaranteed recession (2023 → soft landing).

2 · Credible disinflation (hawk hikes now)
Inverts
CB hikes short rate to prove resolve ⟹ short ↑; market believes inflation will fall ⟹ long ↓ ⟹ inversion.
Why: tight policy today + lower expected inflation tomorrow = high short, low long. e.g. Volcker 1980-81; the 2022-23 hiking cycle.
3 · Hike expectations, long end anchored
Flattens (short end ↑)
Market prices near-term hikes but expects the rate to return to neutral ⟹ short ↑, long ≈ same ⟹ flattens.
Why: the long rate is anchored to the long-run neutral rate + inflation target; a temporary hiking cycle barely moves it. Contrast with #2 (there, long falls because expected inflation drops).
4 · Cuts priced ahead (recession fear)
Inverts (long end ↓)
Market expects the CB to cut soon ⟹ average future short rate falls ⟹ long ↓ below short ⟹ inverts. When the cuts actually arrive, the short end drops too and the curve steepens back to normal.
Why: the long yield is an average of the (now-lower) expected path. The classic recession-signal inversion. But it can also be the soft-landing version of #2.
5 · QE — CB buys long bonds
Flattens (long end ↓)
CB buys long bonds ⟹ higher demand ⟹ higher price ⟹ lower long yield, compressing the term premium ⟹ flattens. The tool for when short rates are stuck at zero.
Why: the bond-supply channel (below). With the short end pinned at the ZLB, the only way to ease is to pull the long end down directly. e.g. BoJ yield-curve control; Fed/ECB post-2008.
6 · Heavy long-dated issuance / fiscal-risk shock
Steepens (long end ↑)
Government floods the market with long bonds (or investors demand more for debt risk) ⟹ higher supply / risk premium ⟹ long yields rise ⟹ steepens. Partly undoes QE.
Why: same long-end move as #1 but via supply / term premium, not expected inflation — the distinction examiners test. e.g. 2025 rising-yield, high-debt environment (L11).
The bond-supply / term-premium channel (powers #5 and #6). Long-term rates are also a price set by the supply of and demand for long bonds. QE = the CB buying long bonds ⟹ higher demand ⟹ higher price ⟹ lower long yield ⟹ flatter curve. Symmetrically, if the government issues more long-dated debt (lengthens its maturity), that extra supply ⟹ lower bond prices ⟹ higher long yields ⟹ it partly undoes QE. So QE and debt-management policy push on the same lever in opposite directions.
Exam lens. Every yield-curve question reduces to: what happens to the expected path of future short rates, to expected inflation, and to the term premium? Then read the slope off the identity. The four moves and their triggers:
  • Steepen — long end up: inflation surprise pre-CB (#1), heavy issuance / fiscal risk (#6).
  • Flatten — short up & long anchored (#3), or QE pulling the long end down (#5).
  • Invert — credible disinflation (#2) or cuts/recession priced ahead (#4); both say "future short rates < today's".
  • Shift up/down — a parallel change in the whole expected-rate path.
Two traps the marker plants: (i) reading inversion as automatic recession — it can be a credible-disinflation soft landing (2023); (ii) confusing the two ways the long end can rise — expected inflation (#1) vs supply/term premium (#6). If a question pits QE against the government lengthening its debt maturity, that's the bond-supply channel. "Disinflation always causes unemployment" is a listed myth — 2021-24 is your counterexample.

11 · Fiscal Policy & Debt Sustainability

The home of the one formula you actually compute. Two roles for fiscal policy: long-run (political — size of government, who's taxed) and short-run (a stabiliser, like monetary policy — deficits in recessions).

The balances

$$\text{Primary balance} = T - G - \text{Transfers}$$ $$\text{Overall (financial) balance} = \text{Primary balance} - \text{Interest payments}$$ $$\text{Interest payments} = r_{nominal}\times \text{Debt}$$

Budgets move with the cycle via automatic stabilisers (taxes fall, unemployment benefits rise in downturns). To see discretionary policy, strip these out → the structural balance. (Europe 2010-14: structural balances show real austerity on top of the recession.)

Sustainability — the key formula

🎙 Intuition: what this formula actually says A primary surplus is your budget before interest: tax revenue minus all spending except debt interest. The whole question is how big it must be to stop your debt-to-GDP ratio drifting up.

Picture a tug-of-war on the ratio each year. Interest (r) makes the debt grow and pulls the ratio up; growth (g) makes the GDP underneath it grow and pulls the ratio down. The net pull is the gap (r − g):

  • r > g: interest wins, so debt/GDP snowballs on its own and you must run a surplus to fight it.
  • r < g: growth wins, so the ratio falls by itself and you can even run deficits.

It is a treadmill: (r − g) is how fast the belt drags you back, Debt/GDP is how heavy your backpack is, and the primary surplus is how hard you must run to stay in place. So the surplus you need = the drift (r − g) applied to the whole debt pile you carry. That one sentence is the boxed formula below.

Governments do not need to repay debt — they need to keep it on a non-explosive path. The test: how big a primary surplus keeps the Debt/GDP ratio constant? Here's where the formula comes from (the "shut the government down for a year" thought experiment):

  1. Imagine zero spending and zero taxes (primary balance = 0).Isolate what debt dynamics do on their own.
  2. Numerator: \(\text{Debt}\) grows at the interest rate \(r\).You can't pay the interest, so you roll it into new debt → debt compounds at \(r\).
  3. Denominator: \(\text{GDP}\) grows at \(g\).The economy grows at its nominal growth rate \(g\).
  4. So \(\tfrac{\text{Debt}}{\text{GDP}}\) drifts at rate \((r-g)\).Ratio rises if \(r>g\), falls if \(g>r\), flat if \(r=g\) — with no government action at all.
  5. \(\dfrac{\text{Required primary surplus}}{\text{GDP}} = (r-g)\times\dfrac{\text{Debt}}{\text{GDP}}\)The surplus you must run is exactly enough to offset that \((r-g)\) drift, scaled by how much debt you carry.
Case A — r > g (must run a surplus) \(r=4\%,\ g=2\%,\ \tfrac{Debt}{GDP}=120\%\): required surplus \(=(0.04-0.02)\times1.2 = \mathbf{+2.4\%}\) of GDP just to hold the ratio flat.
Case B — g > r (deficits forever!) \(r=2\%,\ g=4\%,\ \tfrac{Debt}{GDP}=120\%\): required surplus \(=(0.02-0.04)\times1.2 = \mathbf{-2.4\%}\) — i.e. you can run a 2.4% deficit every year and Debt/GDP still falls.

The decisive insight: sustainability depends on (r − g). If growth exceeds the interest rate (g > r), the required surplus is negative — a government can run primary deficits forever and Debt/GDP still falls (e.g. China, Japan, US in the 2025 slide table, where nominal g > nominal r). High debt is manageable as long as rates stay low relative to growth.

🎙 In class — debt myths & colour

Why "debt burdens the next generation" is only half-true: (a) the next generation inherits the capital and institutions the debt funded; (b) thanks to growth (\(g\)) their incomes are higher, so the same €100 is easier to repay; (c) for domestically-held debt they inherit the asset and the liability together — it nets to zero. "World debt is at a record level — but so are world assets."

What a rich government's budget actually is: mostly pensions + healthcare + income support, with a comparatively small army — "an insurance company with an army."

But \(r\) is not exogenous — it carries a sovereign risk premium. The rate a government pays = a safe rate + a country risk premium that reflects default/political risk. A downgrade, political instability, or loss of credibility raises that premium ⟹ raises \(r\) ⟹ raises the required primary surplus (and can be self-fulfilling: fear of default raises rates, which makes default more likely). This is the bridge between "default is just redistribution" and why markets still punish risky borrowers — and why two countries with the same Debt/GDP can face very different sustainability (compare Italy's ~2.8% rate with Japan's ~0.2% in the slide table).
Government ≠ business. A company default means its business model failed — a real loss of wealth. A government default is a redistribution (who pays for spending already done) — resources aren't destroyed (though the ensuing crisis can destroy some). This is why "high debt = high default risk" is a myth.
Exam lens. This is the computational question (2019, 2021, 2022). Memorise (r−g)·Debt/GDP and be able to compute the extra effort when r rises faster than g. Watch real vs nominal: inflation raises both the nominal rate and nominal growth, so it's roughly neutral for sustainability unless rates rise faster than inflation. Myths to refute: "we must pay back government debt", "debt is always a burden on the next generation."

12 · Exchange Rates

Three theories, layered: long-run arbitrage (PPP), the productivity correction, and the short-run asset-price view. Pool its three question types (PPP / real-vs-nominal at 18, UIP at 12, defending a peg at 14) and exchange rates is the most-tested area of the course. The single most-tested archetype, though, is IS-LM-FX (26 past questions, in L7-L8).

Theory 1 — Law of One Price / PPP (long run)

First, the two definitions you must hold separate:
  • Nominal exchange rate \(e_N\) = how much foreign currency one unit of domestic currency buys. (Convention used throughout: a rise in \(e_N\) = the domestic currency appreciates.)
  • Real exchange rate \(e_R\) = how many foreign goods one domestic good buys: \(e_R = \dfrac{e_N\,P}{P_F}\), with \(P\) the domestic price level and \(P_F\) the foreign one. \(e_R<1\) means your goods are cheaper than abroad.

The Law of One Price says perfect arbitrage equalises prices everywhere, so a domestic good and a foreign good are worth the same — i.e. \(e_R=1\). Set \(e_R=1\) and solve for the nominal rate:

  1. \(e_R = \dfrac{e_N\,P}{P_F}\)Definition of the real exchange rate.
  2. \(e_R = 1\)Law of One Price: arbitrage makes domestic and foreign goods worth the same.
  3. \(\dfrac{e_N\,P}{P_F}=1 \;\Rightarrow\; e_N = \dfrac{P_F}{P}\)Rearrange. The nominal rate is just the ratio of the two price levels.
Worked example If a basket costs \(P=100\) at home and \(P_F=120\) abroad, then \(e_N = 120/100 = 1.2\) — one unit of home currency buys 1.2 units of foreign. If home inflation now runs hotter and \(P\) rises to 110 (foreign unchanged), \(e_N\) falls to \(120/110 = 1.09\): higher domestic inflation ⟹ the currency depreciates.

Reality: PPP doesn't hold exactly — taxes, trade barriers, transport costs, and arbitrage only works for easily-traded goods (an iPhone, not a haircut). Hence "modified PPP." But over the long run nominal rates do track PPP estimates (with big short-run volatility).

Theory 2 — Productivity & the real exchange rate

The clever bit. Tradables' prices are equalised by arbitrage. Within a country, traded and non-traded goods prices are linked through relative productivity. So:

  • Rich/high-productivity countries have higher price levels (a higher real exchange rate) — confirmed in the data (price level vs GDP per capita slopes up).
  • If a country's tradables productivity rises (convergence), wages and non-tradables prices rise ⟹ the real exchange rate appreciates.
  • That real appreciation happens either via higher inflation (if currency is fixed, e.g. Spain in the euro — "good" inflation) or via nominal appreciation (if floating) — the central bank chooses which (Japan 1960-96: appreciated nominally from 360 to ~110 yen/$).

If two countries have similar productivity growth, the real exchange rate stays put, and the nominal rate just absorbs the inflation gap. The derivation is one line:

  1. \(e_N = \dfrac{P_F}{P}\)Start from PPP (real rate constant ⟹ \(e_R\) fixed).
  2. \(\%\Delta e_N = \%\Delta P_F - \%\Delta P = \pi_F - \pi_{domestic}\)Take growth rates of a ratio: the growth of \(P_F/P\) is foreign inflation minus domestic inflation.
Worked example Domestic inflation \(\pi=10\%\), foreign \(\pi_F=2\%\) ⟹ \(\%\Delta e_N = 2-10 = -8\%\): the home currency depreciates ~8% per year. (This is the Italy-vs-Germany story: Italy's higher inflation → the lira fell ~4.9%/yr vs the mark.)

Theory 3 — Currencies as assets (short run)

Two facts: exchange rates are extremely volatile, and FX volume is huge (capital flows dominate). So treat currencies as assets:

The single most important — and counter-intuitive — point. A sudden, unexpected increase in interest rates ⟹ capital inflow ⟹ immediate appreciation. BUT this does not mean high-interest-rate countries have strong currencies. The opposite: arbitrage (r = r_F) means high-rate countries (usually high-inflation) have depreciating currencies. Distinguish "news of an increase in rates" (⟹ appreciation now) from "a high level of rates" (⟹ expected depreciation).
🎙 In class — three sharp exchange-rate points
  • Why FX is so volatile (the scale): ~$10 trillion is traded per day vs world GDP of ~$120 trillion per year — a single day moves ~5% of annual world GDP. Capital flows, not trade, set the price, and they react instantly to news.
  • High rates under a peg = a risk premium, "works until it doesn't." Under a fixed peg (Lebanon, Hong Kong) domestic rates should equal the anchor's. Persistently higher rates aren't free money — they're compensation for the risk the peg breaks. Chase the high rate and you're fine… until the peg snaps and you lose your capital.
  • Singapore: the exchange rate IS the policy tool. The MAS doesn't set an interest rate — it commits to a path (slope) of currency appreciation. Steepening the slope (faster appreciation) is "exactly equivalent to the ECB raising interest rates" — same tightening, different lever; it flattens the slope as growth slows.

Overshooting — adjustment to a monetary expansion

🎙 Intuition: why the rate overshoots The whole puzzle comes from one friction: prices are sticky in the short run. In the long run money is neutral, so +10% money means +10% prices and a currency 10% weaker (PPP). But prices cannot move on day one, so on impact something else has to absorb the shock, and the one price free to jump instantly is the exchange rate. It doesn't just move to its new long-run level, it jumps past it and then walks back. The reason it overshoots is a no-arbitrage squeeze on interest rates, below.
Why overshooting happens, step by step
  1. Money supply rises permanently.
  2. Long-run anchor (PPP): eventually prices rise in proportion and the currency settles at a new, weaker level. That weaker level is the destination.
  3. Short-run friction: prices are sticky, so real money balances (M/P) rise, which pushes the domestic interest rate down below the foreign rate.
  4. No-arbitrage (UIP): investors will only hold the now lower-yielding currency if they expect it to appreciate from here (the expected gain offsets the lower interest).
  5. Reconcile 2 and 4: the currency must be expected to appreciate, yet its destination is weaker. The only way both can be true is if it first overshoots (jumps below the new long-run level on impact), then appreciates back up to it as prices rise and the interest rate normalises.
The one-line version: sticky prices force the exchange rate to do all the short-run adjusting; the low interest rate means it must be expected to appreciate; the only path that both lands weaker and is expected to appreciate is to undershoot the currency's value (overshoot the depreciation) and recover. Bigger price stickiness ⟹ bigger overshoot.
Can the theory explain the data? Works: long-run depreciation of the high-inflation currency (USD trend); episodes of fast US growth/high rates → USD appreciation (early 80s). Fails: FX is far too volatile; the rate–differential link is weak (today's spot rate predicts better than the interest differential); long one-way trends shouldn't exist under the theory. Honest conclusion: exchange-rate "theories" are mostly ex-post rationalisation.
Exam lens. The richest exam topic — appears almost every year. The "priced-in" trick: an expected rate hike that's already priced ⟹ no currency move (the Russia/rouble and Indonesia cases). The productivity→RER→nominal chain (a fast-growing economy can still depreciate if its inflation is high enough — a past T/F: true). And "a country fixing its rate implicitly follows the other's policies" (true) but bilateral trade need not balance (false).

13 · The State of the Global Economy

Not a new model — a synthesis applying the whole course to current events. Useful because Q2–Q6 are always hung on real headlines.

  • The arc: QE era → global pandemic → inflation spike → disinflation → trade war → two military conflicts. Policy lesson learned from 2008's austerity mistake: in 2020, go big and smart (rates to zero, QE, large deficits, subsidies to keep workers attached). Result: fastest-ever labour-market healing, inflation controlled without recession.
  • Tariffs & trade. Anchor everything on the identity: Trade balance = Saving − Investment = (minus) Capital balance. Tariffs don't fix a trade deficit driven by S < I. "Welfare comes from imports, not exports — exports are how you pay for imports." Retaliation → autarky → inefficiency. (Data: high-tariff Egypt still runs a deficit; zero-tariff Singapore a huge surplus — because it's about S−I.)
  • USD dominance & a multipolar world. The dollar still dominates reserves and global bond markets; the renminbi is rising slowly. US policy shifts + China's rise → a multipolar world without a "benevolent" superpower → coercion as economic policy, strategic independence, industrial policy → higher costs and uncertainty.
  • Energy shocks (recap of L10): importers poorer, exporters richer, inflation up everywhere; central banks shouldn't react to supply shocks if expectations are anchored.
  • Optimism vs risk: recovery, record employment, controlled inflation, AI contributing to US growth — against expensive stock markets (low equity risk premium) and fiscal fragility.
Exam lens. Expect a tariff/trade-war prompt. Always reduce it to S−I=CA. Don't claim imports "reduce GDP" (accounting illusion — a listed myth) or that a trade deficit is "always bad."

The 6-step reading of any shock (Session 14):
  1. The shock shifts IS or LM ⟹ new r and GDP.
  2. New r vs world r ⟹ capital inflow (r above) or outflow (r below).
  3. Inflow ⟹ appreciation; outflow ⟹ depreciation.
  4. Floating: appreciation ⟹ ↓NX ⟹ lower demand (depreciation the opposite).
  5. Fixed: no appreciation/depreciation allowed ⟹ monetary policy must intervene.
  6. Position vs potential output ⟹ higher or lower inflation.

EMS Crisis 1992/93 — the two-country set-piece

A fixed-rate system (currencies in a narrow band). German reunification ⟹ German fiscal expansion ⟹ German CB fears inflation ⟹ contractionary German monetary policy ⟹ high German rates. To hold the peg, the Bank of Italy is forced to raise rates too (importing German policy — the Impossible Trinity in action), deepening Italy's recession until the system broke (Italy floats/devalues; bands widen to 15%).

The four nominal-anchor / FX truths to carry in

  1. The nominal anchor determines all nominal variables (inflation, nominal rates, nominal exchange rate).
  2. Long-run real rates are set by global S & I, not the central bank.
  3. Long-run nominal exchange-rate moves ≈ inflation differentials (adjusted for productivity).
  4. Short-run exchange rates are asset prices driven by news about interest rates.

Mechanism-chain drills

The exam rewards causal chains. Cover the right-hand side and reproduce each. (All flow from the IS-LM-FX engine.)

Fiscal expansion, floating FX: ↑G → IS right → r↑ → capital inflow → currency appreciates → NX↓ → output effect offset (crowding out via the exchange rate).
Monetary expansion, floating FX: ↑M → r↓ → capital outflow → depreciation → NX↑ → GDP↑ (powerful).
Monetary expansion, fixed FX: ↑M → r↓ → outflow / depreciation pressure → CB must sell reserves & cut M back → no effect (policy powerless).
Saving glut: ↑desired saving abroad → world S right → world real rate↓ → bigger surpluses there + bigger deficits (US) → wider global imbalances.
Disinflation: tighten money → real rate↑ → unemployment↑ (move down PC₁) → policy becomes credible → expectations & contracts adjust → PC shifts down → unemployment returns to natural, inflation permanently lower.
Energy shock (importer): oil↑ → real income↓ → LRAS left → GDP↓ & inflation↑ (stagflation) → CB holds if expectations anchored.
Unexpected rate hike (FX): surprise i↑ → capital inflow → immediate appreciation → thereafter higher rates predict depreciation (arbitrage). If the hike was priced in → no move.
Debt dynamics: inflation↑ → nominal rate↑ and nominal growth↑ → roughly neutral for sustainability unless r rises faster than g → required surplus = (r−g)·Debt/GDP.

Glossary & the examiner's "myths" list

Core terms

  • NFP — net factor payments (income from abroad − paid abroad).
  • Current account — NX + NFP = S − I.
  • Capital outflow — buying foreign assets / ↑reserves (sign +).
  • Real interest rate — nominal − inflation; return in goods.
  • PPP — purchasing-power-parity; prices equalised by arbitrage.
  • Monetary base — C + R (CB-controlled). Money supply — C + D.
  • Money multiplier — supply/base; collapses in bank runs.
  • Nominal anchor — what fixes long-run inflation (target / peg).
  • Output gap — (actual − potential)/potential; ↔ unemployment.
  • Natural rate — structural unemployment at full employment.
  • Primary balance — budget balance excluding interest.
  • Structural balance — balance stripped of automatic stabilisers.
  • ZLB — zero lower bound on nominal rates (cash pays 0%).
  • QE / QT — buying / selling securities to expand / shrink the base.

Myths Fatás flags (be ready to refute)

  • Current-account deficits are always bad. (No.)
  • Imports reduce GDP. (Accounting illusion.)
  • Long-run real rates are controlled by the central bank. (No — global S & I.)
  • Everyone should save like Singapore/Norway. (Can't all surplus.)
  • Living standards come from "resources". (Productivity + institutions.)
  • Export-led growth is required. (No.)
  • High stock prices / high P/E = bubble. (Depends on RP vs r.)
  • Past returns ⟹ high future returns. (Often the opposite.)
  • We must pay back government debt. (Only keep it sustainable.)
  • High debt = high default chance. (Default is redistribution.)
  • QE is printing money / financing the deficit / always inflationary.
  • Disinflation always causes unemployment. (2021-24 didn't.)
  • A weak (or strong) currency is always good. (Neither.)
