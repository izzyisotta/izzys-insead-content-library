IS-LM-FX: policy & the exchange rate (fixed vs flexible) 26 on past papers

When you see it: A fiscal or monetary policy change (often plus a regime: fixed vs flexible FX), asking about rates, the currency, net exports, GDP — or cross-country spillovers.
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. Run the 4-step routine: shift the curve the shock hits → compare r to r_F → capital flow → appreciation/depreciation → NX adjusts → repeat until r = r_F.
  2. FLEXIBLE FX: fiscal expansion is crowded out via the currency (↑G → r>r_F → appreciation → NX falls → ΔGDP ≈ 0); monetary policy is potent (↓r → depreciation → NX up → GDP up).
  3. FIXED FX: the reverse — fiscal policy is potent (no currency offset; the CB must supply money to hold the peg), monetary policy is impotent (the rate is tied to r_F).
  4. Spillovers: a partner's fiscal expansion, and your trade exposure, transmit via rates and the exchange rate.
Worked model answer
2023 Q3a — Brazil, larger-than-expected fiscal stimulus, central bank inactive, flexible FX: capital flows, the real, net exports?
↑G shifts IS right, pushing the domestic rate above r_F. That draws capital IN, so the real APPRECIATES. The stronger currency makes exports dearer and imports cheaper, so net exports FALL — crowding out part of the stimulus through the exchange rate. Under flexible FX with the central bank inactive, much of the fiscal boost leaks out via the currency.
Traps
  • Mixing up the regimes: fiscal works under FIXED, monetary works under FLEXIBLE.
  • Forgetting the second-round NX offset that drags the rate back to r_F.

Yield-curve reaction to news 18 on past papers

When you see it: News hits — an inflation surprise, a central-bank speech, a fiscal plan, a new central-bank chair — and you must say how the yield curve moves (with a credibility or inversion twist).
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. Decompose: the short end ≈ the expected path of the policy rate; the long end ≈ average expected future short rates + expected inflation (+ a term premium).
  2. Map the news to expectations: does it change the expected policy path (short end), expected inflation (long end), or both?
  3. Credibility decides the long end: a credible disinflation pulls the long end DOWN; a non-credible or pro-inflation signal pushes it UP even with the short rate unchanged.
  4. If asked: an inversion (long < short) means the market expects rate cuts / a recession ahead.
Worked model answer
2014 Q3 — a surprise, credible cut in the inflation target: what happens to the yield curve?
The short end ticks up first (the bank tightens now to hit the lower target), but the dominant move is the LONG end falling: a credible lower target cuts expected inflation, so long yields (real + expected inflation) drop and the curve flattens/pivots down. If the announcement is NOT credible, the long end won't move — expected inflation stays put — and the policy fails.
Traps
  • Treating the whole curve as shifting in parallel — separate the short end (policy path) from the long end (expected inflation).
  • Ignoring credibility: the long-end move is entirely about whether the market believes the central bank.

Real vs nominal exchange rates, PPP & convergence 18 on past papers

When you see it: Real and nominal exchange rates moving in opposite directions; a PPP gap; a fast-converging or high-productivity country's currency.
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. Definitions: nominal e_N (foreign currency per domestic unit), real e_R = e_N·P/P_F. Growth form: %Δe_R = %Δe_N + (π_domestic − π_foreign).
  2. Higher domestic inflation tends to DEPRECIATE the nominal rate (PPP), while the real rate can APPRECIATE if productivity is catching up (Balassa-Samuelson).
  3. So real and nominal can move in OPPOSITE directions: nominal down (higher inflation) and real up (convergence).
  4. A PPP gap (market rate vs PPP rate) shows whether the country looks cheap; it shrinks as the country converges.
Worked model answer
2019 Q2 — Bulgaria, fixed to the euro, with higher inflation than the euro area: what happens to the real exchange rate, and why?
With the nominal rate fixed, higher Bulgarian inflation means the real exchange rate APPRECIATES (e_R = e_N·P/P_F rises as P rises faster than P_F). That is Balassa-Samuelson: a catching-up economy has rising tradables productivity, which shows up as higher inflation and a real appreciation — not a loss of competitiveness but the normal price of convergence.
Traps
  • Assuming higher inflation always means lost competitiveness — under convergence the real appreciation is benign.
  • Confusing nominal and real: they can move in OPPOSITE directions.

Global saving-investment & the world real rate 15 on past papers

When you see it: A shift in desired global saving or investment (ageing/retirement, China rebalancing, a savings glut) and its effect on the world real rate and current-account imbalances.
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. The world real rate is set where global desired saving = global desired investment (loanable funds).
  2. Map the shock to a curve: more desired saving (ageing savers, China saving) shifts S right → world r falls; less investment (China rebalancing away from investment) shifts I left → world r falls.
  3. Then the current-account mirror: a surplus country saving more widens imbalances; a surplus country consuming more (saving less) shrinks its surplus, raising r and shrinking deficit countries' deficits.
  4. Demographics twist: retirees DIS-save → S shifts LEFT → world r RISES.
Worked model answer
2019 Q7 — China succeeds in raising consumption (lowering its saving): effect on global real rates and imbalances?
Higher Chinese consumption means lower Chinese saving — the global saving curve shifts LEFT, so the world real interest rate RISES. China's current-account surplus shrinks, which narrows the matching deficits elsewhere, so global imbalances fall. Because China is large, the rate effect is material, not negligible.
Traps
  • Confusing a saving shift with an investment shift — both lower r but differ in growth/imbalance implications.
  • Getting the demographics sign wrong: retirees dis-saving RAISES the world rate.

FX intervention & defending a currency 14 on past papers

When you see it: A central bank defending or managing its currency; foreign reserves moving; balance-sheet size or losses; a peg under pressure.
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. Identify the direction: resisting APPRECIATION (buy foreign currency, issue your own) vs resisting DEPRECIATION (sell foreign reserves, buy your own).
  2. Put it on the central-bank balance sheet: which entry moves, and does the monetary base grow or shrink.
  3. The asymmetry: you can resist a STRONG currency indefinitely (print your own), but resisting a WEAK one is limited by finite FX reserves — so pegs break on the weak side.
  4. Sterilisation swaps one asset for another: it changes the composition of the balance sheet, not its size, so no macro effect.
Worked model answer
2022 Q1b — "When the central bank of China buys US dollars from the private sector, it removes liquidity and puts upward pressure on rates." (T/F/U)
False. To buy dollars the central bank PAYS in its own currency, crediting banks' reserve accounts — so it ADDS domestic liquidity and the base grows, putting DOWNWARD pressure on rates (the opposite of the claim). Its balance sheet swells: FX reserves up on the asset side, domestic reserves up on the liability side. To stop that liquidity feeding inflation it would have to sterilise.
Traps
  • Getting the liquidity direction backwards — buying FX ADDS domestic liquidity.
  • Forgetting the strong/weak asymmetry: only the weak-side defence can run out of reserves.

UIP: interest rates → the exchange-rate path 12 on past papers

When you see it: An interest-rate change or news and the currency; 'priced-in' expectations; carry-trade claims.
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. UIP: r − r_F = expected depreciation. Distinguish NEWS of a rate rise (immediate appreciation) from a high LEVEL of rates (expected future depreciation).
  2. A SURPRISE hike ⟹ capital inflow ⟹ immediate appreciation. If the hike was already PRICED IN, no reaction on the day.
  3. High-rate (usually high-inflation) currencies are expected to DEPRECIATE over time — so carry returns are not a free lunch.
  4. Credibility: a credible disinflation lowers the FUTURE path of nominal rates while supporting the currency.
Worked model answer
2022 Q6b — Russia raised rates but the rouble didn't move because the hike was 'priced in': what does that mean?
'Priced in' means the market already expected the hike, so it was embedded in the exchange rate before the announcement — UIP had already adjusted, leaving nothing to react to on the day. Had the hike been a SURPRISE, the higher return would have drawn capital in and the rouble would have appreciated immediately.
Traps
  • Equating high rates with a strong currency — UIP says high-rate currencies are expected to depreciate.
  • Forgetting that only SURPRISES move the rate; anticipated moves are already in the price.

Debt sustainability (the primary-surplus calculation) 12 on past papers

When you see it: Will debt/GDP stay constant? What primary surplus is needed? Rising rates or a downgrade and sustainability. This is the one genuine calculation.
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. Core formula: required primary surplus / GDP = (r − g) × (Debt/GDP), with r and g BOTH nominal (or both real).
  2. Plug in: compute (r − g), multiply by the debt ratio. If g > r the required surplus is NEGATIVE — you can run deficits forever and debt/GDP still falls.
  3. A higher r (e.g. a downgrade adding a risk premium) widens (r − g) ⟹ a bigger surplus is needed.
  4. Inflation raises nominal g and erodes the real burden, easing sustainability — unless it pushes nominal r up by as much or more.
Worked model answer
2018 Q4a — South Africa: r = 9.5%, real g 2.5%, inflation 6%, Debt/GDP = 60%: primary surplus to hold debt constant?
Use (r − g)·(D/Y) with NOMINAL figures: r = 9.5%, nominal g ≈ 2.5% + 6% = 8.5%, so r − g ≈ 1.0%. Required primary surplus ≈ 0.01 × 0.60 = 0.6% of GDP. A ratings downgrade raises the interest rate via the risk premium, widening (r − g) and requiring a larger surplus.
Traps
  • Mixing real and nominal — keep r and g in the SAME terms.
  • Forgetting that g > r flips the sign: sustainable perpetual deficits.

ZLB toolkit & the inflation-target choice 11 on past papers

When you see it: QE and whether it is inflationary; raising the target to 4%; the inflation buffer; a low equilibrium real rate; exiting the ZLB.
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. At the ZLB the nominal rate can't fall further, so the central bank uses QE (buy assets, expand the base) and forward guidance.
  2. Why QE isn't automatically inflationary: it swaps bonds for reserves; if the multiplier is dead (banks hoard reserves) the broad money supply and spending needn't rise — inflation depends on demand and expectations, not the base mechanically.
  3. Target choice: a higher target (4%) lifts average nominal rates, giving more room to cut before hitting zero — more valuable when the equilibrium real rate r* is low (so the ZLB binds more often).
  4. Exit: sell the assets back, or keep them and raise the interest paid on reserves.
Worked model answer
2020 Q2a — critics say QE means big money-supply increases and is always inflationary: find the flaw.
The flaw is equating the monetary BASE with the money SUPPLY. QE expands the base by crediting bank reserves, but if banks hoard those reserves (the multiplier collapses, as at the ZLB) the broad supply and spending don't rise — so no automatic inflation. Inflation comes from demand and expectations; 2008–19 QE coincided with persistently below-target inflation.
Traps
  • Assuming base↑ ⟹ supply↑ ⟹ inflation — the multiplier can be dead.
  • Confusing a higher target's LONG-run insurance (more ZLB room) with a SHORT-run stimulus.

Inflation targeting & the Taylor rule 11 on past papers

When you see it: An output-gap revision; why a central bank does or doesn't move rates; whether above-target inflation forces a hike; measuring slack.
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. Taylor rule: i = r* + π + 0.5(π − π*) + 0.5(output gap). π enters TWICE, so a 1pt inflation rise lifts i by 1.5pt (the real rate must rise to cool the economy).
  2. A LESS negative output gap (economy nearer potential) ⟹ the rule prescribes a higher rate.
  3. A central bank may NOT hike despite above-target inflation if it judges the inflation TEMPORARY (a supply shock), expectations anchored, or it is exiting a long deflation and wants expectations to re-anchor first (Japan).
  4. Slack: don't take a single number at face value (e.g. furlough-distorted unemployment) — mis-measuring the gap mis-sets policy.
Worked model answer
2025 Q2a — the Bank of Japan didn't hike despite 3–4% inflation: why might a target-committed central bank not raise rates?
If the bank judges the above-target inflation to be temporary (import/energy-driven) rather than evidence of a positive output gap with entrenched expectations, the Taylor rule does not call for tightening. For the BoJ specifically, after decades below target it wanted expectations to re-anchor ABOVE the floor before risking a premature hike that could tip it back into deflation.
Traps
  • Forgetting π appears twice ⟹ the greater-than-one reaction that raises the REAL rate.
  • Reacting to headline inflation without asking whether it is a supply shock or a true positive output gap.

Current account ↔ capital flows ↔ reserves 10 on past papers

When you see it: A statement reconciling a current-account surplus/deficit with private capital in/outflows and reserve changes (very often a T/F/U).
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. Start from the identity: Current Account = Capital & Financial Account, i.e. CA = net private capital flow + official reserve change (mind the signs).
  2. A CA surplus = the country is a net LENDER abroad (acquiring foreign assets); a deficit = net borrower.
  3. Reserves are the residual: reserve change = CA − net private flow. Solve for whichever term the question asks about.
  4. State whether the claimed combination is possible — give the case where it holds and where it doesn't.
Worked model answer
2025 Q1a — "The only way to combine a current-account surplus with a private capital inflow is through accumulation of foreign reserves." (T/F/U)
True. A CA surplus means the country is acquiring foreign assets overall; if private agents are simultaneously bringing capital IN (a private inflow), the only actor left to do the foreign-asset acquisition is the central bank — so official reserves must rise. By the identity, reserve change = CA − net private inflow > 0. With the standard sign conventions there is no other way to close the books.
Traps
  • Sign confusion: a CA surplus is a capital OUTFLOW overall (net lending abroad), even when paired with a private INflow.
  • Forgetting reserves are the residual — private flows need not equal the CA; the central bank's reserve change closes the gap.

Growth: GDP vs GDP-per-capita, demographics & the 4 Is 10 on past papers

When you see it: Population or working-age growth vs GDP per capita; retirement age; per-worker vs per-capita; whether demographics alone deliver prosperity.
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. Decompose: GDP/Pop = (GDP/Hours) × (Hours/Worker) × (Workers/Working-age) × (Working-age/Pop) = productivity × effort × employment × demographics.
  2. More working-age people raise TOTAL GDP but not necessarily GDP per CAPITA (living standards) — that needs productivity.
  3. The 4 Is: convergence needs Investment (capital) under good Institutions, absorbing Innovation, from its Initial conditions. Population growth alone ≠ rising living standards.
  4. Employment ratio: raising the share of (e.g. older) people in work lifts GDP per capita even as the population ages.
Worked model answer
2020 Q5 — Africa's working-age population overtakes Asia by 2100: does that mean an equal share of world GDP?
No. A larger working-age population raises GDP roughly in proportion to workers only if output PER worker is similar — but GDP per capita is driven by productivity, which depends on investment and institutions. The figures showing Emerging Asia investing ~35–40% of GDP vs Sub-Saharan Africa ~20% are the point: without comparable investment (and the institutions that make it pay), more workers won't deliver an equal GDP share.
Traps
  • Equating population/working-age growth with prosperity — that conflates total GDP with GDP per capita.
  • Forgetting the binding constraint is usually investment and institutions, not labour supply.

Real vs nominal interest rates & the zero lower bound 8 on past papers

When you see it: A T/F linking nominal and real rates, negative real rates, or whether physical cash / the ZLB floors the real rate.
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. Fisher: r = i − π (real = nominal − inflation).
  2. Negative real rates need only i < π — possible with positive inflation; no deflation required.
  3. The ZLB is a floor on the NOMINAL rate (cash pays 0%), not on the real rate: with i ≈ 0 and π > 0, r = −π < 0.
  4. A surprise nominal hike, with sticky inflation/expectations, raises the REAL rate in the short run.
Worked model answer
2023 Q1c — "The existence of printed physical currency prevents real interest rates from going meaningfully below zero." (T/F/U)
False. Physical cash floors the NOMINAL rate near zero (no one holds a bond paying less than 0% cash). But the real rate is r = i − π, so with i pinned at ~0 and positive inflation π, the real rate is simply −π, which can be very negative. Cash constrains the nominal, not the real, rate.
Traps
  • Conflating the nominal floor (ZLB) with a real floor — the ZLB does not stop negative real rates.
  • Thinking negative real rates require deflation — they only require i < π.

The money multiplier & the two aggregates 6 on past papers

When you see it: A T/F about whether the money supply and the monetary base move together, or whether one can change without the other.
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. Define both: Base = C + R (the central bank controls it); Money supply = C + D (what the private sector holds); link is m = (C/D + 1)/(C/D + R/D).
  2. The link is the multiplier, and m is NOT fixed — it depends on behaviour (cash ratio C/D, reserve ratio R/D).
  3. So base and supply need not move together or at the same rate: if m changes, the supply can move while the base is flat.
  4. Apply to the specific claim (e.g. a panic: C/D and R/D jump ⟹ m collapses ⟹ supply falls even with the base unchanged).
Worked model answer
2013 Q1a — "The only way the money supply can increase is if the monetary base increases." (T/F/U)
False. Money supply = m × base, and the multiplier m rises when the public holds less cash (C/D↓) or banks lend more of their deposits (R/D↓). So the supply can increase with the base unchanged if behaviour pushes m up; conversely in a bank run m collapses and the supply falls even as the base is held flat (the 1929 story).
Traps
  • Treating m as fixed — it is behavioural.
  • Confusing base (C+R) with supply (C+D); reserves R are not deposits D.

Monetary neutrality & the nominal anchor 6 on past papers

When you see it: The price level being 'arbitrary'; the initial value of a currency; Bitcoin or parallel currencies; a dollarised economy; whether a fixed supply controls inflation.
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. Long-run neutrality: the LEVEL of money/prices/the initial exchange rate is arbitrary; only the PATH (the growth rate = inflation) has real effects ("add a zero overnight" changes nothing real).
  2. A nominal anchor is a central-bank COMMITMENT to a stable value of money (an inflation target) — not a quantity cap. A fixed supply (Bitcoin) is not an anchor because value = supply ∩ demand, and demand swings ⟹ volatility.
  3. Dollarisation imports another country's anchor and surrenders monetary independence.
  4. So choosing a different INITIAL value (e.g. the euro's launch price) shifts the price level once but not the subsequent inflation.
Worked model answer
2025 Q5a — should the ECB have launched the euro below $1 to get lower inflation?
No. The initial exchange rate sets the price LEVEL at launch, but it has no effect on INFLATION in the following years, which is set by the ECB's policy and its 2% target. By long-run neutrality the starting value is arbitrary — a lower launch value would just mean different nominal numbers, not a lower inflation path.
Traps
  • Confusing the price LEVEL (arbitrary) with the inflation RATE (chosen by policy).
  • Thinking a fixed quantity (gold, Bitcoin) delivers price stability — an anchor is a commitment, not a cap.

Asset pricing: the discount rate & the equity risk premium 3 on past papers

When you see it: The stock market vs interest rates or inflation; P/E ratios; whether high prices are a bubble; the equity risk premium.
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. A stock = the present value of future cash flows discounted at R = r + RP (real rate + risk premium). A higher discount rate ⟹ lower prices.
  2. Rising real rates lower valuations — but if rates rise because GROWTH is stronger, higher expected cash flows can offset it.
  3. A high P/E isn't automatically a bubble: it can reflect a LOW required return (low r or low RP). Note RP = E/P − r.
  4. Risk-off rebalancing into bonds raises the required return on equities ⟹ lower stock prices, higher RP.
Worked model answer
2022 Q4 — the FT argues discount rate up ⟹ stocks down; does this explain the high P/Es since 2009?
The direction is right — equities are the present value of future profits, so a higher discount rate lowers prices. And yes, it explains the elevated P/Es since 2009: discount rates (both the real rate and the risk premium) were exceptionally LOW, which mechanically lifts valuations. The bubble question is only whether investors wrongly assumed low rates and high profits were permanent.
Traps
  • Reading a high P/E as automatically a bubble — it can simply be a low required return.
  • Forgetting the offset: if rates rise because growth is up, cash flows rise too.
