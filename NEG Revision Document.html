Win-Lose vs Win-Win, as a choice (S1–2)

Win-Lose (pie-dividing)Win-Win (pie-expanding)
Adversarial; quick, easier, the default. But it carries costs: uncertainty over who is more powerful, loss aversion, poor sustainability (they may retaliate), and the anxiety of combat.Cooperative problem-solving that grows the pie. Both are choices with risks and rewards. Soyer’s point: don’t label a negotiation, choose an approach for this counterpart, this time, and mitigate its specific risks.

Success in Win-Win (S3–4)

A deal is a win-win when it: satisfies interests (ours well, theirs acceptably, others tolerably); beats your alternatives (BATNA); wastes nothing (best of several options); is legitimate (no one feels taken); has a well-planned commitment; improves the working relationship; and runs on fair, efficient communication. Note that the seven success tests are the 7 Elements.

What negotiation is

🎙 In class Soyer opens by saying yes to every definition the room offers (combat, debate, persuasion, joint solution, discovery) and then reframes the whole thing: "Negotiation is a tool for you to make better decisions in life. You create the opportunity to make a better decision." It is not just about winning a fixed prize; it is an experimentation tool that also tells you the value of your other options.
Negotiation (working definition). Parties with different interests allocate resources: one person is trying to persuade another to reach the best possible option for a decision. Two features matter. (1) There is a pie to be eaten together, and part of the skill is working out whether it can be made bigger before you divide it. (2) There is persuasion: you change their mind, and you must allow them to change yours, otherwise it is a debate, not a negotiation.
Negotiator vs decision-maker. Always ask who actually makes the decision. You may be negotiating hard with someone who has no authority to agree. 🎙 In class Soyer flags the classic move (a student raised it from experience): you agree everything, then the other side says "now I need to go back and check if that works" and the real boss kills it. Knowing the other party is partly knowing whether they can actually say yes.

Great vs bad negotiator (the class-generated lists)

Soyer had the room build both lists. The point of the exercise is the punchline he delivers at the end: this "superhero" is too many things to be all the time, and almost every "good" trait can backfire. You pick and choose; preparation and learning from past negotiations raise your odds of behaving like the left column when it counts.

Traits of a great negotiatorTraits of a bad negotiator
Cultivates alternatives outside the table (the trait nobody names first, yet the most powerful)No power / no alternatives; negotiates from weakness
Separates the person from the problem; doesn't take it personallyEmotional, takes things personally, plots revenge
Clear on own interests; also probes the other side's interestsUnprepared; hasn't thought through what they actually want
Perceptive, curious, asks questions, good listener (hears the underlying interest)Talks too much; nit-picks; drifts off the main subject

Soyer's nuance: "anything too or over tends to be bad." Calm can read as weakness; empathy can be exploited if it isn't reciprocated; confidence can tip into arrogance. Don't treat the left column as virtues to maximise. They are dials to set for the situation, which is exactly why there is no universal "best" negotiator.

The Basics: vocabulary, each defined

Intuition: BATNA Your strength in the room comes mostly from your willingness to walk away, and that depends on what you have outside the room. 🎙 In class "As long as you have a great alternative outside, you become a much better negotiator inside. People obsess about what's going to happen at the table and forget what's out there." Improve your outside option (e.g. a second job offer) and you have just improved this negotiation without saying a word in it.
BATNA: Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement. What you will do if this deal falls through. It is the main source of power in a negotiation. Soyer's tell: "Every book says this, so I have to say it, then I'm not going to use the term again." The label matters less than the habit of cultivating and improving your alternatives before you sit down.
Reservation price: your walk-away point: the least-good deal you would still accept rather than take your BATNA. Aspiration price: your maximum achievable outcome: the best you could realistically hope for. 🎙 In class Soyer: "Nobody really reveals their reservation price. Even at your deathbed you will hide it." In class he reveals the hidden numbers afterwards so you can see what was actually on the table.
Distributive vs integrative. Distributive = a single issue, essentially zero-sum: what one side gains the other loses (dividing a fixed pie). Integrative = non-zero-sum, usually because there are multiple issues the two sides value differently, so trades can make both better off (expanding the pie). Most real negotiations have integrative potential hiding behind a distributive surface.
Position vs interest. A position is what someone says they want ("I need the whole orange"). An interest is why they want it (the juice, or the peel). One of the two is more critical: interests. Positions clash; interests can often be reconciled. Wants vs needs sits alongside this: what someone demands and what they actually require may not be the same thing.
Pulp / seed (the orange) Soyer's shorthand for getting under positions to interests: two people fight over one orange (positions), but one wants the pulp (juice) and the other the seed/peel (for baking). Split by interest and both get everything. 🎙 In class His push in the Oxipouco debrief: most people stop once they find a workable split. "I wouldn't have stopped. I'd create a joint venture, ask how we can solve each other's problems." The strategic-summary slide makes it a rule: "make identifying pulp/seed a priority."

The main philosophy: Win-Lose vs Win-Win

Intuition: the choice Win-Lose and Win-Win are not personality types you are stuck with; they are choices you make per negotiation, each with risks and rewards. 🎙 In class "Combat is very intuitive. Everybody goes to combat first, but then going into the other ones becomes really difficult." Soyer calls the escalation a tunnel of fights: once both sides are colouring the picture "combat", it is hard to switch to any other colour.
Win-Win: cooperative, "pie-expanding" problem-solving. The reward is a higher-value outcome; the cost is that it is complicated and can be misread as weakness. Win-Lose: adversarial, "pie-dividing". It is quick, easier, and the default, which is exactly why people reach for it. Soyer's framing: the question is not "which am I?" but "given this situation, which serves my goals?"
Myth: Win-Win means being soft, nice, or splitting the difference. It doesn't. 🎙 In class Soyer: "The real win-win is understanding what the problem is, to create a long-term collaboration." It is hard, analytical problem-solving, not generosity. (The assigned INSEAD Knowledge article is literally titled "Stop Labelling Negotiations as Win-Win or Win-Lose.") Equally, don't assume Win-Lose is "tougher and therefore smarter": it carries real costs, below.
Why the default (Win-Lose) is expensive.
→ Uncertainty over power. You rarely know who is actually more powerful, so a power play can backfire.
→ Loss aversion. The side being pushed to lose resists hardest: "the person who is resisting has more to lose, so they fight harder, maybe even dirtier."
→ Sustainability. If you beat them today they are unlikely to collaborate tomorrow, and may retaliate, so power can shift against you over time.
→ Risk mitigation. Win-Win partly acts as insurance against that retaliation.
→ Peace of mind. Combat induces anxiety. 🎙 In class Soyer's example: Harvey Specter in Suits is win-lose every day, charismatic, and permanently stressed about who is coming for him next.

Soyer maps the two philosophies across the full decision chain. Note that the assumption at the start (is the pie fixed?) propagates all the way to the result:

Win-LoseWin-Win
AssumptionThe pie is fixedThe pie can be expanded
GoalBeat themMaximise total available benefit
StrategyCompete; claim valueCollaborate to create value; claim fairly
ActionHard on people; use powerHard on the problem; look for joint gains
Likely resultLow-value outcomeHigh-value outcome

Underneath the strategy sits a mindset. Soyer contrasts the two as a battle vs a puzzle:

Battle mindset (Win-Lose)

  • This is a battle.
  • They are my opponent.
  • Resources are scarce.
  • The goal is to beat them.
  • This is about positions.
  • This is one-time.
  • The rules of the game are fixed.
  • They are stupid, crazy, or evil.

Puzzle mindset (Win-Win)

  • This is a puzzle.
  • They can be a teammate.
  • We can "create" resources.
  • The goal is to maximise value.
  • This is about interests.
  • This has future impact.
  • I can change the game.
  • People do what they believe is in their best interest.

Why this is hard: the course framing

The course's three assumptions. (1) Negotiation is everywhere (and you can defer or decline one: you do not have to negotiate every time a negotiation comes to you). (2) Negotiation is an improvable skill, not a fixed talent. (3) There is no universal "best" way: every strategy is a bundle of risks and rewards, and surprises are guaranteed under asymmetric information.
Intuition: wicked learning environment This is Soyer's behavioural-science lens (his own research on kind vs wicked environments). You would expect to get better at negotiating just by doing it a lot. You often don't, because real-life feedback is poor. 🎙 In class "This is a very wicked game. Your life is terrible, literally, because you don't get feedback, and the rules keep changing." Sports improve fast because feedback is immediate and the game is stable next year; negotiation is the opposite.
Kind learning environmentWicked learning environment
Accurate, abundant, immediate feedbackBiased, incomplete, irrelevant or delayed feedback
Situation and rules are stableRules are complex, uncertain and unstable
You learn naturally from experience (e.g. sport)Experience alone can teach the wrong lesson (negotiation)
Why the exercises exist. → Negotiation is a wicked environment, so raw experience doesn't reliably improve you. → You need a "safe" environment where the hidden information is revealed afterwards and the feedback is honest. → Hence the in-class simulations + AI feedback tools + the reflection documents: deliberate practice manufactures the clean feedback that real life withholds. 🎙 In class Soyer's role: "coach": share pragmatic tools and scientific insight, offer a common vocabulary, and force reflection on process (results are not graded).

How Soyer assesses you / what he rewards. There is no written exam, so the grade tracks process, not wins.
He rewards: preparation done in advance (the prep docs are scored on the quality of your thinking, not the result); cultivating alternatives before you sit down (your BATNA is your power); moving the conversation from positions to interests (find the pulp/seed); treating each case as a chance to experiment with a strategy you wouldn't risk in real life; and honest reflection on what went wrong.
He flags as mistakes: defaulting to combat without choosing it; lying or exaggerating constraints (it erodes trust the moment it surfaces); anything done "too" much (too aggressive, too demanding, too patient); reading too much into body language; and confusing activity with progress (stopping at the first workable split instead of expanding the pie).
Carry into the role-plays: ask who actually decides, set your reservation and aspiration prices in advance, keep them hidden, and remember anger or aggressiveness should never be unplanned.

3–4 · Success & the 7 Elements

This cluster hands you the spine of the whole course: a single preparation tool Soyer calls the 7 Elements, plus a definition of what a successful negotiation actually looks like. Sessions 1–2 set up the two philosophies (win-lose vs win-win); here Soyer commits the course to win-win and gives you the checklist that turns that bias into a repeatable process. He frames the 7 Elements as the document that “will be your companion for all this course and then hopefully beyond”: you use it to prepare before, to pause and check assumptions during, and to learn from success and failure after. The live case running underneath the theory is a two-versus-two contract negotiation (a talent agency, Mint Talent Group, joining forces with a studio, Just Movie Studios), which is why the cluster ends on contract-specific advice.

Intuition — the pre-flight + debrief checklist Before any negotiation, run down seven boxes; after it, run down the same seven to see what you missed. That is the entire idea. Soyer’s pitch is that the cost of skipping a box is a regret you only notice driving home (“I should have asked that”), when the window has already closed. The list is the minimum thinking, not the maximum: “sometimes you will care about this more, sometimes this more; it’s up to you. But each negotiation can use this thought process.”
🎙 In class “I’m going to argue that this is the minimum you can get to think about a negotiation. You need to think about all these seven to some degree.” He also warns the boring part is the point: “usually useful things are boring … broccoli is good for you. I hate it. But this is going to be useful.”

The 7 Elements, defined

Soyer walks them in a deliberate order. He insists you start with interests and treat alternatives as the close second, because the rest of the toolkit only pays off once you know what you actually want and where you can walk to.

ElementWhat it isThe question it makes you ask
1. InterestsWhat each side actually cares about (wants and needs), as opposed to the positions they state. List yours generously, then list your assumptions about theirs before you ever speak to them.What do I really want here, and what might they really want beneath what they say?
2. Alternatives (BATNA)Your Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement: the exit door you take if no deal happens. Crucially these sit outside the negotiation, not on the table. Your main source of power.If we don’t agree, where do I go instead, and how do I make that option better?
3. OptionsThe things you could agree on inside the negotiation: the possible terms, packages and trades. Design several, not one.What could we agree on? What are all the ways to slice this?
4. LegitimacyThe objective, external, neutral reasons that justify an ask (laws, market precedents, fairness norms). You give the reason first, then the ask, so people follow your logic and no one feels conned.Why am I entitled to this? Why do they need what they are asking for?
5. CommitmentThe “boring facts” of closing: what we sign or shake on, who actually has authority to commit, the purpose and agenda of the meeting.Are we agreeing to the same thing, and is this person able to commit to it?
6. RelationshipThe ability to keep working together, and the discipline of attacking the problem, not the person. Map the current state, the state you want, and what gets you there (it can be contingent on their behaviour).Where is the relationship now, where do I want to leave it, and am I about to burn a bridge I want to keep?
7. CommunicationWhat you will actually say: a written opening statement, the questions you must ask, and the messages you must send. Soyer writes his out so he doesn’t forget them in the heat of the moment.What do I open with, what must I ask, and what must I make sure they hear?
★Isabella — eyeball this: the wheel positions and the 1–7 numbering are mine, built from Soyer’s spoken walk-through plus the prep-sheet column order. Soyer stresses the order is a default (interests first, alternatives second), not a rigid sequence, so treat the numbers as “where to start” rather than a fixed pipeline.

WHY THIS ORDER

Start at Interests → because everything else is defined relative to what you want; skip it and you negotiate over positions you can’t even justify. → Then Alternatives, because your walk-away power is set outside the room, and if you haven’t cultivated an exit you are stuck with whatever they hand you. → Only now design Options, because good options are the ones that beat your alternative and serve the interests. → Legitimacy arms each option with a neutral reason, so the other side can say yes without feeling beaten. → Commitment, Relationship, Communication govern how you close, with whom, and in what words, so the deal actually holds and the next one is still possible.
🎙 In class — alternatives are second, and you don’t brandish them “Alternatives come number two. After what you care about, you should actually have exit doors. These are not options in the negotiation; these are alternatives to the negotiation.” And on using them: “talking about alternatives is essentially getting your gun and putting it on the table. It’s not necessarily good faith.” You should know your BATNA cold but rarely flag it as a threat. His worked example: you don’t walk into a job offer and announce “by the way, I have other offers, what are you going to do about that?”
Don’t confuse options with alternatives. Options live inside the negotiation (the terms you could agree on); alternatives live outside it (what you do if there is no deal). Soyer is emphatic that these are different boxes. He also warns against inventing alternatives that breach your own interests or legitimacy: in his Oxypoco prep he wrote in “bribe the government” and “sabotage research” to show breadth, then struck them out when a student pointed out they were corruption and crossed the case’s own constraints. Be creative, but “don’t make things up too much” beyond what the case supports.

What “success” means: Success in Win-Win

Before handing out the elements, Soyer made the class define success out loud. The recurring student answers (get something I value; the other side also gains; no one gets screwed; I learned about them; the relationship survives; the process felt right) are not a grab-bag. They map almost one-to-one onto the 7 Elements. Soyer’s own framing: there is an order to success, and multiple things must be true at once. His summary slide lists the criteria below.

Success in Win-Win (the SUMMARY-deck scorecard). A successful win-win outcome:
  • Satisfies interests: ours well, theirs acceptably (at least), others tolerably (at least).
  • Is better than your alternatives (BATNA).
  • Has no waste: it is the best of several options.
  • Is legitimate: no one feels “taken”.
  • Rests on a well-planned commitment.
  • Has a process that improves the working relationship.
  • Has a process that is fair and efficient, with good communication.

The point Soyer is making is structural: the success criteria are the 7 Elements used as a yardstick. The same seven boxes you fill in to prepare are the seven you score yourself against to know whether you succeeded.

Intuition — “others, tolerably” and “no one feels taken” Two of the criteria are easy to overlook. Third parties who hate your deal can derail it later, so a fourth box (people not even at the table) has to be at least tolerably served. And the test for legitimacy is emotional: if either side later feels conned, it failed, even if they signed. Soyer’s gauge is the scam feeling. “People should understand why you shook hands”; the after-the-fact sense that “they took me” should not exist for either party.
🎙 In class — getting a deal is not the same as success A student offered “success = always getting a deal.” Soyer pushed back: “if you always get a deal, you may be getting bad deals. You may be lowering your bar too much.” He recalled a firm whose motto was “we always get deals” and concluded it was a warning sign, not a boast, because they compromised heavily to keep the streak alive. Success is the deal beating your BATNA, not the existence of a deal.

The myths Soyer debunks

Myth: win-win requires common goals. It does not. The SUMMARY deck’s own reading is titled “Common Goals Are Not Necessary for Win-Win Negotiations.” Win-win does not need shared objectives, friendship, or splitting the difference evenly; it needs each side’s interests satisfied better than their alternatives. Soyer’s guiding assumption is “people do what they believe is in their best interest,” so the job is to find overlapping or tradeable interests, not matching goals. His bank anecdote makes the point: a client asked him to give a 1,500-person speech for free. Rather than conclude the man was crazy, Soyer assumed an objective function (“no money”) and started hunting for a different, non-cash deal that could still serve both sides.
Myth: win-win is the soft, weak option. Soyer flags this explicitly: “win-win is not necessarily great. People think you’re weak,” so once you start asking questions some counterparts think “I’ve got this person.” His answer is not to abandon win-win but to lead with it knowingly: “try the green first … let me find out what we can do together. And then we can combat if needed.” Win-lose is the quick, adversarial default, but it carries costs (retaliation, an unknown power balance, no tomorrow), so it is a deliberate choice, not a fallback.

Soyer’s strategic points for this cluster

Strategic summary (SUMMARY deck). Use the 7 Elements both to question and design a good process and to learn from success and failure.
  • Start with interests and potential mutual gains, not positions.
  • Don’t ignore alternatives; invest time improving yours rather than just sharpening your table game.
  • Design multiple options, not specific positions.
  • Question their positions to bring them back to interests (keep asking “why do you need this?”).
  • For contract negotiations: emphasise “drafts” over “standards.”
  • For contract negotiations: use the negotiation itself to test and estimate the post-contract collaboration.
Why improve alternatives rather than just out-negotiate at the table? You obsess over the table and get very good at it → but the table is fixed; your only real leverage is the door you can walk to → so cultivating a better alternative outside the room raises your floor in every round inside it. Soyer’s M&A example: never sign exclusivity when selling, because it kills your ability to shop around while the buyer slowly chips your billion-dollar price down.

Contract negotiations: drafts over standards

The live case was a contract, so Soyer closed on contract craft (SUMMARY deck page on Contract Negotiations, plus the debrief). The recurring issues: both sides can take things personally, and there is usually a power imbalance.

Contract tactics.
  • All contracts are negotiable (a former-lawyer student confirmed: no one has an offer rescinded for negotiating). Standard does not mean fixed.
  • Send your own draft first. Whoever drafts anchors the rules. Soyer sends a simple two-page protocol before the counterparty’s legal template arrives, and they usually accept it to avoid the legal overhead.
  • Use the contract to test the future collaboration. How they behave during drafting predicts how they will behave as a partner.
  • Think about what could go wrong, not just the rosy path: assume your contact person can change, and put in contingencies (“if X, then Y”) for the risks.
  • Ask for legitimacy behind deadlines. If they cite a deadline, ask why; the number is often an anchor you can push back on.
The “standard contract” trap. “Usually a standard contract means ‘I don’t want you to read this.’” Soyer once caught a clause buried on page three assigning his IP to the other firm; they admitted he was “the only person who noticed this for years.” Read it, and prefer adding protective clauses over striking theirs out (additions cost the other side’s lawyer less and provoke fewer red-line wars). Also note the cultural split: in the US, if it is not in the contract it is not valid, so a refusal to write a promise down is a refusal to keep it.
Worked mini-scenario — the Mint Talent / Just Movie Studios debrief The case asked two students (agent side) to negotiate Zander, an ageing action star, plus his daughters, into a studio film (studio budget: 12 million). The debrief is a clinic in the 7 Elements:
  • Interests over money. Zander “doesn’t care about money”; he wants the lead role and to look like a hot shot again. One group paid him 250k and kept him happy; another paid 8 million. Same star, huge variance, all driven by reading the underlying interest.
  • Anchoring. Because the studio held the 12 million budget, the studio speaking first hurt it: open at six and the agent thinks “holy moly.” Whoever names the number first can anchor against themselves.
  • Options as contingencies. “If-then” structures (a base fee plus a bonus above 500–600 million box office) are, in Soyer’s words, “very win-win”: they bridge disagreement about value and motivate better effort. Casting the daughters was the hidden win-win door; groups that never asked “what about the daughters?” left value on the table.
  • Legitimacy to move money. Strong groups justified a lower fee with a neutral reason (Zander is old, so insurance and stunt doubles cost more), rather than just haggling.
  • Alternatives and bluffing. The rival actor “Jet” is secretly dead/out. Don’t reveal that too early, and when the other side exaggerates something you know, use the gap to gauge how much they are bluffing. One group was so open and kind that the counterparty assumed it was “too good to be true.”

5–6 · Value Discovery & Claiming

This cluster is where the framework meets numbers. Sessions 1–4 built the philosophy (win-lose vs win-win) and the diagnostic kit (the 7 Elements). Here Soyer turns to the two jobs you do in every deal at once: growing the pie (value creation / discovery) and dividing it (value claiming). The vehicle was the WABTY job-offer role-play (a candidate, "Larry", negotiating an offer with a hiring manager, "May"), debriefed in class. The throughline: start with interests, discover where your interests differ, and only then put numbers on the table.

Intuition: create then claim Expand the pie before you divide it. If you reach for the knife first (whose slice is bigger?), you trigger the adversarial, scarcity mindset and you never find out the pie could have been larger. Discovery comes first: list what you both want, find the issues you value differently, and trade across them. Claiming a share of a bigger pie beats winning a bigger share of a small one.
🎙 In class "People start with numbers too quickly. You should always talk numbers, and today there will be some numbers. But you should start with interests." Soyer's whole sequencing argument in one line: numbers are unavoidable, but they are the last move, not the first.

The two tasks: value creation vs value claiming

Every negotiation runs both tasks simultaneously, and they pull in opposite directions. Creating value is cooperative and information-hungry; claiming value is competitive and information-guarding. The skill is doing both without letting the claiming reflex shut down discovery before it starts.

Value creation (discovery)

  • Ask questions, share some interests, look for trades.
  • Treats the other side as a potential teammate solving a puzzle.
  • Output: a bigger, better-designed deal (more issues, more options).
  • Risk: can be read as weakness or naivety if you only create and never claim.

Value claiming

  • Anchoring, holding firm, deciding who gets which slice.
  • Treats the deal as a fixed quantity to be split.
  • Output: your share of whatever value exists.
  • Risk: claim too early and you collapse into win-lose, killing the discovery that would have made the pie bigger.

Discover and list: common, different, and conflicting interests

Discovery means sorting interests into three buckets. The crucial insight Soyer hammered in the WABTY debrief: it is the different (non-competing) interests, not the common ones, that are the raw material for trades. If I value an issue cheaply and you value it highly, that issue should move to whoever wants it most, in exchange for something I care about more.

ZOPA: Zone of Possible Agreement

Intuition: ZOPA Before defining it: a deal is only possible if there is room between the worst price you'd accept and the worst price they'd accept. The ZOPA is that overlap. No overlap, no deal (on price alone).
ZOPA (Zone of Possible Agreement): the range of outcomes that both sides would prefer to their walk-away alternatives. It is bounded by each party's reservation price (the walk-away point: the least-good deal you'd still accept rather than take your BATNA, your best alternative to a negotiated agreement). If the buyer's ceiling sits above the seller's floor, the gap between them is the ZOPA; any agreed price inside it beats no deal for both.
Soyer's nuance, the ZOPA is usually multi-dimensional, not a number line. Real ZOPAs are complex: instead of one price point, identify multiple equivalent options for you built from the various interests and variables in play (salary, bonus, timing, perks, promotion review, relocation). Because you and the other side value those variables differently, there are many packages that clear your reservation point. Drawing the deal as a single number forces premature claiming; drawing it as a space of equivalent bundles keeps discovery alive. In the WABTY debrief the deals that worked split the discussion: relationship and structure first, then perks and variable pay, with the fixed number negotiated last, precisely because the action was in the other variables, not the one line.

First offers: structured options before numbers

🎙 In class On whether to anchor first: "Saying the number early is argued to be better for the anchoring effect... I'm going to argue maybe not. I would first test the waters a little bit before I utter the numbers." Soyer's worked example: the studio role had real money to spend while the counterpart didn't care about cash, "so you may say the wrong number very easily."

The conventional advice is to anchor first to pull the outcome your way. Soyer's qualification: a first offer expressed as a single number, before you understand the other side's interests, risks anchoring in the wrong place entirely (too low, or on the wrong dimension). His alternative sequencing:

The first-offer move
Open with structured options, not a number → options reveal which variables the other side cares about, without committing you to a price → you learn their constraints and flexibilities → then put numbers on the table, anchored where the information says they belong. The anchoring effect is real, but a well-placed late number beats a confident early one in the dark.
The information leak (Soyer's shop story) A skilled seller used small talk as reconnaissance: "Are you enjoying our city? Where did you go to eat? How many days are you here?" Each answer leaks your budget and your time pressure. Soyer's rule: "Never ever answer that question" about your favourite restaurant if it's high-end, because now they have a read on your budget. The lesson cuts both ways: ask questions to discover their constraints, and notice when their questions are mining yours.

Avoid early positions: keep questioning back to interests

A position is a stated demand ("I need 100k"); an interest is the underlying need it serves (security, status, putting food on the table, beating a rival offer). Lock onto positions early and the negotiation slides into a win-lose tug of war over a single number. The discipline is to keep questioning positions back to the interests beneath them, where trades become visible.

The positional "dance" is not a negotiation. Soyer's illustration of pure positional bargaining: "I say 200, you say no... I say 180, you say 80... 160, you say 100... and we meet at 130. This is not negotiation. This is a dance." Numbers ping-ponging toward the midpoint feels like negotiating but creates no value; it just splits a pie nobody bothered to grow. Do the relationship and interest work first, then bargain.

JOB NEGOTIATIONS

WABTY was a job offer, and Soyer's job-specific playbook (from the summary deck's Job Negotiations slide and the class debrief) is distinctive because the things that feel central (salary) are often the least flexible, and the things that feel soft (relationship) carry the real leverage.

Soyer's job-negotiation rules
  • Don't underestimate relationship and likeability. One side (the candidate) usually takes it more personally; you are auditing a future colleague, not splitting a pie once.
  • Avoid early positions and "negotiation-as-goal." If you come in "guns blazing" demanding the number, the other side thinks "maybe this isn't for me." Leave the guns for the second half.
  • Main task: find the other side's constraints and flexibilities. "Everything is negotiable, but sometimes salaries people really can't negotiate, so you have to find some other variables that you can." Salary may be a hard cap; bonus timing, perks, promotion review, and relocation may not be.
  • Salary is not the whole deal. Money takes many forms and schedules; perks the firm gives cheaply can become value in your pocket.
  • Treat tough questions as opportunities to learn, and reveal the intent behind them. When you're unsure if you're pushing too hard, ask: "Is this a line you don't want to cross?" Surfacing the intent behind a hard question defuses it and gathers information.
  • Treat the negotiation as a trial run of the working relationship. How they behave now previews how they'll behave once you've joined.
The myth that it all comes down to price. Negotiations should never come down to price alone. Soyer's pyjama-shop story: a student who bargained well at the start, then barely haggled at the end of the trip, was haunted not by the small money lost but by the feeling of being "taken". "The money becomes irrelevant; the bargaining becomes the objective itself." Two warnings here: (1) reducing a rich, multi-issue deal to one number throws away all the trades that differing interests make possible; (2) your gut craves the fight, so you may "negotiate just to negotiate" when you shouldn't. Always know why you're negotiating, and remember the firm version of this trap: companies manufacture rat-race competition to make you work harder without paying more, exploiting the same instinct.
Don't treat a "No" as final, turn it into a "Maybe." A flat refusal is usually a position guarding an unspoken constraint, not the end of the conversation. Rather than accepting the No or pushing harder on the same axis, question it back to the interest behind it ("what would need to be true for this to work?") and reopen the option space on a different variable. In WABTY, when base salary was a hard No, the pairs who got somewhere stopped pushing the number and converted the No into a Maybe elsewhere: bonus timing, a guaranteed promotion review, relocation, perks. A No on one dimension is an invitation to discover another.
Why the "no-deals" happened (WABTY debrief) Roughly half the class reached no deal. The pattern Soyer drew out: the hiring-manager brief contained an explicit instruction to walk away from a candidate who came in purely transactional. Candidates who opened with "tell me the number" or "culture doesn't put food on the table" got rejected even when their underlying point was correct, because they signalled a bad future colleague. The candidates who built the relationship first, then bargained, mostly closed. The medium was the message: the negotiation behaviour was the hiring test.
What Soyer rewards (and what he flags as a mistake), carry into the role-plays and the Negotiation Journal.
  • Start with interests, end with numbers. He explicitly reviews this every session; opening on price is the cardinal error.
  • Discover the differing interests and trade across them. The win-win in WABTY was converting perks the firm valued cheaply into cash the candidate wanted. He rewards students who find and bundle variables rather than haggling one number.
  • Build the relationship first, then bargain, and take a break in the middle. He praised every pair that split relationship from bargaining; he flags the "grind, grind, grind" instinct as a mistake.
  • Set the agenda yourself. "I like to set the agenda. Nobody really says no." Naming the structure ("relationship today, numbers next") is rewarded.
  • Find constraints and flexibilities; reveal the intent behind tough questions. When pushing feels risky, ask whether you're crossing a line rather than guessing.
  • Don't negotiate just to negotiate, and don't let it come down to price. Know why you're at the table; protect the relationship because most negotiations repeat.
  • Experiment in the safe space. Soyer urges trying tactics (aggressive, warm, anchoring games like lowballing the bonus or highballing an outside offer) you would never risk in real life, and journalling what fit you and what didn't. He flags anchoring games as risky: highball an outside offer and you can lose trust if the other side knows the market.
  • Confirm commitments explicitly. "People think they agree, then it turns out they didn't." He recommends sending a "this is what we agreed, right?" email.

Process design: agree the process before the substance

Intuition: process before substance When a negotiation is complicated, the first negotiation is about how you will negotiate. Settle the agenda, the sequence and who is in the room before you argue the issues, the same way you would set rules before a complex game so you do not fight about the rules halfway through.

Partisan perception: same facts, different fouls

Intuition: partisan perception Two fans watch the same replay and each sees a clear foul on the other team. Nobody is lying; complexity and uncertainty leave gaps, and each side fills those gaps in the direction that flatters its own case. In a negotiation this means you and the other party can read the identical contract, email or offer and honestly come away with opposite "facts".
🎙 In class Soyer demonstrated this live with the Green Needle / Brainstorm auditory illusion: the same audio clip is heard as "green needle" or "brainstorm" depending on which word you are looking at. "Whichever I'm looking at, I'm hearing that... It's the same." One stimulus, two perceptions, set entirely by expectation, the cleanest possible demonstration that what you "perceive" is partly constructed by what you came in expecting.
Partisan (biased) perception: the tendency for each side to interpret the same ambiguous information in a self-serving way, sincerely believing its reading is the objective one. The more complex and uncertain the situation, the more room there is for the bias to operate, because there are more gaps to fill. Soyer flags this as a behavioural-science default to actively guard against, not a flaw of "the other guy".
Why complexity and uncertainty breed partisan perception
A deal is complex and the facts are ambiguous → the evidence underdetermines the "right" reading, leaving interpretive gaps → each side's motivation (wanting its case to be true) unconsciously fills those gaps in its own favour → both sides end up sincerely certain of incompatible "facts" → they argue past each other, each thinking the other is being unreasonable or dishonest → the remedy is not "try harder to be objective" (you already feel objective) but to check perceptions explicitly against the other side, which is what the communication-gap tools below are for.

Closing the communication gap: question, paraphrase, repeat

Intuition: close the gap What you said and what they heard are rarely the same thing. The fix is cheap: say it back. Ask, restate in your own words, and confirm, so the misunderstanding surfaces now (when it costs a sentence) rather than later (when it costs the deal).
Active-listening tools to reduce the communication gap
  • Question: probe what they actually mean and want, instead of assuming. Questions also pull interests out from behind positions.
  • Paraphrase: restate their point in your own words ("so what you're saying is..."). If you have it wrong, they correct you for free; if right, they feel heard.
  • Repeat / confirm: play back the key points and agreements so both sides are demonstrably on the same page before moving on.
These are the direct antidote to partisan perception: you cannot tell you are reading the facts differently until you state your reading aloud and let the other side react.
🎙 In class Soyer framed part of the listening toolkit as tactical empathy: "you come and you tap the other party's shoulder... and the other person says 'I didn't hear what you said'. This is how you make people talk more than they intended to." Acknowledging and labelling the other side's position (rather than countering it) keeps them talking, and the more they talk, the more interests and constraints they reveal.

Saying "no" without breaking the relationship

Soyer opened the cluster with a warm-up exercise built entirely around declining: one partner asks (to work two weekends, then for $50,000), the other has to refuse, and then they switch, so everyone practises both giving and refusing. The point is that a flat "no" is a blunt instrument that bruises the relationship and invites retaliation; you can decline while keeping the door, and the working relationship, open.

The "say no" warm-up (in-class exercise) Genuine techniques that surfaced in the room:
  • Reframe the refusal as "I'm not convinced." Soyer's reference: Joschka Fischer, German Foreign Minister, who in 2003 told the US case for the Iraq invasion "I am not convinced." It rejects the substance without rejecting the person, and it invites them to give you more (a better case) rather than slamming the door.
  • Refuse from a constraint, not a whim. "My hands are tied on this... I feel like I've already committed." Locating the no in an external constraint depersonalises it.
  • Empathise while refusing. "That's a bit tough, you've been in a difficult situation." Acknowledge their position even as you hold yours.
  • Make "no" the default you have to be talked out of, rather than a yes you give away.

PARTNERSHIP NEGOTIATIONS

The SUMMARY deck pairs this cluster's case with partnership negotiations, whose defining trait is that both sides take things personally (often literally family or long-standing partners), there are no industry "standards" to fall back on, and the relationship carries baggage. The danger is optimism: partners assume goodwill will carry them, so they skip the hard conversations, and the damage when it goes wrong can be irreversible.

Soyer's partnership-negotiation playbook
  • Don't let optimism prevent the key discussions on risks. Precisely because everyone is hopeful at the start, the "what if this goes wrong" conversation gets skipped; have it anyway.
  • Conduct a yearly vision & objective meeting (especially on the financials), so divergence is caught early rather than discovered in a crisis.
  • Put the key details of the shareholder agreement in writing. Handshake clarity decays; written commitments survive memory and mood.
  • Solve key relationship issues before going to substance. Unresolved personal tension poisons every substantive item.
  • Keep an eye on fairness: damage to a partnership can be irreversible, so a "win" that leaves a partner feeling taken is a loss.
  • Communicate carefully when things get heated, and either prevent escalations or agree de-escalation rules in advance.

Keeping your cool: emotion management and reputation

Intuition: emotions are data A flash of anger or anxiety (yours or theirs) is not noise to be suppressed; it is information about what matters. Read it, name it, and decide what to do with it, rather than being driven by it.
Emotions are information. Treat a strong reaction as a signal: it tells you which issue is loaded, where a value or identity is at stake, or that the other side feels cornered. Keeping your cool is not going numb; it is staying able to use emotion (yours and theirs) instead of being hijacked by it, especially in the heated partnership and refusal moments above.
The myth that a tough reputation always pays. Being known as hard-nosed has real upside (people expect you to push, so they pre-concede), but it has matching costs: counterparts brace for a fight, withhold information, refuse to create value with you, and may decline to deal at all or retaliate later. A tough reputation is a trade-off to manage, not a free asset, and it is especially corrosive in the repeated, relationship-heavy deals (partnerships, internal, cooperation) where you will meet the same people again.
Don't let a bad start, or a hot moment, ruin a good future deal. When emotions spike, the instinct is to win the exchange in front of you. But complexity plus partisan perception means the "outrage" you feel may rest on a misreading of the facts. Pause, check the perception (question, paraphrase, repeat), and protect the relationship, because most of these negotiations repeat.

Don't let a bad start ruin a good future deal

Intuition: recovering from a poor opening When the other side comes in angry, lowballing, or hostile, your instinct is to match it: escalate, defend, prove you can't be pushed around. That instinct is what turns a bad first ten minutes into a dead deal. The skill is to treat the opening as noise, not signal, and give the other side a graceful way back to the table.

The deck frames the bad start as a decision, not a reflex. One party cannot contain their frustration; you now choose between escalation and mitigation, and the slide is explicit that each carries its own risks and rewards. Escalation can establish a boundary but can also burn the deal; mitigation can rescue the deal but can also read as weakness. Soyer's recommended default is the win-win choice: give them the opportunity to back down without losing face.

Why a bad start escalates (the loop)
One side vents frustration → the other reads it as an attack and responds in kind, because matching feels like self-protection → each retaliation confirms the other's belief that this counterpart is hostile → positions harden and the conversation becomes about winning the fight, not the deal → tunnel vision sets in: both sides stop seeing the value still on the table and walk away from a deal that was there. The mechanism is emotional reciprocity feeding a self-fulfilling story about the other side.
How a pause breaks the loop
Call a break → the physiological heat of the moment cools, so neither side is acting from the amygdala → the retaliation reflex loses its trigger, breaking the reciprocity chain → with distance, each side can re-read the opening as frustration rather than attack → you can re-enter aiming for creativity from both sides, and optionally change the person who negotiates on each side so the new pair carries none of the personal baggage from the bad start. The why: the conflict was attached to people and to a moment; remove the moment and swap the people, and the substance is still negotiable.
Dealing with a bad start (Soyer's playbook)
  • Diagnose the choice: escalation or mitigation. Weigh the risks and rewards of each before reacting, rather than escalating on autopilot.
  • Prefer the win-win choice: give them the opportunity to back down. A graceful exit from their own aggression costs you nothing and saves the deal.
  • Pause. Decide deliberately on the timeframe of the break (the Nutrina role-play even allows the negotiation to become two separate meetings).
  • Aim for creativity from both sides after the cool-down, not just a return to the same fight.
  • Change the person who negotiates, on both sides, so the reset is genuine and not poisoned by who said what.
  • The extreme version: can you turn them into a big fan of yours? The most complete recovery is not just salvaging the deal but flipping the relationship.

Avoid tunnel vision: pauses and teams

Intuition: tunnel vision Under pressure your attention narrows to the one number, the one grievance, the one move you are fixated on, and you stop seeing everything else on the table. Two things widen the lens back out: stepping away (a pause), and another set of eyes (a teammate). Both are deliberately built into this cluster.

The strategic summary names the antidote directly: avoid tunnel vision by using pausing and teams. The pause is the time-axis fix (distance from the heat of the moment); the team is the people-axis fix (a second perspective in the room). They are the same idea applied to the two ways your view narrows.

Negotiating in teams

Intuition: why teams cut both ways Adding a teammate is not free upside. You gain brainpower, a second read on the room, and the ability to split roles, but you also create a coordination problem: two people can contradict each other, leak information, or fall into cosy agreement that nobody challenges. A team is a tool you have to manage, not an automatic advantage.

Managing a negotiating team
  • Assign roles before you walk in: one speaks, the other observes. The observer catches what the speaker, deep in the exchange, will miss.
  • Agree what will be communicated and when. The deck's cautionary tag is the "Rumi incident": a teammate revealing the wrong thing at the wrong time. Decide your information plan in advance.
  • Engineer against groupthink: take a break for diversified focus, and treat too much internal harmony as a warning sign rather than comfort.
The myth that more people automatically means a better outcome. A team's headline benefit (more brainpower) and its core risk (coordination failure) are the same fact seen twice. Without assigned roles, an agreed information plan, and a deliberate guard against groupthink, the extra people become extra leaks, extra internal conflict, and diffused responsibility. Don't confuse adding bodies with adding capability; an unmanaged team negotiates worse than a disciplined individual.

Value claiming vs creating: the top tactics

The deck revisits the create-versus-claim distinction from Sessions 5–6 (creating value grows the pie; claiming value divides it) and surfaces the three tactics Soyer ranks highest for doing both, plus the team as a fourth lever.

Top 3 value tactics (plus teams)
  • Breaks. A pause is not dead time; it lets both sides cool down, reflect, and return more creative. It is the same instrument that rescues a bad start, used proactively.
  • Contingency. When the two sides disagree about a future fact, write a contingent term ("if X happens, then Y") instead of arguing the forecast. It lets each side bet on its own optimism and creates value out of the disagreement itself.
  • Bundle / unbundle. Combine issues into packages, or split one issue into parts, so that variables you value differently can be traded across. Bundling is how differing interests become joint gains.
  • Teams. The fourth lever: more minds find more options to create, and role-splitting helps you claim without losing the room.

The fairness of "creative" tactics

Intuition: clever is not the same as fair Some value tactics are genuinely creative; some are just clever ways to mislead. The same move (shaping what the other side believes) can be legitimate framing or a straight lie depending on which side of an ethical line it falls. The deck's instruction is not "be nice", it is: know where that line is, because some popular tactics quietly cross it, and the damage to your reputation and the relationship can be irreversible.

This is where the cluster's readings live (on ethics, the truth about lies in negotiations, the risks of brainstorm negotiating, and the persuasive power of transparency). The ideas, not the articles, fold in here.

Acceptable vs unacceptable tactics: the working distinction the ethics reading turns on. Hard but acceptable: anchoring firmly, arguing from a standard that favours you, declining to volunteer information, walking away. Not acceptable: actively misrepresenting a material fact (your BATNA, the product, a number you will or won't accept). The test is roughly whether the tactic shapes the other side's judgement within a shared game, or corrupts the facts the game is played on. Aggression is permitted; deception about substance is not.
Lies of commission vs lies of omission. Don't assume "I never said anything false" puts you in the clear. A lie of commission is an active false statement ("we have another offer at 200" when you don't). A lie of omission is staying silent while the other side relies on a belief you know to be wrong. The first is almost always over the line; the second is the genuinely grey zone, and it is where most negotiators quietly fool themselves. There is no duty to volunteer everything, but exploiting a known false belief about a material fact is closer to a lie than to shrewdness. When in doubt, the relationship and your reputation outlast the single deal.
The persuasive power of transparency. The counter-intuitive point from the transparency reading: openness is not just the ethical option, it is often the more effective one. Explaining the reasoning behind your offer, naming your constraint, or admitting a weakness can persuade better than concealment, because it gives the other side a reason to believe you and something concrete to engage with. Transparency builds the trust that makes value creation possible at all, and it is the honest twin of using a legitimate standard: both work by giving the other side a reason they can accept rather than a position they must simply swallow.

Fairness norms and legitimacy

Intuition: fairness is leverage, not sentiment People will reject a deal that is good for them if it feels unfair, and accept a worse one that feels fair. So fairness is not a soft add-on to the numbers; it is part of what determines which numbers the other side will actually sign. An outcome anchored to an external standard is one both sides can defend to themselves and to their bosses.

This links straight back to Legitimacy, the fourth of the 7 Elements (Interests, Alternatives, Options, Legitimacy). Legitimacy is the use of objective standards and fair criteria (market rates, precedent, an independent benchmark) to decide what each side will accept. Perceived fairness of both the outcome and the process shapes the zone of agreement: a number that clears both reservation prices can still die if one side feels cornered, and a fair process can rescue a deal where the substance is tight. This is exactly why fairness sits next to ethics in this cluster: the same legitimacy that makes your claiming persuasive is also what keeps it on the right side of the ethical line.

Cooperation negotiations (the Nutrina case)

Intuition: negotiate the future, not the past In an ongoing relationship (a supplier, a partner, a long-term customer) the temptation is to relitigate every old grievance. But you cannot change the past, only the future. The whole orientation of a cooperation negotiation is to spend your energy on the relationship and the deal still ahead, not on winning the argument about what already went wrong.

Nutrina is a cooperation negotiation: an ongoing supply relationship (Vital Materials and Nutrina) where many win-wins exist (the deck names expansion and wheat flour among them), but where frustration and power imbalance can poison the room. The deck's diagnosis and prescription:

Cooperation negotiations

11–12 · Relationship & Trust

This is the cluster where the soft variables stop being a footnote and become the deal. Earlier sessions taught you to discover interests, design options and claim value; here Soyer asks what happens when you have to do all of that with someone you may not like, may not trust, and will have to face again next week. The vehicle was an internal role-play (a newly-promoted manager negotiating with a senior colleague who feels passed over and wronged), plus a behavioural trust game played live in pairs. The throughline: pursue joint gains first, protect the working relationship without bleeding away real value to be liked, and treat trust as something built slowly, broken instantly, and rebuilt only through acknowledgement and consistency.

Intuition: what trust is Trust is the willingness to make yourself vulnerable to another person while expecting they will not exploit it. You hand over something (information, a concession, the first move) that they could abuse, betting that they will reciprocate instead. That is why trust is fragile: a single exploitation tells you the bet was wrong, and you pull your vulnerability back. Reciprocity (responding in kind, returning a favour for a favour) is the engine that makes the bet pay off.
🎙 In class On how trust comes back after it breaks: "It just takes time. The other person has to show consistently that they're up to their word, whatever they say they will do, they end up doing it. And over time I have my trust back. But it's not overnight at all." Trust erodes in one move and rebuilds only over many.

Constructive behaviours: pursue joint gains first

Soyer's opening instruction for relationship-heavy negotiations is sequencing, not softness. Look for what makes you better together before you touch the points where you are pulling against each other. In the internal case the punchline of the debrief was that there was a large amount of win-win available (the two parties were genuinely better off collaborating on the project), and the relationship tension, though real, did not have to derail it.

Why joint gains come first
Open on shared interests and mutual upside → both sides experience the other as a partner solving a problem, not a threat → this builds a stock of goodwill → so when you reach the genuinely contested points (the common obstacles), there is a relationship to spend, and neither side reads a hard ask as an attack. Reverse the order (lead with the fight) and you spend goodwill you never accumulated.

Substance vs relationship: don't consistently trade one for the other

Intuition: the trade-off Every move trades on two axes at once: how much substance you claim (the real value: money, terms, scope) and how much relationship you build or preserve (being liked, trusted, kept as a partner). Giving something away to keep the peace is fine occasionally. Doing it consistently is a slow leak: you train the other side that your goodwill is for sale and you end up liked and poor.
Soyer's rule: don't consistently exchange substance for relationship. The danger is the well-meaning negotiator who, every time things get tense, concedes real value to smooth the mood. One concession to repair a relationship can be a good investment. A pattern of concessions is a habit the other side learns to exploit. The goal is the top-right quadrant: claim value and keep the relationship, by trading across differing interests rather than buying affection with substance.

Trust: how it is built and how it erodes

Soyer ran the class as a confessional: what kills your trust in someone, and what (if anything) brings it back. The answers, drawn from the room, cluster cleanly. Notice the asymmetry: the breakers are single, sharp acts; the rebuilders are slow and require the offender to do work.

What breaches trust

  • Lying, even about something minor. A small needless lie is a "wrecking ball": if they lie about this, what about the big things?
  • No-shows and broken commitments, especially repeated. Once with an apology is forgivable; four times is a pattern.
  • Deflecting / never giving a straight answer. A clear "no" is fine; refusing to answer is not.
  • Not following through on a promise.
  • Throwing you under the bus: agreeing a shared line, then deviating in front of the boss to dump the blame on you.
  • Not reciprocating when you went out of your way for them.
  • Too good to be true: the salesperson who promises the world. Soyer trusts these people less.

What rebuilds it

  • Explicit acknowledgement (an "act of contrition"): saying out loud, plainly, that you did or did not do the thing.
  • Shared understanding of what went wrong: both sides hear each other's version. If the offender never acknowledges it, trust does not return.
  • Consistency over time: doing what you said, repeatedly, until the record speaks for itself.
  • A second chance (people do forget; using it as gentle leverage is fine).
  • A common enemy: "amazing" at re-bonding former opponents.
  • Changing the people or the roles: a fresh negotiator can rekindle trust two soured parties cannot.
  • Changing the context: a new city, a new financial year, "the game has changed."
  • Transitive trust / vouching and low-downside test deals to probe compatibility safely.
🎙 In class On repair: "If they don't acknowledge, that really kills trust. They have to acknowledge at least and tell you their story. But they have to listen to you." Acknowledgement plus mutual listening, not just an apology, is the mechanism.

THE TRUST GAME (the investment exercise)

To make the abstraction concrete, Soyer ran the classic behavioural-economics trust (investment) game live, in silent pairs, then with talking, then with the roles reversed.

The trust game, rules and what happened One player is the Investor (holds $10), the other the Receiver. The Investor secretly chooses how much of the $10 to send. Whatever is sent is tripled on the way over. The Receiver then chooses how much of that tripled sum to return. Game ends; totals are tallied.

Round 1 (silent, one-shot, strangers): with no relationship and no future, several investors sent $0 ("I don't know anybody, I'll give zero") and some receivers planned to return nothing. The pairs who did trust mostly landed on a fairness split: invest 10, receiver now holds 30, returns 15, both walk with 15+.

Later rounds (talking allowed, repeated): cooperation jumped once people could communicate and knew they would play again. Investors put more in; receivers reciprocated, often splitting fairly.

Final round (roles reversed): some receivers used their turn as Investor to repay earlier generosity ("I was unfair to you, let me be fair now"). Soyer's tell: "Some games are a repeatance, even if they seem not." The lesson he wanted: even a "last" round is rarely the last interaction, so reciprocate.
Why trust is hard in the one-shot game, and what fixes it
One-shot, anonymous, no communication → the Receiver has no reason to return anything (no future, no relationship) → the Investor anticipates this and sends $0 → both end near $10 each, far below the $15+ they could have shared. Now add the fixes: communication lets you signal intent and agree a norm; repetition creates a shadow of the future where defecting today costs you tomorrow; reciprocity rewards the first mover so vulnerability pays. Each is exactly the lever you pull in a real relationship to move a counterpart from defect to cooperate.

Internal negotiations

The hardest negotiations are often the ones inside your own organisation: with a colleague, a peer, a boss, a direct report. The stakes feel personal, the relationship is permanent, and there is no clean BATNA (your Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement, your walk-away option) because you both still work there on Monday.

Internal negotiations, issues and strategies
  • Issue 1: people take it personally. The line between the issue and the relationship blurs; a disagreement on substance gets read as a personal attack.
  • Issue 2: office politics and power (im)balance. Formal hierarchy, informal influence and history all sit on the table whether named or not.
  • Be prepared: recognise that an ordinary conversation may in fact be a negotiation, and treat it as one.
  • Understand the legitimacy; ask why; check their KPIs. A colleague's stance usually traces to what they are measured on. KPIs (Key Performance Indicators, the metrics their performance is judged against) reveal the underlying interest behind the position.
  • Pause to make both parties think about the other side. Deliberately force empathy in both directions before pushing for terms.
  • Don't reach out to others only when you need something. Relationships you only touch transactionally won't be there when you need them.
  • Avoid trust-eroding strategies. They are far more costly internally, where you cannot walk away from the counterpart.

The aggrieved-colleague dynamic (the internal role-play)

The case put a newly-promoted manager opposite a senior colleague who believes, with some justification, that he was wronged: he did much of the work the new role was built on, he was passed over, and he is bitter enough that he could quit or quietly sabotage. The relationship was designed to be bad. The teaching point is that even then, the substance was full of win-win, and the negotiators who reached good deals were the ones who refused to let the resentment dictate the outcome.

Handling a counterpart who feels wronged Soyer contrasted two played-out versions. The bad one: the manager keeps asserting status ("as the new head of...") and pushing the agenda, which fuels the colleague's resentment and goes nowhere. The good one: the manager treats the hostility as a symptom, not an insult. She keeps every option open (including, later, parting ways), but she buys the information and the relationship first.
Separate the person from the problem. The colleague's anger is data about an unmet interest (recognition, fairness, status, a real disagreement on strategy), not a verdict on you. Acknowledging the grievance ("I hear that you feel passed over, and I can see why") is not the same as conceding the substance. You can validate the feeling, surface the interest beneath it, and still hold your line on the decision. Acknowledgement is cheap, it repairs the relationship, and it does not cost you the deal.

13–14 · Commitment & Conclusions

This is the closing cluster and the course wrap-up. Soyer uses the final sessions to do three things: tighten the last stage of any deal (how you commit, so a "yes" becomes a durable, implementable agreement rather than a handshake that unravels); run one more case, the Boost M6700 sales role-play, precisely because money-heavy deals are the ones most likely to collapse into win-lose; and then flag the advanced terrain the course did not have time to case (virtual, multiparty, culture, gender, generation, career) before pulling the whole arc back to its spine. The Boost case was a sales negotiation between a seller ("John", a salesperson under quota pressure facing his second consecutive failure) and a buyer ("Cindy", a consulting firm with a roughly $210K budget for around 50 high-end laptops, with leasing and various discount schemes in play).

Intuition: closing the deal A negotiation is not finished when both sides nod. The last move is commitment, and it has its own failure modes: lock in too fast and you leave value (or sign something you regret); let the other side bolt on demands at the very end and the deal you thought you had quietly changes shape; agree verbally and "yes" turns out to mean different things to each of you. Treat the close as a stage to be planned, not a formality to rush through.
🎙 In class "Go into the money and get out of it. When you get stuck, get out of it, build a bit of relationship, and then come back to the money." Soyer's instruction for the whole sales case: the deal is 70–90% numbers, so you must deliberately step out of the numbers tunnel, repair the relationship, and step back in, rather than grinding price to exhaustion.

Commitment: speed, sequence, and the nature of the agreement

Intuition: well-planned commitment "Well-planned commitment" is one of the seven elements of a win-win outcome. The point is that how and when you lock things in is itself a decision. A good agreement is one both sides can actually implement, that names who does what, and that survives contact with the future. A premature "yes" is worse than no yes, because now you are bound and still exposed.
The three nuances of commitment
  • Speed: don't lock in under time pressure. The instinct at the end of a long, tiring deal is to seal it fast; that is exactly when you accept a worse package or skip the check that the other side means what you mean. Slow the close down.
  • Sequence: commit to the whole package at the end, not issue-by-issue along the way. Conceding and "agreeing" one variable at a time gives away your trades and lets the other side bank each win before the deal is balanced. Keep issues open and provisional until you can commit to the bundle.
  • Nature of the agreement: aim for a durable, implementable agreement, not just a verbal "yes". People think they agree and then discover they did not. Pin down what was actually agreed (and, in higher-stakes deals, put the key details in writing) so the commitment holds.

Why premature commitment hurts
You feel time pressure or fatigue near the close → you commit fast to "be done" → you stop testing whether the other side's "yes" means the same as yours, and you stop looking for the trade that would have improved the package → the agreement is either ambiguous (it falls apart in implementation) or simply worse than the one a slower close would have produced. The fix: separate "we're close" from "we're committed", and don't let the clock dictate the terms.
The end-game nibble: don't let new demands at the close re-write the deal. A counterpart agrees on everything, then at the very end adds "and two more laptops, free." In the Boost case this broke several deals. The nibble works because you are psychologically already committed and don't want to lose the whole thing over a small ask. Soyer's menu of responses, none automatic: (1) trade for it ("you want that, then I want this"); (2) push it to the next negotiation ("fine this time, but I'll remember this", risky if the negotiator changes); (3) show your resentment without being offensive, so the move carries a cost; (4) reopen the whole thing ("if you're adding this at the last minute, let's renegotiate from scratch"), high-risk but legitimate for a deal you have to live with. The underlying rule: the trust clock starts ticking the moment a promise is made. If a demand or a promise lands that you can't honour, draw the line in the sand right there, before it sets.

Discuss multiple issues simultaneously, not one at a time

Intuition: put everything on the table at once If you settle issues one by one in sequence, each becomes a mini tug-of-war with a winner and a loser, and you can never trade across them. Put several issues on the table together and you can give ground on what you value cheaply in exchange for what you value highly. Simultaneous discussion is what makes "create then claim" mechanically possible.
Simultaneous vs sequential bargaining: discuss various issues together rather than closing them one at a time. In Boost this meant combining variables: some units bought and some leased, a mix of laptop models (not the top spec for everyone), payment timing, delivery, plus the buyer's repeat-purchase relationship. Each variable opened a trade that a single price fight would have missed. Sequential, single-issue bargaining collapses the deal to a series of zero-sum splits; simultaneous discussion turns it into a search for the package that clears both sides' interests.
Why simultaneous beats sequential
You and the other side value the various issues differently → if all issues are open at once, you can swap a concession on something cheap to you for a gain on something cheap to them → the package grows in total value → both sides can land above their walk-away point. Settle issue-by-issue and each one is split in isolation, so the trades never appear and the pie is never grown.

Sales negotiations (the Boost M6700 case)

Soyer ran a sales case last on purpose: it is the negotiation type where the slide says "financials play a large role, easy to get into win-lose", and both sides are anxious about leaving money on the table. The behaviour the class produced (90% of the discussion was numbers, half the deals went to a lease just to hit the seller's KPI, several broke on an end-game nibble) is exactly what makes sales the sternest test of everything taught earlier.

Intuition: sales is win-lose by default When the headline is a price, both sides assume every euro you gain is a euro they lose, so the deal slides into combat. The job is to resist that gravity: bring in the other issues, find the other side's real interests behind the budget, and keep the relationship alive through the number-fight, because most sales relationships repeat.
Sales negotiations: issues and strategies (slide)
  • Issues. Financials dominate, so it is easy to slip into win-lose; both parties are anxious about leaving money on the table.
  • Beware anchors, nudges and psychological tricks. There will be manipulation; keep returning to your main objectives and interests.
  • Know what you're buying / selling: and the market and the alternatives. Familiarise yourself with the product; Soyer: "I buy musical instruments and I hate the people selling them, they don't know what they're selling."
  • Estimate the other side's interests beyond budget. The salesperson has KPIs and quotas; the buyer has constraints and a career; talk relationship and come back to it.
  • Don't enter bargaining too early (it is hard to get out). Build relationship and understanding first; be a little cagey about your number early on.
  • Discuss various issues simultaneously, not sequentially. Mix lease and buy, models, timing.
  • Money can take many forms and schedules. Discounts, commission given as a discount, leasing, staged payment, delivery terms, bundling.
🎙 In class "I would acknowledge that I'm a seller and you're a buyer... I don't want to start with numbers. Can you tell me about your company and your vision? And I'd like to know you as a person." Soyer's model open for the seller: roughly 10 minutes on the company and the individual, 5 minutes on his own situation, and only then "now we're going into numbers, but let's not get stopped by numbers", naming the bargaining phase before entering it.
Anchors. An anchor is an opening number that drags the whole discussion toward itself. In sales it is everywhere, so notice it and hold your objectives. And if you anchor hard, legitimise it: "It's not recommended that you do extreme anchors. If you're going to, you should legitimise even the extreme anchor." An unjustified extreme number ("I want one million") makes the other side think you are bluffing, which "contaminates your other statements" and damages your reputation inside the negotiation. Equally, when you move off your anchor, legitimise the move ("I can do 20% off because this is part of a bigger, longer relationship"), or the other side learns that your numbers are arbitrary. Soyer is openly irritated by a counterpart who just says "I'll pay 80" with no reason: "give me why; that's not negotiation."
Nudges. A nudge steers your choice without an argument: "experts like you are buying this." It exploits the seller's information advantage about the product. Countering nudges is genuinely hard ("we gave a Nobel Prize to nudging in 2017"); the defence is two-step: (1) notice you are being nudged, then (2) go back to your objectives. Soyer: "It's like having a shopping list when you go to a supermarket. If you don't, you buy stuff because it's at eye level, because it smells nice. Go back to your shopping list, always." This is the practical reason the preparation tool exists: you write your interests down so you can return to them mid-fight. Note nudges cut both ways: a buyer can build a three-band menu (premium / standard / basic) so the seller's choice reveals their budget without ever being asked, an "elaborate nudge".
Other psychological tricks, and the "judgment from AI" trap. Don't badmouth a competitor outright: as a seller you have skin in the game, so trashing rivals reads as a cheap sales move and erodes trust ("you're not an independent evaluator"). Better to be honest about a real trade-off and show rather than tell (the live demo beats the assertion). And don't outsource your judgment to a single AI mid-deal: a student asked one model "would management accept this lease?" and got a flat "no". Soyer's warning: you asked an AI "for a judgment, not for a competition", and it doesn't know what you actually want, and on a real (unwritten) deal it can hallucinate constraints. Use AI to compute quickly; never to decide.
Boost M6700 debrief: what actually happened Roughly 90% of the discussion was price. The deals that worked mostly used leasing, because a lease was the only way for the seller to hit his KPI given the budget gap. Several deals broke on a nibble (a buyer adding "two more, free" at the very end). The seller's hidden facts, revealed at the end: John is under brutal quota pressure (second consecutive miss means he is out), so a commission can be given back to the buyer as a discount, and he has a past reputation for inflating list prices, which the buyer could legitimately raise as a trust issue (almost nobody did). Soyer's praise went to the pair who stepped out of numbers mid-deal to ask "what are you actually trying to get out of this?" and learned the seller wanted a Southeast Asia base, surfacing a non-price trade.
"Money can take many forms and schedules." The headline price is only one variable. Commission can be returned as a discount; a purchase can become a lease; payment can be staged or deferred; delivery can be immediate, warehouse-pickup, or three weeks; a repeat-purchase commitment can buy a better price now. Because each of these is valued differently by the two sides, every one of them is a potential trade, which is why the close in a sales deal should be a package, not a number.
Don't be too transparent about your number too early. A student asked: if the gap is huge, why not just show the budget sheet? Soyer's answer: in this classroom case there is a real piece of paper with a final number, so you can. In real life there is no such paper, so if you declare "this is all I have" and then budge even slightly, "I know you're lying about everything now." Be cagey about your number early; reveal interests and constraints first, hard figures later.

Further issues Soyer flags for the future

The course could not run cases on these (an advanced negotiation class owns that), so Soyer covered them as advice. Each gets the key risk and the adjustment.

Virtual: risk: thinner relationship signal over a screen. Adjustment: use it the other way. Soyer treats Zoom as a relationship-builder: his bookshelf, a DeLorean model, a cat wandering in become genuine conversation hooks he could never deploy in a suit across a table. Plus the tooling (auto-summaries) reduces the communication gap. Virtual has real advantages if you use the medium deliberately.
Multiparty: risk: alliances form that don't exist in two-party deals. Adjustment: the number-one rule is to decide first, are you the designer of the process or a player? If designer, shape the process to your benefit and to prevent the other parties forming coalitions against you. If a player, find out who the designer is, understand the process, and negotiate the process itself (e.g. "I want to talk to you before we all meet"). Mind the process at all times.
Culture: risk: a style clash read as rudeness, or as a tactic. Adjustment: distinguish country, company and even industry culture (tech vs banking vs consulting all differ), and adapt to the other side. Soyer's rule of thumb: when power is roughly balanced, both sides drift toward the average (e.g. a 5-minute small talk compromise). Examples raised: Germans direct vs Swedes opening with extended small talk; Gulf deals that take months of relationship-building vs transactional US deals; Chinese deals done over dinner and historically over drinking; Indian business email that is terse where Western email is warm. One caveat: he treats punctuality as "a bit above culture", so be timely regardless.

Gender: risk: an asymmetric perception bias. The same assertiveness that reads as "he cares" in a man can read as "she's emotional / too demanding" in a woman; older counterparts may dismiss a woman upfront. Adjustment: Soyer notes the gap has narrowed a lot (women now negotiate much more, and on average prepare better and are less overconfident, which helps), but the perception bias persists, so a woman often has to manage it actively, lean on the authority of her role, and pivot to the relationship to get past an upfront dismissal.
Generation: risk: a style mismatch (phone calls vs messaging) sours the start. Adjustment: don't let it. Older negotiators may carry ego or a midlife-crisis "I'll beat the next generation" streak, so don't take friction personally; you can even play it to advantage ("I'm learning from you") since people like to feel they are teaching.
Career: flagged only as a pointer: there is a dedicated mini-course on negotiating your career (and an Advanced Negotiation course) for anyone who wants to go further.

Course wrap-up: the spine of everything

Soyer closed by pulling the whole course back to a handful of through-lines that recur in every session and every case.

The spine of the course
  • Win-win vs win-lose is a choice, not a personality. Both carry risks and rewards; you decide which game to play, and you can change the game.
  • Interests over positions. Question stated demands back to the underlying needs; that is where trades live.
  • Create, then claim. Grow the pie (discover differing interests, discuss issues simultaneously) before dividing it.
  • The 7 Elements as the standard. Interests, alternatives (BATNA), options, legitimacy, commitment, relationship, communication: the checklist for a win-win outcome and the lens for learning from any deal. (BATNA = your best alternative to a negotiated agreement, your main source of power.)
  • Process and relationship carry the substance. Build relationship first, take breaks, go in and out of the numbers, and protect the relationship because most negotiations repeat.
🎙 In class "Use the 7 elements beyond this course. This is not a gimmick. We use this in real life with teams. Teach it to your team members, make them prepare your negotiation using the 7 elements." Soyer's closing instruction: the framework is a working tool, not an exam artefact, and preparation is where it pays off.
What Soyer rewards, what he flags as a mistake, and what to carry into the role-plays and the Negotiation Journal.
  • Plan the commitment. He rewards a deliberate close: slow down under time pressure, commit to the package not issue-by-issue, and make the agreement durable and explicit. He flags the premature "yes" and the unguarded end-game nibble as classic mistakes.
  • Go in and out of the numbers. He explicitly praises pairs who left the price tunnel to rebuild relationship and surface a non-price interest; he flags "grind, grind, grind" on price as the error.
  • In sales, return to your objectives. He rewards spotting anchors and nudges and going back to your "shopping list"; he flags entering bargaining too early, badmouthing competitors, and outsourcing judgment to a single AI.
  • Legitimise every number. Both your anchor and every move off it need a reason; he is visibly irritated by unjustified figures.
  • Estimate the other side's interests beyond the budget, and remember money takes many forms and schedules; he rewards bundling, leasing, staged payment, repeat-business trades.
  • Experiment in this safe space. He urges trying tactics you would never risk in real life (nibble, anti-nibble, the "no" exercise of deliberately seeking a refusal) and recording what fit you.
  • For the Negotiation Journal (deadline 12 June): Soyer's closing brief is to reflect across the whole course on what you want to keep doing and what you want to stop, run your experience through the 7 Elements, and (today, before leaving) give your counterpart one piece of feedback on what you would each do differently. The journal is graded reflection, not a write-up of who won.

Case-type playbooks

Soyer’s own situational checklists (from the course summary deck). Each role-play in the course exercised one of these. Same 7 Elements underneath, but the typical issues and the moves that work shift by situation.

Contract negotiations

Typical issues: both sides can take things personally; power imbalance. Moves that work: treat all contracts as negotiable; prefer a draft over the lawyers’ “standard” contract; use the negotiation to test the subsequent collaboration; think realistically about what could go wrong; assume the contact person can change, so put contingencies in writing (if-then).

Job negotiations (the WABTY role-play)

Typical issues: one side takes it more personally; power imbalance and “standards”. Moves that work: don’t underestimate likeability; avoid early positions and treating the offer as the only goal; the main task is finding the other side’s constraints and flexibilities; salary is not the whole deal; reveal the intent behind tough questions; treat the negotiation as a trial run of the working relationship.

Partnership negotiations (the PrimeGeo role-play)

Typical issues: both sides take it personally (family?), are over-optimistic, and carry baggage with no standards to anchor on. Moves that work: hold a yearly vision-and-objectives meeting (especially on finances); put key details of the shareholder agreement in writing; solve the key relationship issues before the substance; keep an eye on fairness (damage can be irreversible); communicate carefully when things heat up; either prevent escalations or agree de-escalation rules in advance.

Cooperation negotiations

Typical issues: bottled-up frustrations; power imbalance. Moves that work: anchor on the possible future, not the past; don’t let a bad start ruin a possible deal; assume there will be emotional reactions; understand the internal politics; beware weaponising your BATNA too early; use breaks and different people to keep ideas fresh; give and get frequent feedback and agree a communication style; preserve the working relationship and build a better model going forward.

Internal negotiations (the Indonesia role-play)

Typical issues: people take it personally (issue vs relationship); office politics and power imbalance. Moves that work: be prepared, because you may be in a negotiation without realising it; understand the other side’s legitimacy, ask why, check their KPIs to find the underlying interest; pause to make both parties think about the other side; don’t reach out only when you need something; avoid trust-eroding tactics.

Sales negotiations (the Boost M6700 role-play)

Typical issues: financials dominate, so it is easy to slip into win-lose; both sides are anxious about leaving money on the table. Moves that work: beware anchors, nudges and psychological tricks; know what you are buying and selling (and the market and alternatives); estimate the other side’s interests beyond budget; don’t enter bargaining too early; discuss several issues simultaneously, not one at a time; remember money can take many forms and schedules (terms, timing, bundling).

Myths Soyer explicitly debunks

Don’t believe the myths.

  • “Negotiations are either win-win or win-lose.” No: those are choices with risks and rewards, not fixed labels. Stop labelling, start choosing.
  • “Win-win needs common goals / friendship.” No: common goals are not necessary. Win-win means satisfying interests better than the alternatives, even between rivals.
  • “It always comes down to price.” No: reducing a deal to one number is what creates win-lose; almost always there are other issues to trade.
  • “A tough reputation pays off.” It has real costs (information flow, future deals), not just benefits.
  • “Brainstorming is always good.” Brainstorm negotiating carries risks (premature anchoring, leaking, false consensus).
