How to use this.

The course is one long answer to a single question, what price should a firm charge for its product?, and it builds in four parts. Read them in order; each one is the raw material for the next.

  • Part I, Markets & Demand (L1 to L3). The demand-and-supply engine: valuations and costs, the two surpluses, why competitive markets are efficient, what a tax does, and how you measure buyer responsiveness with elasticity.
  • Part II, Firm costs & pricing (L4 to L7). The cost side, then the central managerial tool: a firm with market power sets \(MR=MC\), which collapses to the Golden Pricing Rule. Then segmentation, explicit (L6) and implicit (L7), to extract more of the surplus.
  • Part III, Competition (L8 to L9). The firm with no power: \(P=MC\), the shutdown test, and the entry/exit dynamics that compete profit away to zero.
  • Part IV, Games & oligopoly (L10 to L13). Strategic interdependence: Nash equilibrium, commitment, Bertrand and Cournot, and how cartels form and fail. L14 is wrap-up only and is explicitly off the exam.

A one-paragraph orientation, a plain-language intuition box leading each dense idea, every equation from the slides (defined and, where derived, shown step by step), every diagram redrawn, worked numeric examples, and an "Exam lens" box at the end with how Glover tests that material.

Exam technique (distilled from the lecture exam-lenses, and the L10 / L12 transcript tells)
  • Every pricing answer starts from \(MR=MC\) (or \(P=MC\) for a price taker). Say which regime applies and why before you compute. Glover's closing line is literal: "All life's questions have a simple answer: \(MR=MC\)."
  • State the assumptions first (L12 tell). "Two firms drive price to marginal cost" is only true under Bertrand with homogeneous goods, constant \(MC\) and no capacity limit; it is false for differentiated goods or quantity (Cournot) competition. Name the regime, then answer.
  • Solve every payoff matrix column by column (L10 tell). Hold the rival's choice fixed, pick your best row, repeat for each column. Write the explicit comparison ("if rival plays X, I compare a vs b"); do not eyeball the grid. Dominant = best response in every column; dominated = best response in no column.
  • Do not over-generalise the prisoner's dilemma (L10 tell). The Nash equilibrium is Pareto inferior only in the PD specifically (GM/Ford is the counter-example). Payoffs are ordinal: argue the ranking, not the literal cash in a cell.
  • Threat versus commitment (L11). Test a threat at its own node: is carrying it out the threatener's best response? If not it is cheap talk. A commitment must be visible, costly and irreversible; "a reversible commitment is no commitment."
  • Phrase collusion as a present-value comparison (L12 tell), \(PV(\text{cooperate}) \gt PV(\text{cheat})\) as two perpetuities, not a one-shot payoff. Lower discount rate and harsher punishment sustain it.
  • Watch the recurring slips: marginal (curve height) vs total (area beneath); slope vs elasticity; statutory vs economic tax incidence; \(AVC\) (shutdown) vs \(ATC\) (exit); sunk capital (ignore for exit, count for entry).

Foundations: gains from trade, surplus & taxes [L1 to L2]

Marginal rule (the optimum condition)$$ MB\gt MC \Rightarrow \text{do more};\quad MB\lt MC \Rightarrow \text{do less};\quad MB=MC \Rightarrow \text{stop (optimal)} $$
Gains from a single trade$$ \underbrace{V-P}_{\text{buyer}} + \underbrace{P-C}_{\text{seller}} = \underbrace{V-C}_{\text{total surplus}} $$
Marginal valuation and marginal cost (discrete derivatives)$$ mv(Q)=v(Q)-v(Q-1),\qquad mc(Q)=c(Q)-c(Q-1) $$
Consumer and producer surplus$$ CS = v(Q)-P\cdot Q,\qquad PS = P\cdot Q - c(Q) $$
Equilibrium (two equivalent statements)$$ d(P)=s(P)\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \text{trade every unit with } mv(Q)\ge mc(Q) $$
Surplus at the competitive equilibrium$$ CS=\sum (mv-P^\*),\qquad PS=\sum (P^\*-mc),\qquad CS+PS \text{ maximised (Pareto efficient)} $$
Per-unit tax: incidence is independent of who remits$$ P_s=P_b-\tau,\quad d(P_b)=s(P_s);\qquad \text{tax revenue}=\tau\,Q_\tau,\qquad DWL=\tfrac12\,\tau\,(Q^\*-Q_\tau) $$

Demand & elasticity [L3]

Own-price elasticity (point form; \(\varepsilon\lt 0\), reported as \(E=|\varepsilon|\))$$ \varepsilon=\frac{\%\Delta Q}{\%\Delta P}=\frac{dQ}{dP}\cdot\frac{P}{Q} $$
Elasticity and total revenue$$ |\varepsilon|\lt 1\ (\text{inelastic}): P\uparrow\Rightarrow TR\uparrow;\quad |\varepsilon|=1: TR\text{ flat};\quad |\varepsilon|\gt 1\ (\text{elastic}): P\uparrow\Rightarrow TR\downarrow $$
Income and cross-price elasticity (sign classifies the good)$$ \varepsilon_I=\frac{dQ}{dI}\frac{I}{Q}\ (\gtrless 0:\text{normal/inferior}),\qquad \varepsilon_{xy}=\frac{dQ_x}{dP_y}\frac{P_y}{Q_x}\ (\gtrless 0:\text{substitute/complement}) $$
Log-log (constant-elasticity) demand: coefficients ARE the elasticities$$ \log Q = B_0 + B_1\log P + B_2\log I + \dots,\qquad \varepsilon=B_1,\ \varepsilon_I=B_2,\ \dots $$

Costs [L4]

Linear demand and its choke price$$ Q=A-B\,P\quad\Rightarrow\quad P_c=\frac{A}{B}\ (\text{set }Q=0) $$
Point elasticity on a linear demand curve (slope constant, elasticity not)$$ E=\frac{B\,P}{A-B\,P}=\frac{P}{P_c-P}\qquad(E=1\text{ at }P=\tfrac12 P_c) $$
Cost decomposition and the per-unit objects$$ TC=FC+VC,\quad AFC=\frac{FC}{Q},\ AVC=\frac{VC}{Q},\ ATC=AFC+AVC,\ MC=\frac{dTC}{dQ} $$
Marginal cuts average at its minimum$$ MC\lt ATC\Rightarrow ATC\downarrow;\quad MC\gt ATC\Rightarrow ATC\uparrow;\quad MC=ATC\text{ at }\min ATC $$

Market-power pricing [L5 to L7]

Marginal revenue (price minus the margin given up on existing units)$$ MR = P + \frac{dP}{dQ}\,Q = P\left(1-\frac{1}{|\varepsilon|}\right);\qquad\text{linear }P=a-bQ:\ MR=a-2bQ $$
Profit-maximisation rule (market power)$$ \frac{d\pi}{dQ}=MR-MC=0\ \Longrightarrow\ MR=MC\quad(\text{read }P^\*\text{ up to demand, not to }MR) $$
Lerner index / inverse-elasticity rule and the Golden Pricing Rule$$ \mathcal{L}=\frac{P-MC}{P}=-\frac{1}{\varepsilon}=\frac{1}{E}\qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad P^\*=\frac{E}{E-1}\,MC $$
Mid-point pricing rule (linear demand, constant \(MC\))$$ P^\* = \frac{\bar P + MC}{2}\qquad(\bar P=\text{choke price}) $$
Explicit (third-degree) segmentation: equate \(MR\) across segments$$ MR_A=MR_B=MC;\qquad \frac{P_A-MC}{P_A}=\frac{1}{|\varepsilon_A|}\Rightarrow |\varepsilon_A|\lt |\varepsilon_B|\ \text{means}\ P_A\gt P_B $$
Coupons (screen): issue when the marginal sale beats the redeemers' concession alone$$ MR_{\text{coupon}}=C-A,\qquad MR_{\text{uniform cut}}=C-A-B;\qquad \text{issue coupons if } C\gt A $$
Implicit (second-degree) screening: two binding constraints$$ \text{PC (low type): } v_L-P_{low}\ge 0;\qquad \text{SS (high type): } v_H^{high}-P_{high}\ge v_H^{low}-P_{low} $$
Screening menu solution (low type's PC binds, high type's SS binds)$$ P_{low}=v_L,\qquad P_{high}=v_H^{high}-\big(v_H^{low}-P_{low}\big) $$

Competitive supply [L8 to L9]

Price taker: revenue equals price; output rule$$ AR=MR=P\quad\Longrightarrow\quad P=MC(q) $$
Short-run supply and the shutdown test$$ q^S(P)=\begin{cases} q:\ P=MC(q), & P\ge \min AVC\\ 0\ (\text{idle}), & P\lt \min AVC \end{cases}\qquad\text{Market: } Q^S=n\,q^S $$
Producer surplus$$ PS = TR - VC = \text{area between } P \text{ and } MC = \pi + FC $$
Long-run free-entry equilibrium$$ \pi=(P-ATC)\,q=0\ \Longleftrightarrow\ P=ATC;\qquad P^\*=MC=\min ATC $$
The three price cutoffs (which cost, which horizon)$$ \text{idle if } P\lt AVC;\quad \text{exit if } P\lt ATC;\quad \text{enter if } P\gt AVC+AFC+\text{capital cost} $$
Entry break-even (price-linked input, Alusaf form) and the NPV identity$$ P\ge AC=\alpha P + (\text{AVC}+\text{AFC}+\text{capital})\ \Rightarrow\ P\ge\frac{\text{fixed+capital}}{1-\alpha};\qquad \pi=0 \Leftrightarrow NPV=0 $$

Games & oligopoly [L10 to L13]

Market concentration$$ \text{HHI}=\sum_i s_i^{\,2}\qquad(\gt 1000:\text{oligopoly};\ \gt 2000:\text{highly concentrated}) $$
Solution concepts$$ \text{Best response: best reply to a given rival choice};\ \text{Dominant: best in every column};\ \text{NE: mutual best responses} $$
Bertrand, homogeneous goods (the trap)$$ p_1=p_2=MC\qquad(\text{perfect-competition price with only two firms}) $$
Bertrand, differentiated goods: reaction functions (prices are strategic complements)$$ p_i^{\,choke}=\frac{a_i+\beta\,p_j}{b_i},\qquad p_i=\frac{p_i^{\,choke}+MC_i}{2}\ \Rightarrow\ p_i=\gamma_i+\delta_i\,p_j\ (\delta_i\gt 0) $$
Cournot (quantity) competition: FOC and reaction function$$ \pi_i=(a-q_i-q_j-c)\,q_i,\quad \frac{\partial\pi_i}{\partial q_i}=0\ \Rightarrow\ q_i=\frac{(a-c)-q_j}{2}\quad(P\gt MC\text{ in equilibrium}) $$
Collusion / cartel: maximise joint profit like a monopolist$$ MR=MC\ \text{on total }Q\ \Rightarrow\ Q_{cartel}\lt Q_{Cournot},\ P_{cartel}\gt P_{Cournot},\ \pi_{cartel}\gt \pi_{Cournot} $$
Grim-trigger: when does collusion hold?$$ PV(\text{cooperate})=\pi_c+\frac{\pi_c}{r}\ \gt \ PV(\text{cheat})=\pi_d+\frac{\pi_p}{r}\quad(\text{lower }r,\ \text{harsher }\pi_p \Rightarrow \text{sustainable}) $$

FOUNDATIONS: HOW ECONOMISTS THINK

🎙 In class Economics does not give you recipes, data, or definitive answers. It gives you frameworks of enduring value for pricing, entry, regulation and strategy. The honest answer to almost every strategic question is "It depends", and the framework tells you what it depends on.

Glover's Three Golden Rules, used relentlessly all term:

Incentives can defeat strategy. In the 1994 Shell Caribbean Cup, the rule "a golden goal in extra time counts double" plus "Barbados must win by two to advance" gave Barbados an incentive, at 2–1 up near full time, to score an own goal (forcing 2–2 and extra time, where a single golden goal = two), while Grenada were left trying to score at either end. Clever strategic play, but the designer set the wrong incentives.

Let \(MB\) be the marginal benefit (the change in benefit from a small increase in an activity) and \(MC\) the marginal cost (the change in cost from that same increase). The marginal rule that underlies every optimum in the course:

Marginal rule (the optimum condition) $$ MB \gt MC \Rightarrow \text{do more} \qquad MB \lt MC \Rightarrow \text{do less} \qquad MB = MC \Rightarrow \text{stop (optimal)} $$
Intuition: think at the margin Keep buying coffees while the next cup is worth more to you than it costs; stop at the cup where worth equals cost. The optimum is never "total benefit beats total cost"; it is the knife-edge where the next step breaks even.

GAINS FROM TRADE

Define a buyer's valuation \(V\) (her willingness to pay, WTP): the cutoff below which she wants to trade. Define a seller's cost \(C\): the cutoff above which he wants to trade. Let \(P\) be the price actually paid.

Intuition: gains from trade Trade is not zero-sum. When \(V \gt C\) there is a pie of size \(V-C\) that exists only if the trade happens. The price \(P\) does not create or destroy that pie; it only decides how the buyer and seller split it.
Gains from a single trade $$ \underbrace{V - P}_{\text{buyer's gain}} \;+\; \underbrace{P - C}_{\text{seller's gain}} \;=\; \underbrace{V - C}_{\text{total surplus}} $$

The pit market: equilibrium emerges from haggling

In the in-class "Pit Market", each buyer is dealt a card with a private valuation \(V\) and each seller a card with a private cost \(C\); prices are negotiated bilaterally in 50-cent increments, and zero earnings beat negative earnings. Run repeatedly, the scatter of trading prices converges to a single equilibrium price.

Price as a signal. In a well-functioning competitive market the equilibrium price reflects both sides at once: underlying sellers' costs and buyers' valuations. A price change therefore tells you something changed in demand (valuations), in supply (costs), or both. Prices are signals carrying an enormous amount of dispersed private information.
Intuition: bargaining vs the market Bilaterally, with equal bargaining power, a single buyer and seller tend to split the pie 50:50 (price midway between \(C\) and \(V\)). But that is not always the case: once many buyers and sellers compete, the split is pinned down by the market, not by who is the better haggler.
Worked example: a single trade A buyer with \(V=700\) meets a seller with \(C=100\) and they agree \(P=400\).
  • Buyer's gain: \(V-P = 700-400 = 300\)
  • Seller's gain: \(P-C = 400-100 = 300\)
  • Total surplus: \(V-C = 700-100 = 600\) (= 300 + 300) ✓
At \(P=400\) the pie of 600 happens to split 50:50; any \(P\) strictly between 100 and 700 still realises the full 600, only the division changes.

DEMAND, SUPPLY & EQUILIBRIUM

Let \(D\) be market demand and \(S\) market supply, with \(P\) on the vertical axis and \(Q\) on the horizontal. Stack the buyers from highest valuation to lowest: the resulting step curve is the demand curve. Stack the sellers from lowest cost to highest: that step curve is the supply curve.

Intuition: a curve is a sorted queue The demand curve is just every buyer lined up tallest-WTP first; at price \(P\) you draw a horizontal line and count how many heads poke above it (those are the buyers who still want to trade). Supply is the same trick with sellers sorted cheapest-cost first.

Demand, total and marginal valuation, consumer surplus

Let \(v(Q)\) be the total valuation of the first \(Q\) buyers (the area under the demand curve up to \(Q\)), and \(mv(Q)\) the marginal valuation of the \(Q\)-th unit (the height of the demand curve at \(Q\)).

Marginal valuation = discrete derivative of total valuation $$ mv(Q) = \frac{dv(Q)}{dQ} = v(Q) - v(Q-1) $$

Define consumer surplus \(CS\) as buyers' total gains from trade: the area below the demand curve and above the price line.

Consumer surplus $$ CS = v(Q) - P\cdot Q $$
Consumer surplus is large and real. Empirical estimates Glover cites: the internet is worth roughly $3,800 per user per year (≈ $8.7 bn aggregate, Goolsbee–Klenow), search ≈ $500 per user per year (Varian), and Uber generates about $1.60 of surplus per dollar spent, ≈ $6.8 bn in the US (Cohen et al.). The price you pay badly understates the value you get.

Supply, total and marginal cost, producer surplus

Symmetrically, let \(c(Q)\) be the total cost of the first \(Q\) sellers (area under the supply curve up to \(Q\)) and \(mc(Q)\) the marginal cost of the \(Q\)-th unit (height of supply at \(Q\)).

Marginal cost = discrete derivative of total cost $$ mc(Q) = \frac{dc(Q)}{dQ} = c(Q) - c(Q-1) $$
Producer surplus $$ PS = P\cdot Q - c(Q) $$
Don't confuse marginal with total. The height of the demand or supply curve at \(Q\) is the marginal valuation or marginal cost of that single unit; the area under it up to \(Q\) is the total valuation or total cost. \(CS\) and \(PS\) are areas (height differences accumulated), never single heights.

Equilibrium

Equilibrium is the price and quantity where demand meets supply, \(d(P)=s(P)\). Equivalently, by the marginal rule, keep trading as long as the marginal valuation of the next unit covers its marginal cost, \(mv(Q)\ge mc(Q)\); stop at the last such unit.

WHY ARE MARKETS GOOD?

Intuition: the invisible hand as a calculator Imagine a central planner ("Czar of agriculture") who must produce 1m bushels of wheat at minimum cost. He cannot see each farmer's costs. The market price does the planner's job for free: every farmer optimises privately against the same price, and the outcome is exactly the cost-minimising allocation the planner wanted.

Suppose Farmer Brown has \(MC=\$5\) and Farmer Smith has \(MC=\$7\). The current allocation is not cost-minimising: shift one bushel from Smith to Brown and total cost falls by \(\$7-\$5=\$2\). Keep reallocating until marginal costs are equal across all farmers. That is the condition for productive efficiency.

How the market reaches it without a planner. Every farmer faces the same price \(P\) and applies the marginal rule: \(P\gt MC\Rightarrow\) produce more, \(P\lt MC\Rightarrow\) produce less, \(P=MC\Rightarrow\) optimal. Since all set \(P=MC\), all end with the same \(MC\) (= \(P\)) automatically. The single price equalises marginal cost across every producer and induces the socially optimal quantity: productive efficiency (least-cost production) plus allocative efficiency (the right amount produced). This is Adam Smith's invisible hand.

Pareto efficiency (two equivalent statements) $$ \text{No one can be made better off without making someone worse off} \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \text{all gains from trade are realised} $$
The welfare result. At the competitive equilibrium, \(CS+PS\) is maximised: every trade with \(V\ge C\) happens and no trade with \(V\lt C\) does. A competitive market is therefore Pareto efficient. This is the benchmark against which every later distortion (market power, taxes, externalities) is measured.

TAXES: INCIDENCE & DEADWEIGHT LOSS

Now introduce a per-unit sales tax \(\tau\). In the in-class experiment a $2 tax on sellers means each seller must add $2 to the number on his card before computing earnings; equivalently it is a $2 increase in every seller's cost, so the negotiated price must be at least $2 above the card.

Intuition: the tax wedge A per-unit tax drives a wedge between what the buyer pays, \(P_b\), and what the seller keeps, \(P_s\). The wedge is exactly \(\tau\). Some marginal trades that were just barely worth doing (\(V\) only a little above \(C\)) now fail to clear the wedge and simply do not happen. Those vanished trades are pure lost value.

Tax incidence is independent of who remits (\(P_b\) = buyer price, \(P_s\) = seller price) $$ \text{Tax on sellers: } P_s = P_b - \tau,\;\; d(P_b)=s(P_s) \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad \text{Tax on buyers: } P_b = P_s + \tau,\;\; d(P_b)=s(P_s) $$

Both legal arrangements produce the same two equations, hence the same \(P_b\), \(P_s\) and \(Q\). The economic burden is split by the relative steepness (later: elasticity) of demand and supply, not by who hands the cash to the government.

Tax levied on sellers
add \(\tau\) to each cost → supply curve shifts up by \(\tau\) → buyers pay \(P_b\), sellers keep \(P_s=P_b-\tau\) → quantity falls to \(Q_\tau\)
Tax levied on buyers
subtract \(\tau\) from each valuation → demand curve shifts down by \(\tau\) → sellers get \(P_s\), buyers pay \(P_b=P_s+\tau\) → quantity falls to the same \(Q_\tau\)
Worked example: the Pit Market with a $2 tax Full Pit Market (51 buyers, 51 sellers). Reading equilibrium off the aggregated demand and supply:
  • No tax: equilibrium price \(P^*=\$5\), equilibrium quantity \(Q^*=27\).
  • With \(\tau=\$2\): price buyers pay \(P_b=\$6\), price sellers receive \(P_s=\$4\) (wedge \(P_b-P_s=\$2=\tau\) ✓), quantity falls to \(Q_\tau=21\).
  • Trades destroyed: \(Q^*-Q_\tau = 27-21 = 6\).
  • Tax revenue \(=\tau\times Q_\tau = 2\times 21 = \mathbf{\$42}\).
  • Deadweight loss (triangle approx.) \(=\tfrac12\,\tau\,(Q^*-Q_\tau) = \tfrac12\times 2\times 6 = \mathbf{\$6}\).
The DWL is the gains from trade on the 6 marginal trades that no longer clear the wedge; the triangle formula is the smooth-curve approximation of that sum.
Don't confuse statutory with economic incidence. "A tax on sellers" and "a tax on buyers" of the same \(\tau\) give identical \(P_b\), \(P_s\) and \(Q\). Who is legally obliged to remit the tax is irrelevant to who actually bears it. A T/F question that hinges on "the tax was placed on the seller, so the seller pays it" is false.
Two lessons from the tax exercise. (1) A per-unit sales tax destroys the gains from trade on the marginal units, creating a deadweight loss. (2) A per-unit tax has the same effect whether the buyer or the seller is responsible for paying it.
Exam lens. This session is the calculation-and-trap foundation of the course.
  • Calculation: given a short list of buyer valuations and seller costs, build the demand and supply schedules, find \(Q^*\) (last unit with \(mv\ge mc\)) and \(P^*\), then compute \(CS=\sum(mv-P^*)\), \(PS=\sum(P^*-mc)\), and total surplus. Adding a tax \(\tau\): find \(P_b\), \(P_s=P_b-\tau\), the new \(Q_\tau\), tax revenue \(\tau Q_\tau\), and \(DWL\).
  • T/F traps: "who remits the tax determines who bears it" (false); "the equilibrium price reflects only costs" (false, it reflects valuations and costs); "competitive equilibrium maximises \(CS+PS\)" (true, Pareto efficient); "a tax raises revenue at no cost to welfare" (false, DWL).
  • Definition watch: marginal valuation/cost = curve height; total = area beneath; surplus = the gap accumulated. Mixing these up is the most common slip.
Wrap-up. Demand is built from valuations; supply from costs; equilibrium is where \(d(P)=s(P)\). Both sides gain: \(CS\) is the area below demand and above price, \(PS\) the area above supply and below price. Competitive markets are Pareto efficient (all gains from trade realised, MC equalised across producers). Taxes that move costs or valuations are distortionary and impose a deadweight loss. 1 · Own-price elasticity of demand

Intuition: why not just use the slope? The slope of a demand curve, \(\mathrm{d}P/\mathrm{d}Q\), depends on the units you happen to measure price and quantity in (cents vs euros, cans vs crates), so it is useless for comparing across goods. Elasticity strips the units out by working in percentages: "if I raise price by 1%, by what % does quantity fall?" A pure number you can compare across New York and Los Angeles, across Amazon and Barnes & Noble, across petrol and books.
$$\varepsilon \;=\; \frac{\%\,\text{change in }Q}{\%\,\text{change in }P} \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{d}Q}{\mathrm{d}P}\cdot\frac{P}{Q}$$
Own-price elasticity (point form). \(\varepsilon\) is the percentage change in quantity demanded per one-percent change in own price.
Sign convention (do not lose this). Demand slopes down, so \(\mathrm{d}Q/\mathrm{d}P\lt 0\) and therefore \(\varepsilon\lt 0\) always. Glover reports elasticities as positive magnitudes by carrying a leading minus sign in the definition (his BMW slide writes "Price Elasticity \(=-\dfrac{\%\Delta Q}{\%\Delta P}=0.4\)"). So when a slide quotes "elasticity = 0.6", read it as \(|\varepsilon|=0.6\), i.e. \(\varepsilon=-0.6\). When this section uses \(|\varepsilon|\) it means the magnitude.

POINT VS DISCRETE CALCULATION

The boxed formula is the point elasticity (a derivative, the response at one price). In practice the slides also compute elasticity from two observed points using finite percentage changes, as in the carmaker case below.

Worked example: BMW / Daimler (FT, Sept 2021)
Chip shortages let carmakers push sticker prices up roughly 15% and slash discounts; the surprise was that revenue rose. The slide's illustrative elasticity: a 100% price increase produces a 40% fall in quantity demanded, so $$|\varepsilon| \;=\; -\frac{-40\%}{+100\%} \;=\; 0.4.$$ Because \(|\varepsilon|=0.4\lt 1\), demand is inelastic: quantity barely flinches, so raising price raises total revenue. BMW's operating margin moved 8.6% → 16.0% and Mercedes 8.4% → 12.2%.

ELASTICITY AND TOTAL REVENUE

Intuition: the revenue tug-of-war Revenue \(TR=P\times Q\). Raising price pulls \(P\) up but drags \(Q\) down; the net effect is just a race between the two percentage moves, and elasticity is the scoreboard. Inelastic (\(|\varepsilon|\lt 1\)): the price gain wins, revenue rises. Elastic (\(|\varepsilon|\gt 1\)): the quantity loss wins, revenue falls.
Inelastic demand, price ↑
\(P\uparrow\) ⟹ \(Q\) falls by a smaller % (buyers have few alternatives, so they swallow the rise) ⟹ \(TR\uparrow\). Hold prices high.
Elastic demand, price ↑
\(P\uparrow\) ⟹ \(Q\) falls by a larger % (buyers defect to substitutes) ⟹ \(TR\downarrow\). Better to cut price.
Worked example: Cola Wars, the Pepsi gambit (1933)
Pepsi president Guth bought recycled 12oz beer bottles, doubling volume per bottle.
  • First try (fails): sell the 12oz Pepsi at 10c, double the price of Coke's 6oz at 5c. Doubling the price drove buyers away: demand for soda was highly elastic, so the move lost volume and money.
  • The winning move: sell 12oz Pepsi at 5c, the same nickel as a 6oz Coke ("Twice as Much for a Nickel, Too"). In price-per-ounce terms this is a halving of price. Because demand was elastic, the huge quantity response sent revenue up: profits ran $2m (1936) → $4m (1938) and pulled Pepsi out of bankruptcy.
The "brilliant marketing" was really a textbook elastic-demand price cut.
Worked example: oil price volatility (appendix)
"When demand is inelastic, a small withdrawal of capacity leads to disproportionate increases in price." In 1979, roughly 5.68% of global production was withheld and prices jumped almost 100%. Implied magnitude \(|\varepsilon|\approx 5.68\%/100\% \approx 0.06\): extreme inelasticity, which is exactly why oil prices are so volatile to small supply shocks.

Traps. (1) Do not confuse slope with elasticity: a straight-line demand curve has constant slope but elasticity that varies along it. (2) Do not drop the sign: \(\varepsilon\) is negative; only quote it as positive once you have written it as \(|\varepsilon|\). 2 · Income and cross-price elasticity

Intuition: two more responsiveness dials Own-price elasticity answers "what if my price moves?". Income elasticity answers "what if my customers get richer?" and cross-price elasticity answers "what if a rival's (or a partner good's) price moves?". Same percentage logic, different variable on the bottom.
$$\varepsilon_I \;=\; \frac{\%\,\text{change in }Q}{\%\,\text{change in }I} \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{d}Q}{\mathrm{d}I}\cdot\frac{I}{Q}$$
Income elasticity. \(I\) is consumer income. Measures how demand for a good responds to a change in buyers' income.
$$\varepsilon_{xy} \;=\; \frac{\%\,\text{change in }Q_x}{\%\,\text{change in }P_y} \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{d}Q_x}{\mathrm{d}P_y}\cdot\frac{P_y}{Q_x}$$
Cross-price elasticity. \(Q_x\) is quantity of good \(x\); \(P_y\) is the price of a different good \(y\). Measures how demand for \(x\) responds to a price change in \(y\).
The sign IS the economics. For income and cross-price elasticity, the sign classifies the good. This is the taxonomy from the slide.

3 · Estimating demand (the estimation primer)

Intuition: you never see the demand curve Nobody hands you a demand curve; you see scattered (price, quantity) outcomes muddied by income, rivals' prices and the calendar. Estimation is reverse-engineering the curve from that mess. The modelling step (writing the equation before you fit it) is what makes the recovered numbers mean something.
The three-step procedure (slide). (1) Write down a model (an equation for demand with unknown coefficients). (2) Collect data, clean it, manipulate it. (3) Fit a line or curve to the data points using a statistical technique (regression).

FROM A SCARY DEMAND FUNCTION TO A LINEAR ONE

The assignment models petrol sales \(G\) as a multiplicative function of own price \(P_G\), income \(Y\), the price of new cars \(P_{NC}\), the price of used cars \(P_{UC}\) (both complements to petrol), and time (\(\text{Year}\)). The \(B_0\dots B_5\) are the unknown coefficients to be estimated.

$$G \;=\; e^{B_0}\,(P_G)^{B_1}\,Y^{B_2}\,(P_{NC})^{B_3}\,(P_{UC})^{B_4}\,e^{B_5\,\text{Year}}$$
Non-linear (constant-elasticity) demand model. \(G\) petrol quantity; lagged quantity \(G_{-1}\) is dropped for simplicity. Cannot be fed to linear regression as-is.

Take natural logs of both sides and the products become sums: the model is now linear in the coefficients and ready for regression.

$$\log G \;=\; B_0 + B_1\log P_G + B_2\log Y + B_3\log P_{NC} + B_4\log P_{UC} + B_5\,\text{Year}$$
Log-log demand model. Linear in \(B_0\dots B_5\), so ordinary regression recovers them.
Why log-log is beautiful: the coefficients ARE the elasticities.

WHY THE LOG-LOG COEFFICIENT EQUALS THE ELASTICITY (appendix)

  1. Start from elasticity in derivative form: \(\varepsilon=\dfrac{\mathrm{d}G/G}{\mathrm{d}P_G/P_G}\). percentage change in quantity over percentage change in price
  2. Rewrite using \(\mathrm{d}(\log x)=\mathrm{d}x/x\): \(\varepsilon=\dfrac{\mathrm{d}\log G}{\mathrm{d}\log P_G}\). a log-derivative IS a proportional change, by the natural-log property
  3. Differentiate the fitted log-log model \(\log G = B_0 + B_1\log P_G+\dots\) with respect to \(\log P_G\): \(\dfrac{\mathrm{d}\log G}{\mathrm{d}\log P_G}=B_1\). every other term is held fixed, so only the \(B_1\log P_G\) term survives
  4. Therefore \(\varepsilon = B_1 = \dfrac{\mathrm{d}G}{\mathrm{d}P_G}\cdot\dfrac{P_G}{G}\). The same logic gives income and cross elasticities as \(B_2, B_3, B_4\).
Where the data comes from. (1) Consumer focus groups and surveys. (2) A/B testing and randomised controlled trials (e.g. the Uber Egypt experiment). (3) Historical observational data: cross-section, time-series, or both (panel). Examples cited: Walmart scanner data (~500TB), the US Consumer Expenditure Survey, the UK Family Expenditure Survey, France's Budget des Ménages (INSEE), Euro Monitor. Data itself is a competitive advantage (Uber, Amazon, Google). Regression then fits the best line conditional on the other control variables, so each coefficient is the effect of its variable holding the rest fixed.

5 · Application: minimum wage as a price floor (appendix)

Intuition: the same supply/demand diagram, applied to labour Treat the labour market like any other: wage \(w\) on the vertical axis, hours worked on the horizontal, an upward labour supply curve (unskilled workers) and a downward labour demand curve (employers). A minimum wage is a price floor. Whether it destroys jobs is not a matter of ideology; it is a matter of the elasticity of labour demand.
The textbook prediction (Forbes columnist Art Carden)
minimum wage set above the market wage \(w^*\) ⟹ employers move up their demand curve and hire fewer hours, while more workers want to supply hours ⟹ quantity supplied > quantity demanded ⟹ unemployment.
The empirical puzzle (Alan Krueger, "Myth and Measurement"). When New Jersey raised its minimum wage above Pennsylvania's in 1992, employment did not measurably fall. Krueger: minimum wages at observed US levels have had little or no effect on employment; they raise pay without losing jobs. How can both stories be right?
Take-away (slide). A minimum wage above the market wage may lower employment and raise unemployment. But if the demand curve for unskilled labour is relatively inelastic (steep), the employment loss is small and the policy mostly raises the wages of those still employed. For thought, posed by Glover: does this then mean we should set very high minimum wages? (The catch: demand inelasticity holds only over a range; push the wage far enough and the job losses arrive.)

FROM DEMAND: LINEAR DEMAND & ELASTICITY

Symbols used here: \(P\) price, \(Q\) quantity demanded, \(A\) the demand intercept (quantity at a zero price), \(B\) the slope coefficient, \(P_c\) the choke price (the price at which quantity demanded hits zero), and \(E=|\varepsilon|\) the magnitude of the own-price elasticity of demand (the underlying \(\varepsilon\lt 0\); the slides report it as a positive number, so \(E=|\varepsilon|\)).

In the long run you can buy a smaller car, move closer to work, or switch to public transport. More margins of adjustment open up over time, so long-run elasticity is larger than short-run elasticity.

The crucial subtlety of a linear demand curve: its slope is constant (\(-B\) everywhere), but its elasticity is not. Elasticity is a percentage-on-percentage response, and it changes as you move along the line.

$$ E = |\varepsilon| = -\frac{dQ}{dP}\,\frac{P}{Q} = \frac{B\,P}{A-B\,P} = \frac{P}{P_c - P} $$
Point elasticity for linear demand (slide 9): rises from \(0\) at \(P=0\) to \(\infty\) at the choke price.
  1. Start from \(Q=A-BP\), so \(\dfrac{dQ}{dP}=-B\). differentiate the demand curve; the slope is the constant \(-B\)
  2. \(E=-\dfrac{dQ}{dP}\,\dfrac{P}{Q}=\dfrac{B\,P}{A-BP}\). insert the slope and \(Q=A-BP\); the leading minus cancels the negative slope so \(E\gt 0\)
  3. Divide top and bottom by \(B\) and use \(P_c=A/B\): \(\;E=\dfrac{P}{P_c-P}\). elasticity depends only on where \(P\) sits relative to the choke price
Worked example: Apple Watch, choke price 422.8 (slides 7, 9) With \(P_c=422.8\), evaluate at the mid-point \(P=211.4=\tfrac{1}{2}P_c\): $$ E=\frac{211.4}{422.8-211.4}=\frac{211.4}{211.4}=1. $$ At half the choke price demand is exactly unit elastic. Above it demand is elastic (\(E\gt 1\)); below it, inelastic (\(E\lt 1\)). This is the "empirical regularity" on the slide: as price rises along a linear curve, demand becomes more elastic.
Elasticity is not slope. Two traps from slide 10: (1) do not read off elasticity from the steepness of a linear demand curve, since elasticity changes along it while slope does not; (2) to compare the elasticity of two different linear demand curves you must compare them at the same price. At a given price, the curve with the higher choke price is more inelastic (look at \(E=P/(P_c-P)\): a larger \(P_c\) shrinks \(E\)).
$$ \text{Linear: } Q=A-B\,P \qquad\qquad \text{Log-linear: } Q=A\,P^{-B}\;\Leftrightarrow\;\log Q=\log A - B\log P $$
Two functional forms (slide 11). Linear demand has changing elasticity; the log-linear (constant-elasticity) form has the same elasticity \(B\) at every price.
Calculus toolkit (slide 8). The derivative rules you reuse for marginal cost and elasticity: \(\frac{d}{dx}(\text{const})=0\); \(\frac{d}{dx}(x)=1\); \(\frac{d}{dx}(x^k)=k\,x^{k-1}\); \(\frac{d}{dx}(\log x)=\tfrac1x\). Applied to a cost or demand polynomial these give the marginal object directly.

COST CONCEPTS

Intuition: fixed vs variable Split everything the firm spends into two buckets. Fixed costs are what you pay no matter how much you make (the rent on the plant). Variable costs are what scales with output (the materials and labour per unit). Total cost is just the two added together, and the interesting per-unit objects (average and marginal cost) are different ways of slicing that total.
$$ TC = c(Q) = \underbrace{FC}_{\text{fixed}} + \underbrace{VC(Q)}_{\text{variable}} $$
Total cost decomposition (slide 27). \(FC\) is independent of \(Q\); \(VC\) varies with \(Q\).

The per-unit and marginal cost objects, each defined before use:

$$ AFC=\frac{FC}{Q},\quad AVC=\frac{VC(Q)}{Q},\quad ATC=AC=\frac{TC}{Q}=AFC+AVC,\quad MC=\frac{dTC}{dQ} $$
\(AFC\) average fixed cost, \(AVC\) average variable cost, \(ATC\) (= \(AC\)) average total cost, \(MC\) marginal cost = change in total cost from one extra unit (slide 27).
Short run vs long run (slides 26-27). Short run: the usage of one or more inputs cannot be changed, usually plant and equipment (capacity), so some costs are fixed. Long run: usage of all inputs can change, the firm can shut down, and new firms can enter. The longer the decision horizon, the more inputs become variable (you can add capacity). Fixed costs must be incurred even to produce one unit, yet can be eliminated entirely by shutting down or never starting.

WHICH COSTS TO COUNT

Intuition: sunk costs A sunk cost is money already spent that no current decision can recover; it is identical across every option in front of you. Because it cannot change with the choice, it cannot change the ranking of the choices. The heuristic is blunt: ignore sunk costs. Counting them risks the sunk-cost fallacy (and the related error of counting them for some options but not others).
Worked example: the consultant (slide 14) You have already paid a consultant \$10,000 (sunk). The reorganisation she proposes costs a further \$15,000 and will save \$18,000 in inventory costs. Decision rule: compare only the costs the decision still controls. $$ \text{Net benefit} = 18{,}000 - 15{,}000 = +\,3{,}000 \gt 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \textbf{do it.} $$ The \$10,000 is irrelevant to this choice. (If you wrongly add it back, you would see \$18,000 vs \$25,000 and reject a profitable move.)
Intuition: opportunity cost The opportunity cost of a resource is the return from its next best alternative use. The heuristic mirrors sunk costs: include opportunity cost as an explicit cost of the project, so that the cost-benefit and marginal-benefit-vs-marginal-cost comparisons are correct.
Worked example: Enron to Kaiser Aluminum (slide 15) Kaiser holds a long-term contract to buy electricity (from BPA) at \$23 per MWh. In late 2000 the market price of electricity spiked to ~\$1,000 per MWh while aluminium prices had collapsed. Smelting was still profitable measured against the \$23 contract price, but that is the wrong cost. $$ \text{Opportunity cost of the power} = \$1{,}000/\text{MWh}\ (\text{its resale value}),\ \text{not the }\$23\text{ contract price.} $$ Once the power is valued at what it could be sold for, smelting is loss-making: the right move is to stop making aluminium and resell the electricity.

DERIVING THE COST CURVES FROM \(c(Q)\)

$$ c(Q) = 100 + 10Q + Q^{2} $$
The session's worked cost function (slides 13, 16). Read off: \(FC=100\), \(VC(Q)=10Q+Q^2\).
  1. \(FC=100\) (the constant term), \(VC(Q)=10Q+Q^2\). split the constant from the output-dependent part
  2. \(AFC=\dfrac{100}{Q}\). divide fixed cost by output; a falling hyperbola
  3. \(AVC=\dfrac{10Q+Q^2}{Q}=10+Q\). divide variable cost by output; here a straight upward line
  4. \(ATC=AFC+AVC=\dfrac{100}{Q}+10+Q\). add the two averages; the falling \(AFC\) plus rising \(AVC\) gives a U-shape
  5. \(MC=\dfrac{dc}{dQ}=10+2Q\). differentiate total cost; steeper than \(AVC\) (slope 2 vs 1)
Intuition: why MC cuts the averages at their minimum Think of a running average exam mark. If the next paper scores below your average, your average falls; if it scores above, your average rises; your average stops changing exactly when the next paper equals the current average. Marginal cost is "the next paper", average cost is the running average. So \(MC\) sits below \(ATC\) while \(ATC\) is falling, above \(ATC\) while it is rising, and crosses \(ATC\) precisely at its minimum.
\(MC\lt ATC\) ⟹ the extra unit costs less than the running average ⟹ it drags the average down ⟹ \(ATC\) falling.
\(MC\gt ATC\) ⟹ the extra unit costs more than the running average ⟹ it pulls the average up ⟹ \(ATC\) rising.
Therefore \(MC=ATC\) exactly where \(ATC\) is flat, i.e. at its minimum. The same averaging logic applies to \(AVC\).
  1. Minimise \(ATC\): \(\dfrac{d}{dQ}\!\left(\dfrac{100}{Q}+10+Q\right)= -\dfrac{100}{Q^{2}}+1=0\). set the derivative of average total cost to zero
  2. \(\Rightarrow Q^{2}=100 \Rightarrow Q=10\). solve; the cost-minimising scale
  3. \(ATC(10)=\dfrac{100}{10}+10+10=30\). evaluate the average at the minimum
  4. \(MC(10)=10+2(10)=30=ATC(10)\). confirms \(MC\) cuts \(ATC\) at its minimum
This \(AVC\) is not U-shaped. For \(c(Q)=100+10Q+Q^2\), \(AVC=10+Q\) is strictly increasing, so \(MC=10+2Q\) lies above it for every \(Q\gt 0\) and they meet only at \(Q=0\) (both equal 10). Do not over-generalise: the "\(MC\) cuts \(AVC\) at the minimum of \(AVC\)" picture from the textbook is degenerate for this particular function. The U-shape that matters here is \(ATC\), with its minimum at \(Q=10\).

5 · Pricing with Market Power

The answer is a single rule, \(MR = MC\), from which everything else (the inverse-elasticity "Golden Pricing Rule", the markup, the irrelevance of fixed costs) falls out. The lecture also inoculates you against four pricing fallacies that wreck this logic in practice. 5.1 The volume-margin trade-off and marginal revenue

First, definitions used throughout. \(P\) is price, \(Q\) is quantity, and \(D\) is the demand curve. \(AR\) is average revenue, which equals \(P\). Total revenue is \(TR\). The demand curve reflects willingness to pay (WTP) and sensitivity to price: total valuation is the whole area under the curve, and consumer surplus \(CS\) (the value buyers capture above what they pay) is the area between the demand curve and the horizontal line at the price they actually pay.

Intuition: marginal revenue To sell one more unit a firm with market power must cut the price for everyone, not just the marginal buyer. So the extra unit brings in its price as new revenue, but you simultaneously sacrifice margin on every unit you were already selling. Marginal revenue is that net figure, and it can easily be negative. "The marginal unit is worth the price, plus an adjustment term which is a negative number."
Worked example: the struggling airline (slides 13 to 17)

The airline currently prices at \(P = \$300\) and sells \(Q = 330\) seats, below capacity. Demand is \(Q(P) = 480 - 0.5P\), so the inverse demand curve is \(P(Q) = 960 - 2Q\). Should it drop price to win one more passenger?

  • Current total revenue: \(TR = \$300 \times 330 = \$99{,}000\).
  • To sell a 331st seat, price must fall to \(P = 960 - 2(331) = \$298\).
  • New total revenue: \(TR = \$298 \times 331 = \$98{,}638\).
  • Marginal revenue of the extra seat \(= 98{,}638 - 99{,}000 = -\$362\). Selling one more seat destroys revenue, because the \(\$2\) price cut applied to all 330 existing seats (a \(\$660\) loss of margin) swamps the \(\$298\) gained from the new passenger.
Total revenue (inverse-demand form) $$TR = P(Q)\cdot Q = (960 - 2Q)\,Q = 960Q - 2Q^{2}$$

Differentiate to get marginal revenue. \(MR\) is the contribution of the extra unit to revenue:

  1. \(TR = P(Q)\cdot Q\) revenue is price times quantity, with price itself depending on quantity sold.
  2. \(\dfrac{dTR}{dQ} = P(Q) + \dfrac{dP}{dQ}\,Q\) product rule: the \(P(Q)\) term is the revenue from the new unit; the \(\frac{dP}{dQ}\,Q\) term is the margin given up on existing units. Since demand slopes down, \(\frac{dP}{dQ}\lt 0\), so this term is negative.
  3. \(MR = P(Q) + \dfrac{dP}{dQ}\,Q\) for our airline, \(\frac{dP}{dQ}=-2\), so \(MR = 960 - 4Q\). At \(Q=330\), \(MR = 960 - 4(330) = -\$360\), matching the discrete \(-\$362\) up to the size of the one-unit step.
\(P \gt MR\) always (for a downward-sloping demand curve). The adjustment term is negative, so marginal revenue lies strictly below the demand curve. For a linear inverse demand \(P = a - bQ\), the \(MR\) line has the same intercept \(a\) but twice the slope (\(MR = a - 2bQ\)); it hits the horizontal axis at exactly half the quantity that demand does.

5.2 The profit-maximisation rule: \(MR = MC\)

Intuition: MR = MC Walk quantity up one unit at a time. As long as the next unit adds more to revenue than it adds to cost (\(MR \gt MC\)), make it: profit rises. The moment the next unit costs more than it brings in (\(MR \lt MC\)), stop. The frontier where you can no longer improve is exactly \(MR = MC\). It is the same logic as the airline, generalised: weigh the marginal gain against the marginal cost.

Let \(MC\) be marginal cost and \(\pi\) be profit. The firm maximises \(\pi(Q) = TR(Q) - TC(Q)\). The first-order condition sets the derivative to zero:

Profit-maximisation rule (market power) $$\frac{d\pi}{dQ} = MR - MC = 0 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; MR = MC$$
\(MR \gt MC\)→raise \(Q\) (each extra unit adds more revenue than cost)→\(\pi\) up
\(MR \lt MC\)→cut \(Q\) (the last unit cost more than it earned)→\(\pi\) up

Optimal quantity \(Q^{*}\) is read off the \(MR = MC\) crossing; optimal price \(P^{*}\) is read up from \(Q^{*}\) to the demand curve, never to the \(MR\) curve. A price taker instead sets \(P = MC\) directly, because for a price taker the demand curve is flat at the market price, so \(MR = P\); the market-power firm faces a downward-sloping demand curve, which is why \(MR\) and \(P\) diverge.

Revenue maximisation is not profit maximisation. A manager paid on sales (or chasing market share) maximises revenue, which happens where \(MR = 0\), to the right of \(Q^{*}\). That over-produces and under-prices relative to the profit-maximising point. The slides flag this as a real principal-agent trap: "Where is revenue maximised? Who might do this?"

5.3 The Golden Pricing Rule (inverse-elasticity / Lerner)

Intuition: the Golden Rule \(MR = MC\) tells you to equate marginals, but you rarely know your \(MR\) curve. You usually can estimate your price elasticity. The Golden Rule rewrites \(MR=MC\) purely in terms of elasticity and marginal cost: the less elastic (less price-sensitive) your demand, the fatter the markup you can charge over \(MC\). "The price elasticity allows you to do whatever you want." Big-data elasticity estimation in retail has been claimed to lift operating margins by up to 60%.

Define own-price elasticity of demand \(\varepsilon = \dfrac{dQ}{dP}\cdot\dfrac{P}{Q}\), with the sign convention \(\varepsilon \lt 0\) (demand slopes down). Where the rule is written with a positive number, that number is the magnitude \(E = |\varepsilon|\). The markup itself, \(\dfrac{P-MC}{P}\), is the Lerner index \(\mathcal{L}\) (using \(\mathcal{L}\), not \(L\), since \(L\) is labour elsewhere in the course).

  1. \(MR = P + \dfrac{dP}{dQ}\,Q\) marginal revenue from 5.1.
  2. \(MR = P\left(1 + \dfrac{Q}{P}\dfrac{dP}{dQ}\right) = P\left(1 + \dfrac{1}{\varepsilon}\right)\) factor out \(P\); the bracketed term is exactly \(1/\varepsilon\) because \(\varepsilon = \frac{dQ}{dP}\frac{P}{Q}\) is the reciprocal of \(\frac{Q}{P}\frac{dP}{dQ}\).
  3. \(P\left(1 + \dfrac{1}{\varepsilon}\right) = MC\) impose the profit-max condition \(MR=MC\).
  4. \(P - MC = -\dfrac{P}{\varepsilon}\) expand and move \(MC\) across: \(P + P/\varepsilon = MC\), so \(P - MC = -P/\varepsilon\).
  5. \(\dfrac{P-MC}{P} = -\dfrac{1}{\varepsilon} = \dfrac{1}{E}\) divide through by \(P\). This is the Lerner index: the optimal markup equals one over the absolute elasticity.
Lerner index (inverse-elasticity rule) $$\mathcal{L} = \frac{P-MC}{P} = -\frac{1}{\varepsilon} = \frac{1}{E}$$

Rearranging the same condition for \(P\) gives the form the slides call the Golden Rule, the multiplicative markup over marginal cost:

Golden Pricing Rule (markup form) $$P^{*} = \frac{E}{E-1}\,MC$$
Reading the rule. As \(E \to \infty\) (perfectly elastic, the price-taker limit), \(\frac{E}{E-1}\to 1\) and \(P^{*}\to MC\): no markup, exactly the competitive result. As \(E \to 1^{+}\), the markup explodes. If \(E \le 1\) the firm is on the inelastic part of demand where \(MR\lt 0\), so it should never optimally price there: a profit-maximiser always operates where demand is elastic (\(E\gt 1\)).
Worked example: iPhone pricing (slide 22)

Slide inputs: estimated elasticity \(E = 1.5\) and marginal (component) cost \(MC = \$490.5\).

  • \(P^{*} = \dfrac{E}{E-1}\,MC = \dfrac{1.5}{0.5}\times 490.5 = 3 \times 490.5 = \$1{,}471.5\).
  • Check via the Lerner index: \(\dfrac{P-MC}{P} = \dfrac{1471.5 - 490.5}{1471.5} = 0.667 = \dfrac{1}{1.5}\). Consistent.

5.4 Why fixed costs do not enter the pricing decision

Intuition: fixed costs are invisible to price Notice what was absent from every equation above: fixed costs. Pricing is set by \(MR=MC\), and \(MC\) is the cost of the next unit, which fixed costs by definition do not change. Fixed costs decide a different question, whether to be in the business at all, not what to charge once you are in it.

Let \(FC\) be fixed cost, \(VC\) variable cost, \(AFC\) average fixed cost, \(AVC\) average variable cost, and \(AC\) (or \(ATC\)) average total cost. Then:

Average cost decomposition $$AC = AFC + AVC, \qquad MC = AVC \text{ at the relevant margin}$$
Two separate questions. The pricing decision uses \(MR=MC\) and ignores \(FC\) entirely. \(FC\) only determines profitability: once \(P^{*}\) and \(Q^{*}\) are set, compare \(P^{*}\) with \(AC\). If \(P^{*} \gt AC\) the business earns positive net profit; if \(P^{*} \lt AC\) it makes a net loss. Same price, same quantity, different fixed cost, opposite verdict on whether the firm should exist.
Fixed-cost fallacy. Average-cost pricing and cost-plus pricing that bakes in a share of fixed cost both let an irrelevant number distort price. The danger is the death spiral: a firm prices to recover fixed cost, the higher price loses volume, spreading the same fixed cost over fewer units raises average cost, which "justifies" a further price rise, which loses more volume, and so on. The slides label this "Beware the Death Spiral" (Wang Laboratories is the named cautionary case).

Before entry, the sunk/setup cost \(SC\) is decision-relevant (enter only if expected profit covers it). Once committed and the world changes (costs, demand shift), that \(SC\) is sunk and the pricing decision reverts to pure \(MR=MC\) on the costs that remain. This sets up the sunk cost fallacy: never let a cost that no current decision can change affect that decision.

6 · Explicit Market Segmentation

So far the course has priced a single product at a single price: a firm with market power sets one price by equating marginal revenue and marginal cost. This lecture asks the obvious follow-up. If different customers value the good differently, why charge them all the same? Price discrimination is the answer: charging different customers (or segments) different prices for the same good. We build from the two extremes (one uniform price for everyone, versus a bespoke price for every customer) to the workhorse middle case, explicit market segmentation, where a firm splits the market on an observable trait and charges each group according to its elasticity. The mechanics are just the firm-pricing toolkit from L05 applied segment by segment.

RECAP: THE PRICING TOOLKIT WE BUILD ON

Before discriminating across segments we need the single-segment rule sharp. Define for the rest of this section: \(P\) price, \(Q\) quantity, \(D\) (or \(d(P)\)) demand, \(MR\) marginal revenue (the extra revenue from selling one more unit), \(MC\) marginal cost, \(AR\) average revenue \(=P\), \(\varepsilon\) the own-price elasticity of demand (with the sign convention \(\varepsilon\lt 0\), so we write \(|\varepsilon|\) for its magnitude), \(\pi\) profit, \(CS\) consumer surplus, \(PS\) producer surplus, and \(DWL\) deadweight loss.

Intuition: the markup is the inverse of elasticity A monopolist's price is just marginal cost plus a markup, and the size of that markup is set entirely by how price-sensitive customers are. Customers who barely flinch at a price rise (inelastic demand) get a fat markup; customers who would walk at the first euro of increase (elastic demand) get a thin one. Everything in this lecture is one move: find the trait that sorts the flinchers from the walkers, then markup each group separately.

Starting from the canonical profit-max rule (a firm with market power sets \(MR=MC\)), and using the definition \(TR=P(Q)\cdot Q\), marginal revenue can be written in terms of elasticity:

$$ MR \;=\; P\left(1 - \frac{1}{|\varepsilon|}\right) $$
Marginal revenue in elasticity form (Apple Watch slide)

Setting \(MR=MC\) and rearranging gives the Lerner index \(\mathcal{L}\) (the proportional markup, introduced in L05; reused here verbatim), also called the Golden Pricing Rule or inverse-elasticity rule:

$$ \mathcal{L} \;=\; \frac{P-MC}{P} \;=\; \frac{1}{|\varepsilon|} \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad P \;=\; MC\cdot\frac{|\varepsilon|}{|\varepsilon|-1} $$
Lerner index / inverse-elasticity rule
  1. \(TR = P(Q)\cdot Q\). Revenue is price times quantity, with price falling in quantity along the demand curve.
  2. \(MR = \dfrac{d(TR)}{dQ} = P + Q\dfrac{dP}{dQ} = P\left(1 + \dfrac{Q}{P}\dfrac{dP}{dQ}\right)\). Differentiate as a product, then factor out \(P\).
  3. \(MR = P\left(1 + \dfrac{1}{\varepsilon}\right) = P\left(1 - \dfrac{1}{|\varepsilon|}\right)\). Recognise \(\dfrac{Q}{P}\dfrac{dP}{dQ} = 1/\varepsilon\); since \(\varepsilon\lt 0\), write it as \(-1/|\varepsilon|\).
  4. Set \(MR=MC\): \(P\left(1-\dfrac{1}{|\varepsilon|}\right)=MC\). Impose the profit-max condition for a price-setter.
  5. \(\dfrac{P-MC}{P}=\dfrac{1}{|\varepsilon|}\), i.e. \(P = MC\cdot\dfrac{|\varepsilon|}{|\varepsilon|-1}\). Divide through by \(P\). The markup falls as demand gets more elastic.

Two takeaways the slides flag explicitly: (1) as elasticity rises, the profit-maximising price falls; (2) a monopolist always produces on the elastic portion of demand, where \(|\varepsilon|\gt 1\) (otherwise the markup term \(\frac{|\varepsilon|}{|\varepsilon|-1}\) would be negative, which is nonsense, so it never prices where demand is inelastic).

THE MID-POINT PRICING RULE (LINEAR DEMAND, CONSTANT MC)

Intuition: split the difference If demand is a straight line and marginal cost is flat, you do not need calculus to find the optimal price. It sits exactly halfway between the highest price anyone will pay (the choke price, where quantity hits zero) and marginal cost. Pricing is literally splitting the difference.

Let \(\bar P\) be the choke price, the price at which quantity demanded falls to zero. For linear demand the point elasticity has a clean form, and the optimal price collapses to a midpoint:

$$ |\varepsilon| \;=\; \frac{P}{\bar P - P} \qquad\qquad P^{*} \;=\; \frac{\bar P + MC}{2} $$
Point elasticity for linear demand · Mid-point pricing rule
  1. Linear demand: \(P = \bar P - bQ\). \(\bar P\) is the price-axis intercept (choke price); \(b\gt 0\) is the slope.
  2. \(TR = \bar P Q - bQ^2 \Rightarrow MR = \bar P - 2bQ\). MR has the same intercept as demand but twice the slope.
  3. Set \(MR=MC\): \(\bar P - 2bQ = MC \Rightarrow Q^{*} = \dfrac{\bar P - MC}{2b}\). Solve for the profit-max quantity.
  4. \(P^{*} = \bar P - bQ^{*} = \bar P - \dfrac{\bar P - MC}{2} = \dfrac{\bar P + MC}{2}\). Substitute \(Q^{*}\) back into demand. Price is the midpoint of choke price and MC.

This is the lever for the whole lecture: a higher choke price means more inelastic demand, which means a higher profit-maximising price. Segments differ precisely in their choke prices, so the segment that values the good more (higher \(\bar P\)) gets charged more.

Beware four pricing fallacies (recap). (1) Monopoly-power fallacy: a monopolist cannot charge any price she likes; she is still bound by demand and \(MR=MC\) (Microsoft). (2) Imitation fallacy: pricing to match a competitor regardless of your own costs and demand (Eurotunnel). (3) Fixed-cost fallacy: pricing to recover fixed costs rather than off marginal cost and elasticity (Wang Laboratories). (4) Sunk-cost fallacy: letting unrecoverable costs influence a forward-looking decision. Caveat: sunk costs were not always sunk; cost is a dynamic concept.
Cost is dynamic. A cost can be highly relevant at one moment and irrelevant once an irreversible commitment has been made. Before entry, the start-up cost \(SC\) is avoidable and belongs in the decision; after committing to production it is sunk and the only forward-looking choice is whether to keep operating versus exit.

Price discrimination: the three degrees

Intuition: capturing the pie Under one uniform price the firm leaves money on the table twice: customers who value the good above the price keep the difference (consumer surplus), and customers who value it between marginal cost and the price are not served at all (deadweight loss). Price discrimination is the set of tricks for clawing back both: charge the high-value customers more, and serve the low-value customers at a lower price you would never offer everyone.

The uniform-pricing conundrum

To see why a firm wants to discriminate, look at what uniform monopoly pricing wastes. With one price set at \(MR=MC\) (assume \(FC=0\)), the market splits into four areas: cost, profit, the consumer surplus the firm fails to capture, and the deadweight loss from customers it does not serve.

First degree: Perfect Price Discrimination

Intuition: the benchmark, not the plan Perfect price discrimination is the fantasy case: you know every customer's exact valuation and charge each of them precisely that. There is no consumer surplus left and no deadweight loss, because you serve everyone whose valuation exceeds marginal cost. It is the upper bound on what pricing can extract. Real firms never reach it; it is the yardstick.

Under PPD the firm's marginal revenue from each unit is just that unit's price (there is no need to cut price on earlier units), so demand itself acts as the marginal-revenue curve. The firm produces all the way to \(Q^{*}\) where \(d(P)=MC\), and the entire area between demand and MC becomes profit.

PPD is efficient but not equitable. Because every customer with valuation above MC is served, there is no deadweight loss, total surplus is maximised. But the firm captures all of it, so consumer surplus is zero. Efficiency and equity are distinct questions; PPD aces the first and fails the second.

Feasibility (very, very hard). Perfect price discrimination requires three things at once: (a) knowing each customer's valuation; (b) the ability to make take-it-or-leave-it offers; and (c) stopping arbitrage so a low-priced buyer cannot resell to a high-valuation one. Big data is making it less fantastical: Shiller (2014) estimates first-degree pricing of Netflix using demographics plus web-browsing data, and Bill Gates argued vaccines should be priced in three tiers (rich countries cover most fixed costs, middle-income some, poor countries pay true marginal cost) which is PPD logic across countries.

Third degree: Explicit Market Segmentation

Intuition: sort by a visible label, then markup each group You cannot read each customer's mind (so no PPD), but you can often see a label that is correlated with willingness to pay: a student card, a passport, a business-vs-leisure travel pattern, a residential-vs-industrial meter. Split the market broadly on that visible label, then run the inverse-elasticity rule inside each group. The inelastic group gets the higher price.

Explicit (third-degree) segmentation means not all customers have the same trading opportunities: the firm offers different groups different prices for the same good. Three conditions must all hold:

Conditions for explicit segmentation to work. (1) Market power: the firm must be able to set prices (a price taker has no markup to vary). (2) Observability / no deception: an easily observed trait that is correlated with willingness to pay, and customers cannot masquerade as a cheaper segment. (3) No arbitrage / no resale: customers in the cheap segment cannot resell the good to the expensive segment, or the two prices collapse to one.

The firm treats each segment as its own market. With a common marginal cost \(MC\) (one production line serving both segments), profit is maximised by setting marginal revenue equal across every segment and equal to marginal cost:

$$ MR_A \;=\; MR_B \;=\; MC $$
Optimal pricing across segments A and B
  1. \(\pi = P_A(Q_A)\,Q_A + P_B(Q_B)\,Q_B - C(Q_A+Q_B)\). Total profit is segment-A revenue plus segment-B revenue minus the cost of producing the combined output.
  2. \(\dfrac{\partial \pi}{\partial Q_A} = MR_A - MC = 0\) and \(\dfrac{\partial \pi}{\partial Q_B} = MR_B - MC = 0\). Take the first-order condition in each segment's quantity; the cost term contributes \(MC\) in both because output is shared.
  3. \(MR_A = MR_B = MC\). If the two marginal revenues differed, the firm could shift a unit from the low-MR segment to the high-MR one and raise profit, so at the optimum they must be equal.

Applying the Lerner index inside each segment gives the operational rule: charge the higher price in the more inelastic segment.

$$ \frac{P_A - MC}{P_A} = \frac{1}{|\varepsilon_A|}, \qquad \frac{P_B - MC}{P_B} = \frac{1}{|\varepsilon_B|} \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; |\varepsilon_A| \lt |\varepsilon_B| \Rightarrow P_A \gt P_B $$
Lerner per segment: lower elasticity ⟹ higher price
Which of these are explicit segmentation? The class test: explicit segmentation means different groups face different trading opportunities on an observable trait. Yes: child / student / senior discounts; different airline fares for business vs leisure; different drug prices by country or use; residential vs business utility rates; academic discounts on hardware and software. The odd one out is mail-in coupons: anyone can redeem them, so customers self-select by effort. That is implicit (second-degree) segmentation, not explicit. Do not lump it in.
Worked example: US railroad freight: coal vs grain (constant MC = $10/ton)

The railroad serves two segments off one network. Inverse demands and the resulting marginal revenues (MR has the same intercept, twice the slope):

Coal: \(P_C = 90 - 10Q_C\), so \(MR_C = 90 - 20Q_C\). Set \(MR_C = MC = 10\): \(20Q_C = 80 \Rightarrow Q_C = 4\), and \(P_C = 90 - 10(4) = \$50\).

Grain: \(P_G = 60 - 5Q_G\), so \(MR_G = 60 - 10Q_G\). Set \(MR_G = MC = 10\): \(10Q_G = 50 \Rightarrow Q_G = 5\), and \(P_G = 60 - 5(5) = \$35\).

Coal has the higher choke price (\(\$90\) vs \(\$60\)), so it is the more inelastic segment and is charged more: \(P_C/P_G = 50/35 \approx 1.4\). This matches the real pattern, US railroads charge roughly 1.5 to 2 times as much to move coal as grain. Check: \(MR_C = MR_G = MC = 10\) at the optimum, as required.

Worked example: Merck antifungal: US vs Canada (constant MC = $20)

Merck produces in one Mexican plant at constant \(MC=\$20\) and sells into two segments with different elasticities: \(|\varepsilon_{US}|=2\), \(|\varepsilon_{CA}|=3\). Apply the Lerner index segment by segment.

US: \(\dfrac{P_{US}-20}{P_{US}}=\dfrac{1}{2} \Rightarrow \dfrac{20}{P_{US}}=\dfrac12 \Rightarrow P_{US}=\$40\).

Canada: \(\dfrac{P_{CA}-20}{P_{CA}}=\dfrac{1}{3} \Rightarrow \dfrac{20}{P_{CA}}=\dfrac23 \Rightarrow P_{CA}=\$30\).

Canada has the more elastic demand, so it gets the lower price. Equivalently \(P=MC\cdot\frac{|\varepsilon|}{|\varepsilon|-1}\): US \(=20\cdot\frac{2}{1}=40\), Canada \(=20\cdot\frac{3}{2}=30\).

Welfare and the ethics of price discrimination

Intuition: does it grow the pie or just reslice it? Price discrimination is not automatically good or bad for consumers. The single question is whether it brings new customers into the market. If charging low-value customers a lower price lets the firm serve people it otherwise would not, total trade rises and overall welfare can go up. If it is purely extracting more from people who would have bought anyway, it just transfers surplus from consumers to the firm.
Welfare rule of thumb. If price discrimination expands the market (more trades happen), it raises overall welfare, some low-value buyers are served who would have been priced out. If it does not expand the market, it reduces consumer welfare, it only redistributes surplus toward the firm. This is the lens for the Gates vaccine-tiering argument and the "pink tax" debate alike.
Exam lens. Expect three move types. (1) True/False trap: "a monopolist may price where demand is inelastic" is false, the markup rule \(P=MC\cdot\frac{|\varepsilon|}{|\varepsilon|-1}\) only makes sense for \(|\varepsilon|\gt 1\). Also classic: "explicit segmentation needs identical trading opportunities" (false, it needs different ones); "mail-in coupons are explicit segmentation" (false, that is implicit self-selection). (2) Two-segment calculation: given two linear demands and a common \(MC\), find each \(P\) and \(Q\) by setting \(MR_A=MR_B=MC\), then confirm the more inelastic (higher-choke-price) segment is charged more (coal/grain). (3) Conditions / classification: list the three conditions (market power, observability with no deception, no arbitrage) or classify a list of real schemes as PPD vs explicit vs implicit. 7 · Implicit Market Segmentation

Explicit segmentation (L06) works only when the seller can observe who is who and fence the segments apart. This session asks the harder question: what do you do when you know the composition of your market (how many high-value and low-value buyers there are) but cannot tell which individual customer is which? The answer is to stop sorting customers yourself and instead design a menu that makes them sort themselves. This is implicit segmentation, also called screening or second-degree price discrimination, and it is the engine behind coupons, versioning, damaged goods, intertemporal pricing and restricted airfares.

RECAP: EXPLICIT SEGMENTATION (THE BENCHMARK YOU ARE LEAVING BEHIND)

Intuition: equalise the squeeze When you can see the segments, profit-maximisation means the last dollar of revenue earned in each segment must be equal, otherwise you would shift a sale from the low-MR segment to the high-MR one. Charge the inelastic segment more, the elastic segment less.

Define \(P\) price, \(MC\) marginal cost, \(MR\) marginal revenue, and \(\varepsilon\) the own-price elasticity of demand (negative; we work with its magnitude \(|\varepsilon|\)). With separable segments \(C\) and \(G\):

$$ MR_C = MR_G = MC $$
Equalise marginal revenue across observable segments (the railroad coal-vs-grain rule from L06).

Equivalently, each segment obeys the inverse-elasticity (Lerner / Golden Pricing) rule, so the less elastic segment carries the higher markup:

$$ \mathcal{L}=\frac{P-MC}{P}=\frac{1}{|\varepsilon|} $$
Lerner index \(\mathcal{L}\) = markup as a share of price = reciprocal of elasticity magnitude.
Worked recap: Merck anti-fungal (US vs Canada)

The screening problem

🎙 In class "Economists don't hunt elephants, but believe that if elephants are given sufficient incentives, they will hunt themselves." You cannot identify each customer's valuation, so you design the incentives such that high-value buyers walk into the expensive option on their own.

Let WTP denote a customer's willingness to pay (valuation \(v\)). The classic dilemma: you know the market is a mix of high-WTP and low-WTP buyers, but you cannot observe any individual's \(v\). You therefore offer a menu and let choice reveal type.

The three building blocks (memorise these labels).
  • Menu: the set of options (product + price pairs) you put in front of every customer.
  • Objective: customise the trades so that high-valuation consumers freely choose the more expensive option.
  • Participation constraint (PC, also "individual rationality"): each customer prefers the deal designed for them over not trading at all: surplus \(\ge 0\).
  • Self-selection constraint (SS, also "incentive compatibility"): each customer prefers their option over every other option on the menu.
  • Key (binding) constraint: the high-WTP buyer can always masquerade as low-WTP and grab the cheap deal. Your design must stop that switch. This is the constraint that actually bites.
Don't confuse the two constraints. The participation constraint stops the low-WTP buyer from walking away (it pins down the cheap price). The self-selection constraint stops the high-WTP buyer from buying the cheap option (it pins down the expensive price). Get them the wrong way round and your menu collapses to a single price.

COUPONS: THE SIMPLEST SCREEN

Intuition: a coupon is a price cut that only the patient claim A coupon or mail-in rebate is a deliberately annoying hassle. The hassle filters on the opportunity cost of time: low-WTP, price-sensitive buyers redeem; high-WTP, time-rich buyers cannot be bothered. So you get two effective prices from one shelf price, without ever asking anyone their income.

Coupons look wasteful (printing, circulation, processing, cutting cheques). So why not just cut the price for everyone? Because a uniform cut bleeds revenue from the high-WTP buyers who would have paid full price. A coupon avoids that bleed. Consider lowering price from \(P_0\) to \(P_1\) to sell one extra unit:

$$ MR_{\text{uniform cut}} = C - A - B \qquad MR_{\text{coupon}} = C - A $$
\(C\) = revenue gained on the new marginal sale; \(A\) = revenue lost on low-WTP buyers who redeem; \(B\) = revenue lost on high-WTP buyers: avoided under a coupon because they do not redeem.
$$ \text{Issue coupons whenever } \; C \gt A $$
The marginal sale is worth more than the price concession to the redeemers alone, even though it would be a loser as a blanket cut (\(C\lt A+B\)).

Airline screening: the canonical model

Intuition: sell the annoyance, not the seat Airlines want to charge business travellers more, but cannot see who is a business traveller. They know valuation correlates with the opportunity cost of time, and that high-value travellers will not accept a Saturday-night stay or a 14-day advance purchase. So they bolt artificial restrictions onto a cheap fare. The restrictions cost the airline nothing operationally; their only job is to repel high-WTP buyers so the menu self-sorts.

$$ \text{PC (leisure buys restricted):}\quad v_L^{R}-P_r \ge 0 \;\Rightarrow\; P_r \le 500 $$
Participation: the low-WTP type must prefer trading to walking away. It binds, so \(P_r=500\).

  4. Menu: \(P_u = 1000-100 = \$900\) unrestricted, \(P_r=\$500\) restricted. Profit \(\pi_C = B(900-300)+L(500-300) = 600B+200L\).

ETHICS OF PRICE DISCRIMINATION

Intuition: does the menu grow the pie or just slice it? Price discrimination that expands the market (enables trades that would not otherwise happen) raises total welfare. Price discrimination that does not expand the market simply transfers surplus from consumers to the firm, reducing consumer welfare.
Bill Gates on tiered vaccine pricing. "The price needs three tiers where rich countries are paying back a lot of the fixed costs, middle-income countries are paying back some of the fixed costs, and the poorer countries are paying a true marginal cost." This lecture flips the viewpoint from the firm with market power (L05-L07) to the firm with none. PERFECT COMPETITION AND THE PRICE-TAKING FIRM

Intuition: price taker A wheat farmer or an aluminium smelter is a drop in an ocean of identical sellers. Whatever the market settles on, that is the price; the firm can sell as much as it likes at that price and zero at a hair above it. So the demand curve facing one firm is flat, even though the demand curve for the whole market still slopes down. The firm's only lever is how much to produce.
A market is perfectly competitive when (slide "What is a Competitive Market?"):
  • Sellers are price takers with little or no pricing power.
  • Firms produce a homogeneous (nearly identical) good.
  • There is free entry and exit (developed in L09).
Slide examples: commodity markets, aluminium (104 producers), steel (80; 135 US mills), logging (12,985 US firms), agriculture in developing countries.

Let \(P\) be the market price, \(Q\) market quantity, and \(q\) one firm's output. Total revenue is \(TR = P\,q\). Because the firm takes \(P\) as given, every extra unit sells at exactly \(P\):

$$ AR \;=\; \frac{TR}{q} \;=\; \frac{P\,q}{q} \;=\; P, \qquad MR \;=\; \frac{\partial TR}{\partial q} \;=\; P. $$
Average revenue and marginal revenue both equal the price for a price taker. \(AR\) = revenue per unit, \(MR\) = revenue from one more unit.

The universal profit-max rule is \(MR = MC\), where \(MC\) is marginal cost (the cost of one more unit). Substituting \(MR = P\) gives the competitive output rule:

$$ \boxed{\,P \;=\; MC(q)\,} $$
The price-taker's rule: produce up to the point where price equals marginal cost. (Contrast L05, where a firm with market power sets \(MR = MC\) with \(MR < P\).)
Why \(P=MC\) and not more or less. If \(P > MC\) at the current \(q\), the next unit earns \(P\) but costs less than \(P\) to make ⟹ producing it adds to profit ⟹ expand. If \(P < MC\), the last unit cost more than it earned ⟹ cut back. Profit stops rising only where \(P = MC\), on the upward-sloping part of \(MC\).

SHORT-RUN SUPPLY AND THE SHUTDOWN CONDITION

Intuition: the MC curve is the supply curve Ask the firm "at price \(P\), how much will you make?" The answer is always "the \(q\) where \(MC = P\)." So as you sweep the price up and down, the firm's chosen quantity simply traces out its own marginal-cost curve. The firm's supply curve is its \(MC\) curve, with one cut-off at the bottom.

Define the cost pieces (per period). \(FC\) = fixed cost (incurred even at \(q=0\), sunk in the short run); \(VC(q)\) = variable cost; \(TC = FC + VC\). The averages are \(ATC = TC/q\) (also written \(AC\)), \(AVC = VC/q\), and \(AFC = FC/q\). The firm's short-run supply curve is:

$$ q^{S}(P)=\begin{cases} \;q \text{ such that } P = MC(q), & P \ge \min AVC \\[4pt] \;0 \;(\text{idle}), & P < \min AVC \end{cases} $$
Short-run supply = the \(MC\) curve above minimum \(AVC\); below that the firm produces nothing.

Where does the cut-off come from? In the short run \(FC\) is sunk: the firm pays it whether or not it produces, so it is irrelevant to the produce-or-idle choice. The only question is whether running the line at least covers the variable cost of running it.

  1. Operating profit relative to idling: \(\pi_{\text{operate}} - \pi_{\text{idle}} = (TR - VC - FC) - (-FC)\). Idling still pays the sunk \(FC\); subtract the two states.
  2. \(= TR - VC = P\,q - VC\). The sunk \(FC\) cancels: it cannot affect the decision.
  3. Operate iff \(P\,q \ge VC\), i.e. \(P \ge VC/q = AVC\). Divide by \(q\): keep producing as long as price covers average variable cost.
The slide's two-line survival test (verbatim from "Supply Decision in Short-Run"):
Short run: is \(P > AVC\)? If not, go idle.
Long run: is \(P > ATC\)? If not, exit (developed in L09).

Shutdown is not exit, and \(AVC\) is not \(ATC\). Idling (short run, test against \(AVC\)) is temporary: the firm stops producing but keeps paying sunk fixed costs, hoping price recovers. Exit (long run, test against \(ATC\)) is permanent: the firm leaves and sheds all costs. A firm earning \(\min AVC < P < \min ATC\) is losing money yet correctly keeps running, because shutting down would lose even more (the whole \(FC\)).

FROM FIRM TO MARKET: THE INDUSTRY SUPPLY CURVE

Intuition: add sideways, not upways To get total quantity supplied at a price, ask every firm how much it makes at that price and add up. You add quantities at a fixed price, so you sum the supply curves horizontally. With \(n\) identical firms the market curve is just the firm's curve stretched \(n\) times wider, i.e. \(n\) times flatter.
$$ Q^{S}(P) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{n} q_i^{S}(P) \;=\; n\,q^{S}(P), \qquad n \text{ fixed in the short run.} $$
Market supply is the horizontal sum of the \(n\) firms' supply curves; \(n\) = number of firms, frozen in the short run.

SHORT-RUN COMPETITIVE EQUILIBRIUM

Equilibrium price clears the market: the quantity all firms want to supply at \(P\) equals the quantity buyers demand at \(P\). With \(D\) = market demand and \(S\) = market supply, set \(Q^{D}(P) = Q^{S}(P)\) and solve for \(P^{*}\).

Worked model (slide "Example: Short-Run"). Demand \(Q^{D} = 900 - 20P\). Short run: \(n = 100\) firms, fixed. Each firm's total cost \(c(q) = 100 + 10q + q^{2}\) (so \(FC = 100\), \(VC = 10q + q^{2}\)).
  1. \(MC = \dfrac{dc}{dq} = 10 + 2q\). Differentiate the cost function to get marginal cost.
  2. Firm supply from \(P = MC\): \(P = 10 + 2q \Rightarrow q = \dfrac{P-10}{2}\). Apply the price-taker rule and solve for the firm's quantity.
  3. Market supply: \(Q^{S} = n\,q = 100\cdot\dfrac{P-10}{2} = 50P - 500\). Multiply the firm's supply by the \(n=100\) firms (horizontal sum).
  4. Clear the market: \(900 - 20P = 50P - 500\). Set demand equal to market supply.
  5. \(1400 = 70P\). Collect terms: add \(20P\) and \(500\) to both sides.
  6. \(P^{*} = 20,\quad Q^{*} = 500,\quad q^{*} = 5\). Divide; back-substitute to get market and per-firm quantities.
Worked example: operate or idle, and at what profit?

Check buyers: \(Q^{D} = 900 - 20(20) = 500 = Q^{*}\). ✓

Per-firm profit: \(\pi = P^{*}q^{*} - c(q^{*}) = 20(5) - \big(100 + 10(5) + 5^{2}\big) = 100 - 175 = -\,75.\) Each firm loses \(\$75\); the industry loses \(100 \times 75 = \$7{,}500\).

Why keep running at a loss? Compare price to the two averages at \(q^{*}=5\):

  • \(ATC = c(q)/q = 175/5 = \$35\). So \(P = 20 < ATC = 35\): a loss (matches the slide).
  • \(AVC = VC/q = (10q+q^{2})/q = 10 + q = \$15\). So \(P = 20 > AVC = 15\): variable costs are covered.

The firm is between the lines: \(\min AVC < P < \min ATC\). It operates because each unit earns \(\$20\) and costs only \(\$15\) in variable terms, contributing \(\$5\) per unit toward the sunk \(\$100\) fixed cost. Idling would forfeit that contribution and still owe \(\$100\), a \(\$100\) loss versus the \(\$75\) loss from operating.

Forward link to L09. Losses with \(P < ATC\) are stable in the short run (\(n\) is frozen) but not in the long run: \(\pi < 0\) ⟹ firms exit ⟹ \(n\) falls ⟹ market supply shifts left ⟹ price rises until \(P = \min ATC\) and \(\pi = 0\). That entry-exit adjustment to "zero economic profit" is the subject of L09.

PRODUCER SURPLUS

Intuition: producer surplus Producer surplus is the gap between what sellers receive and the minimum they would have accepted, summed over every unit. The minimum acceptable price for each unit is its marginal cost, so \(PS\) is the area between the price line and the supply (\(MC\)) curve. It is the variable-cost analogue of consumer surplus, and it equals revenue minus variable cost (not minus total cost).
$$ PS \;=\; TR - VC \;=\; P\,q - VC(q) \;=\; \text{area between } P \text{ and } MC. $$
Producer surplus = revenue above variable cost = the area above the supply curve and below the market price.
$$ PS \;=\; \pi + FC \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad \pi \;=\; PS - FC. $$
\(PS\) excludes fixed cost; profit subtracts it. So \(PS\) and \(\pi\) differ by exactly \(FC\), and a firm can have positive \(PS\) while making a loss.
Worked example: producer surplus in the running model

At \(P^{*}=20\), \(q^{*}=5\): \(VC(5) = 10(5)+5^{2} = 75\), so \(PS = TR - VC = 100 - 75 = \$25\) per firm.

Geometrically, \(MC = 10+2q\) rises from \(10\) (at \(q=0\)) to \(20\) (at \(q=5\)); \(PS\) is the triangle between \(P=20\) and \(MC\): \(PS = \tfrac12 \times 5 \times (20-10) = \$25\). ✓

Cross-check: \(PS = \pi + FC = -75 + 100 = \$25\). ✓ Industry \(PS = 100 \times 25 = \$2{,}500\), even though the industry is losing \(\$7{,}500\) (the \(\$10{,}000\) of total fixed cost is the difference).

9 · Dynamics of Competitive Markets

L08 gave the static price-taking firm: it produces where \(P=MC\) along the part of its marginal-cost curve above average variable cost. L09 makes the picture dynamic. Once entry and exit are free, profits and losses are not permanent: they are signals that pull new firms in or push weak firms out, and that flow keeps shifting market supply until economic profit is competed away. This is the engine behind the line "you will never make profits in an easy-to-enter market", and it is the framework Alusaf needs to decide whether to build a multi-billion-dollar smelter on the basis of a price forecast, not today's depressed price. (The same session also opened game theory, the strategic-interaction toolkit that L10 develops; this page covers the competitive-dynamics half.)

THE LONG-RUN ADJUSTMENT MECHANISM

Intuition: profit as a signal Positive economic profit is a flashing "come in" sign over an industry; losses are an eviction notice. As long as the sign is lit, new firms keep arriving, and each arrival adds to market supply and nudges the price down. The lights only switch off when price has fallen to the point where the marginal firm exactly breaks even. The long-run equilibrium is not a place anyone chooses; it is wherever the entry and exit pressure finally cancels out.

First fix the vocabulary, because the whole lecture turns on the word "profit" meaning economic profit, not the accountant's.

  • \(\pi\) = economic profit = total revenue minus all opportunity costs, including the return the owner's capital and the entrepreneur's effort could have earned elsewhere.
  • \(P\) price (= \(AR\), average revenue, for a price taker), \(q\) one firm's output, \(Q\) total market output, \(n\) the number of firms.
  • \(TC\) total cost, \(MC=\mathrm{d}TC/\mathrm{d}q\) marginal cost, \(ATC\) (also written \(AC\)) average total cost \(=TC/q\), \(AVC\) average variable cost, \(AFC\) average fixed cost, so \(ATC = AVC + AFC\).
Economic profit per firm$$\pi = TR - TC = (P - ATC)\,q$$

Free entry and exit means the long-run equilibrium is wherever this quantity is driven to zero for the marginal firm:

Long-run free-entry condition$$\pi = 0 \;\Longleftrightarrow\; P = ATC$$

A profit-maximising price taker also sets \(P = MC\). Both hold at once only at the bottom of the \(ATC\) curve, because \(MC\) cuts \(ATC\) exactly at its minimum:

Long-run competitive equilibrium (the firm)$$P^\* = MC = \min ATC$$
In long-run competitive equilibrium every surviving firm earns zero economic profit: revenues exactly cover the opportunity cost of every resource, including capital and the entrepreneur. Firms still maximise profit and still break even; the two are not in conflict. The market has been "commoditised" and no firm has a sustainable competitive advantage. In Glover's word on the slide: depressing.

Why entry and exit are self-correcting (both directions)

Run the mechanism in each direction. The market panel holds demand \(D\) fixed and lets the number of firms move the supply curve.

Entry (price above break-even): \(P \gt \min ATC\) ⟹ \(\pi\gt 0\) for incumbents ⟹ outsiders enter because the lit "profit" sign means capital earns more here than its opportunity cost ⟹ each entrant adds capacity, so market supply shifts right ⟹ \(P\) falls ⟹ repeat until \(P=\min ATC\) and \(\pi=0\), when the incentive to enter is gone.
Exit (price below break-even): \(P \lt \min ATC\) ⟹ \(\pi\lt 0\) ⟹ firms leave because staying earns less than redeploying the capital elsewhere ⟹ capacity leaves, so market supply shifts left ⟹ \(P\) rises ⟹ repeat until \(P=\min ATC\) and \(\pi=0\), when the incentive to exit is gone.

The firm-level picture is the same equilibrium seen from one factory: in the long run the horizontal price line is pushed down (or up) until it just kisses the bottom of the \(ATC\) curve, where \(MC\) passes through.

Long-run equilibrium: worked example

Intuition: how the long run undoes a loss Start a market in the red. Each firm is too small and the price too low, so everyone bleeds. Firms leave one by one; with fewer firms the price climbs; output per surviving firm rises toward its efficient scale; the climbing stops the instant the survivors break even. The arithmetic below is just that story with numbers.

Slide data: market demand \(Q_d = 900 - 20P\); each (identical) firm has total cost \(c(q) = 100 + 10q + q^2\). In the short run there are \(n=100\) firms and the price is \(P=\$20\), below average cost of \(\$35\), so firms are losing money.

  1. \(MC = \dfrac{\mathrm{d}c}{\mathrm{d}q} = 10 + 2q\) differentiate the cost function
  2. \(ATC = \dfrac{c(q)}{q} = \dfrac{100}{q} + 10 + q\) divide total cost by output; the \(100/q\) term is \(AFC\), the \(10+q\) is \(AVC\)
  3. At \(\min ATC\), \(MC = ATC\): \(10 + 2q = \dfrac{100}{q} + 10 + q\) impose the long-run break-even-at-the-minimum condition
  4. \(q = \dfrac{100}{q} \;\Rightarrow\; q^2 = 100\) cancel the \(10\), subtract \(q\) from both sides
  5. \(q^\* = 10\), and \(P^\* = MC(10) = 10 + 2(10) = \$30\) (check: \(ATC(10)=10+10+10=\$30\) ✓)

Now close the market: find how many firms the demand will support at that price.

  1. \(Q = 900 - 20(30) = 300\) plug the long-run price into market demand
  2. \(n = \dfrac{Q}{q^\*} = \dfrac{300}{10} = 30\) firms total output divided by each firm's efficient output
Worked example: confirming the short-run loss and the shake-out

With \(n=100\) firms, each firm's short-run supply is \(P = MC = 10+2q\), i.e. \(q = (P-10)/2\). Market supply is \(100 \times (P-10)/2 = 50(P-10)\). Set supply equal to demand:

\(50(P-10) = 900 - 20P \;\Rightarrow\; 70P = 1400 \;\Rightarrow\; P = \$20.\)

Each firm then makes \(q = (20-10)/2 = 5\), at which \(ATC = 100/5 + 10 + 5 = \$35\). Loss per firm \(= (P - ATC)\,q = (20-35)\times 5 = -\$75\). Losses drive exit; the firm count falls from \(100\) to \(30\) and the price rises from \(\$20\) to \(\$30\), exactly the long-run equilibrium above.

SURVIVAL: THE THREE CRITICAL PRICE CUTOFFS

Intuition: three different bars, three different horizons A firm faces three thresholds, not one, and which bites depends on the time horizon and on whether the money is already spent. In the short run you keep producing as long as you cover the costs that vary with output (you would lose the fixed costs anyway). In the long run you only stay if you also cover fixed operating costs. And you only build in the first place if, on top of all that, the price covers the cost of the capital you are about to sink. Once the capital is sunk it stops mattering, which is why the price that justifies entry is higher than the price that triggers exit.

Let capital cost be the annualised opportunity cost of the money tied up in the plant (its purchase price times the required rate of return, expressed per unit of capacity per year). A sunk cost is one already spent and unrecoverable. The incumbent rules on the slide use constant marginal cost, so \(MC = AVC\) up to capacity.

Short-run idling (shutdown)$$P \lt AVC \;\Rightarrow\; \text{idle the plant; otherwise produce at capacity}$$
Long-run exit (incumbent, capital already sunk)$$P \lt AVC + AFC \;(= ATC) \;\Rightarrow\; \text{exit; otherwise stay}$$
Entry (greenfield, must also earn a return on capital)$$P \gt AVC + AFC + \text{capital cost} \;\Rightarrow\; \text{enter}$$
Trap: the red herring of current prices. "Aluminium prices are at historic lows, so Alusaf should drop the project." Wrong logic. Short-run price is set by today's demand and supply curves and says almost nothing about long-run profitability. The entry decision turns on a forecast of long-run price against all-in costs, not on the spot price. Equally, do not let sunk capital into the exit decision: an incumbent stays while \(P \ge ATC\) even though a new entrant at that same price would not build.

ALUSAF'S ENTRY DECISION: WORKED EXAMPLE

Alusaf is not an incumbent deciding whether to keep running; it is contemplating building. So the relevant cutoff is the entry price, which must cover variable cost, fixed operating cost, and the cost of the capital about to be sunk. Case cost structure (per ton): electricity and alumina run at \(41\%\) of the aluminium price; the remaining variable items are other raw material \(\$143\), consumables \(\$32\), freight \(\$40\); the fixed-in-short-run items are plant power and fuel \(\$17\), maintenance \(\$38\), labour \(\$68\), G&A \(\$32\).

  1. \(AVC = MC = 143 + 32 + 40 = \$215\) (plus \(0.41P\) for electricity and alumina, which scales with price) sum the variable line items
  2. \(AFC = 17 + 38 + 68 + 32 = \$155\) sum the fixed-in-short-run line items
  3. Capital cost per ton of capacity \(= \dfrac{\$1.6\text{bn}}{466{,}000} = \$3{,}433\) spread the plant's build cost over its annual capacity (466 ktpy)
  4. Annualised capital cost \(= 3{,}433 \times 0.15 = \$515\) per ton-year (at a \(15\%\) required return; \(\times 0.10 = \$343\) at \(10\%\)) multiply by the required rate of return: this is the opportunity cost of the capital
  5. All-in unit cost \(AC = 0.41P + 215 + 155 + 515 = 0.41P + 885\) add variable + fixed + capital; the \(0.41P\) keeps the price-linked input on the left later
  6. Entry requires \(P \ge AC\): \(P \ge 0.41P + 885 \;\Rightarrow\; 0.59P \ge 885\) collect the price terms
  7. \(P \ge \dfrac{885}{0.59} = \$1{,}500\) in the long run to justify the investment
Being a cost leader is not enough. Alusaf has a large variable-cost advantage, but the entry decision needs a forecast of long-run price above \(\$1{,}500\) to clear the all-in (variable + fixed + capital) hurdle. Cost leadership wins the survival game; it does not by itself justify sinking \(\$1.6\)bn.
Worked example: Alusaf's variable-cost advantage

At a price of \(\$1{,}110\), Alusaf's variable cost is \(0.41 \times 1{,}110 + 215 = 455 + 215 = \$670\) per ton. The marginal firm setting that price has \(AVC = \$1{,}110\). Alusaf's cost is therefore about \(670/1{,}110 \approx 60\%\) of the marginal firm's, and only \(9\) of the \(104\) plants in the industry are cheaper. Alusaf comfortably survives at \(\$1{,}110\); the open question is only whether long-run price clears the \(\$1{,}500\) entry bar.

Intuition: zero economic profit ⟺ zero NPV Charging capital its opportunity cost inside the annual cost figure is just the flow version of discounting the build cost up front. 10 · Static Games & Nash Equilibrium

This lecture opens Part IV, the move from price-taking and monopoly into oligopoly: a market with a finite number of firms whose profits depend on each other's choices. Everything in L08-L09 assumed a firm could optimise in isolation. Here it cannot. We build the toolkit of one-shot simultaneous-move games (players, strategies, payoffs, the payoff matrix), the two "Golden Rules" of dominant and dominated strategies, and the solution concept that ties them together, the Nash equilibrium. L11 adds timing and commitment; L12 puts real numbers on it with Cournot and Bertrand.

10.1 What makes a market an oligopoly

🎙 In class "My profits depend on others' decisions; my strategies depend on others' strategies." Glover's word for this is strategic interdependence. A monopolist reads demand off a curve and picks a point. An oligopolist is playing against other minds that are simultaneously reading her. The whole lecture is about how to reason when the thing you are optimising over is reacting to you.

How concentrated does a market have to be to count? The standard measure is the Herfindahl-Hirschman Index. Let \(s_i\) be firm \(i\)'s market share (as a percentage), so the sum of shares is 100.

HHI (market concentration)\[ \text{HHI} = \sum_i s_i^{\,2} \]
  • \(\text{HHI} > 1000\) is treated as an oligopoly.
  • \(\text{HHI} > 2000\) (some agencies use 1800) is highly concentrated and heavily scrutinised by competition authorities.
  • A complementary lens is the concentration ratio: the combined share of the four largest firms, the next four, the next twelve.
🎙 In class The beer industry profit-vs-concentration curve "is actually quite steep at the beginning. When you pull down to really that monopoly power, that's when you make the highest profits. As soon as you start to enter duopoly, triopoly, there is profit erosion." Why so fast? "If you have very similar goods even in just duopoly, your goods are almost perfect substitutes, the only thing you can compete on is prices, and so it's a race to the bottom very, very quickly." That race-to-the-bottom is Bertrand competition (L12); flag it now, prove it later.

10.2 The anatomy of a game

Intuition: normal form A simultaneous game is fully described by three things and nothing else: who is playing, what each can do, and what each gets for every combination of choices. Lay the last one out as a grid and you have the entire strategic situation on one page. That grid is the engine for every result below.
The four primitives of a game (Glover's framing)
ElementDefinition
PlayersThe participants in the strategic interaction.
StrategiesThe choices available to each player.
PayoffsA numerical representation of each player's objective; every player maximises her own payoff.
Type of gameSimultaneous vs sequential; one-shot vs repeated. This lecture is simultaneous, one-shot ("static").

The normal form (or payoff matrix) writes one player's strategies down the rows, the other's across the columns, and puts the pair of payoffs \((\pi_1,\pi_2)\) in each cell, by convention row player's payoff first. \(\pi_i\) is firm \(i\)'s profit (or, in a non-business game, its utility).

Payoffs are ordinal, not literal cash. A student asked whether a (0, 0) outcome should really be (−10, −10). Glover's answer: what matters is the ranking of outcomes, not the units. You may rescale freely as long as you preserve each player's ordering of the four cells. Do not waste exam time arguing the exact number in a cell; argue the ranking.

10.3 Best responses and dominant strategies

🎙 In class Glover solves every matrix the same mechanical way, out loud: "I'm Tony. If Lucy splits, what's my best option? I compare which two numbers? If Lucy steals, I compare these two." You hold the opponent's choice fixed (read down one column), then pick your own best row. Do that for each of the opponent's choices. That single habit generates best responses, dominance, and Nash all at once. Examinable method: state the comparison column by column; never eyeball the whole matrix.

A best response to a given strategy of the opponent is the strategy that gives you the highest payoff against that specific choice. A dominant strategy is one that is a best response no matter what the opponent does.

Golden Rule 1 (dominant strategy). A player has a dominant strategy if it gives higher payoffs regardless of what others do; it is uniformly better than all her other strategies. If you have one, use it. Expect your opponent to use hers if she has one. If both players have a dominant strategy, the Nash equilibrium is both playing it.

10.4 The Prisoner's Dilemma

🎙 In class Glover's live example is the TV game show Golden Balls: two contestants each secretly choose Split or Steal for a jackpot. Split together and you share it; steal while they split and you take the lot; both steal and you both get nothing (plus, he adds, "humiliation", the −0.01). It is a one-shot simultaneous game, "just like Bonnie and Clyde."
Golden Balls / Prisoner's Dilemma, payoffs (Tony, Lucy). Best responses in bold.
Lucy: SplitLucy: Steal
Tony: Split0.5 , 0.5−0.01 , 1
Tony: Steal1 , −0.010 , 0

Best-response reasoning, done column by column:

If Lucy Splits: Tony compares 0.5 (Split) vs 1 (Steal)⟹Steal
If Lucy Steals: Tony compares −0.01 (Split) vs 0 (Steal)⟹Steal

Steal beats Split in both columns, so Steal is Tony's dominant strategy; by symmetry it is Lucy's too. Each playing her dominant strategy gives the unique outcome (Steal, Steal) = (0, 0). Yet (Split, Split) = (0.5, 0.5) would have made both better off. That is the dilemma: individually rational play produces a collectively bad result.

🎙 In class "The Z-score is a prisoner's dilemma. How you'll be graded in all of your classes at INSEAD." Everyone's dominant strategy is to study; everyone does; but because grading is on the distribution, "a level change will not do anything", so the whole cohort could go skiing, sit the exam cold, and end up with the identical ranking and far more fun. Same structure as zero effort in group work: each person's dominant strategy is to free-ride, so the project does not get done. The exam wants you to recognise this structure, not memorise the show.
The Nash equilibrium is NOT always the worst outcome for everyone. Asked "will the Nash equilibrium always be worse for all parties than the Pareto-efficient outcome?", Glover answered: only in the prisoner's dilemma specifically, not in games generally. The PD's defining feature is that its NE is Pareto inferior (everyone could be made better off). Do not generalise that property to every game, and do not assume every NE is "bad". The GM/Ford game in 10.6 is a counter-example where the dominant-strategy outcome is also the best one.

10.5 Finding the Nash equilibrium

Nash equilibrium (NE). A combination of strategies, one per player, such that no player can do strictly better by unilaterally changing her own strategy, given what everyone else is doing. Equivalently: every player is simultaneously playing a best response to the others. With perfect information about the payoffs and both players rational, this is the predicted outcome of the game.

The fastest route: if every player has a dominant strategy, the NE is the cell where they all play it. The cigarette-advertising game shows the full method with real numbers.

Worked example: finding the NE in the TV advertising game

Players: RJR and Philip Morris. Strategies: Advertise ("Ads") or "No Ads". Setup: each firm earns $50m from its own customers; advertising costs $20m; advertising captures $30m from the rival.

  1. (No Ads, No Ads): nobody spends, nobody is poached: \(50\) and \(50\). baseline
  2. (Ads, No Ads): the advertiser earns \(50-20+30=60\); the rival loses $30m of customers: \(50-30=20\). spend $20m, capture $30m vs lose $30m
  3. (No Ads, Ads): by symmetry, \(20\) and \(60\). mirror of step 2
  4. (Ads, Ads): each spends $20m, each captures and loses $30m, which cancels: \(50-20+30-30=30\) and \(30\).
TV advertising game, payoffs (RJR, Philip Morris). Best responses in bold.
PM: No AdsPM: Ads
RJR: No Ads50 , 5020 , 60
RJR: Ads60 , 2030 , 30

RJR: if PM plays No Ads, 50 vs 60 ⟹ Ads; if PM plays Ads, 20 vs 30 ⟹ Ads. Ads is dominant; symmetric for PM. NE = (Ads, Ads) = (30, 30), Pareto inferior to (No Ads, No Ads) = (50, 50). A classic prisoner's dilemma.

Solve a PD by changing the game. "There's always a larger game being played." The 1970 US ban on TV cigarette advertising (firms accepted it in exchange for immunity from federal lawsuits) removed the Ads option for everyone, forcing the (No Ads, No Ads) outcome. Result: advertising fell by $63m and industry profits rose by $91m. The regulator did for the firms what they could not credibly do for themselves. The other documented escape is to make your rival believe you are irrational: in the second Golden Balls clip a contestant won by credibly committing to Steal, which collapsed the game to his favour (a preview of L11 commitment).

10.6 Dominated strategies and iterated elimination

Intuition: when no dominant strategy is obvious Sometimes no single strategy wins in every column. The trick is to work from the other end: delete the strategies that are never a best response (dominated strategies), because a rational player will never use them. Deleting them shrinks the matrix; in the smaller matrix new strategies may become dominated; repeat. If you keep going and one cell survives, that is the Nash equilibrium.
Golden Rule 2 (dominated strategy). A strategy is dominated if it is never a best response (a strictly worse choice whatever the opponent does). Avoid dominated strategies. Successively eliminate them; if only one outcome survives the iteration, it is the Nash equilibrium.

🎙 In class The "guess 2/3 of the average" game (everyone picks an integer 0-100, winner is closest to two-thirds of the mean) is iterated elimination in disguise. The NE assumes everyone iterates fully; real players stop after a round or two, which is why the winning guess is usually well above 0.

11 · Coordination & Sequential Games

L10 gave you the static, simultaneous toolkit: dominant and dominated strategies and the Nash equilibrium of a one-shot game. This lecture changes two things about the game itself and shows how each one changes the outcome. First, when a game has more than one Nash equilibrium (coordination games and battle-of-the-sexes), the question stops being "what is the equilibrium" and becomes "which equilibrium, and can a player tilt the game towards the one she prefers." Second, when moves happen in sequence rather than at once, the order of play and the ability to commit become weapons. The single big idea is commitment: a visible, costly, irreversible move made today that changes what your rival rationally does tomorrow. It is the bridge from one-shot Nash (L10) into the dynamics of oligopoly that follow (Cournot and Bertrand in L12, collusion in L13).

Notation for this section. A 2-player payoff pair is written \((\pi_1,\pi_2)\) where \(\pi_i\) is the payoff to player \(i\); the first number is the row player, the second the column player, unless a tree states otherwise. NE = Nash equilibrium. A dominant strategy gives a player a higher payoff regardless of what the other does; a dominated strategy is never a best response (uniformly worse), so it is eliminated. A best response is the strategy that maximises a player's payoff given the other's choice.

RECAP: THE GOLDEN RULES SO FAR

Intuition: how to read any game Work down a priority ladder. (1) If you have a dominant strategy, play it, and expect a rational rival to play hers. (2) If you have a dominated strategy, delete it, then look again at the smaller game; repeat. (3) If nobody has a dominant or dominated strategy, hunt for a Nash equilibrium: a pair of mutual best responses where neither player can profit by deviating alone.

Coordination games: more than one Nash equilibrium

Intuition: coordination Some games are not about beating the other player; they are about landing on the same square. The danger is getting stuck on the bad-but-stable equilibrium when a better-but-stable one exists.

Definition (reused all course). A Nash equilibrium is a profile of strategies, one per player, where no player can raise her payoff by unilaterally changing her own strategy: each is playing a best response to the others.

WHY THE NUMBER OF PLAYERS MATTERS

With three players the same structure gives two Nash outcomes, [All invest] and [None invest]. Coordination gets harder as the number of players rises: each extra player is one more party who has to be confident everyone else will move, so the good equilibrium becomes less likely to be reached even though it still exists. The slides list real coordination games with exactly this knife-edge:

  • Nuclear standoff: "go nuclear" vs "do not" has multiple equilibria; the job (Thomas Schelling's point) is to make the non-nuclear, peaceful outcome focal so both sides expect it.
  • Bank runs: if everyone keeps their money in, you keep yours in; if everyone runs, you should run too. Two equilibria, "All keep" and "All run". The system must coordinate on the better but riskier "All keep".
  • Sovereign debt and currency crises: the same all-or-nothing logic. Draghi's 2012 "Whatever it takes", backed by the LTRO liquidity programme, worked by shifting expectations and collapsing Spanish and Italian bond spreads, that is, by moving the market off the bad equilibrium onto the good one.

Sequential (dynamic) move games and the extensive form

Intuition: sequence is power Kierkegaard, quoted on the slide: "Life must be understood backward, but it must be lived forward." A sequential game is one you live forward, observing your rival's move before you choose, but solve backward, by asking what each player will rationally do at the very end and reasoning back to now. Crucially, by moving today you can shape what your rival rationally does tomorrow.
Definitions. The extensive form (a game tree) shows moves in order. A decision node is a point where one named player chooses among branches; a terminal node (leaf) carries the payoff pair reached by that path. A subgame is the whole tree hanging below any decision node. In a sequential game you choose after observing your opponent's action, and you can use today's action to influence tomorrow's.

The slides map the toys onto the business versions: Chess maps to entry-deterrence games; Tic-Tac-Toe to repeated pricing and capacity games; Cut-the-cake to union-management bargaining.

Golden Rule 4 (sequential games): Look forward and reason back. Anticipate what your rivals will do tomorrow in response to your action today, then pick the action today that is optimal given that anticipated response.

THE CENTIPEDE GAME (WHY PURE BACKWARD INDUCTION IS UNSETTLING)

A pie starts at $1 and grows by $1 each round. Players A and B alternate choosing Stop (S) or Continue (C). The first to say Stop takes the whole current pie; the other gets nothing. If nobody stops after 10 rounds, both get nothing.

  1. At round 10, B compares Stop (10) with Continue (0). B takes 10, so B stops.
  2. At round 9, A foresees that continuing hands the move to a B who will stop, leaving A with 0; stopping now gives 9. A stops (9 > 0).
  3. The same logic unravels every earlier node: each player, foreseeing the next player will stop, stops first. Every node resolves to Stop.
  4. At round 1, A stops and takes the $1 pie: outcome (1, 0). The cooperative path that would have grown the pie to $10 is never reached, even though both could do far better by continuing for a while.
Trap: the centipede shows the limit of pure rationality, not a prediction you should bet on. In experiments players continue for several rounds. Backward induction is the benchmark the exam wants; the behavioural gap (trust, reputation, repeated play) is the nuance.

Backward induction, subgame-perfection and first-mover advantage

Backward induction (Golden Rule 4 as a procedure). (1) Start at the end of the tree. (2) At each final decision node, the mover picks her best branch; trim the branches she would never play (her dominated paths). (3) Replace that node with its surviving payoff and repeat one step up. (4) Continue to the root. The path of best choices that survives is the predicted play.

Return the superjumbo to a sequence: Airbus moves first (Enter / Not enter), then Boeing responds having seen Airbus's choice.

  1. Lower subgame (Airbus did Not enter): Boeing compares Enter (100) vs Not enter (0). Boeing enters; this subgame is worth (0, 100).
  2. Upper subgame (Airbus did Enter): Boeing compares Enter (-20) vs Not enter (0). Boeing stays out, because fighting a committed first mover loses money; this subgame is worth (100, 0).
  3. Root: Airbus now chooses between a path worth 100 (Enter) and a path worth 0 (Not enter). Airbus enters.
  4. Outcome: Airbus enters, Boeing stays out, payoff (100, 0). Moving first selected the battle-of-sexes equilibrium that Airbus prefers. This is the first-mover advantage.
First-mover advantage. When a battle-of-the-sexes has two equilibria that each player ranks differently, the player who can commit first can pick her preferred equilibrium, provided the commitment is real (see below). Sequence converts a coordination standoff into a determinate win.

Credible vs non-credible threats: talk is cheap

Intuition: credibility A threat only changes behaviour if your rival believes you would actually carry it out when the time comes. Backward induction tests exactly that: at the node where you would have to execute the threat, is executing it your best response? If not, it is a non-credible threat, mere cheap talk, and the rival ignores it. To make a threat bite you must change the game so that carrying it out becomes your rational choice.

Boeing announced a 600-to-800-seat plane that some called a bluff "to preempt Airbus". It was cheap talk: Airbus launched the A380 anyway, and Boeing then chose to modernise the 747 rather than build a megaseater. What made Airbus's move stick was not an announcement but a commitment: it locked in orders from Singapore, Emirates, Qantas, Virgin and FedEx, six firms given 30% discounts off the $390m price, a move that was visible and costly to reverse. The A380 entered service in 2007 with 321 orders. The threat to compete was credible because backing out had been made expensive.

NUCLEAR DETERRENCE: A NON-CREDIBLE THREAT, AND HOW TO FIX IT

  1. If the USSR invades, the USA compares Nuclear (-100) with Accommodate (-1). The USA accommodates, because mutual annihilation is far worse than living with an invasion; the nuclear threat is not its best response.
  2. The USSR foresees accommodation, so invading yields 1 versus 0 for staying home. The USSR invades (1 > 0).
  3. Equilibrium: invasion, payoff (1, -1). The threat "we will go nuclear" failed because it was non-credible.

The slide's fix: commit to the "irrational" strategy by removing your own ability to back down, that is, take the choice out of human hands and automate the retaliation (mutually assured destruction, the doomsday machine). If the USA cannot choose to accommodate, the only outcome after invasion is (-100, -100), so the USSR now compares -100 with 0 and stays home. Making your own future options worse, and visibly so, is what makes the threat credible.

ENTRY DETERRENCE: BOTH CASES SIDE BY SIDE

An incumbent earns 100 as a monopolist. A potential entrant chooses Enter or Out; if it enters, the incumbent chooses to Fight (a price war) or Accommodate (share the market). The two trees below differ only in the incumbent's Accommodate payoff, and that one change flips the whole outcome.

  1. Base case. If the entrant enters, the incumbent compares Fight (-50) with Accommodate (50). It accommodates (50 > -50); fighting would only burn money, so the threat is not credible.
  2. The entrant foresees accommodation, so Enter (50) beats Out (0). Entry occurs; outcome (50, 50). The incumbent's monopoly profit of 100 is gone.
  3. Commitment case. The incumbent first spends on a costly campaign building a reputation as a fighter, so that accommodating later would destroy that investment and is now worth only -60. At the Fight-or-Accommodate node it compares Fight (-50) with Accommodate (-60).
  4. Fight is now the incumbent's best response (-50 > -60): the threat has become credible. The incumbent deliberately worsened its own Accommodate payoff to make Fight rational.
  5. The entrant foresees a fight (Enter would yield -50), so it stays Out: outcome (0, 100). By spending to make backing down painful, the incumbent keeps its whole monopoly profit. Commitment is worth 100 versus 50.
Worked example: does the reputation investment pay?

Without commitment the incumbent ends at the Accommodate leaf and earns 50. With a credible fighter reputation the entrant stays out and the incumbent earns the monopoly 100. The gain from committing is \(100-50 = 50\). So any reputation-building spend with a present cost below 50 is worth it: paying to lower your own Accommodate payoff from 50 to -60 is rational because it changes the entrant's behaviour, not because you ever intend to sit at the (50, -60) leaf. You never reach it; you are paid 100 precisely because you would have fought.

Trap, the heart of the lecture: do not confuse a threat with a commitment. A threat is cheap talk and is ignored if carrying it out is not the threatener's best response at the relevant node. A commitment is a move that is visible, costly, and irreversible: it changes the payoffs so that the once-incredible action becomes rational. A reversible commitment is the same as no commitment at all, because backward induction will see straight through it.

The slides press the same tree onto current events (Russia-Ukraine: continue fighting vs withdraw, accommodate vs fight; then "Trump moves" by adding a third player who can grant or withhold support, which changes the credible outcome) and into bargaining, below. The mechanics are identical: redraw the tree, solve backward, ask at each node whether the threatened action is actually a best response.

CAPACITY AND DELEGATION AS COMMITMENT DEVICES

The unifying idea: an irreversible up-front move (sunk capacity that you would now want to use, an agent instructed not to concede, an automated retaliation) shifts the rival's backward-induction reasoning in your favour. The formal capacity-commitment model (irreversible capacity choice before the pricing or quantity stage) is developed in the imperfect-competition lecture (L12); here it is one more instance of "change the game first, then play it".

Ultimatum bargaining and the behavioural caveat

Intuition: ultimatum Split $10. The Proposer offers a division "$x for me, $(10 - x) for you"; the Responder either Accepts (the split stands) or Rejects (both get 0). Backward induction is brutal: a rational Responder accepts any positive crumb, because anything beats 0, so the Proposer should grab almost everything.
  1. At the Responder's node, Accept yields \(10-x \ge 0\), Reject yields 0. The Responder accepts any offer with \(10-x > 0\).
  2. Foreseeing this, the Proposer makes \(10-x\) the smallest acceptable amount and keeps the rest. Offer the minimum, for example $1; keep $9.
  3. Backward-induction (SPE) prediction: roughly (9, 1), the Proposer takes almost the whole pie.

Real behaviour rejects this. Common offers cluster near a fair split, and low offers are frequently rejected. The slides give the reasons: Proposers act on fairness or inequity-aversion, fear that an angered Responder will reject, and the shadow of repeated interaction; Responders weigh reputation, the size of the stakes, and whether the money was earned or unearned. The Dictator game (where the Responder cannot reject) still shows giving: across 616 experiments about 28% give something to a stranger. Cultural differences turn out to matter very little; the design of the rules (anonymous, double-blind, pie size, prior experience, a market framing) matters far more. The exam point: state the SPE prediction first, then name the behavioural forces that pull real outcomes towards fairness.

Wrap-up (the lecture's own checklist). Rules matter (adding players can hand someone a dominant strategy). Timing and order of moves matter (first-mover advantage). Context matters (pie size, culture, anonymity). Commitments change the game, but only if they are visible, credible, costly (cheap talk is not enough), and inflexible and irreversible (a reversible commitment is no commitment). Sometimes an apparently "irrational" pre-commitment (delegation, a negotiating agent, mutually assured destruction) is exactly what makes a threat work.
Exam lens.
  • Tree-solving is the core skill. Expect a small extensive-form game (entry deterrence, a Russia-Ukraine or Airbus-Boeing variant) and "find the outcome by backward induction." Method: start at the last node, pick each player's best branch, trim the rest, fold the payoff up, repeat to the root. Write the surviving path and the final payoff pair.
  • Credible vs non-credible threat is the favourite trap. A T/F or short question will hand you a threat and ask whether it deters. Test it at the node: is carrying out the threat the threatener's best response there? If not, it is cheap talk and does not deter. State that explicitly.
  • "How would you change the game?" Standard application: add a player (a subsidy or guarantor), change the order of moves (move first), or add a commitment that lowers your own off-path payoff (a costly reputation, sunk capacity, delegation). Show the new payoffs and re-solve.
  • Commitment must satisfy all four properties. If asked why a commitment fails, point to the missing one: not visible, not credible, not costly, or reversible. "Reversible commitment = no commitment" is a quotable line.
  • Coordination vs battle-of-the-sexes. Be able to find both pure NE in a 2x2 and say which equilibrium each player prefers; in battle-of-the-sexes, name the first-mover or commitment move that selects the preferred one.
  • Ultimatum / centipede: give the backward-induction (SPE) prediction first, then the behavioural deviation (fairness, reputation, repeated play). Examiners want both, in that order.

1 · Bertrand competition with homogeneous goods: the trap

🎙 In class Glover's set-up: in 1986 Nynex sold its New York phone directory on CD-ROM for $10,000 a disk. The data-entry cost is enormous (a "first-copy cost"), but burning the next CD costs almost nothing, so marginal cost is near zero and the $10,000 price is almost pure margin. That margin screams "come in". ProCD enters with an identical product (it even ships the typing to China to be cost leader). Now there are two firms selling perfect substitutes with no capacity limit. "You only compete on price to steal market share. Therefore it is a race to the bottom. And the bottom is marginal cost." The duopoly reproduces the perfect-competition outcome: \(P=MC\), zero economic profit, with only one rival. Consumers win; the product ends up "free".
The Bertrand paradox (homogeneous goods). With two or more firms selling identical goods, no capacity constraints, and the same constant \(MC\), the unique Nash equilibrium is
$$ p_1 = p_2 = MC $$
Bertrand outcome with homogeneous goods: price collapses to marginal cost, the same prediction as perfect competition, with only two firms.
Intuition — why undercutting is dominant Let \(\varepsilon\) be a one-cent price cut. If my rival prices high, I shave \(\varepsilon\) off and, because the goods are identical, every customer switches to me: I take the whole market. So whatever the rival does, undercutting pays. Both firms reason this way, so both keep cutting until neither can profitably cut further, which is exactly \(P=MC\).

Glover frames the two-firm game as a Prisoners' Dilemma (the "Bertrand trap"). Using the deck's payoffs (firm gets \(5\) if both price high, \(10\) if it alone undercuts while the rival prices high, \(0\) if it alone prices high, \(3\) if both undercut):

Pricing as a Prisoners' Dilemma (payoffs: Nynex, ProCD)
ProCD: price high \(P_{mon}\)ProCD: undercut \(P_{mon}-\varepsilon\)
Nynex: price high \(P_{mon}\)5 , 50 , 10
Nynex: undercut \(P_{mon}-\varepsilon\)10 , 03 , 3  (NE)
Rival prices high⟹I undercut by \(\varepsilon\): identical goods mean ALL demand switches to me, so 10 > 5⟹undercutting is a dominant strategy for both⟹both undercut: (3,3)⟹the only stable price gap that survives undercutting is zero, i.e. \(P=MC\)

The non-obvious step is the first arrow: it works only because the goods are perfect substitutes, so the cross-price response is total. Both firms would be better off at (5,5), but the dominant strategy drags them to (3,3), the classic dilemma. Real cases Glover runs through: India mobile tariffs (Rs 16.8 → Rs 0.3/min), France telecoms once Free entered (his own bill fell to ~$1/month), Didi vs Uber in China ($2bn loss for Uber), Northwest "Kids fly free" provoking American Airlines' 50% fare cut ($13bn of industry losses), and cigarettes in Costa Rica.

Don't confuse the Bertrand prediction with the Cournot one. Bertrand (price competition, identical goods) predicts \(P=MC\) and zero profit with just two firms, the most extreme result in the course. Quantity competition (Cournot, next session) gives an interior price above \(MC\) that sits between monopoly and perfect competition. Same number of firms, very different verdict: the prediction hangs entirely on whether firms compete on price or on quantity, and on whether goods are identical.
🎙 In class Predatory pricing, Dow vs the Bromine Convention (1904, slide 11). The German cartel priced bromine at 46¢/lb; Herbert Dow's electrolysis made him efficient at 34¢. The cartel tried to kill him with a price war, flooding the US at 15¢ then 10¢. But they only cut prices in the US, so Dow quietly bought the cheap bromine, re-labelled it, and sold it back into Europe at 27¢. The cartel thought its own members were defecting. Lesson Glover draws: deep pockets are not enough; a price war on a homogeneous good only works if you cut everywhere and leave no arbitrage gap.

2 · Bertrand with differentiated products: the Cola Wars

🎙 In class "With just slight differentiation, things are not so bad. Profits may actually even be possible." Even fictitious differentiation (pink vs blue packaging, a different scent) gives each firm a sliver of loyal demand, so a one-cent undercut no longer steals the whole market. The trap softens from "\(P=MC\)" to "an interior Nash above cost". Coke and Pepsi are the worked case (real estimated data from Gasmi-Laffont-Vuong). Prices/costs are in constant $ per 10 cases; quantities in tens of millions of cases per year.

The only change from monopoly demand is that the rival's price now sits inside your demand curve instead of being buried in the constant. Each firm faces a residual demand: its own demand once the rival's price is fixed.

$$ Q_{coke} = 64 - 4\,p_{coke} + 2\,p_{pepsi}, \qquad MC_{coke}=5 $$
Coke's residual demand. The \(+2\,p_{pepsi}\) term is the positive cross-price effect: a Pepsi price rise lifts Coke's demand (substitutes).
$$ Q_{pepsi} = 50 - 5\,p_{pepsi} + 1\,p_{coke}, \qquad MC_{pepsi}=4 $$
Pepsi's residual demand. Pepsi is the "fringe" player: it reacts more to its own price (steeper, \(-5\)) and Coke's price helps it less (\(+1\) vs Coke's \(+2\)). Pepsi's edge is the lower \(MC\) (4 vs 5).
Intuition — the choke price now moves In monopoly the choke price (where demand hits zero) was a fixed number. Under Bertrand it is the only thing that changes: your choke price slides up when your rival prices higher. Strategic interaction is, in Glover's words, just "how your competitor's strategy changes your own choke price."
$$ p_{coke}^{\,choke} = \frac{64 + 2\,p_{pepsi}}{4} = 16 + 0.5\,p_{pepsi} $$
Coke's choke price (set \(Q_{coke}=0\)). It rises with \(p_{pepsi}\).

With linear residual demand and constant \(MC\), the optimal price is the midpoint pricing rule from earlier in the course (the profit-maximising price for a linear demand sits halfway between the choke price and \(MC\)):

$$ p^{*} = \frac{p^{\,choke} + MC}{2} $$
Midpoint pricing rule. Equivalent to \(MR=MC\) for linear demand; reused here with a choke price that depends on the rival.

Substituting the choke price gives each firm's best-response (reaction) function: its profit-maximising price as a function of the rival's price.

  1. Start from the midpoint rule for Coke: \( p_{coke} = \dfrac{p_{coke}^{\,choke} + MC_{coke}}{2}\). Optimal price is halfway between choke price and marginal cost.
  2. Substitute Coke's choke price and \(MC_{coke}=5\): \( p_{coke} = \dfrac{(16 + 0.5\,p_{pepsi}) + 5}{2}\). Plug in the choke price that moves with Pepsi's price.
  3. \( p_{coke} = \dfrac{21 + 0.5\,p_{pepsi}}{2} \). Collect constants in the numerator.
  4. Coke's reaction function: \( p_{coke}(p_{pepsi}) = 10.5 + 0.25\,p_{pepsi} \).

The identical steps for Pepsi (choke price \(p_{pepsi}^{\,choke} = 10 + 0.2\,p_{coke}\), \(MC_{pepsi}=4\)) give:

$$ p_{pepsi}(p_{coke}) = 7 + 0.1\,p_{coke} $$
Pepsi's reaction function.
Strategic complements. Both reaction functions have a positive slope (+0.25, +0.1): if my rival raises its price, my best response is to raise mine too. Prices are strategic complements, and the reaction lines slope upward. (Under quantity competition the reaction functions slope down, making quantities strategic substitutes; that is the contrast the next session draws out.)

I have not seen Glover use the exact term in this transcript, so treat the label, not the maths, as my addition.

The Bertrand-Nash equilibrium is the pair of prices where both reaction functions hold at once (each firm is best-responding to the other).

Worked example — solving for the Bertrand-Nash prices and profits

Solve the two reaction functions simultaneously.

  1. Substitute Pepsi's BR into Coke's: \(p_{coke} = 10.5 + 0.25(7 + 0.1\,p_{coke})\). Replace \(p_{pepsi}\) with Pepsi's best response.
  2. \(p_{coke} = 10.5 + 1.75 + 0.025\,p_{coke} = 12.25 + 0.025\,p_{coke}\). Expand.
  3. \(0.975\,p_{coke} = 12.25 \Rightarrow p_{coke}^{*} = 12.56\). Collect \(p_{coke}\) and divide.
  4. \(p_{pepsi}^{*} = 7 + 0.1(12.56) = 8.26\).

Quantities: \(Q_{coke}=64-4(12.56)+2(8.26)=30.3\); \(Q_{pepsi}=50-5(8.26)+12.56=21.3\).

Profits \(\pi=(p-MC)\,Q\): Coke \((12.56-5)(30.3)=\mathbf{229}\); Pepsi \((8.26-4)(21.3)=\mathbf{90.6}\); industry \(\mathbf{319.6}\). (Real market prices were close: \(p_{coke}=12.90,\ p_{pepsi}=8.16\), so the firms played roughly the Bertrand-Nash, not collusion.)

3 · Resolving the trap: tacit collusion

🎙 In class "Cartels are just monopoly pricing." A cartel maximises the sum of the firms' profits, which means charging more than the Nash prices. The catch is that collusion is not a Nash equilibrium of the one-shot game: each firm is tempted to undercut. The escape, Glover stresses, is that the game is repeated to infinity and played by stable firms that value the future. Then "profits out to infinity when you are colluding are necessarily higher than making a quick buck and playing the Nash."
$$ \max_{p_{coke},\,p_{pepsi}}\ \big[p_{coke}Q_{coke} - 5\,Q_{coke}\big] + \big[p_{pepsi}Q_{pepsi} - 4\,Q_{pepsi}\big] $$
Cartel objective: maximise joint profit. Solution: \(p_{coke}=13.8,\ p_{pepsi}=10.14\), total profit \(336.34 \gt 319.6\) (the Bertrand-Nash total). Both firms charge more and both earn more.

GRIM TRIGGER: WHEN DOES COLLUSION HOLD?

Intuition — grim trigger Cooperate (keep prices high) for as long as the rival does. The instant anyone cheats, punish by reverting to the cut-throat Bertrand price forever. Cheating buys a one-period windfall but forfeits the cooperative stream for all time. Collusion holds if the future you give up outweighs the quick buck.

Using the deck's stylised numbers (cooperate pays 700 every period; cheating pays 800 this period, then the grim punishment leaves you with 200 forever after), with discount rate \(r\):

  1. \(PV(\text{cooperate}) = 700 + \dfrac{700}{r}\). 700 today plus a perpetuity of 700 from next period on.
  2. \(PV(\text{cheat}) = 800 + \dfrac{200}{r}\). 800 today (the windfall) plus a perpetuity of the punishment payoff 200.
  3. Collusion holds when \(700 + \dfrac{700}{r} \gt 800 + \dfrac{200}{r}\). Require the cooperative PV to beat the cheating PV.
  4. \(\dfrac{500}{r} \gt 100\). Subtract 700 and \(200/r\) from both sides.
  5. \(r \lt 5\): collusion is sustainable only if the discount rate is below 500%.
Reading the condition \(r\lt 5\)

\(r=5\) is absurdly impatient: "if I loan you a dollar today, I demand $5 tomorrow." Real long-lived firms (Coke, Pepsi, KLM) have per-period discount rates near \(1.00001\), nowhere near 5, so the condition holds easily. Two levers make it easier still: a harsher punishment (lower the 200) widens the gap, and more patience (lower \(r\)) raises the weight on the future. The danger is short-horizon "hit and run" entrants with high \(r\), who will cheat and trigger a price war.

Tit-for-tat. Anatol Rapoport's one-line algorithm won Axelrod's tournament: start by cooperating, then simply "do what was done to you" the previous period. It is nice (cooperates first), provocable (punishes a cheat immediately), and forgiving (returns to cooperation as soon as the rival does). Its flaw: two tit-for-tat players can get stuck in a mutual punishment loop, so a small dose of randomness (occasionally reset to cooperation) is added in practice. Glover: "half Jesus, half beast." Business versions: "I will match the competition" / "I will not be undercut" clauses.
Tactics that facilitate tacit collusion (slide 31, exam-relevant list). Repeated interaction with a long horizon; fewer players of similar size (smaller temptation, easier to detect cheating); a price leader who signals (the airline "jet-fuel surcharge" newspaper quote that borders on explicit collusion); credible punishment (Saudi Arabia flooding oil to force out shale; P&G's marketing blitz when share drops); low industry turnover and a growing market (both raise the value of the future).
Exam lens. (1) The headline T/F trap. "Two firms are enough to drive price to marginal cost." TRUE under Bertrand with homogeneous goods; FALSE if goods are differentiated or if firms compete on quantity. Always state the assumptions before answering: identical goods, no capacity limit, constant \(MC\), price competition. (2) The standard calculation. Given two linear residual-demand curves and constant \(MC\), be able to: derive each choke price, apply the midpoint rule to get both reaction functions, solve them simultaneously for the Bertrand-Nash prices, then compute \(\pi=(p-MC)Q\) for each firm. The Cola-Wars numbers (\(p_{coke}^*=12.56,\ p_{pepsi}^*=8.26\); \(\pi=229,\ 90.6\)) are the template. (3) Collusion sustainability. Phrase the answer as a comparison of PV streams, not a one-shot payoff. (4) Differentiated vs homogeneous. Glover's recap (slide 19): identical goods ⟹ price wars inevitable and market-share games are "PD on steroids"; differentiated goods ⟹ the dilemma persists but Nash profits are positive, just below collusive profits, and mergers help (but "check the HHI", expect antitrust opposition). Know which regime a scenario is in before you predict the outcome.

13 · Cournot Competition & Altering Incentives

This lecture closes Part IV by asking what L10 to L12 set up but did not resolve: rivals at a Nash equilibrium leave money on the table, so could they jointly do better by acting like one firm, and if so, what holds the arrangement together? The answer is built around three things the slides make concrete: a fully worked Cournot capacity game (iron ore, BHP and Rio Tinto), the prisoner's-dilemma tension that makes every cartel want to cheat (OPEC quota-busting), and a set of "change the game" devices firms use to sustain tacit collusion without an illegal agreement (switching costs, loyalty programmes, price-match guarantees). The recurring punchline is that quantity (Cournot) competition is far gentler on profits than price (Bertrand) competition, and that the right contractual or marketing move can quietly convert a price war into a stable truce.

Intuition ·collusion Collusion just means the firms together behave like a monopolist: restrict total output, push price up, and split the larger pie. The problem is that the monopoly output is not each firm's private best response. Once the rival holds back, you personally do better by selling a bit more. Everyone reasons this way, output creeps back up, and the cartel unravels. That gap, between what is collectively best and what is individually tempting, is the prisoner's dilemma, and the whole lecture is about it.

SETUP: THE MARKET

Two firms produce an identical good (iron ore) and compete on capacity / quantity \(q_i\) rather than price. Let \(P\) be price, \(Q=q_1+q_2\) total output, and the cost per unit of capacity be 30 (this is the constant marginal cost \(MC\)). The slides quote the iron-ore market as concentrated: \(CR4\) (the four-firm concentration ratio, the combined share of the four largest sellers) of 50% and an \(HHI\) (Herfindahl-Hirschman Index, the sum of squared percentage market shares) of 3400, well into "highly concentrated" territory and exactly the kind of structure where collusion is feasible.

Inverse demand \( P = 150 - Q, \qquad Q = q_1 + q_2 \)
Firm 1 profit (margin × output) \( \pi_1 = (P - 30)\,q_1 = (150 - q_1 - q_2 - 30)\,q_1 = (120 - q_1 - q_2)\,q_1 \)

The Cournot capacity game

Intuition ·best-response function A reaction (best-response) function answers one question: "given how much my rival is producing, how much should I produce?" Because the two firms' capacity choices move in opposite directions (if BHP builds more, the price falls, so the marginal mine is worth less to Rio Tinto, who builds less), each firm's best output slopes down in the rival's output. The equilibrium is where the two answers are mutually consistent: each is doing the best it can given the other.
  1. \( \pi_1 = (120 - q_1 - q_2)\,q_1 \) profit as a function of both capacities; treat \(q_2\) as fixed
  2. \( \dfrac{\partial \pi_1}{\partial q_1} = 120 - 2q_1 - q_2 = 0 \) first-order condition: this is firm 1's \(MR=MC\) written out (the \(-30\) cost is already inside the 120)
  3. \( q_1 = \dfrac{120 - q_2}{2} \qquad\text{and by symmetry}\qquad q_2 = \dfrac{120 - q_1}{2} \) firm 1's reaction function; the rival's is the mirror image
Reaction (best-response) functions \( q_1 = \dfrac{120 - q_2}{2}, \qquad q_2 = \dfrac{120 - q_1}{2} \)

The Cournot-Nash equilibrium (NE: a profile where neither firm wants to change its choice given the other's) is the intersection of the two reaction functions. By symmetry \(q_1=q_2\).

  1. \( q = \dfrac{120 - q}{2} \) impose symmetry: both firms pick the same \(q\)
  2. \( 2q = 120 - q \;\Rightarrow\; 3q = 120 \) clear the fraction and collect terms
  3. \( q_1 = q_2 = 40, \quad Q = 80 \) each firm's equilibrium capacity
  4. \( P = 150 - 80 = 70 \) plug \(Q\) back into demand
  5. \( \pi_i = (70 - 30)\times 40 = 1600 \) profit per firm; industry profit \(=3200\)
Worked example:Cournot-Nash, iron ore Demand \(P=150-Q\), unit cost 30, two symmetric firms. Reaction function \(q_i=(120-q_j)/2\). Collusion: the firms act as one

Intuition ·merged firm = monopolist If the two firms collude (or literally merge), they stop competing against each other and maximise joint profit. There is now effectively one decision-maker choosing total \(Q\), so the answer is the textbook monopoly quantity where \(MR=MC\). The output is lower, the price higher, and the pie bigger than under Cournot.

Why cartels are unstable: the deviation incentive

Intuition ·the temptation to cheat Suppose your rival loyally sticks to the cartel quota of 30. The price is high, so the most profitable thing you can do is quietly sell a bit more. You ride the high price the cartel created while grabbing extra volume. Every member faces the same temptation, which is exactly why real cartels need monitoring and threats to survive.
  1. Rival holds the collusive quota: \( q_2 = 30 \) assume the other firm cooperates
  2. \( q_1 = \dfrac{120 - 30}{2} = 45 \) your best response: read off the reaction function, not the quota
  3. \( Q = 75, \quad P = 150 - 75 = 75 \) extra output drags the price down a little, from 90 to 75
  4. deviator \( \pi = (75-30)\times 45 = 2025 \); loyal firm \( \pi = (75-30)\times 30 = 1350 \) cheating beats the collusive 1800; the loyal "sucker" earns below even the Cournot 1600

So the private payoff ranking for one firm is: cheat on a cooperating rival (2025) > both collude (1800) > both compete at Cournot (1600) > be the loyal sucker while the rival cheats (1350). That ordering is the signature of a prisoner's dilemma. To see the dominant strategy cleanly, compare two pure strategies, "Collude" (\(q=30\)) and "Compete" at the Cournot level (\(q=40\)):

Whatever the rival does, "Compete" pays more for firm 1 (2000 > 1800 if the rival colludes; 1600 > 1500 if the rival competes), so Compete is a dominant strategy and the unique NE is (Compete, Compete) = (1600, 1600), the bold cell. Capacity (Cournot) competition is a prisoner's dilemma in which the firms end up at \((1600,1600)\) when they could jointly reach \((1800,1800)\). Implicit collusion or a merger captures that missing 400 of joint profit. This is why the slides call merger / implicit collusion "beneficial" for the firms.
Real-world cheating: OPEC quotas The slides plot OPEC members' excess production relative to quota, monthly, 1986 to 2004. Almost every member overproduces: roughly 1 to 5% per month for most, with Saudi Arabia (the swing producer) the largest quota-buster at about 17.6%. This is the deviation incentive in the wild: each member gains by selling above quota while everyone else restrains output, so the cartel chronically leaks.
Do not confuse the collusive split with an equilibrium. \((30,30)\) maximises joint profit but it is off both reaction functions, so it is not self-enforcing in a one-shot game. The only one-shot Nash outcome is Cournot \((40,40)\). Collusion needs something extra (repetition, monitoring, punishment, or a "change the game" device) to survive.

Under Cournot, your weapon is capacity, which is sticky and only moves price gradually, so even perfect substitutes leave price above cost. Capacity is the long-run, slow-moving choice (think building a mine or a plant); price is the short-run, fast-moving one.

Resolving the prisoner's dilemma: change the game

If the one-shot game traps the firms at the competitive outcome, the way out is to change the game itself so that cutting price (or expanding) stops being a dominant strategy. The slides give the general principle and then three concrete devices.

Intuition ·sustaining tacit collusion Tacit collusion (no illegal agreement, just a stable mutual restraint) survives when a firm that cheats is detected quickly and punished, so the one-off gain from cheating is outweighed by the lost future cooperation. The slides put it as a two-part test: detection ("Customers!") and punishment (a contractual clause that automatically retaliates). DEVICE 1: SWITCHING COSTS & CONSUMER LOYALTY

  • Low switching costs mean customers jump ship the moment a rival cuts price, which makes price-cutting attractive and collusion hard to hold.
  • Raise switching costs to lock in customers: now a rival's price cut steals fewer of your customers, so the gain from undercutting shrinks and the truce holds.
  • Promises to customers ("I will keep prices low", a B2C pledge) look pro-consumer but the real question is how they change rivals' incentives, which is the theme of devices 2 and 3.

Complementors game (asymmetric Bertrand with complements)

Intuition: complements, not rivals Two firms make goods that are bought together. If one good gets cheaper or better, demand for the other rises. So you are not fighting over a fixed pie: you actively want your partner to succeed. The tension is who pays for the effort that benefits both.
Definition. Two firms whose goods are complements: if the price of one good rises, demand for the other falls. Slide examples: Microsoft (Windows) and Intel (CPUs) (the "Wintel" pairing); Sony (PS5) and Rockstar (games).
Rockstar (GTA) advertises more / lowers its price / spends more on R&D / expands into a new geography ⟹ the joint product becomes more attractive or cheaper ⟹ demand for the complement (the PS5) rises ⟹ Sony's sales rise too. Higher sales for one partner mean higher sales for the other, so complementors do not compete for market share.

Mechanism-chain drills

The causal chains the course tests most. Drill each one in both directions where the mechanism is symmetric: state the WHY at every link, not just the endpoints.

Entry and exit drive price to \(\min ATC\) [L9].
\(P\gt \min ATC\) ⟹ \(\pi\gt 0\), the lit "profit" sign ⟹ outsiders enter (capital earns more here than its opportunity cost) ⟹ market supply shifts right ⟹ \(P\) falls ⟹ repeat until \(P=\min ATC\), \(\pi=0\).
Reverse: \(P\lt \min ATC\) ⟹ \(\pi\lt 0\) ⟹ firms exit (redeploy capital) ⟹ supply shifts left ⟹ \(P\) rises ⟹ until \(P=\min ATC\), \(\pi=0\).
Tax incidence and deadweight loss [L1 to L2].
Per-unit tax \(\tau\) ⟹ a wedge \(P_b-P_s=\tau\) between buyer and seller price ⟹ marginal trades with \(V\) only just above \(C\) no longer clear the wedge ⟹ quantity falls \(Q^\*\to Q_\tau\) ⟹ those vanished trades are pure lost value, the \(DWL\) triangle \(\tfrac12\tau(Q^\*-Q_\tau)\). The split of the burden depends on relative elasticity, not on who legally remits.
Market power creates deadweight loss [L5 to L6].
Downward-sloping firm demand ⟹ \(MR\lt P\) (cutting price for one more unit sacrifices margin on all earlier units) ⟹ firm stops at \(MR=MC\), left of the efficient \(P=MC\) point ⟹ \(P^\*\gt MC\), output restricted ⟹ the trades between \(MC\) and \(P^\*\) never happen ⟹ ceded \(CS\) plus a \(DWL\) triangle. Price discrimination targets exactly this ceded surplus and lost trade.
Death spiral (the fixed-cost fallacy) [L5].
Price to recover fixed cost ⟹ higher price loses volume ⟹ the same \(FC\) spreads over fewer units ⟹ average cost rises ⟹ "justifies" a further price rise ⟹ more volume lost (Wang Laboratories). The break: \(FC\) never enters \(MR=MC\); it decides only whether to be in the business.
Bertrand race to the bottom [L12].
Identical goods, no capacity limit ⟹ a one-cent undercut steals the whole market ⟹ undercutting is dominant for both ⟹ each keeps cutting ⟹ the only stable price gap is zero ⟹ \(p_1=p_2=MC\), zero profit with just two firms. Slight differentiation breaks the first link, so the Nash price sits above \(MC\).
Prisoner's dilemma to sustained collusion [L10, L12 to L13].
Each firm's dominant strategy (undercut / overproduce) ⟹ one-shot Nash is the jointly worse outcome (Bertrand \(P=MC\); Cournot over-supply) ⟹ firms want the collusive (monopoly) outcome, but it is off both reaction functions, so each is tempted to cheat ⟹ repeat the game to infinity with patient firms ⟹ grim-trigger / tit-for-tat makes cheating forfeit the cooperative stream forever ⟹ collusion holds when \(PV(\text{cooperate})\gt PV(\text{cheat})\), i.e. \(r\) low and punishment harsh. Devices that help: switching costs, loyalty programmes, price-match guarantees (detection + automatic punishment).

Glossary & myths

Alphabetical definitions of every key term and symbol defined in the lectures, then the list of myths Glover flags. Be ready to refute each myth in one line.

TermDefinition
Allocative efficiencyThe right quantity is produced: every trade with \(V\ge C\) happens, none with \(V\lt C\). Achieved at the competitive equilibrium.
Average cost (\(ATC=AC\))Total cost per unit, \(TC/Q=AFC+AVC\). Governs entry/exit and scale decisions.
Average variable cost (\(AVC\))Variable cost per unit, \(VC/Q\). Governs the short-run idle/shutdown decision.
Backward inductionSolving a sequential game from its final nodes upward; the surviving path is the predicted play (the formal label is subgame-perfect equilibrium).
Bertrand competitionOligopoly where firms set prices. Homogeneous goods drive \(P=MC\); differentiation gives an interior Nash above cost.
Best responseThe strategy giving the highest payoff against a specific rival choice.
Choke price (\(P_c\), \(\bar P\))The price at which quantity demanded hits zero (the demand curve's vertical intercept).
Concentration ratio (\(CR4\))Combined market share of the four largest firms; a complement to the HHI.
Consumer surplus (\(CS\))Buyers' total gains from trade: the area below demand and above the price, \(v(Q)-PQ\).
Cournot competitionOligopoly where firms set quantity/capacity. Price stays above \(MC\) even for perfect substitutes; outcome between monopoly and Bertrand.
Cross-price elasticity (\(\varepsilon_{xy}\))% change in demand for \(x\) per % change in the price of \(y\). Positive = substitutes, negative = complements.
Deadweight loss (\(DWL\))Gains from trade destroyed by a distortion (tax, market power): trades that should happen but do not.
Dominant / dominated strategyDominant: a best response whatever the rival does (use it). Dominated: never a best response (eliminate it).
Economic profit (\(\pi\))Revenue minus all opportunity costs, including the return capital and effort could earn elsewhere. Zero in long-run competitive equilibrium.
Economies / diseconomies of scaleAverage cost falling / rising as the firm grows with all inputs variable; a long-run concept. MES is the output at the trough.
Elasticity, own-price (\(\varepsilon\), \(E\))% change in quantity per % change in own price; \(\varepsilon\lt 0\), magnitude \(E=|\varepsilon|\). Inelastic \(\lt 1\), elastic \(\gt 1\).
Explicit (third-degree) segmentationDifferent prices to groups split on an observable trait; needs market power, observability without deception, and no arbitrage.
First-mover advantageIn a battle-of-the-sexes, the player who can commit first selects her preferred equilibrium (if the commitment is real).
Fixed / variable cost (\(FC\), \(VC\))\(FC\) is independent of output (sunk in the short run); \(VC\) scales with output; \(TC=FC+VC\).
Gains from tradeThe surplus \(V-C\) created when a buyer's valuation exceeds a seller's cost; price only divides it.
Golden Pricing RuleThe markup form of the optimum, \(P^\*=\dfrac{E}{E-1}MC\): less elastic demand, fatter markup.
Grim triggerCooperate until anyone cheats, then revert to the cut-throat price forever. Sustains collusion if the future outweighs the windfall.
HHIHerfindahl-Hirschman Index, \(\sum s_i^2\); \(\gt 1000\) oligopoly, \(\gt 2000\) highly concentrated.
Implicit (second-degree) segmentation / screeningOffer a menu so customers self-select by type; the high type's self-selection constraint binds.
Income elasticity (\(\varepsilon_I\))% change in demand per % change in income. Positive = normal good, negative = inferior good.
Lerner index (\(\mathcal{L}\))The markup as a share of price, \((P-MC)/P=1/E\); a direct measure of market power.
Marginal cost / revenue (\(MC\), \(MR\))The cost / revenue of one more unit. Profit is maximised where \(MR=MC\).
Marginal / total valuation (\(mv\), \(v\))Height of demand at \(Q\) / area under demand up to \(Q\). The marginal vs total trap is the most common slip.
Market powerThe ability to raise price without losing all customers; a spectrum, not an on/off switch. Sources: scale, patents, unique resources, technology, branding, network effects.
Mid-point pricing ruleFor linear demand and constant \(MC\), \(P^\*=(\bar P+MC)/2\).
Nash equilibrium (NE)A strategy profile where no player can do better by unilaterally deviating: mutual best responses.
Natural monopolyWhen economies of scale persist over the whole market, one firm serves it at lower average cost than several could.
Opportunity costThe return from a resource's next-best alternative use; must be counted as a cost (Kaiser/Enron power resale).
Pareto efficiencyNo one can be made better off without making someone worse off; equivalently, all gains from trade are realised.
Perfect price discrimination (first-degree)Charge each buyer exactly their valuation; \(CS=0\), no \(DWL\). The efficiency benchmark, never fully reached.
Price takerA firm too small to move price; faces flat demand, so \(MR=P\) and it sets \(P=MC\).
Prisoner's dilemmaA game whose dominant-strategy Nash equilibrium is Pareto inferior to the cooperative outcome.
Producer surplus (\(PS\))Revenue above variable cost, \(TR-VC\); the area between price and supply; equals \(\pi+FC\).
Reaction (best-response) functionA firm's optimal price/quantity as a function of the rival's; the Nash equilibrium is where they cross.
Shutdown vs exitIdle (short run, test \(P\lt AVC\), \(FC\) sunk) is temporary; exit (long run, test \(P\lt ATC\)) is permanent.
Strategic complements / substitutesUpward-sloping reaction functions (Bertrand prices) / downward-sloping (Cournot quantities).
Sunk costMoney already spent and unrecoverable; identical across options, so it must be ignored. Counting it is the sunk-cost fallacy.
Tax wedge / incidenceA per-unit tax \(\tau\) separates \(P_b\) and \(P_s\) by \(\tau\); who bears it is set by relative elasticity, not by who remits.
Threat vs commitmentA threat is cheap talk unless executing it is the threatener's best response; a commitment is visible, costly and irreversible.
Myths Glover flags, be ready to refute.
  • "Who remits a tax determines who bears it." False: statutory and economic incidence differ; the same \(\tau\) on buyers or sellers gives identical \(P_b\), \(P_s\), \(Q\). [L1]
  • "Marginal and total are the same." False: marginal is curve height, total is the area beneath; \(CS\) and \(PS\) are areas. [L1]
  • "A linear demand curve has constant elasticity." False: constant slope, but \(E=P/(P_c-P)\) varies from \(0\) to \(\infty\) along it. [L3, L4]
  • "Sunk costs should be included; ignore opportunity cost if you signed a cheap contract." Both false: ignore sunk costs, count opportunity cost at resale/next-best value. [L4, L5]
  • "A monopolist can charge any price she likes." False: still bound by demand; she solves \(MR=MC\). Power sets the markup level, not freedom from the rule. [L5]
  • "Higher fixed costs should raise the price." False: \(FC\) does not enter \(MR=MC\); pricing to recover it triggers the death spiral. [L5]
  • "Price where demand is inelastic." False: \(MR\lt 0\) there; a profit-maximiser always operates where \(E\gt 1\). [L5, L6]
  • "Maximise revenue." Not profit max: revenue peaks at \(MR=0\), to the right of \(Q^\*\) (a principal-agent trap). [L5]
  • "Mail-in coupons are explicit segmentation." False: anyone can redeem, so customers self-select; that is implicit. [L6, L7]
  • "The damaged/cheap version costs less to make." Often false: crippling a product can cost more (Tesla software lock). [L7]
  • "The participation constraint pins the expensive price." False: participation pins the cheap price, self-selection pins the expensive one. [L7]
  • "A firm losing money should shut down." False in the short run: if \(\min AVC\lt P\lt \min ATC\) it covers variable cost and should keep operating. [L8]
  • "Today's low price means the industry is unprofitable long-run." False red herring: spot price reflects only current \(D\) and \(S\); entry turns on a long-run price forecast. [L9]
  • "Weigh sunk capital in the exit decision." False: sunk capital is ignored for exit; it matters only for entry. [L9]
  • "The Nash equilibrium is always Pareto inferior." False: true only of the prisoner's dilemma (GM/Ford is the counter-example). [L10]
  • "A threat always deters." False: a non-credible threat is cheap talk; a reversible commitment is no commitment. [L11]
  • "Two firms are always enough to drive price to marginal cost." True only under Bertrand with homogeneous goods; false for differentiated goods or Cournot. [L12, L13]
  • "The collusive (monopoly) split is a Nash equilibrium." False: it is off both reaction functions, so each firm is tempted to deviate. [L13]
