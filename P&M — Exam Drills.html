The exam in one breath. Three hours, closed-book except two A4 sheets and a simple calculator. Three parts: Part I multiple choice (5 pts each), Part II short-answer (~15–20 pts each), Part III longer multi-part problems (25 pts each). The real 2026 final was 215 pts (12 MC + 4 short + 3 long). Every question hangs on a business or news scenario, partial credit rewards shown work, and questions are not equally weighted so time management is graded in spirit. Two engines win most marks: the golden pricing rule / midpoint rule (price from elasticity and marginal cost) and price discrimination (segmentation, versioning, arbitrage). Game theory (Nash, Cournot, Bertrand, sequential, collusion) is the second pillar.

Price discrimination & segmentation 24 on past papers

When you see it: two customer types or two markets with different willingness-to-pay; words like "implicitly segment", "versioning", "Basic vs Pro", "coupons", "arbitrage / parallel imports", "two-part tariff", "bundle", or a firm selling the same good at different prices in two countries.
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. Identify the segments and write each type's willingness-to-pay (WTP) for each version/market.
  2. Benchmark with perfect price discrimination: charge each type its WTP; this is the upper bound on profit.
  3. If types are unobservable, you must satisfy participation (each type's surplus ≥ 0) and self-selection / incentive-compatibility (each type weakly prefers its intended product). Bind PC on the low type and SC on the high type.
  4. Solve for the high price by leaving the high type exactly the surplus it could get by buying the cheap version ("information rent").
  5. Compare profit across schemes (one version, segment, perfect PD, bundle) and pick the max. For two separate markets, set MR equal across markets (third-degree): charge more where demand is less elastic.
  6. For arbitrage: a price gap is sustainable only up to the transport cost; beyond it, collapse to a single combined-market price.
Worked model answer
Final 2026, Part III Q3 (Software Versioning at DataPro). MC = $0. 200 Large Corps: WTP_Pro = $800, WTP_Basic = $300. 500 Small Biz: WTP_Pro = $400, WTP_Basic = $200. Find the optimal versioned prices and profit.

Participation: bind the low type, PC_S gives P_Basic ≤ 200, so set P_Basic = 200. Self-selection of the high type: SC_L is 800 − P_Pro ≥ 300 − P_Basic, i.e. P_Pro − P_Basic ≤ 500, so P_Pro = 200 + 500 = 700. Check the rest: SC_S is 200 − 200 = 0 ≥ 400 − 700 = −300 ✓; PC_L is 800 − 700 = 100 ≥ 0 ✓.

Optimal prices: P_Pro = $700, P_Basic = $200. Profit = 200×700 + 500×200 = $140,000 + $100,000 = $240,000. For comparison, a uniform Pro-only price of $400 to everyone yields 400×700 = $280,000, and perfect PD yields 800×200 + 400×500 = $360,000. Here uniform pricing beats versioning because the self-selection constraint forces DataPro to leave $100 of rent with each large corporation. Deliberately damaging the Basic edition (lowering Large Corps' WTP for Basic toward $0) relaxes SC_L and lets DataPro raise the Pro price, the standard versioning rationale (Microsoft Office tiers, Intel disabling cores on cheaper SKUs).

Traps. Binding the wrong constraint (bind PC on the LOW type and SC on the HIGH type, not the reverse). Forgetting that perfect PD is the ceiling, not the answer when types are hidden. In two-market problems, charging more where demand is more elastic (it is the reverse). Treating arbitrage as all-or-nothing: with a positive transport cost the sustainable price gap equals that cost. Forgetting that under perfect PD some consumers pay MORE than before, so not everyone is better off.

Elasticity, revenue & the golden pricing rule 19 on past papers

When you see it: a percentage price change with an elasticity number; "where is demand unit-elastic / where is total revenue maximised"; a log-linear demand regression (coefficients = elasticities); or "what price should the firm set" with a marginal cost and an elasticity.
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. %ΔQ = −E × %ΔP. If E > 1 (elastic) a price rise lowers revenue; if E < 1 (inelastic) a price rise raises revenue; revenue is maximised at E = 1.
  2. On a linear demand curve, elasticity rises as you move up (toward the choke price); the lower-choke-price curve is more elastic at any common price.
  3. In log-linear demand, each coefficient IS that elasticity; sign of a cross-price coefficient tells substitutes (+) vs complements (−); income coefficient sign tells normal (+) vs inferior (−).
  4. Golden pricing rule: P* = [E / (E − 1)] × MC. Equivalently the midpoint rule P* = (choke + MC)/2 for linear demand.
  5. Back out an unknown (MC or E) by plugging into the golden rule and checking which value is economically sensible.
Worked model answer
Practice Quiz 2, Q9 (20th Century Fox DVD). Demand is log-linear: log(Q) = 400 − 1.5 log(P) + 2 log(P_T) + 3.0 log(S&P). Per-copy costs are $0.60 packaging + $1.20 copying + $2.00 shipping. What profit-maximising price should Fox charge?

The coefficient on log price is the (constant) own-price elasticity: E = 1.5. Marginal cost = 0.60 + 1.20 + 2.00 = $3.80 (the $40m star fee is not yet paid, so it is a fixed cost and irrelevant to pricing). Apply the golden rule: P* = [E/(E−1)]×MC = (1.5/0.5)×3.80 = 3×3.80 = $11.40. Answer (a).

Traps. Confusing %ΔP with the elasticity itself. Saying "always price in the elastic region and raise prices", you raise price only when demand is inelastic; the rule is to move TOWARD unit elasticity. Using market-level elasticity for a single firm's demand (a firm's demand is far more elastic than the market's, the LG flat-panel trap). Putting fixed or sunk costs into MC. Forgetting that for E < 1 the golden rule gives a negative or nonsensical price, which tells you the firm is not even on the profit-maximising part of the curve.

Nash equilibrium & dominance (payoff matrices) 14 on past papers

When you see it: two firms choosing simultaneously from a small menu (Enter/Out, High/Low, Advertise/Don't, Big/Small expansion); a payoff grid; "dominant strategy", "iterated elimination", "how many Nash equilibria", "what type of game is this".
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. Write the matrix cleanly: row player's payoff first in each cell.
  2. Check for a dominant strategy: does one action give a higher payoff for every action of the rival? If both have one, that cell is the unique Nash.
  3. Otherwise use iterated elimination of dominated strategies, then find cells that are mutual best responses (underline best responses column-by-column and row-by-row).
  4. Count the Nash equilibria; name the game (Prisoner's Dilemma if a dominant-strategy equilibrium is Pareto-dominated; coordination game if there are two aligned pure-strategy Nash).
  5. Comment: in a PD the dominant strategy makes both worse off than cooperation.
Worked model answer
Final 2026, Part II Q4(a)–(b) (Renewable Energy). Both Enter (2,2); one enters / other stays out: entrant €8bn, other −€1bn; both Out (3,3). Build the matrix, find dominant strategies, Nash equilibria, and the game type.

Matrix (SunCorp row, WindPower column): Enter/Enter (2,2); Enter/StayOut (8,−1); StayOut/Enter (−1,8); StayOut/StayOut (3,3). For SunCorp: if WindPower Enters, 2 > −1 → Enter; if WindPower Stays Out, 8 > 3 → Enter. Enter is dominant for SunCorp, and by symmetry for WindPower. The unique Nash is (Enter, Enter) = (2,2), even though (Stay Out, Stay Out) = (3,3) is Pareto-superior. This is a Prisoner's Dilemma: dominant strategies drive both to a jointly worse outcome.

Traps. Reading the matrix with the wrong player's payoff first. Calling a Pareto-superior cell "the Nash", the Nash is the mutual-best-response cell, which can be worse for both. Confusing "dominant" (best regardless of the rival) with "best response" (best given a specific rival action). Missing that some games (Iron-ore-wars-style) have a dominant-strategy PD while others (drone-market) have two asymmetric pure Nash and no dominant strategy.

Cost curves, shutdown & the fixed/sunk-cost fallacy 11 on past papers

When you see it: a variable-cost function plus a fixed cost; "AFC / AVC / AC", "economies vs diseconomies of scale", "should you shut down / keep producing", or a fixed cost suddenly rising and a question of how to react.
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. Write FC, AVC = VC/Q, AC = (FC + VC)/Q, MC = dC/dQ.
  2. Economies of scale where AC is falling (MC < AC); diseconomies where AC rising (MC > AC); the minimum of AC (MC = AC) is minimum efficient scale; a U-shaped AC has both.
  3. Short-run shutdown rule: produce only if P ≥ AVC; if P < AVC, shut down and lose only fixed costs.
  4. Long-run exit rule: exit if P < min AC (total). Find min AC by setting MC = AC.
  5. Fixed/sunk-cost fallacy: a change in fixed cost does NOT change the optimal price or quantity (it only affects the discrete enter/exit decision). Sunk costs are ignored entirely.
Worked model answer
Final 2026, Part II Q1 (Cost Curves and the Competitive Firm). 8 staff × €55,000 + €60,000 rent; V(Q) = 3Q + Q²; Q = 12,000; market price P = €10,000/hl. Find AFC; total cost, profit/loss and the short-run decision; economies of scale; the long-run exit price.

(a) Fixed costs = 8×55,000 + 60,000 = €500,000; AFC = 500,000/12,000 ≈ €41.67/hl. (b) TC = 500,000 + 3(12,000) + 12,000² = €144,536,000; revenue = 10,000×12,000 = €120,000,000 → loss of €24.5m. AVC = 3 + Q = 12,003 > P = 10,000, so P < AVC: shut down immediately and lose only the €500,000 fixed cost. (c) AC = 500,000/Q + 3 + Q is U-shaped, so the firm shows BOTH economies (low Q) and diseconomies (high Q) of scale. (d) Set MC = AC: 3 + 2Q = 500,000/Q + 3 + Q → Q² = 500,000 → Q ≈ 707; AC_min ≈ €1,417/hl, the long-run exit price.

Traps. Using AC instead of AVC for the short-run shutdown test (shutdown is P < AVC; exit is P < AC). Letting a higher fixed cost change your price or output, it must not (fixed-cost fallacy). Treating already-spent (sunk) R&D as relevant to pricing. Forgetting to find min AC by equating MC and AC.

Competitive markets: equilibrium, entry/exit & welfare 10 on past papers

When you see it: "perfectly competitive", "free entry", "entry price / exit price", "how many firms in long-run equilibrium"; a competitive fringe plus a "price leader" / dominant firm; a labour market with a minimum wage; "why does the supply curve slope up".
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. Firm supply: set P = MC and invert to get q(P); market supply = N × q(P).
  2. Entry price = min AC including the annualised (not-yet-sunk) capital cost; exit price = min AC ignoring sunk capital. Find each by MC = AC.
  3. Long-run price settles at the entry price (if demand supports entry) or the exit price (if demand has fallen and firms are leaving); number of firms = market Q ÷ firm q.
  4. Price leader: residual demand = market demand − fringe supply; the leader prices as a monopolist on residual demand (midpoint rule), then the fringe supplies the rest at that price.
  5. Welfare / intervention: a binding minimum wage above equilibrium cuts employment, opens an unemployment gap, and creates a deadweight-loss triangle; the bigger the demand elasticity, the bigger the job loss.
Worked model answer
Practice Final 1 (2010), Part II Q16 (Unobtanium). Q = 900 − P; competitive with free entry; AC = 5q + 320/q; annualised sunk capital cost €180/yr. Find the entry price, the exit price, the long-run price and number of firms, then the new long-run outcome after demand drops to Q = 240 − P.

Pre-entry AC = 5q + 320/q + 180/q = 5q + 500/q, so TC = 5q² + 500 and MC = 10q. Entry price: MC = AC → 10q = 5q + 500/q → q = 10, P = 100. Exit price: ignore sunk 180 → 10q = 5q + 320/q → q = 8, P = 80. Long run: P = entry price = 100, market Q = 800, each firm makes 10 → 80 firms. After demand falls to Q = 240 − P, firms exit until no one wants to leave, so price settles at the exit price = 80; market Q = 160; each surviving firm produces 8 → 20 firms remain.

Traps. Mixing up entry price (capital cost counted) and exit price (capital cost sunk, ignored). After a demand drop, pricing at the entry price rather than the exit price. For a price leader, forgetting to subtract fringe supply to get residual demand. Drawing the minimum-wage DWL on the wrong side, or forgetting it requires the wage to be ABOVE equilibrium to bind.

Demand & supply shifts + tax incidence 9 on past papers

When you see it: two events hitting a market at once ("titanium prices spike AND jet fuel falls", "mad cow ban AND bird flu"); a quota lifted; a per-unit tax; "what happens to P and Q"; complements/substitutes.
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. Classify each shock: does it move SUPPLY (cost/quantity-offered) or DEMAND (tastes, income, price of a related good)? Which direction?
  2. For complements, a rise in one good's price shifts the other's demand left; for substitutes, right.
  3. Trace P and Q. When both curves shift, one of {P, Q} is determined and the other is ambiguous (depends on relative shift sizes).
  4. Tax incidence: solve with a wedge (P_buyer = P_seller + tax). The side with the more inelastic curve bears more of the burden; compute the buyer's share = (rise in buyer price)/tax.
Worked model answer
Final 2026, Part I Q1. Commercial aircraft market: titanium prices spike (cost shock) while jet-fuel prices fall (aircraft are a complement to air travel, so demand rises). What happens to price and quantity?

Higher titanium cost shifts SUPPLY left (prices up). Cheaper flying raises demand for air travel and hence aircraft, shifting DEMAND right. Both forces push price up, so price unambiguously rises. Quantity has two opposing shifts (supply contraction vs demand expansion), so quantity is ambiguous. Answer (a).

Traps. Declaring both P and Q determinate when both curves shift, one is always ambiguous. Mislabelling a related-good price change as a movement along the curve rather than a shift. Getting complement/substitute direction backwards. In tax problems, assuming a 50/50 split regardless of elasticities.

Monopoly pricing, DWL & the midpoint rule 8 on past papers

When you see it: a single firm with a linear (inverse) demand curve and a marginal cost; "profit-maximising price/quantity", "deadweight loss", "consumer vs producer surplus", "should it raise or lower price"; a per-unit tax on a monopolist.
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. From inverse demand P = a − bQ, MR = a − 2bQ. Set MR = MC for Q*, read P* off demand. (Shortcut: P* = (choke + MC)/2.)
  2. Profit = (P* − AC)Q*. Be careful AC may include fixed cost.
  3. DWL = the triangle between demand and MC from the monopoly Q to the competitive Q (where P = MC): ½ × (P_mon − MC) × (Q_comp − Q_mon).
  4. To raise/lower: compare current price to golden-rule price P = [E/(E−1)]MC.
  5. A per-unit tax τ raises MC to MC + τ; re-optimise; for a target tax revenue, solve τ(Q(τ)) and pick the smaller root.
Worked model answer
Practice Review Problem Set 1, Q6. Demand 700 − Q = 3P (i.e. Q = 700 − 3P); monopolist's MC = Q. Find monopoly P and Q; the marginal-cost-pricing P and Q; compare consumer and producer surplus and the deadweight loss from monopoly.

Monopoly: set MR = MC → P = 400, Q = 100. Marginal-cost (competitive) pricing: P = MC → Q = 175, P = 175. Surplus: under monopoly CS = ½(700−400)(100) = 15,000 and PS = 100×400 − ½×100×100 = 35,000, total 50,000; under competition CS = ½(700−175)(175) = 45,937.50 and PS = ½(175)(175) = 15,312.50, total 61,250. Deadweight loss = 61,250 − 50,000 = €11,250 (equivalently ½(400−100)(175−100)).

Traps. Setting P = MC for a monopolist (that is the competitive benchmark, not the monopoly choice). Forgetting MR has twice the slope of inverse demand. Saying "monopoly means consumers get no benefit", consumer surplus under uniform monopoly pricing is still positive. Subtracting fixed costs when computing the optimal price (they do not affect it). Choosing the larger tax root when a smaller one yields the same revenue.

Cournot (quantity competition) 5 on past papers

When you see it: two firms choosing CAPACITY or QUANTITY of a homogeneous good; "best-response / reaction functions", "Cournot equilibrium", "collusive vs Cournot quantities", a merger to monopoly, Stackelberg first-mover.
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. Market price P = a − (q₁ + q₂). Firm 1's TR = (a − q₁ − q₂)q₁, so MR₁ = a − 2q₁ − q₂.
  2. Best response: set MR₁ = MC → q₁ = (a − MC − q₂)/2. By symmetry q₂ = (a − MC − q₁)/2.
  3. Solve simultaneously (set q₁ = q₂ if symmetric) for Cournot quantities, then price and each firm's profit.
  4. Collusion/merger: act as a single monopolist (midpoint rule), split output; each firm earns more than Cournot but has a unilateral incentive to over-produce, which is why Cournot, not collusion, is the stable prediction.
  5. Stackelberg leader commits to MORE than the Cournot quantity (reaction curves slope down → strategic substitutes), grabbing share.
Worked model answer
Practice Final 2 (2022), Part III Q3 (Iron ore wars II). Q = 200 − P; cost per unit of capacity = 50 for each of BHP and Rio Tinto (duopoly). Find the best-response functions, the Cournot equilibrium quantities, price and profits, and the collusive outcome.

MR₁ = 200 − 2q₁ − q₂ = MC = 50 → q₁ = (150 − q₂)/2; by symmetry q₂ = (150 − q₁)/2. Solving with q₁ = q₂: q₁ = q₂ = 50. Price P = 200 − 100 = 100; each firm's profit = (100 − 50)×50 = 2,500. Collusion: act as a monopolist with choke 200 and MC 50 → P = (200 + 50)/2 = 125, Q = 75, joint profit = (125 − 50)×75 = 5,625, split as 2,812.5 each (each produces 37.5). Collusion pays more per firm, but either firm can deviate to its best response and earn more, which unravels to the Cournot quantities, so Cournot is the good predictor.

Traps. Using MR with the wrong slope (in Cournot the rival's quantity enters linearly). Confusing Cournot (quantities, downward-sloping reaction curves) with Bertrand (prices, upward-sloping reaction curves). Forgetting that collusive quantities are LOWER than Cournot quantities. Splitting the merged monopolist's output incorrectly.

Bertrand (price competition) 5 on past papers

When you see it: two firms setting PRICES; demand for each as a function of both prices (e.g. Q₁ = a − bP₁ + cP₂); "reaction function", "Bertrand-Nash prices", a graph with P₁ and P₂ on the axes; or homogeneous goods where the low-cost firm undercuts.
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. Differentiated goods: from Q₁ = a − bP₁ + cP₂, the choke price is (a + cP₂)/b; apply the midpoint rule → best response P₁ = ½[(a + cP₂)/b + MC].
  2. By symmetry write firm 2's reaction function; solve simultaneously for the Bertrand-Nash prices (intersection of upward-sloping reaction curves).
  3. Comparative statics: a tax/MC rise shifts that firm's reaction curve out → both prices rise; a fixed-cost change shifts nothing; switching to revenue-maximisation acts as if MC = 0 → that firm's reaction curve shifts in (more aggressive).
  4. Homogeneous goods: undercutting drives price to MC; with asymmetric costs the low-cost firm prices just below the rival's MC and takes the whole market.
Worked model answer
Practice Final 3 (2020), Part III Q2 (Costa vs Starbucks). MC = 1 for both. Starbucks demand Q₁ = −10P₁ + 2P₂ + 10; Costa demand Q₂ = −10P₂ + P₁ + 20. Find the choke price, Starbucks' best response, and the Bertrand-Nash prices.

Substitutes (cross-price coefficient positive). Choke price for Starbucks: 0 = −10P₁ + 2P₂ + 10 → P₁ = 1 + 0.2P₂. Best response via midpoint rule: P₁(P₂) = (choke + MC)/2 = (1 + 0.2P₂ + 1)/2 = 1 + 0.1P₂, an upward-sloping line. Costa's is P₂(P₁) = 1.5 + 0.05P₁. Solving simultaneously: P₁ ≈ $1.16, P₂ ≈ $1.56. Iterating from any starting prices (e.g. both at $4) converges quickly to this intersection, which is why the Bertrand-Nash is a good predictor.

Traps. Treating a fixed-cost reduction as if it shifted the reaction function (it does not, only MC and the demand intercept matter). Forgetting that a revenue-maximising manager behaves "as if" MC = 0. Drawing Bertrand reaction curves downward-sloping (they slope UP for substitutes, prices are strategic complements). For homogeneous goods, landing on a split-the-market price instead of the undercut-to-MC logic.

Sequential games, commitment & strategic design 4 on past papers

When you see it: "first-mover", "game tree", "backward induction", "how much would you pay to move first", a bargaining/ultimatum split, or choosing a mechanism (which auction format) given bidders' attitudes.
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. Draw the tree with the first mover at the root and the follower's responses below each branch.
  2. Backward induction: solve the follower's best response at each node first, then the leader picks the branch with the highest leader payoff.
  3. The subgame-perfect outcome is that path; compare to the simultaneous game to value the first-mover advantage (= leader's sequential payoff − simultaneous payoff). If equal, there is NO first-mover advantage.
  4. Bargaining: roll back from the last round; the player who can make the final credible offer captures the surplus, leaving the other just indifferent.
  5. Mechanism design: pick the format that exploits the stated bidder psychology (e.g. risk-averse, desperate-to-win bidders shade less in a first-price sealed-bid auction, so it raises more revenue).
Worked model answer
Practice Review Problem Set 2, Q11 (Silvia & Xanthos). Split €10 in whole euros. Silvia proposes; if Xanthos rejects, the pie shrinks to €8 and Xanthos proposes; if Silvia then rejects, both get 0. Each accepts when indifferent. Find the backward-induction outcome.

Work backwards. Final round: if no deal, both get 0, so Silvia accepts any offer, even (0 for her, 8 for Xanthos). Therefore in round 2 Xanthos offers himself 8 and Silvia 0. Knowing he can secure 8 by rejecting, Xanthos in round 1 accepts only ≥ 8. So Silvia offers €2 for herself, €8 for Xanthos, and Xanthos accepts. The shrinking pie hands the bargaining power to the second mover here.

Traps. Solving the tree forwards instead of by backward induction. Claiming a first-mover advantage without checking it against the simultaneous game (Prisoner's-Dilemma-type games have none). Forgetting that only credible (subgame-perfect) threats count. In auction design, defaulting to "revenue equivalence" when the bidders' risk attitude breaks the assumptions.

Collusion sustainability (repeated games) 3 on past papers

When you see it: "infinitely repeated", "grim trigger", "tit-for-tat", "critical discount rate", "can implicit collusion be sustained"; or a conceptual question on what makes collusion easier/harder.
Ideal-answer skeleton
  1. Pin down three per-period payoffs: collusive (π_C), one-shot deviation (π_D, the best you can grab while the rival still cooperates), and punishment (π_N, the static Nash forever after).
  2. Collusion is sustained under grim trigger if PV(cooperate) ≥ PV(deviate): π_C + π_C/r ≥ π_D + π_N/r.
  3. Solve for the critical discount rate r (or discount factor δ): firms must be patient enough.
  4. Conceptual drivers: collusion is EASIER with a higher discount factor, a growing market, fewer firms and high transparency; HARDER with more competitors, lumpy infrequent interaction, and big one-shot deviation gains.
  5. Note tit-for-tat's flaw: a single mistake can trigger an endless punishment cycle.
Worked model answer
Final 2026, Part II Q4(d). Grim-trigger collusion where both Stay Out for 3 each; static Nash gives 2 each; a one-shot deviation when the rival stays out earns 8, then 2 forever. Find the critical discount rate.

π_C = 3, π_D = 8, π_N = 2. PV(cooperate) = 3 + 3/r; PV(defect) = 8 + 2/r. Sustain collusion when 3 + 3/r ≥ 8 + 2/r → 1/r ≥ 5 → r ≤ 0.20. Collusion holds if the discount rate is at most 20% (firms sufficiently patient).

★ In the analogous Practice Final 2 (2022) Part II Q4(b) Bertrand grim-trigger question (π_C = 8, π_D ≈ 16, π_N = 0), the correct condition is 8 + 8/r ≥ 16 → r ≤ 1. The official solution PDF prints "8 + 8/r > 16 or r < 100", which looks like a typo for r < 1. Use the r ≤ 1 derivation and verify against Glover's class wording.
Traps. Using the wrong deviation payoff (it is the best single-period grab while the rival still cooperates, e.g. just undercutting the collusive price to take the whole market). Forgetting the punishment continuation is the static Nash, not zero (unless Bertrand homogeneous, where Nash profit IS zero). Saying more firms make collusion easier (it makes it harder). Treating tit-for-tat as flawless.
