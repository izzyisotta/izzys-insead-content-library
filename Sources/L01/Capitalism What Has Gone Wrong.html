L01 · the framing essay on market failure

Capitalism: What Has Gone Wrong, and How It Can Be Fixed

This introductory essay to a special issue frames the question the lecture is built around: why have market economies, so productive in aggregate, generated such disaffection. Collier, Coyle, Mayer and Wolf trace it to a post-1970s ideology resting on three propositions they judge mistaken: that handing companies and policy to the ‘smartest’ agents maximises commercial value; that commercial value is a good proxy for social value without government intervention; and that any resulting losers can simply be compensated by transfers.

Each, they argue, failed. Maximising for the talented neglected trust, commitment and shared purpose; some of the largest profit opportunities turned out to be predatory wealth extraction rather than wealth creation; and political systems rarely made the compensating transfers, partly because winners bought disproportionate influence over policy. The remedy they sketch is to keep the gains of globalisation, technology and markets while rebuilding around devolution to local decision-making, cooperation in communities, and a recognition of radical uncertainty. For the course it supplies the vocabulary of market failure and the case for an active public-policy role.

Key takeaways

  • The post-1970s model rested on three propositions the authors argue all proved false
  • Commercial value is not a reliable proxy for social value; some profit is extractive, not creative
  • Compensating transfers mostly did not happen; winners captured policy influence
  • Proposed fixes: devolution, community cooperation, and taking radical uncertainty seriously
  • Supplies the course's framing of why markets fail and why public policy must respond
This is a synthesis written for revision, not the original text.
Source: P. Collier, D. Coyle, C. Mayer & M. Wolf, ‘Capitalism: what has gone wrong, what needs to change, and how it can be fixed’, Oxford Review of Economic Policy 37(4), 2021, pp. 637–649.
