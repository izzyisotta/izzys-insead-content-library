FPM Ch.2 · Supply & Demand

The chapter builds the supply-and-demand engine that underpins the whole competitive half of the course. The demand curve slopes down (it traces buyers' marginal willingness to pay) and the supply curve slopes up (it traces sellers' marginal cost). The market clears where they cross, at the price and quantity at which the quantity demanded equals the quantity supplied; away from it, shortages or surpluses push price back toward equilibrium.

It then separates shifts of a curve (driven by income, the price of substitutes and complements, input costs or expectations) from movements along a curve (driven only by the good's own price). Welfare is measured by consumer surplus (value minus price) plus producer surplus (price minus cost); their sum is maximised exactly at the competitive equilibrium.

That maximisation is the efficiency benchmark for the rest of the course: every later distortion (market power, taxes, externalities) is scored against the surplus the competitive market would have produced. The 'invisible hand' is the claim that decentralised, self-interested decisions coordinated only by price reach this efficient outcome.

Key takeaways

  • Equilibrium = the single price that clears the market; off-equilibrium prices create shortages or surpluses
  • Distinguish a shift of the whole curve from a movement along it: only the own price moves you along
  • Total surplus (consumer + producer) is maximised at the competitive equilibrium — the efficiency benchmark
  • Demand = marginal willingness to pay; supply = marginal cost
  • The invisible hand: price coordinates decentralised choices to an efficient allocation
