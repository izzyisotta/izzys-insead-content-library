L01 · the inequality data behind the course

Inequality in the Long Run

Piketty and Saez compress a generation of historical-tax-record research into the long-run story that opens the course. Their central finding is a reversal of fortunes: a century ago income and wealth were far more concentrated in Europe than in the United States, but the two have swapped places. On the eve of WWI the top decile took roughly 45 to 50 percent of income in the UK, France, Germany and Sweden; today Europe sits near 35 percent while the US has climbed to almost 50 percent, its highest ever.

The mid-century European collapse in inequality was not a spontaneous market correction. It was forced by capital destruction in two wars and the Depression, then locked in by progressive taxation and post-war institutions. Their interpretive engine is r > g: when the return on capital exceeds the growth rate, inherited wealth compounds faster than earned income and concentration rises mechanically. The lesson the lecture draws is that distributional outcomes are shaped by institutions and policy, not handed down by nature.

Key takeaways

  • The great reversal: pre-WWI Europe was more unequal than the US; today the US is far more unequal than Europe
  • Top-decile income share: ~45 to 50 percent in pre-war Europe, ~50 percent in the US now
  • Europe's mid-century equalisation came from war, depression and progressive tax, not market forces
  • r > g: when capital returns outpace growth, wealth concentrates across generations
  • Inequality is institutionally determined, which is precisely why public policy can move it
This is a synthesis written for revision, not the original text.
Source: T. Piketty & E. Saez, ‘Inequality in the Long Run’, Science 344(6186), 23 May 2014, pp. 838–843.
