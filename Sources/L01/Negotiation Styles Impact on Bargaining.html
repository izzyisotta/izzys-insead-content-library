Negotiation Styles: The Impact on Bargaining Transactions

Craver's article maps the personal styles negotiators bring to the table and how those styles interact, providing context for the course's 'battle versus puzzle' mindset contrast. The central distinction is between competitive/adversarial styles, which treat the encounter as a contest to be won, and cooperative/problem-solving styles, which treat it as a joint task.

His key empirical point is that effectiveness is not the same as style: skilled cooperative negotiators tend to do as well as or better than competitive ones, because they preserve information flow and relationships, while aggressive negotiators often trigger defensiveness and impasse. Style is a behavioural choice, not a fixed personality trait.

It also warns about style mismatches — a cooperative negotiator facing a hard competitor must adapt or risk exploitation — which connects directly to the course's argument that you should consciously choose and flex your approach rather than default to one mode.

Key takeaways

  • Two broad styles: competitive/adversarial vs cooperative/problem-solving
  • Skilled cooperative negotiators often match or beat competitive ones
  • Aggression tends to cut off information and provoke impasse
  • Style is a behavioural choice, not a fixed trait
  • Watch for style mismatches and adapt, or risk exploitation
This is a synthesis written for revision, not the original text.
Source: C. B. Craver, ‘Negotiation Styles’, Dispute Resolution Journal, 2003
