Slower Productivity and Higher Inequality: Are They Related?

Furman and Orszag ask whether the two great post-1973 economic disappointments, slowing productivity and rising inequality, share a common cause. Real median family income grew 3 percent a year before 1973 and only 0.4 percent since; productivity growth halved over the same span. They argue the standard story, skill-biased technological change met by a slowing supply of educated workers, cannot explain the pattern, since it would predict less inequality when technology slows, not more.

Their alternative is a decline in competition and business dynamism. Returns have become increasingly skewed toward a small set of top firms earning durable economic rents, and the widening gap is largely between firms rather than within them. Rising concentration and falling firm entry are consistent with both weaker productivity growth and a larger slice of income captured as rent. For the course this is the bridge from the inequality data to its policy hook: if market power is part of the cause, then competition policy and regulation become tools for addressing inequality, not just efficiency.

Key takeaways

  • Median family income growth collapsed from 3 percent to 0.4 percent a year after 1973
  • Skill-biased technical change alone cannot explain simultaneous slow productivity and rising inequality
  • Falling competition and dynamism can drive both at once
  • Top firms earn skewed, persistent rents; inequality is increasingly a between-firm phenomenon
  • Implication: competition policy is also distributional policy
This is a synthesis written for revision, not the original text.
Source: J. Furman & P. Orszag, ‘Slower Productivity and Higher Inequality: Are They Related?’, Peterson Institute for International Economics Working Paper 18-4, June 2018.
