Stop Labelling Negotiations as Win-Win or Win-Lose

This is the framing piece for Soyer's whole course. It argues that win-win and win-lose are not types of negotiation that you diagnose from the outside; they are strategic choices a negotiator makes, each carrying its own risks and rewards. Labelling a situation in advance is a category error that closes off options.

A purely competitive (win-lose) stance can claim value when the relationship and future are unimportant, but it risks impasse, damaged trust and leaving joint gains on the table. A cooperative (win-win) stance can expand the pie, but it risks being exploited by a hard bargainer. Neither is universally 'right'.

The practical conclusion is to stop diagnosing and start deciding: choose your approach deliberately based on the stakes, the relationship and the other side's likely behaviour, and stay able to switch. This sets up the course's recurring 'battle versus puzzle' contrast and the strategic, choice-centred view of negotiation.

Key takeaways

  • Win-win and win-lose are strategic choices, not fixed types of negotiation
  • Each approach has both risks and rewards; neither is universally correct
  • Competitive stance: can claim value but risks impasse and broken trust
  • Cooperative stance: can expand the pie but risks exploitation
  • Decide your approach deliberately from stakes, relationship and counterpart
