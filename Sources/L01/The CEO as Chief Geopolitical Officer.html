The CEO as Chief Geopolitical Officer

KPMG and Eurasia Group argue that geopolitics has moved from a peripheral risk to a boardroom issue, and that the CEO must own it personally as the firm's de facto Chief Geopolitical Officer. The old model, a public-affairs team that reacted to events and bought insurance against expropriation or war, no longer fits a world where politics moves at the speed of social media, where a single label can reshape a sector overnight, and where 69 of the 100 wealthiest economic entities are companies rather than countries. Firms have become assumed political actors, and silence ("no comment", "we don't take positions") now reads as aloof or inauthentic.

The report prescribes three responses: appoint a CGO at CEO level with single-point accountability, run a geopolitical stress test on the three-year plan (modelling shocks such as a doubling of cross-border costs, loss of a key market, or a week of zero cross-border travel), and invest in dedicated forecasting rather than relying on the news cycle. For BSPE this is the framing reading: it establishes why non-market strategy and political analysis belong at the centre of strategy, not as an afterthought.

Key takeaways

  • Geopolitics is now a core strategic variable, not an insurable side-risk; speed and interconnectedness leave little time to react.
  • The CEO must personally be the Chief Geopolitical Officer, giving the public-affairs team "cover and connections".
  • A geopolitical stress test pressure-checks the business plan against concrete shocks (border costs, market loss, transport and travel disruption).
  • Boards no longer accept "external conditions" as an excuse; geopolitics belongs on the risk register with mature mitigation plans.
