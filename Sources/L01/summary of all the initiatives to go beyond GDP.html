Beyond GDP: Rethinking How We Measure Progress

Coyle, Allin and Jackson argue that GDP, despite being designed in the 1930s-40s to gauge wartime productive capacity, has become an inappropriate arbiter of social progress. It counts "bads" (pollution, congestion, disaster reconstruction) as gains while omitting unpaid care work, the depletion of natural capital, the value of free digital services, and the distribution of income. Its persistence rests on technocratic elegance (one figure that is simultaneously output, income and expenditure) and political path-dependence rather than on fitness for purpose.

The authors survey reform efforts: subjective wellbeing measures, dashboards (New Zealand's Living Standards Framework, the UK's Measuring National Wellbeing), inclusive-wealth accounting (Dasgupta), and single composites like the Genuine Progress Indicator. The recurring tension is technical robustness versus social resonance: a single number communicates but oversimplifies; a dashboard is meaningful but hard to act on. They back the ongoing UN System of National Accounts revision, especially natural-capital accounting.

For MGE, this frames the opening question of what growth is for, sharpening critiques of GDP as a welfare proxy and connecting to inequality, sustainability and the limits of conventional macro aggregates.

Key takeaways

  • GDP measures market activity, not welfare: it includes 'bads' and omits unpaid work, nature depletion, inequality and free digital goods
  • Its dominance is path-dependence (single figure = output, income, expenditure) not analytical superiority as a progress measure
  • Core trade-off in alternatives: a single index communicates but oversimplifies; a dashboard is richer but harder to use
  • Leading alternatives: subjective wellbeing, inclusive/comprehensive wealth (Dasgupta), and adjusted composites like the Genuine Progress Indicator
  • GDP remains valid for macro analysis but should not be the sole gauge of social progress; reform centres on the UN SNA revision
