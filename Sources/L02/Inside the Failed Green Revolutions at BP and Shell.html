L02 · the climate-externality case

Inside the Failed Green Revolutions at BP and Shell

This FT Big Read anchors the lecture on climate as a market failure that firms alone cannot fix. It tells how BP under Bernard Looney (from 2020) and Shell under Ben van Beurden bet heavily on the energy transition, hiring thousands and pledging deep emissions cuts, only to reverse course and write off billions. Looney's ‘big bang’ pledge tied BP's net-zero target even to the emissions from oil and gas its customers burned; a rival boss privately judged that the only way there was zero production.

The article's argument is that the strategies failed less on conviction than on economics and capital-market discipline. Both firms tried to leap from oil and gas into low-margin electricity; both traded at a discount to US peers who stayed focused on hydrocarbons; investors who once cheered the pivot punished it. The relevance to the course is the diagnosis: with the social cost of carbon unpriced and shareholders demanding returns, even willing incumbents cannot voluntarily internalise the externality, which is exactly why the climate problem is a case for public policy, carbon pricing and regulation rather than corporate goodwill.

Key takeaways

  • BP and Shell launched ambitious transition strategies (2014 to 2020), then scaled them back and wrote off billions
  • Both tried to jump from high-margin oil and gas into low-margin electricity
  • Capital markets punished the pivot: the UK majors traded at a discount to US oil-focused rivals
  • Neither strategy survived the CEO who launched it
  • Lesson: unpriced carbon plus shareholder pressure means firms cannot self-correct; the externality needs policy
