Saving Glut or Investment Dearth?

Fatás interrogates the Bernanke (2005) global saving glut hypothesis, which attributed the post-1990s fall in world real interest rates to a rightward shift of the saving (supply) curve in the global market for funds. In a two-bloc diagram, surplus countries (China, Germany, Japan, oil exporters) raise saving and run current-account surpluses, while the US, UK and southern Europe run matching deficits, a story that fits the data on global imbalances well.

But the saving-glut story makes a prediction that failed: with lower interest rates, investment should have risen everywhere. Instead, aggregate investment rates in advanced economies trended down through the 2000s, even before 2008. Fatás argues the only way to reconcile this is that an inward shift of the investment (demand) curve in some economies (an "investment dearth") accompanied the saving shift. This dual movement also depresses interest rates but additionally explains weak growth and labour-market performance during the 2000s expansion, a source of pessimism about secular stagnation.

For MGE this is the core loanable-funds framework: real rates are set by global S and I, and identifying which curve moved matters for diagnosing low rates and weak growth.

Key takeaways

  • Global real interest rates are set by the world supply (saving) and demand (investment) for funds
  • Saving glut = rightward shift of the saving curve; explains both lower rates AND the pattern of global current-account imbalances
  • Key falsified prediction: lower rates should have raised investment everywhere, but advanced-economy investment rates fell
  • Reconciliation: an 'investment dearth' (inward shift of the investment curve) also lowers rates and additionally explains weak 2000s growth
  • Distinguishing saving glut from investment dearth matters: same low rates, different growth implications (the secular stagnation debate)
