L2 · Saving, investment & the current account

Are Trade Deficits Bad, and Can Tariffs Fix Them?

Martínez García and Yi reframe trade deficits through the national-accounting identity S − I = CA (saving minus investment equals the current account). A trade deficit simply means investment exceeds national saving, with the gap financed by foreign capital inflows at the world real interest rate, which is typically below the autarky rate. So a deficit can reflect strength, an investment boom (e.g. the 2017 corporate tax cut) or fiscal expansion (CARES Act), funded cheaply abroad rather than crowding out domestic borrowers.

On tariffs, theory gives an ambiguous verdict: input-cost increases hurt export competitiveness, final-goods tariffs cut imports, and currency appreciation offsets both, so the channels largely cancel. A 189-country study (1988-2022) finds no significant effect of moderate tariffs on trade balances. Persistent US deficits since the 1970s stem from structural forces, superior US productivity/growth, the global savings glut and demographics, and the dollar's reserve-currency status (the Triffin dilemma).

For MGE this is the central twin-deficits and saving-investment lens: deficits are a capital-account mirror, not an automatic weakness, and trade policy is the wrong tool to address them.

Key takeaways

  • Accounting identity S − I = CA: a trade deficit means investment exceeds saving, financed by foreign capital inflows
  • Deficits can signal dynamism (investment booms, fiscal expansion) financed at the lower world real interest rate, avoiding crowding out
  • Twin-deficits link: fiscal deficits lower public saving and can widen the trade deficit
  • Tariffs have theoretically ambiguous and empirically negligible effects on trade balances at moderate levels (offsetting input-cost, import and exchange-rate channels)
  • US deficits are structural: superior productivity/growth, the global savings glut, demographics, and dollar reserve status (Triffin dilemma)
  • Caveat: persistent deficits build external liabilities (IOUs) that must eventually be repaid; harmful only if driven by poor policy
