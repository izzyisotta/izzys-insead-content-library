Apple Must Pay €13 Billion to Ireland: Vestager Vindicated

This INSEAD case (co-authored by Roulet) anchors the lecture on taxation and profit-shifting. It traces the decade-long dispute that ended in September 2024, when the EU's highest court, the CJEU, ordered Apple to pay Ireland €13 billion in back taxes, overturning a 2020 General Court ruling that had sided with Apple. The Commission, led by competition commissioner Margrethe Vestager, had argued in 2016 that Ireland granted Apple illegal state aid through tax rulings.

The economic substance: since the early 1990s, Apple booked profits from European sales through Irish subsidiaries (Apple Sales International and Apple Operations Europe), so that profit earned across the continent was recorded in Ireland rather than where customers actually bought. The case lets the class work through how multinationals shift profit to low-tax jurisdictions, why a small country competes by offering favourable treatment, and the limits of using state-aid law to police tax. A pointed coda notes Vestager left office days after her win, replaced under a mandate friendlier to corporate scaling, signalling a possible shift in EU competition priorities.

Key takeaways

  • CJEU (Sept 2024) ordered Apple to pay Ireland €13bn, reversing the 2020 General Court ruling
  • The legal hook was illegal state aid, not tax law directly
  • Apple booked Europe-wide sales profit through Irish subsidiaries, far from where sales occurred
  • Illustrates profit-shifting and tax competition between jurisdictions
  • Vestager's exit under a pro-scaling successor hints at shifting EU competition priorities
