L3 · elasticity

FPM Ch.3 · Consumer Choice & Demand

This chapter is the elasticity toolkit behind Part I. Own-price elasticity is the percentage change in quantity demanded per one-percent change in price; demand is elastic when its magnitude exceeds one and inelastic when below one. The practical pay-off is the revenue test: cutting price raises total revenue only when demand is elastic, and raises it when demand is inelastic.

It adds the other elasticities: income elasticity (positive for normal goods, above one for luxuries, negative for inferior goods) and cross-price elasticity (positive for substitutes, negative for complements). Determinants of how elastic demand is include the availability of substitutes, the share of the budget the good takes, whether it is a necessity, and the time horizon (demand is more elastic in the long run).

Finally it contrasts linear demand (whose elasticity changes along the curve) with the constant-elasticity (log-log) form that the gasoline reading exploits, so that elasticity can be estimated directly as a regression coefficient.

Key takeaways

  • Own-price elasticity = %ΔQ / %ΔP; |ε|>1 elastic, |ε|<1 inelastic
  • Revenue test: a price cut raises revenue only if demand is elastic
  • Income elasticity flags normal/inferior/luxury goods; cross-price elasticity flags substitutes vs complements
  • Elasticity rises with more substitutes, a bigger budget share, and a longer time horizon
  • Linear demand has changing elasticity; the log-log form has constant elasticity
