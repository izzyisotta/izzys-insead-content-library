L03 · the evidence base on tax avoidance

Global Tax Evasion Report 2024

Produced by the EU Tax Observatory and coordinated by Gabriel Zucman, this report supplies the lecture's hard numbers on how the rich and multinationals lower their tax. Drawing on new data unlocked by the last decade's reforms (automatic exchange of bank information, country-by-country reporting), it reaches a mixed verdict. The good news: offshore tax evasion by wealthy individuals has fallen roughly threefold since automatic information exchange began, proof that political will can move the needle.

The bad news dominates. The 2021 global minimum tax of 15 percent on multinationals has been gutted by loopholes, cutting its expected revenue by about half. And avoidance is moving onshore: global billionaires now face effective tax rates equivalent to just 0 to 0.5 percent of their wealth, largely via shell companies. The report's headline proposal, a 2 percent global minimum tax on billionaires, would raise roughly $250 billion a year from fewer than 3,000 people. Its framing line, that tax evasion is a policy choice rather than a law of nature, is the spine of the lecture's argument that coordinated policy can reclaim lost revenue.

Key takeaways

  • Offshore evasion by individuals has fallen ~3x since automatic bank-information exchange began
  • The 15 percent global minimum tax on multinationals has been halved in effect by loopholes
  • Billionaires pay effective rates of just 0 to 0.5 percent of wealth, mainly via shell companies
  • Proposed fix: a 2 percent global minimum tax on billionaires, raising ~$250bn a year
  • Core message: tax evasion is a policy choice, addressable by coordination or even unilateral action
This is a synthesis written for revision, not the original text.
Source: A. Alstadsæter, S. Godar, P. Nicolaides & G. Zucman, Global Tax Evasion Report 2024, EU Tax Observatory (foreword by J. Stiglitz).
