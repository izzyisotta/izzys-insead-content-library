L3 · elasticity in practice

“The Elasticity of Demand for Gasoline”

This case is the empirical anchor for the elasticity material. It estimates the demand for gasoline using a constant-elasticity (log-log) specification: the log of quantity is regressed on the log of the petrol price, income, the prices of new and used cars, and a time trend. Because the model is linear in logs, ordinary regression recovers each elasticity directly as a coefficient.

The headline finding is that gasoline demand is highly price-inelastic in the short run — drivers cannot quickly change cars or commutes — and noticeably more elastic in the long run, as the vehicle fleet and habits adjust. Income is a strong positive driver of demand.

Methodologically it teaches the difference between a movement along the demand curve (a price change) and a shift of it (income, demographics, fuel-efficiency), and why mixing the two corrupts an estimate. It is the worked bridge from the elasticity formulas to real data.

Key takeaways

  • Log-log model makes each elasticity a directly estimated regression coefficient
  • Gasoline demand is price-inelastic short run, more elastic long run
  • Income is a strong positive determinant of demand
  • Separates movement along the curve (price) from shifts (income, demographics)
  • Connects the elasticity formulas to actual estimation
