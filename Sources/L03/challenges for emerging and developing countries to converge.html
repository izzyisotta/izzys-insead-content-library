The End of an Era of Global Income Convergence

Yonzan, Mahler and Lakner document that the three-decade era in which poorer economies grew faster than rich ones, narrowing the global income gap, stalled with COVID-19. Using World Bank Poverty and Inequality Platform data, they show 2020 delivered the single largest annual rise in global inequality in three decades, larger than the Asian financial crisis: the global Gini rose about 1% (roughly 0.7 points net of the expected pre-pandemic decline).

Their decomposition is the key analytical point. The shock was global (all income percentiles lost), but the richest fared best. Crucially, within-country inequality changed little (transfers cushioned the poor in many places); the rise in global inequality was driven almost entirely by between-country divergence, because developing economies were hit harder and recovered more slowly. At the pre-pandemic pace of reduction it would take over four years just to undo the 2020 increase.

For MGE this complicates the convergence hypothesis: with weakened globalisation, war, inflation and (potentially) AI eroding the low-wage advantage, the default for many developing countries is now stalled or reversed convergence, the backdrop for growth-scenario building.

Key takeaways

  • Three decades of global income convergence (poorer countries growing faster) stalled in 2020 with COVID-19
  • 2020 was the largest single-year rise in global inequality in three decades, larger than the Asian financial crisis
  • Decomposition: the increase came from BETWEEN-country divergence, not within-country inequality (transfers cushioned the poor)
  • Developing countries were hit harder and recovered more slowly than high-income economies
  • Weakened globalisation tailwinds mean the default scenario for many EMDEs is now stalled or reversed convergence, not automatic catch-up
