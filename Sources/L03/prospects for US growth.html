Is Slow Still the New Normal for US GDP Growth?

Fernald and Li decompose sustainable US trend growth into its two ingredients, growth in hours worked and growth in labour productivity (output per hour), and conclude the new-normal pace is just 1.5-1.75%, well below the ~3% of 1987-2007. The exercise separates trend from cycle: post-2009 growth above trend simply reflected unemployment falling from ~10% back toward its sustainable level.

The first driver is demographic and near-certain: labour-force growth has slowed sharply as baby-boomers retire and female participation plateaus, with the CBO projecting hours growth of only ~0.5% a year. The second, productivity, is the larger uncertainty. Treating productivity as moving in persistent regimes, the authors note we have been in a slow regime since 2004 (~1-1.25% per hour, versus 2.5% in 1995-2004) and assume it continues. Combining 0.5% hours with 1.1% productivity yields ~1.6% trend growth.

For MGE this is the canonical growth-accounting application: returning to 3% would require a productivity revival the US has not seen since the IT boom, and what is historically unprecedented is slow productivity combined with weak hours growth (note: Fernald is an INSEAD professor).

Key takeaways

  • Trend growth = growth in hours worked + growth in labour productivity (growth accounting)
  • US new-normal trend is ~1.5-1.75%, far below the ~3% of 1987-2007
  • Slower hours growth is demographically near-certain (ageing, plateauing female participation); CBO projects ~0.5%/yr
  • Productivity is the big uncertainty; it moves in persistent regimes and has been slow (~1-1.25%) since 2004
  • Returning to 3% needs a productivity revival like 1995-2004; the novel risk is slow productivity AND weak hours together
