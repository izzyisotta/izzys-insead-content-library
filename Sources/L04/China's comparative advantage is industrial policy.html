L04

China's comparative advantage is industrial policy

Tej Parikh's argument is a warning against imitation. As China's share of global manufacturing has grown, the West has tried to copy Beijing's playbook with subsidies and protectionism. Parikh's inconvenient truth: China's real comparative advantage is industrial policy itself, not just cheap production, so playing China at its own game is a losing strategy. Its edge comes from governance. Centralised control lets Beijing mobilise resources and co-ordinate across provincial governments, state banks and regulators, while limited democratic accountability lets it pursue long-horizon plans and sidestep opposition. That central power is paired with intense decentralised competition: regional officials and firms compete for state backing that is conditional on performance, so output and innovation are prioritised over profits. The model has been refined over decades into dense, vertically integrated supply chains (with a lot of waste alongside).

Liberal market democracies cannot easily replicate this: they answer to voters and shareholders, decide in a more decentralised way, and would need a costly, multi-decade effort to match China's heft. The telling figure: less than 15 per cent of BYD's per-vehicle cost advantage over Tesla comes from subsidies (Rhodium Group); the rest is supply-chain control, clustering and scale. Parikh's prescription is to lean into the West's own institutional comparative advantage. Citing Carl Benedikt Frey, centralised bureaucracies are best at catch-up, but decentralised systems are better at exploring new technological trajectories, so the West should prioritise frontier innovation (AI, biotech, advanced computing) that thrives in open research and venture capital, target the few distortive measures narrowly at national-security-critical supply chains, treat Chinese manufacturing as a source of low-cost inputs, and spend on upskilling displaced workers and pro-investment reforms. The risk, Frey warns, is "fighting the last war."

Key takeaways

  • Thesis: China's comparative advantage is industrial policy; copying it with broad subsidies and tariffs is the wrong fight.
  • The source of the advantage is governance: centralised co-ordination plus performance-conditional decentralised competition, refined over decades.
  • Evidence against the "it's all subsidies" view: under 15% of BYD's cost edge over Tesla is subsidy-driven; the rest is supply chains, clustering and scale.
  • Prescription: play to the West's strength in frontier innovation; narrow industrial policy to security-critical supply chains; use cheap Chinese inputs; upskill and reform rather than recreate China's manufacturing dominance.
  • Exam use (Session 4): the cleanest articulation of the political vs market-failure and static vs dynamic comparative advantage debate, and a ready counter to "the West should just out-subsidise China."
