FPM Ch.4 · Production & Costs

The cost chapter introduces the cost-curve family used throughout Parts II and III. Total cost splits into fixed and variable components; dividing by output gives average total, average variable and average fixed cost, while marginal cost is the cost of one more unit. The defining geometric fact is that marginal cost cuts both average-cost curves at their minimum points.

It draws the crucial decision-relevance distinction: sunk costs (already spent and unrecoverable) are irrelevant to any forward-looking choice, whereas opportunity cost (the value of the next best use) always is. Confusing the two is the source of many pricing fallacies later in the course.

It closes with returns to scale: economies of scale (falling average cost), diseconomies, and the minimum efficient scale, plus the short-run/long-run split — in the short run some inputs are fixed and diminishing marginal returns drive marginal cost upward, while in the long run every input can vary.

Key takeaways

  • Cost family: fixed vs variable; average total/variable/fixed; marginal cost cuts average cost at its minimum
  • Ignore sunk costs; always count opportunity cost
  • Economies of scale = falling average cost; minimum efficient scale sets the smallest competitive size
  • Short run: some inputs fixed, diminishing returns push MC up; long run: all inputs variable
  • These curves feed directly into the shutdown, entry and pricing rules
