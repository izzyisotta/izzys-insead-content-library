L04 · how to measure competition

How to Spot a Monopoly

This essay supplies the conceptual toolkit behind the antitrust lecture: how do you actually tell whether a market is competitive? It argues the usual proxies mislead. Concentration (how much share the biggest firms hold) can rise simply because more productive firms are winning customers, and national figures can hide intense local competition. Markups are no better: a high price-to-cost gap may just reward genuine innovation, as with a breakthrough drug.

The better question, the author argues, is whether a market rewards efficiency. The Olley-Pakes decomposition measures exactly this by checking whether more productive firms are gaining share. A positive covariance means customers are switching to better producers (healthy competition); a negative one means something is blocking good firms from growing. The essay illustrates with the post-1968 breakup of AT&T's phone monopoly, Colombia's 1990s trade liberalisation (tariffs on cars cut from ~200 to ~38 percent, models on offer leaping from 26 to 71), and Britain's post-2008 productivity puzzle. For the course it reframes monopoly diagnosis away from crude headcounts toward whether markets are dynamic.

Key takeaways

  • Concentration and markups are unreliable proxies: high values can signal healthy competition
  • Better test: is the market rewarding more productive firms with greater share?
  • The Olley-Pakes decomposition measures this; positive covariance = dynamism
  • Cases: AT&T breakup, Colombia's trade liberalisation, post-2008 UK productivity stagnation
  • Competition can strengthen even as concentration rises, if the best firms are doing the consolidating
