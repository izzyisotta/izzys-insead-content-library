Growth Scenarios: Innovation, Institutions and the Middle-Income Trap

This session synthesises three growth threads behind recent Nobel prizes. Aghion's Schumpeterian model (2025 Nobel) makes innovation endogenous: R&D, pursued for monopoly profit, drives "creative destruction" in which new technologies displace old. Unlike Solow's exogenous-technology story, growth becomes policy-responsive (competition has an inverted-U effect, IP and finance matter), with TFP, not just factor inputs, as the engine.

Acemoglu, Johnson and Robinson (2024 Nobel) argue institutions are the deep cause of prosperity. Their settler-mortality instrument shows colonisers built inclusive institutions where they could survive and extractive ones where they could not, and these persist; inclusive institutions (secure broad property rights, contestable politics) enable the creative destruction Aghion describes, while extractive ones block it to protect elite rents (China is the main anomaly).

The middle-income trap is the empirical signature: many countries escape poverty but stall around $10-20k, failing to converge. The World Bank's 3i framework (investment → infusion → innovation) shows the trap forms when countries cling to the strategy that previously worked. For MGE, every country scenario must specify its 3i stage, institutional trajectory, innovation capacity, demographics and exposure to AI/geopolitical shocks.

Key takeaways

  • Aghion (2025 Nobel): growth is endogenous innovation via creative destruction; R&D, competition (inverted-U), IP and finance are growth levers, not exogenous tech
  • AJR (2024 Nobel): inclusive vs extractive institutions are the deep cause of prosperity; settler-mortality IV shows colonial institutions persist
  • The two prizes are complementary: growth = innovation under inclusive institutions that allow incumbents to be displaced
  • Middle-income trap: countries stall at $10-20k by sticking with the strategy that got them there; World Bank 3i = investment → infusion → innovation
  • China is the key anomaly (closed political, increasingly inclusive economic institutions) and the central escape-or-stall question for the next 20 years
