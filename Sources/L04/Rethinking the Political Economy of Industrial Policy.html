Rethinking the Political Economy of Industrial Policy

In this article (the course professor's own work, with Sonia N. Aggarwal), the authors argue that the industrial-policy debate has moved from "whether" to "how", and that the binding constraint on success is political rather than technical. They first review the economic rationales for state intervention: market failures, knowledge spillovers, coordination problems and the entrepreneurial-state case revived by the rise of China, green-transition industrial efforts, and the reorganisation of global value chains. The Great Recession, COVID-19, the Ukraine energy shock and US-China rivalry have all pushed sectoral intervention back into the mainstream, even in Anglo-Saxon economies historically sceptical of governments "picking winners".

The heart of the paper is its political-economy framework. Whether either a conforming (Lin-style, comparative-advantage-following) or a defying (Chang-style, ladder-climbing) industrial policy succeeds depends on three variables: international drivers and constraints (security threats that discipline rent-seeking; WTO and regional rules), domestic state-society relations (state autonomy from capture, Evans's "embedded autonomy"), and elite ideological commitment to an activist state. For BSPE this is the analytic spine of the industrial-policy session, linking the economics to the politics that decide outcomes.

Key takeaways

  • The debate has shifted from whether to do industrial policy to how; political conditions, not economics, are the binding constraint.
  • Three rationales justify intervention: market failures, broader economic and political goals (security, distribution, green), and firm rent-seeking.
  • Success hinges on three political variables: international threats and rules, domestic state autonomy from capture, and elite ideological consensus.
  • International constraints (WTO, regional pacts) are real but only weakly binding; domestic political structure dominates.
