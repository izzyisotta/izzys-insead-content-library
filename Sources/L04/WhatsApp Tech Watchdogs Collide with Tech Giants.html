WhatsApp: Tech Watchdogs Collide with Tech Giants

This INSEAD case (supervised by Roulet) is the spine of the lecture on competition policy and Big Tech. It opens with Lina Khan's 2021 appointment to the FTC and her decision to revisit the antitrust complaint against Facebook, which singled out two of more than eighty acquisitions: Instagram (2012) and WhatsApp (2014). The framing question is whether Facebook bought WhatsApp, then the world's most popular messaging app, to neutralise a future competitor rather than to build on it.

The case gives students the raw material to argue both sides. The 2021 terms-of-service change, which prompted a user backlash and a surge of downloads for rivals Telegram and Signal, shows that switching costs are not absolute and that competitive constraint may still exist. Against that sit the network effects, data advantages and acquisition pattern that animate trustbusters like Khan and Tim Wu. It forces the core modern antitrust questions: how to define the market, whether to judge mergers on consumer prices or on innovation and potential competition, and whether ‘killer acquisitions’ should ever have been allowed.

Key takeaways

  • Central question: was Facebook's 2014 WhatsApp purchase a defensive ‘killer acquisition’?
  • Lina Khan and Tim Wu represent the assertive, structuralist turn in US antitrust
  • The 2021 ToS backlash drove users to Telegram and Signal, evidence of residual competition
  • Network effects and data scale cut the other way, entrenching incumbents
  • Surfaces the hard issues: market definition, the consumer-welfare standard versus innovation, merger review of nascent rivals
