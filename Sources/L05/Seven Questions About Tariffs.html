L05 · the trade-policy primer

Seven Questions About Tariffs Everyone Should Know

This CEPR chapter is the analytical backbone of the lecture on trade policy, written as the second Trump administration revived sweeping tariffs. Costinot and Rodriguez-Clare work through tariffs from first principles. A tariff is simply a tax that drives a wedge between what domestic buyers pay and what foreign suppliers receive, killing mutually beneficial transactions and distorting production toward higher-cost domestic makers. The aggregate welfare loss is the classic Harberger triangle.

Crucially they separate the bad rationale from the defensible ones. Using tariffs to shrink bilateral trade deficits is dismissed as ‘textbook policy lunacy’: exports are the price a country pays for imports, not a virtue in themselves. But tariffs can raise welfare where other distortions already exist, for instance to correct a genuine market failure in a strategic high-tech sector, exploit market power in trade through an optimal tariff, or as bargaining leverage, always with the caveat of retaliation. For the course it gives a disciplined framework for judging real tariff announcements: name the distortion the tariff is supposed to fix, and ask whether it is the best instrument for the job.

Key takeaways

  • A tariff is a tax: it creates a price wedge, foregoes beneficial trades, and distorts production (Harberger triangles)
  • Using tariffs to cut bilateral trade deficits is mercantilist nonsense; exports pay for imports
  • Tariffs can help only when correcting a pre-existing distortion (market failure, market power, strategic sectors)
  • Optimal-tariff and bargaining arguments exist but invite retaliation
  • Discipline: identify the specific distortion, then ask if a tariff is the right tool
This is a synthesis written for revision, not the original text.
Source: A. Costinot & A. Rodríguez-Clare, ‘Seven questions about tariffs that everyone should know the answers to’, Ch. 27 in The Economic Consequences of the Second Trump Administration, CEPR Rapid Response Economics 6, 2nd ed., 2025.
