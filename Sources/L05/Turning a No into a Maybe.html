Turning a “No” into a “Maybe”

This article is the engine behind the course's 'No' exercise. Its central reframe is to treat a refusal not as a wall but as information about the other side's constraints. A 'no' rarely means 'never'; it usually means 'not on these terms', 'not yet', or 'not given a pressure I can't reveal'.

The practical move is to get curious rather than defensive: ask what would have to change for the answer to become yes, probe for the constraint behind the refusal, and look for the issue you have not yet put on the table. Often the no is about budget timing, internal authority or risk, none of which a better price would fix.

By converting a flat no into a conditional 'maybe, if...', the negotiator keeps the conversation alive and surfaces the very interests that make a creative deal possible. It is the same investigative instinct the communication readings develop, applied at the moment of apparent deadlock.

Key takeaways

  • Treat 'no' as information about constraints, not a final wall
  • A no usually means 'not on these terms / not yet', not 'never'
  • Ask what would have to change for a yes; probe the underlying constraint
  • Many refusals are about timing, authority or risk — not price
  • Convert 'no' into 'maybe, if...' to keep the deal alive
