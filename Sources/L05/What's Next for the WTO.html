What's Next for the WTO

This Council on Foreign Relations backgrounder explains why the World Trade Organization, the principal forum for the rules of international trade, is under existential strain. The WTO has 164 members, succeeded GATT in 1995, and rests on openness, non-discrimination and a dispute-settlement system that handled over 500 disputes and helped avert tit-for-tat trade wars. The United States is the most active user, filing 124 complaints, many of them aimed at Chinese state subsidies and intellectual-property abuses.

Two failures define the crisis. The Doha Development Agenda, launched in 2001 around agricultural liberalisation for poorer countries, collapsed in 2008 over subsidies and safeguards, surviving only in fragments like the Trade Facilitation Agreement. More damaging, the Trump administration blocked appointments to the Appellate Body until it fell below quorum in December 2019, and the Biden administration maintained the block, so adverse rulings can be appealed "into the void". Members have drifted to bilateral, regional and plurilateral deals instead. For BSPE this is the spine reading on the erosion of multilateral trade governance and the rise of minilateral alternatives.

Key takeaways

  • The WTO governs trade via negotiation, monitoring and binding dispute settlement across 164 members; consensus decision-making makes reform slow.
  • The Doha Round collapsed in 2008 over agricultural subsidies; the multilateral negotiating function has largely stalled since.
  • US blocking of Appellate Body appointments since 2019 has paralysed enforcement, letting countries ignore rulings under indefinite appeal.
  • Stalemate has pushed members toward bilateral, regional and plurilateral deals; renewed US leadership is widely seen as necessary to revive the body.
