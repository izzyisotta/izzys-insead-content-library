L6 · third-degree price discrimination

FPM Ch.7 · Explicit Segmentation

Explicit (third-degree) price discrimination is the simplest way to extract more surplus than a single price allows. The firm sorts customers using an observable signal — age, student status, geography, time of purchase — and charges each segment its own price, applying the Lerner rule separately in each. The segment with more elastic demand gets the lower price.

Three conditions must hold: the firm needs market power, it must be able to identify and separate segments by an observable trait, and it must prevent arbitrage so the low-price segment cannot resell to the high-price one. Where these hold, discrimination raises profit and frequently raises total output too, since some low-value customers who would be priced out of a single-price market are now served.

This sets up the harder case in the next chapter, where the signal is not observable and customers must be made to sort themselves.

Key takeaways

  • Third-degree = segment on an observable trait, then price each segment separately
  • Charge the more elastic segment the lower price (Lerner per segment)
  • Needs market power, separable segments, and no resale/arbitrage between them
  • Usually raises both profit and total output versus a single price
  • Sets up second-degree screening, where the signal is unobservable
