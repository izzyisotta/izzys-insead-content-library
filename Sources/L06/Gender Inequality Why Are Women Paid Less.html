L06 · the gender-gap policy case

Gender Inequality: Why Are Women Paid Less than Men?

This INSEAD case (co-authored by Roulet) frames the lecture on gender and labour-market policy around two real disclosure regimes. The UK's 2017 rule under the Equality Act forced firms with 250-plus employees to publish their median hourly pay gap; across the ~10,500 reporting firms the median unadjusted gap was 9.9 percent, widest in construction and finance. Iceland went further, legislating mandatory equal-pay certification in 2018 after women symbolically walked off the job at 14:38 (the point in the day past which, given the gap, they were effectively working unpaid).

The case is careful about measurement: the unadjusted gap mixes genuine discrimination with differences in occupation, hours and experience, which is exactly the analytic distinction students must wrestle with. It also documents persistent segregation, the scarcity of women in senior roles, and how national approaches diverge. The policy question it sets up is whether transparency mandates and certification actually narrow the gap, or merely measure it, which positions the lecture's move into Goldin's deeper explanation of the gap's causes.

Key takeaways

  • UK 2017 disclosure (250+ employees): median unadjusted gap of 9.9 percent, widest in finance and construction
  • Iceland (2018) went further with mandatory equal-pay certification
  • Distinguish the unadjusted gap from the adjusted gap (controlling for occupation, hours, experience)
  • Drivers include occupational segregation and few women in senior roles
  • Policy question: do transparency and certification close the gap or only reveal it?
