L6 · personalised pricing

“How Online Shopping Makes Suckers of Us All”

Jerry Useem's article shows how online retail pushes price discrimination toward its theoretical limit. Where third-degree discrimination sorts customers into a few observable segments, e-commerce firms can exploit individual-level data — browsing history, device, location, time of day, past purchases — to set a different price for almost every shopper, approximating first-degree (perfect) discrimination.

It documents the mechanics: dynamic and personalised pricing, steering, and prices that change second to second, all opaque to the buyer. The economic upshot is that firms capture far more consumer surplus than a single posted price would allow, and the familiar idea of 'the price' of a good starts to dissolve.

The article also surfaces the fairness and trust backlash: customers react badly when they discover they paid more than someone else for the same item, which constrains how aggressively firms can personalise even when the data would let them. It is the real-world face of the price-discrimination chapters.

Key takeaways

  • Online data lets firms approach first-degree (perfect) price discrimination
  • Signals used: browsing, device, location, time, purchase history
  • Personalised/dynamic pricing captures far more consumer surplus than one posted price
  • Fairness backlash and loss of trust limit how far firms can push it
  • The notion of a single 'price' for a good erodes
