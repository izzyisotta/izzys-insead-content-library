Nesting and Minilateralism in the Asia-Pacific

Vinod Aggarwal's Global Asia essay (the professor's own paper) tracks the rise of "minilateralism", small groupings of countries cooperating on trade and security, across the Asia-Pacific. In trade these include RCEP (the ten ASEAN states plus China, Japan, South Korea, Australia and New Zealand), the CPTPP that survived America's 2017 exit from the TPP, and the US-led IPEF, which is notably not a market-opening deal. In security there is an "alphabet soup" of accords: the Quad, AUKUS, and assorted trilaterals including the revived China-Japan-South Korea dialogue that prompted the piece.

Aggarwal's analytical contribution is a typology that classifies arrangements along two axes, the number of participants (unilateral, bilateral, minilateral, multilateral) and geography (concentrated or dispersed), and then asks whether these smaller deals "nest" within broader institutions or undermine them. The context is a weakened multilateral order: with the WTO's dispute mechanism paralysed and the US retreating into unilateralism on trade, minilateral accords are filling the vacuum. For BSPE this reading anchors the Asia-Pacific session and supplies the institutional-design vocabulary for judging whether minilateralism complements or corrodes the multilateral system.

Key takeaways

  • Minilateralism (small trade and security groupings) has risen sharply in the Asia-Pacific as multilateral institutions weaken.
  • Trade examples: RCEP, CPTPP, IPEF; security examples: Quad, AUKUS, and various trilaterals.
  • Aggarwal classifies arrangements by number of participants and by geography (concentrated vs dispersed).
  • The central question is "nesting": whether these smaller accords fit within broader institutions or undermine them.
