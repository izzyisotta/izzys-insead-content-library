Why We Haven't Fixed Gender Inequality at Work

Roulet, the course's own professor, distils Claudia Goldin's Nobel-winning research into three explanations for why the gender wage gap persists. First, children, not hiring discrimination, drive most of the gap. In Goldin's study of MBA graduates, pay is near-equal for the first few years after business school, then diverges sharply about a decade out, almost always after a woman's first child, through interruptions and reduced hours. Closing the gap therefore starts with ‘couple equity’: a fairer division of domestic labour at home.

Second is ‘greedy work’: the highly paid jobs (in law, finance) that reward long, inflexible hours disproportionately. Because women still carry more domestic duties, they take such roles less often, and the pay premium attached to them widens the gap. Third, progress has not been linear but U-shaped over two centuries, shaped by industrialisation, the service economy and the contraceptive pill. For the lecture this is the analytic engine behind the gender case: the gap is less about overt discrimination than about how work and home are jointly structured, which reframes what policy should target.

Key takeaways

  • Most of the gap opens after the first child, via career interruptions and shorter hours
  • ‘Couple equity’ at home is a precondition for workplace equality
  • ‘Greedy work’ (long, inflexible, high-paying jobs) widens the gap because women take it less often
  • Female participation followed a U-shaped path over 200 years, not a steady rise
  • Reframes the gap as structural (work + home design) rather than simple hiring discrimination
This is a synthesis written for revision, not the original text.
Source: A. Roulet, ‘Why We Haven't Fixed Gender Inequality at Work’, INSEAD Knowledge, 2024 (on Claudia Goldin's research).
