Recession: When Bad Times Prevail

Claessens and Kose define a recession as a sustained, broad-based decline in economic activity with rising unemployment. The popular rule of thumb, two consecutive quarters of falling real GDP, is convenient but narrow; the US NBER instead dates recessions using a wider set of measures (output, employment, real income, sales, industrial production), a peak-to-trough judgement that can take a year to finalise.

Recessions have varied causes: input-price shocks (e.g. oil spikes that raise the price level and cut aggregate demand), overly contractionary monetary or fiscal policy, financial-market problems following credit booms and asset-price collapses (the costliest type, as in 2007), and falling external demand transmitted through trade. Because candidate predictors (credit, asset prices, unemployment, confidence) may be causes or effects (endogenous), recessions are very hard to forecast.

Empirically, in 21 advanced economies (1960-2007) there were 122 recessions, with economies in recession ~10% of the time. A typical recession lasts about a year and costs ~2% of GDP (severe ones ~5%); investment and industrial production fall more than consumption, trade and inflation ease, unemployment jumps. A depression is an extreme recession (GDP down >10%). For MGE this anchors business-cycle vocabulary and dating.

Key takeaways

  • No official definition; rule of thumb is two consecutive quarters of falling real GDP, but NBER uses a broader basket (output, employment, income, sales, production)
  • Causes: input-price/oil shocks, over-tight monetary/fiscal policy, credit-boom/financial busts (costliest), and collapsing external demand
  • Recessions are hard to predict because indicators can be endogenous (both cause and effect)
  • Stylised facts: ~1 year long, ~2% GDP cost (severe ~5%); investment and industrial production fall most, unemployment jumps, inflation eases
  • A depression is an extreme recession with GDP falling >10% (Great Depression ~30%); financially-driven recessions are deeper
