L7 · second-degree screening

FPM Ch.8/9 · Implicit Segmentation & Nonlinear Pricing

When the firm cannot observe who is price-sensitive, it designs a menu so customers sort themselves — second-degree discrimination, or screening. The classic devices: coupons (impose a small hassle cost that only the price-sensitive will bear), versioning and deliberately 'damaged' goods, quantity discounts and two-part tariffs, and the airline Saturday-night-stay rule that separates leisure from business travellers.

The design problem is governed by two constraints. The participation constraint ensures each type still wants to buy at all, and pins down the cheap option; the incentive-compatibility (self-selection) constraint ensures the high-value type does not defect to the cheap option, and pins down the expensive one. A common exam trap reverses these.

Coupons look like an inefficient way to cut prices, but because they screen they can be profitable precisely when a blanket price cut would not be: the firm captures the extra sale to redeemers without conceding margin to everyone.

Key takeaways

  • Second-degree = build a menu so customers self-select when type is unobservable
  • Screening devices: coupons, versioning/damaged goods, quantity discounts, two-part tariffs, Saturday-stay
  • Participation constraint pins the cheap option; incentive-compatibility pins the expensive option
  • Coupons can beat a blanket price cut because they concede margin only to the price-sensitive
  • Nonlinear pricing extracts surplus progressively along the demand curve
