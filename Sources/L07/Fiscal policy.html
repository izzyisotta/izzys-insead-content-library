Fiscal Policy: Taxing and Spending to Steer the Economy

Horton and El-Ganainy define fiscal policy as the use of government spending and taxation to pursue stabilisation and long-run growth. From the identity GDP = C + I + G + NX, governments control G directly and influence C, I and NX through taxes and transfers; raising aggregate demand is "loose/expansionary," cutting it "tight/contractionary." The 2008-09 crisis revived activist fiscal policy after decades of laissez-faire retreat.

Governments stabilise through two mechanisms: automatic stabilisers (taxes fall and transfers rise in downturns without new legislation, larger in advanced economies and free of implementation lags) and discretionary stimulus (new spending or tax cuts, which can be targeted but suffer design and reversal lags). The response depends on fiscal space: countries lacking creditor confidence, or with high inflation or external deficits, may be unable to stimulate effectively.

The multiplier governs effectiveness: larger when leakage (saving, imports) is low, monetary policy is accommodative, and the fiscal position looks sustainable; spending multipliers exceed those of tax cuts, and open economies have smaller ones. Stimulus should be timely, targeted and temporary. For MGE this is the counterpart to monetary policy and the foundation for debt-sustainability discussion.

Key takeaways

  • GDP = C + I + G + NX: government controls G directly and influences C, I, NX via taxes and transfers
  • Automatic stabilisers (progressive taxes, unemployment benefits) act without lags; discretionary stimulus is targeted but slow to deploy and reverse
  • Fiscal space (creditor confidence, low inflation, manageable deficits) determines whether a government can stimulate effectively
  • Multipliers are larger with low leakage (saving/imports), accommodative monetary policy, and credible sustainability; spending > tax cuts; smaller in open economies
  • Good stimulus is timely, targeted and temporary; persistent large deficits erode the confidence stimulus relies on
