Foundations of International Relations Theory

This is the opening of McGlinchey, Walters and Gold's E-International Relations Foundations textbook, the required primer on IR theory. Its central image is that theories are like maps: each one plots only the variables its theorists think matter (states, power, ideas, class, gender) and leaves the rest out, so that no single theory is "true" but each clarifies a different slice of a complicated world. Studying IR without theory, the authors warn, is like travelling without a map; you may arrive somewhere interesting but cannot say how you got there or defend your route.

The text lays out a spectrum from traditional theories through middle-ground to critical approaches. Liberalism (Kant, Wilson, democratic-peace theory) is optimistic, expecting trade, democracy and institutions to tame conflict. Realism is pessimistic, built on Hobbesian anarchy and the state's need for security and survival. The English School and constructivism (Wendt's "anarchy is what states make of it") occupy the middle, stressing shared norms and ideas. Critical theories, Marxism, postcolonialism, feminism, poststructuralism, challenge the field's mainstream assumptions. For BSPE this grounds the IR session, giving students the toolkit to read state behaviour through competing lenses.

Key takeaways

  • Theories are simplifying maps: each foregrounds different variables, none is the whole picture.
  • Liberalism is optimistic (democracy, trade, institutions); realism is pessimistic (anarchy, power, survival).
  • The English School and constructivism sit in the middle, emphasising shared norms and the power of ideas.
  • Critical theories (Marxism, postcolonialism, feminism, poststructuralism) contest mainstream IR's core assumptions.
