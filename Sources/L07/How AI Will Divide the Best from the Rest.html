L07 · AI, work and inequality

How AI Will Divide the Best from the Rest

This Economist piece anchors the closing lecture on technology, the labour market and inequality. It challenges the optimistic view (voiced by tech leaders at the 2025 Paris summit) that AI will be a great equaliser. Early studies did suggest levelling: generative AI raised novice call-centre productivity by 34 percent and helped weaker writers and law students most, by transferring best practice downward.

But newer evidence on complex tasks points the other way. When AI assists research, management or investing, high performers gain far more because evaluating and filtering AI output requires expertise and judgment. An AI materials-discovery tool nearly doubled top scientists' output while doing nothing for the bottom third; strong Kenyan entrepreneurs raised profits over 15 percent while weak ones lost ground; sophisticated investors beat novices using the same tool. Meanwhile a job is a bundle of tasks: AI commoditises some (like cashiers' work) and augments others, so low-skill roles face wage stagnation. The article's conclusion, that AI will likely widen workforce divides just as past technological revolutions favoured the skilled, is the lecture's bridge between technological change and the inequality theme that opened the course.

Key takeaways

  • The equaliser hope: AI boosted novices most on simple tasks (e.g. +34 percent for call-centre rookies)
  • On complex tasks the effect reverses: high performers gain most because judgment is needed to use AI well
  • Evidence: top scientists' output doubled, strong entrepreneurs and sophisticated investors pulled ahead
  • A job is a bundle of tasks; AI commoditises low-skill ones, stagnating those wages
  • Likely net effect: AI widens, not narrows, workforce divides, echoing past tech revolutions
