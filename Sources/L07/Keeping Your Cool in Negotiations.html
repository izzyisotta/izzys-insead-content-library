S7–8 · emotion as information

Keeping Your Cool in Negotiations

Kolb's piece addresses the management of emotion in the negotiation room. Its core claim is that emotions are not noise to be suppressed but information — your own feelings signal what matters to you, and the other side's signal what matters to them. Ignoring or bottling up emotion wastes that signal and often makes you reactive.

The practical discipline is self-regulation: notice the emotional charge, name it, and create a pause before responding, so that a provocation or a hardball tactic does not pull you into an unplanned concession or an escalating fight. Preparation and a clear BATNA are themselves emotional anchors that keep you steady under pressure.

It also covers reading and responding to the other party's emotions constructively — acknowledging frustration can defuse it — which connects to the relationship and communication elements of the course. Staying cool is not coldness; it is the composure that lets you keep choosing your approach rather than being provoked into one.

Key takeaways

  • Treat emotions as information, not noise to suppress
  • Your feelings flag your interests; theirs flag the counterpart's
  • Self-regulate: notice, name and pause before responding to provocation
  • Preparation and a clear BATNA are emotional anchors under pressure
  • Acknowledging the other side's emotion can defuse it
This is a synthesis written for revision, not the original text.
Source: D. M. Kolb, ‘Keeping Your Cool in Negotiations’, 2004
