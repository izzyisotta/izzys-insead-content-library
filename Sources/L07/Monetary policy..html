Monetary Policy: Stabilising Prices and Output

Mathai explains monetary policy as adjusting the money supply to balance inflation and output stabilisation. Money is neutral in the long run (output is fixed, so more money just raises prices), but because prices and wages are sticky in the short run, monetary policy can move real activity, making it the countercyclical "first line of defence" (faster and more reversible than fiscal policy). Central banks balance price and output goals; the Fed has an explicit dual mandate.

Independence matters because of time inconsistency: a politically-controlled central bank is tempted to engineer inflation surprises for short-run output gains, but the public anticipates this, creating an inflation bias. Delegating to an insulated central bank yields lower, more stable inflation. Operationally, banks conduct open-market operations to steer a short-term policy rate, which transmits through several channels: interest-rate, balance-sheet, bank-lending, exchange-rate, and crucially expectations (the self-fulfilling component anchored by credible communication).

When rates hit the zero lower bound, central banks turn to unconventional tools: quantitative easing (buying assets to expand the balance sheet and money supply) and credit easing (targeting the composition of assets to unblock specific markets), both of which raise questions about independence and the boundary with industrial policy. Core MGE material on how and why central banks act.

Key takeaways

  • Money is long-run neutral but short-run sticky prices let monetary policy affect real output; it is the preferred countercyclical tool
  • Dual objective: balancing inflation and output/employment (the Fed's explicit dual mandate)
  • Central bank independence solves the time-inconsistency/inflation-bias problem and delivers lower, more stable inflation
  • Transmission channels: interest-rate, balance-sheet, bank-lending, exchange-rate, and expectations (credible communication anchors inflation)
  • At the zero lower bound: quantitative easing (balance-sheet size) and credit easing (asset composition), both straining independence boundaries
