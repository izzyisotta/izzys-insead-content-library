One World, Rival Theories

Jack Snyder's Foreign Policy essay surveys the three classic lenses of international relations and argues that, even in a changed post-9/11 world, none is obsolete and each works best as a check on the others. Realism sees international affairs as a struggle for power among self-interested states in an anarchic system; it explains the persistence of conflict and America's forceful response to 9/11, but struggles with non-state actors like al-Qaeda and with the absence of any balancing coalition against US power. Liberalism, descending from Kant and Wilson, holds that democracy, trade and institutions pull the world toward cooperation, but its "divided house" warns that democratic transitions are often violent and that democracies are prone to crusading.

Constructivism (a revived idealism) treats ideas, norms and identities as the building blocks of world politics, explaining the influence of transnational networks and of political religion alike. Snyder's core point: none of the three predicts change well, so the prudent move is to use each as a corrective on the others' blind spots. For BSPE this reading supplies the theoretical vocabulary, realism versus liberalism versus constructivism, behind the course's analysis of how states and firms behave.

Key takeaways

  • Realism: anarchy and the balance of power; states pursue security and self-interest, war is recurrent.
  • Liberalism: democracy, trade and institutions foster cooperation, but transitions to democracy can be violent and destabilising.
  • Constructivism: ideas, norms and identities, not fixed interests, shape state behaviour and can change over time.
  • All three theories are weak at predicting change; their real value is acting as mutual checks against simplistic policy.
