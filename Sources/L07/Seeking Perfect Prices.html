L7 · pricing experimentation

“Seeking Perfect Prices”

This article makes the case for experimental, data-driven pricing over intuition and cost-plus rules of thumb. Its centrepiece is the kind of randomised field experiment associated with the ZipRecruiter / Dube–Misra work: by quietly charging different prices to comparable customers, a firm can trace out its own demand curve rather than guessing it.

The recurring discovery is that firms systematically under-price: demand turns out less elastic than managers fear, so raising price raises profit with little volume loss. That is the 'eat your free lunch' point — money was being left on the table because nobody had measured the elasticity the Lerner rule depends on.

The deeper lesson links straight to the markup theory: the optimal price depends on the elasticity of demand, and the only reliable way to learn that elasticity is to vary price and watch what happens, treating pricing as an ongoing experiment rather than a one-off calculation.

Key takeaways

  • Treat pricing as an experiment: vary price across comparable customers to estimate demand
  • Firms routinely under-price because they overestimate how elastic demand is
  • 'Eat your free lunch': raising price often raises profit with little volume loss
  • Experiments supply the elasticity the Lerner/Golden Pricing Rule needs
  • Empirical pricing beats cost-plus intuition
