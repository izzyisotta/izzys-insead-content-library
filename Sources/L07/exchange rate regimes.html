L7 · Exchange-rate regimes

Choosing an Exchange Rate Regime

Ghosh and Ostry review decades of IMF evidence on whether countries should fix, float or choose an intermediate regime. The pendulum swung from 1990s pegs (used to stabilise after liberalisation) to the post-crisis bipolar prescription (only hard pegs or free floats), which then collapsed: Argentina's 2002 currency-board failure discredited the hard end, while "fear of floating" (worry over imported inflation and foreign-currency balance-sheet effects) meant few would truly float.

Using both de jure and de facto classifications over 1980-2006, the 2009 review finds nuanced trade-offs. Pegs deliver the best inflation performance (the credibility of a formal commitment anchors expectations), and rigid regimes foster lower volatility and deeper trade/FDI links. Intermediate regimes deliver the best growth (about half a percentage point a year), balancing the benefits of pegs against overvaluation risk.

But rigidity has three costs: it constrains other macro policy (the impossible trinity: a peg, open capital account and independent monetary policy cannot coexist, and countercyclical fiscal policy is also limited), it raises crisis susceptibility, and it impedes external adjustment. For MGE this is the core regime-choice framework, illustrated by European emerging markets that grew fast under rigid regimes but built dangerous imbalances.

Key takeaways

  • The bipolar view (only hard pegs or free floats) collapsed: Argentina 2002 killed the hard end, 'fear of floating' the soft end
  • Pegs deliver the best inflation outcomes, mainly via the credibility of a formal (de jure) commitment anchoring expectations
  • Intermediate regimes deliver the best growth (~+0.5pp/yr), balancing peg benefits against overvaluation risk
  • Costs of rigidity: the impossible trinity (peg + open capital account + independent monetary policy is impossible) plus constrained countercyclical fiscal policy
  • Rigid regimes also raise crisis susceptibility and impede timely external adjustment; using both de jure and de facto classifications reveals these trade-offs
