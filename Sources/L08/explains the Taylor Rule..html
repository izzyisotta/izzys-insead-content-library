L8 · Inflation & the Taylor Rule

The Taylor Rule and the Fed's Inflation Target

Taylor defends rules-based monetary policy and explains the construction of his eponymous rule, i = π + 0.5y + 0.5(π − π*) + r*, with the inflation target π* = 2% and the equilibrium real rate r* = 2%, implying a 4% equilibrium nominal rate. Neither parameter was arbitrary: 2% inflation reflected measurement bias and the zero-bound buffer, and r* tracked trend real GDP growth a little above 2%. The rule prescribes raising rates more than one-for-one with inflation above target and responding to the output gap.

His central claim is that the framework, not the specific number, matters: the Great Moderation reflected rules-consistent policy and the Great Recession partly reflected deviating from it. Proposals to raise the target to 4% (Blanchard, Summers) or switch to price-level/nominal-GDP targeting to address the zero lower bound can all be evaluated simply by changing π* within a rule; estimates of a lower r* may be temporary "fog" from unconventional policy.

Taylor cautions against two practical pitfalls: treating any small undershoot of 2% as demanding maximum easing (risking bubbles), and over-fixating on π* at the expense of other rule parameters and the discipline of having a rule at all. Core MGE material on monetary-policy rules.

Key takeaways

  • Taylor rule: i = π + 0.5y + 0.5(π − π*) + r*, originally with π* = 2% and r* = 2% (so equilibrium nominal rate = 4%)
  • 2% target chosen for inflation-measurement bias and a zero-lower-bound buffer, not arbitrarily; respond >1-for-1 to inflation above target
  • Taylor's thesis: rules-based policy underpinned the Great Moderation; deviating from rules worsened the Great Recession
  • Alternative proposals (higher target, price-level or nominal-GDP targeting) can all be assessed by plugging values into a rule
  • Pitfalls: over-easing on tiny inflation undershoots (bubble risk) and over-emphasising π* relative to other rule parameters
