The Art of the Deal: Is Ethics in the Picture?

This article applies a fairness lens to negotiation tactics, insisting that effectiveness and legitimacy are different questions. The fact that a move works — that it extracts a better outcome — does not make it right; many manipulative tactics are effective precisely because they exploit the other side's trust or information disadvantage.

It surveys ethically dubious moves (manufactured pressure, misleading framings, exploiting a counterpart's mistake) and asks negotiators to test them against standards beyond self-interest: would the tactic survive disclosure, reciprocity, or being done to you? Reputation, relationship and long-run trust all push toward restraint.

The conclusion reinforces the course's ethics thread: a skilled negotiator should choose tactics that are both effective and defensible, because short-term wins bought with sharp practice tend to be repaid in lost trust and forfeited future deals. Ethics is not a constraint bolted on afterwards but part of the strategic calculation.

Key takeaways

  • Effectiveness and legitimacy are different questions
  • A tactic working does not make it ethical — it may just exploit trust
  • Test tactics by disclosure, reciprocity, and 'would I accept this done to me?'
  • Sharp practice is usually repaid in lost trust and future deals
  • Choose tactics that are both effective and defensible
