L9 · Zero lower bound & QE

The New Tools of Monetary Policy (Bernanke)

In his 2020 AEA presidential address, Bernanke assesses the unconventional tools central banks adopted once policy rates hit the effective lower bound (ELB) after 2008: large-scale asset purchases (quantitative easing) and forward guidance. Reviewing the evidence, he argues these tools worked: QE lowered long-term yields through portfolio-balance and signalling effects, and credible forward guidance shaped rate expectations, together providing meaningful stimulus when short rates could fall no further.

His central quantitative claim is that QE and forward guidance can together substitute for roughly 3 percentage points of conventional rate cuts. So when the neutral rate (r*) is around 2-3%, the combined toolkit gives policymakers effective room equivalent to a higher starting point, partially offsetting the constraint of a low r* and the more frequent ELB episodes it implies.

Bernanke nonetheless cautions the new tools may be insufficient in a severe downturn or where r* is very low, and he is open to complementary frameworks, makeup strategies such as price-level or average-inflation targeting, and a supporting role for fiscal policy. For MGE this is the key reference on post-2008 unconventional policy and the limits of monetary policy at the lower bound.

Key takeaways

  • The effective lower bound (ELB) constrains conventional rate cuts; the new tools are QE (asset purchases) and forward guidance
  • QE lowers long-term rates via portfolio-balance and signalling channels; forward guidance steers rate expectations
  • Bernanke's estimate: QE + forward guidance ≈ 3 percentage points of extra rate-cut equivalent, easing the low-r* constraint
  • A lower neutral rate (r*) means more frequent ELB episodes, raising the importance of unconventional tools
  • Tools may be insufficient in severe downturns; makeup strategies (price-level/average-inflation targeting) and fiscal support may be needed
