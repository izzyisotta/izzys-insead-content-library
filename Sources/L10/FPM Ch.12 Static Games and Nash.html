FPM Ch.12 · Static Games & Nash

Part IV introduces game theory for strategic interaction. A game is specified by its players, the strategies available to each, and the payoffs for every combination of choices, usually displayed in a payoff matrix for simultaneous moves.

The first solution tool is dominance: a dominant strategy is best regardless of what rivals do, a dominated strategy is never best, and iterated deletion of dominated strategies can sometimes solve the game outright. The central concept is the Nash equilibrium — a strategy profile in which no player can do better by deviating unilaterally, given what everyone else is doing.

The prisoner's dilemma is the canonical illustration: individually rational defection produces a jointly worse outcome, which is exactly the tension that makes cartels and price competition unstable in the chapters that follow.

Key takeaways

  • A game = players, strategies, payoffs (payoff matrix for simultaneous moves)
  • Dominant strategy: best whatever rivals do; dominated: never best — delete iteratively
  • Nash equilibrium: no player gains by deviating unilaterally
  • Prisoner's dilemma: individually rational choices give a jointly worse outcome
  • This tension underlies cartel instability and the Bertrand trap to come
