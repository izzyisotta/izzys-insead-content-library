L10 · Managing high inflation

The 2021-22 Inflation Surge and the ECB's Response

In this Marjolin Lecture, ECB chief economist Philip Lane uses macroeconomic models to explain why the 2021-22 inflation surge was so badly under-forecast and whether the policy response was appropriate. Forecast errors were enormous (the December 2021 projection missed end-2022 inflation by ~8 percentage points). Decomposition shows roughly half the error came from wrong technical assumptions about energy and food prices, repeatedly revised up after Russia's invasion of Ukraine, with the rest from core-inflation surprises and statistical uncertainty (models estimated on a low-volatility 1995-2016 sample understated possible shock sizes).

Structural VARs identify external supply shocks (energy, commodities, supply-chain bottlenecks) as the main initial driver, justifying the early "looking-through" stance; demand, including reopening of contact-intensive services, mattered more in 2022 as inflation broadened. Non-linearities amplified the surge: firms raised prices more frequently, effectively steepening the Phillips curve, and short-term expectations became more reactive.

Policy counterfactuals (NAWM, MMR, ECB-BASE) show that with perfect foresight rates should have risen earlier and harder (peak inflation ~8% not 10%) but at large output cost; given real-time information, the actual path was broadly optimal, and tightening kept inflation ~2pp lower in 2023-26. For MGE this is the definitive supply-vs-demand and policy-evaluation case study.

Key takeaways

  • The 2021-22 surge was massively under-forecast (~8pp error for end-2022); about half traced to wrong energy/food price assumptions, not model failure
  • External supply shocks (energy, commodities, bottlenecks, Ukraine war) drove the initial surge, justifying the early 'looking-through' stance
  • Demand (post-pandemic reopening of contact-intensive services) broadened inflation in 2022, warranting aggressive tightening
  • Non-linearities: more frequent price changes effectively steepened the Phillips curve and made short-term expectations more reactive
  • Counterfactuals: perfect foresight implied earlier/harder hikes (but big output cost); actual policy was broadly optimal and cut inflation ~2pp in 2023-26
