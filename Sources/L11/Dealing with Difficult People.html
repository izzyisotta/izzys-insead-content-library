Dealing with Difficult People

This PON report collects tactics for counterparts who are genuinely hard to deal with — people who take things personally, negotiate in bad faith, or use aggression and manipulation. Its starting point is diagnostic: distinguish a difficult situation from a difficult person, because much apparent difficulty is driven by circumstances, fear or constraints rather than character.

For genuinely adversarial counterparts it advises not mirroring the behaviour: stay anchored to your BATNA and interests, slow the pace, and refuse to be drawn into an emotional tit-for-tat. Naming the behaviour, reframing personal attacks as problems to solve, and using objective standards all shift the encounter back onto substance.

It also stresses protecting yourself against bad faith — verify claims, get commitments in writing, and keep your alternative strong — while leaving the other side a face-saving path. The report supplies the practical counterpart-management toolkit behind the course's difficult-conversations material.

Key takeaways

  • Separate a difficult situation from a genuinely difficult person
  • Don't mirror aggression; stay anchored to your BATNA and interests
  • Reframe personal attacks as shared problems; use objective standards
  • Protect against bad faith: verify claims, get commitments in writing
  • Leave the other side a face-saving path
This is a synthesis written for revision, not the original text.
Source: M. Bazerman et al., ‘Dealing with Difficult People’, PON Report, 2013
