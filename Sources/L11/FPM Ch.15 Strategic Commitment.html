L11 · sequential games

FPM Ch.15 · Strategic Commitment

Sequential games are solved by backward induction on the game tree: work out the last mover's best choice, fold it back, and repeat, yielding the subgame-perfect equilibrium. This rules out equilibria that rely on threats a rational player would never actually carry out.

That distinction — credible versus non-credible threats — is the chapter's core. A threat is only effective if executing it would be in your interest when the moment comes; otherwise rivals ignore it. The way to make a threat credible is a commitment device that changes your own payoffs by removing your future options: burning bridges, pre-emptive capacity investment, binding contracts, or a public reputation.

The counter-intuitive lesson is that limiting your own choices can strengthen your position, and that a first mover can shape the game in its favour. The centipede game shows the unsettling flip side: strict backward induction predicts immediate stopping, which players rarely do.

Key takeaways

  • Solve sequential games by backward induction → subgame-perfect equilibrium
  • Only credible threats matter; a threat must be in your interest to execute when the time comes
  • Commitment devices make threats credible by removing your own options
  • Limiting your own choices can strengthen your strategic position; first-mover advantage
  • Centipede game: pure backward induction predicts immediate stopping (rarely observed)
