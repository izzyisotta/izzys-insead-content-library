L12 · oligopoly

FPM Ch.13 · Imperfect Competition

Oligopoly sits between monopoly and perfect competition, and the outcome depends sharply on what firms compete over. Under Bertrand competition (firms set prices, products identical), undercutting drives price all the way down to marginal cost even with only two firms — the 'Bertrand trap' or paradox, in which competition is fierce despite high concentration.

Under Cournot competition (firms set quantities), each firm's best output depends on the other's, captured by reaction (best-response) functions; their intersection is the Cournot-Nash equilibrium. Here price lands above marginal cost but below the monopoly level, and it falls toward the competitive price as the number of firms rises.

The practical lesson is that product differentiation softens price competition: distinguishing your product escapes the Bertrand trap and restores margin, which is why real oligopolists invest so heavily in differentiation and brand.

Key takeaways

  • Bertrand (price, homogeneous goods): two firms can be enough to drive price to MC — the Bertrand trap
  • Cournot (quantity): price between monopoly and competition, set by reaction-function intersection
  • Cournot price falls toward MC as the number of firms grows
  • Differentiation softens price competition and escapes the Bertrand trap
  • Reaction functions show each firm's best response to the rival's choice
