Does Eating Together Improve Negotiations?

This is a behavioural-science aside in Soyer's style, showing how small situational choices shift negotiation outcomes. The headline finding is that sharing a meal while negotiating tends to increase cooperation and the size of the joint outcome, compared with negotiating without food or while eating separately.

The proposed mechanisms are familiar from social psychology: eating together builds rapport and trust, synchronises mood, and subtly frames the interaction as collaborative rather than adversarial. Shared consumption signals a common endeavour, which makes value-creating moves easier to attempt.

The broader lesson is that negotiation is embedded in context: factors that look incidental — the setting, timing, whether you break bread together — measurably affect behaviour and results. A savvy negotiator manages these situational levers deliberately, reinforcing the course's relationship-and-trust theme that how you create the conditions for a deal matters as much as the substance you bring to it.

Key takeaways

  • Sharing a meal while negotiating tends to raise cooperation and joint gains
  • Mechanisms: rapport, trust, synchronised mood, a collaborative frame
  • Shared consumption signals a common endeavour
  • Negotiation is embedded in context: setting and timing matter
  • Manage situational levers deliberately, not just the substance
