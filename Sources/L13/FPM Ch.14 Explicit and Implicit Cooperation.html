L13 · cartels and collusion

FPM Ch.14 · Explicit & Implicit Cooperation

A cartel tries to behave like a single monopolist, setting total output where joint marginal revenue equals marginal cost; the collusive quantity is below the Cournot quantity and the collusive price above it. The problem is that this split sits off each firm's reaction function, so every member has a private incentive to deviate and expand — which is why a one-shot cartel is inherently unstable.

Repetition can sustain cooperation. With a long enough horizon and a high enough discount factor, a grim-trigger strategy (revert permanently to competition after any cheating) makes the future punishment outweigh the one-period gain from defecting, so tacit collusion becomes a Nash equilibrium of the repeated game.

Various facilitating devices stabilise tacit collusion without an illegal agreement: price transparency, price-matching guarantees, most-favoured-nation clauses and announced list prices all make deviation easier to detect and punish.

Key takeaways

  • Cartel acts like a monopolist: Q_cartel < Q_Cournot, P_cartel > P_Cournot
  • The collusive split is off the reaction functions, so each firm is tempted to deviate — unstable in one shot
  • Repetition + high discount factor + grim trigger can sustain tacit collusion
  • Facilitating devices: transparency, price-matching, MFN clauses, list prices
  • Tacit collusion needs no explicit (illegal) agreement
