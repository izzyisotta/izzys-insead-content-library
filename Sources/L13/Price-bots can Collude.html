L13 · algorithmic collusion

“Price-bots can Collude”

This piece is the modern face of the cartel-stability problem. Increasingly, prices are set by pricing algorithms that observe rivals' prices and adjust in real time. The unsettling finding from experiments and theory is that such bots can learn to sustain supra-competitive prices — effectively tacit collusion — without their owners ever agreeing to anything.

Conceptually it is the repeated-game result running on autopilot: fast, transparent, automated price-matching makes deviation instantly detectable and punishable, which is exactly the condition that stabilises tacit collusion. The algorithms reach the collusive outcome by trial and error rather than by instruction.

The hard part is for competition policy: existing law targets explicit agreement, but here there is no communication and no smoking gun, only software independently converging on high prices. The article frames the open regulatory question of how to treat collusion that emerges from code rather than from a meeting.

Key takeaways

  • Pricing algorithms can learn to hold prices above the competitive level — tacit collusion by machine
  • It is the repeated-game logic automated: transparency + fast reaction stabilise collusion
  • No explicit agreement or communication is required
  • Antitrust struggles because there is no smoking-gun agreement to point to
  • Modern, automated version of the cartel-stability chapter
